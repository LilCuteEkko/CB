Vital Sign Monitoring During Exercise
Every exercise session should begin with baseline vital signs and include monitoring throughout. Abnormal responses are your earliest warning of cardiac, pulmonary, or metabolic decompensation. Know when to stop.

Immediate Stop Criteria — Terminate Exercise

SBP drops >10 mmHg with increasing workload • SBP >250 or DBP >115 mmHg • SpO2 <88% • New onset chest pain, pressure, or tightness • Severe dyspnea disproportionate to exertion • Dizziness, lightheadedness, near-syncope • Pallor, cyanosis, cold/clammy skin • New arrhythmia (irregular pulse) • Ataxia or confusion • Patient requests to stop. When in doubt, STOP.

Heart Rate Training Zones
Heart rate is the most common intensity metric. Know the formulas, their limitations, and when NOT to use HR (beta-blockers, pacemakers, AFib).

HR Calculation Methods

▼
MethodFormulaProsCons
Age-Predicted HRmax220 − ageSimple, quick, no equipment neededStandard deviation ±10–12 bpm. Inaccurate for older adults, deconditioned, or athletes. Least accurate method
Karvonen (HRR)THR = (HRmax − HRrest) × %intensity + HRrestAccounts for resting HR (fitness level). More individualized. %HRR correlates closely with %VO2RStill relies on predicted HRmax unless measured. Gold standard calculation for clinical use
Measured HRmaxFrom graded exercise test (GXT)Most accurate. Uses actual patient dataRequires exercise testing equipment and physician supervision for high-risk patients
Karvonen Example

Patient: 50 years old, resting HR = 70 bpm. Target: 60–80% intensity.
HRmax = 220 − 50 = 170 bpm. HRR = 170 − 70 = 100 bpm.
Lower target: (100 × 0.60) + 70 = 130 bpm
Upper target: (100 × 0.80) + 70 = 150 bpm
Training zone = 130–150 bpm

When NOT to Use Heart Rate

Beta-blockers blunt HR response — patient cannot reach target HR. Use RPE (Borg 12–14) or METs instead. Pacemaker (rate-responsive or fixed-rate) — HR is artificially controlled. Atrial fibrillation — HR is irregular and unreliable. Autonomic neuropathy (diabetic) — HR response is blunted. Transplant heart — denervated, relies on circulating catecholamines for HR increase (delayed response). In all these cases: use RPE as primary intensity monitor.

RPE & Intensity Monitoring
Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE) is the most versatile intensity tool — works for every patient including those on beta-blockers, with pacemakers, or with irregular rhythms. Teach it to every patient.

Why 6–20?

Borg designed the scale so that RPE × 10 ≈ heart rate in young healthy adults. RPE 13 × 10 = ~130 bpm. This correlation breaks down with age and medications but the scale itself remains valid as a subjective measure.

Talk Test — Simplest Intensity Monitor

Can talk comfortably in full sentences = light/moderate (<VT1). Can talk in short phrases only = moderate/vigorous (at VT1). Cannot talk at all = vigorous/near-max (>VT2). VT1 = first ventilatory threshold. The talk test correlates well with ventilatory thresholds and is easiest for patients to use independently.

1 MET Definition

1 MET = 3.5 mL·kg&supmin;¹·min&supmin;¹ oxygen consumption = resting metabolic rate. A 4-MET activity uses 4× resting oxygen consumption. Key clinical point: older or deconditioned adults working at the same MET level as younger adults are at a higher % of their VO2max — same absolute intensity = greater relative intensity.

ACSM Exercise Prescription Guidelines
The ACSM FITT-VP framework is the clinical standard for exercise dosage. These are the numbers you need for the NPTE and for writing every exercise prescription in clinical practice.
Aerobic Exercise Prescription
Clinical Example 1 — Aerobic Rx for Deconditioned SNF Patient

72-year-old female, post-hip fracture ORIF, deconditioned, resting HR 82, BP 138/78, on metoprolol (beta-blocker). Rx: Frequency 5x/week. Intensity: RPE 11–13 (cannot use HR due to beta-blocker). Time: start 10 min, progress to 20–30 min over 4 weeks. Type: seated NuStep → standing recumbent bike → hallway ambulation. Volume target: increase 10%/week. Key: use RPE not HR because of beta-blocker. Monitor BP response — SBP should rise proportionally, not drop.

Clinical Example 2 — Resistance Rx for Post-TKA Quad Strengthening

65-year-old male, 6 weeks post-TKA, cleared for progressive resistance. Goal: rebuild quad strength to restore stair negotiation. Rx: Frequency 3x/week (48 hr rest between sessions). Intensity: 60–70% estimated 1RM (endurance → hypertrophy). Reps: 10–15. Sets: 3. Rest: 60 sec between sets. Exercises: seated knee extension, leg press (limited ROM per surgeon), wall squats, step-ups (4-inch). Progression: when patient performs 2 extra reps over target on 2 consecutive sessions → increase weight 5%. Key: progress by reps first, then load. Pain >4/10 NPRS during exercise = back off.

Progression Rule

When patient can comfortably perform 1–2 extra reps over target on 2 consecutive sessions, increase load by 2–10%. Upper body: increase 2–5%. Lower body: increase 5–10%. Avoid >10% jumps — injury risk increases sharply.

Exercise Precautions & Contraindications
Know the absolute contraindications (NEVER exercise) vs relative contraindications (may exercise with modifications). These are patient safety fundamentals — tested heavily on NPTE.

Absolute Contraindications to Exercise

▼
DO NOT EXERCISE — Medical Clearance Required

• Acute MI (within 2 days) • Unstable angina not yet stabilized by medical therapy • Uncontrolled cardiac arrhythmias causing symptoms or hemodynamic compromise • Active endocarditis or myocarditis • Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis • Decompensated heart failure • Acute pulmonary embolism or pulmonary infarction • Acute aortic dissection • Acute systemic infection with fever • Uncontrolled diabetes (glucose >300 mg/dL or <70 with symptoms) • Resting SBP >200 or DBP >110 mmHg • Acute deep vein thrombosis

Relative Contraindications — Exercise With Caution

▼
May Exercise with Modifications & Close Monitoring

• Known obstructive left main coronary stenosis • Moderate stenotic valvular heart disease • Electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia) • Tachyarrhythmias or bradyarrhythmias • High-degree AV block • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy • Mental or physical impairment leading to inability to exercise adequately • Uncontrolled hypertension (SBP >180 or DBP >110) • Recent stroke or TIA (timing depends on type/severity)

Warm-Up & Cool-Down Protocols
Every exercise session has 3 phases: warm-up, conditioning (training), and cool-down. Skipping these increases injury risk and adverse cardiac events.
Warm-Up (5–10 minutes)
Purpose

Gradually increases HR, BP, core temperature, muscle blood flow, and nerve conduction velocity. Reduces viscosity of synovial fluid. Prepares the cardiovascular system for increased demand — prevents ischemia from sudden high-intensity work on a cold heart.

Components

General warm-up: 5–10 min of low-intensity aerobic activity at 40–60% HRmax (walking, light cycling, arm ergometer). Dynamic stretching: active movements through ROM (leg swings, arm circles, walking lunges). NOT static stretching before activity — static stretching pre-exercise can reduce force production by 5–10% and does NOT prevent injury.

Cool-Down (5–10 minutes)
Purpose

Gradually returns HR and BP toward resting levels. Prevents venous pooling in lower extremities (which can cause hypotension and syncope if you stop abruptly). Facilitates metabolite clearance. Reduces post-exercise cardiac arrhythmia risk — the post-exercise period is actually the highest-risk time for cardiac events.

Components

Active cool-down: 5–10 min of progressively decreasing intensity (slow walking, gentle cycling). Static stretching: NOW is the appropriate time. Hold 15–30 sec per stretch (60 sec for older adults), 2–4 reps. Muscles are warm = better stretch tolerance and reduced injury risk. Never have a patient sit/lie down immediately after intense exercise — keep them moving until HR is within 20 bpm of resting.

Lifting Mechanics & Safety
Proper body mechanics protect both the patient and the clinician. Teach every patient these principles before progressing to loaded exercise.

Principles of Safe Lifting

▼
Core Principles

• Keep load close to body — moment arm increases exponentially with distance. Holding 10 lbs at arm’s length = 100+ lbs of spinal compressive force
• Maintain neutral spine — lumbar lordosis preserved. Avoid flexion under load. Hinge at hips, not lumbar spine
• Brace core before lifting — Valsalva-lite (not full breath-hold): inhale, brace abdominals as if bracing for a punch, lift on exertion exhale
• Wide base of support — feet shoulder-width, staggered stance for asymmetric loads
• Lift with legs — squat pattern (quads + glutes) not stoop pattern (lumbar erectors)
• Avoid twisting under load — pivot feet to turn, don’t rotate trunk with weight
• Breathe — exhale on exertion (concentric), inhale on return (eccentric). Never hold breath during sustained effort (Valsalva → dangerous BP spike in cardiac patients)

Valsalva Maneuver — When to Avoid

▼
Valsalva Response

Forced expiration against a closed glottis → increases intrathoracic pressure → brief BP spike (can exceed 300/200 mmHg in heavy lifters) followed by BP drop when released. Dangerous in: hypertension, cardiac disease, cerebral aneurysm, glaucoma (increases intraocular pressure), post-surgical patients, pregnancy. Teach patients to exhale continuously during exertion — counting aloud or saying “blow out the candles” prevents inadvertent Valsalva.

Recovery Science
Recovery is when adaptation occurs. Without adequate recovery, training produces injury instead of improvement. Understand the mechanisms, timelines, and evidence for recovery strategies.

Overtraining Syndrome

▼
Signs & Symptoms

Performance: decreased performance despite maintained/increased training. Inability to recover between sessions. Physical: persistent fatigue, elevated resting HR, frequent illness/infection, persistent muscle soreness, insomnia despite exhaustion, loss of appetite, weight loss. Psychological: mood disturbance, irritability, depression, loss of motivation, decreased concentration. Management: primary treatment is REST (days to weeks depending on severity). Reduce volume 50–70%. Ensure nutrition adequacy (protein, carbohydrate, hydration). Gradual return to training. This is NOT “mental weakness” — it is a physiological failure of the recovery/adaptation system.

Active Recovery vs Passive Rest

▼
Evidence-Based Recovery Strategies

Active recovery (light walking, swimming, cycling at 30–50% HRmax) is superior to passive rest for: lactate clearance, maintaining blood flow, reducing perceived soreness, maintaining ROM. Do this 24 hours after hard training. Sleep: 7–9 hours. Most important recovery variable. Growth hormone release peaks during deep sleep. Nutrition: 20–40g protein + carbohydrate within 2 hours post-exercise for optimal muscle protein synthesis. Hydration: replace 150% of fluid lost (1.5L per kg of body weight lost during exercise).

Individual on metoprolol (beta-blocker). Describe how to prescribe exercise intensity?
tap
Do NOT use heart rate — beta-blockers blunt the HR response. Use RPE (Borg 12–14 for moderate) or METs as primary intensity guide. Talk test also valid. The patient will never reach their age-predicted HRmax.
Calculate THR using Karvonen for a 60-year-old, resting HR 75, at 50–70% intensity.
tap
HRmax = 220 − 60 = 160. HRR = 160 − 75 = 85. Lower: (85 × 0.50) + 75 = 117.5 bpm. Upper: (85 × 0.70) + 75 = 134.5 bpm. THR zone = 118–135 bpm.
Individual’s SBP drops from 140 to 128 mmHg as treadmill speed increases. What do you do?
tap
STOP exercise immediately. SBP drop >10 mmHg with increasing workload = cardiac pump failure (the heart can’t increase output to match demand). This is a serious sign — notify physician. Do NOT resume exercise.
ACSM resistance training Rx for hypertrophy: load, reps, sets, rest in this scenario?
tap
Load: 67–85% 1RM. Reps: 6–12. Sets: 3–6. Rest: 30–90 seconds. Frequency: 2–4 days/week. Progress when patient can do 1–2 extra reps on 2 consecutive sessions — increase load 2–10%.
Should you do static stretching before exercise in this scenario?
tap
No. Static stretching before exercise reduces force production by 5–10% and does NOT prevent injury. Use dynamic stretching (active movements through ROM) as part of warm-up. Save static stretching for the cool-down when muscles are warm.
Diabetic patient’s glucose is 310 mg/dL before PT. In this case, What do you do?
tap
Do not exercise. Blood glucose >300 mg/dL = risk of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) which exercise can worsen. Check for ketones if possible. Hydrate. Recheck glucose. Notify nursing/physician. Exercise only when glucose is in safe range with no symptoms.
Client with osteoporosis — which exercises should be AVOIDED?
tap
Avoid trunk flexion exercises (crunches, sit-ups, toe touches) — vertebral compression fracture risk. Avoid high-impact, explosive loading, and twisting under load. Emphasize weight-bearing exercise, resistance training, and balance training for fall prevention.
