Shoulder Complex Joints
SC · AC · GH · Scapulothoracic
Key Concept
Full shoulder abduction requires 120° at GH + 60° of scapular upward rotation = 180° total. Movement at SC and AC joints allows scapular rotation. All shoulder girdle joints must function to reach overhead.
Articulations
SC Joint (Sternoclavicular)
Only true synovial joint connecting UE to trunk. Medial clavicle articulates with manubrium. Most common fracture site: junction of middle and lateral thirds of clavicle.
AC Joint (Acromioclavicular)
Lateral clavicle articulates with acromion of scapula. Severe OA at AC or SC joints limits functional shoulder movement.
GH Joint (Glenohumeral)
Head of humerus articulates with glenoid fossa of scapula. Ball-and-socket; inherently unstable — dependent on rotator cuff for dynamic stability.
Scapulothoracic "Articulation"
Not a true joint — posterior attaches to axial skeleton via muscle/fascial connections. Scapula must upward rotate ~60° for full shoulder ABduction.

Clinical Note — Infraspinatus vs. Teres Minor
Same function (ER), same spinal roots (C5–C6), different nerves: Infraspinatus = suprascapular n.; Teres minor = axillary n. This distinction matters clinically for nerve injury localization.

High-Yield Callouts
Exam & Clinical Priorities
Fracture Site
Clavicle fractures: MC at junction of middle and lateral thirds. Surgical neck of humerus: very common fracture site.
Nerve Injuries
Long thoracic n. (C5–C7): serratus anterior palsy → winged scapula, impaired shoulder flexion/abduction.

Axillary n. (C5–C6): deltoid + teres minor palsy → loss of abduction.

Dorsal scapular n. (C4–C5): rhomboids → scapula deviates laterally from spine.

CN XI (Accessory): trapezius palsy → scapular winging (different pattern from serratus).
Key Muscle Facts
• SITS all originate from scapula, insert on tubercles
• PLT all insert in bicipital groove, all = medial rotation
• Supraspinatus tendon passes under acromion (impingement)
• Serratus anterior = upward rotation (critical for overhead)
• Infraspinatus + Teres minor: same function, same roots, different nerves
Scapular Rotation
Full ABduction = 120° GH + 60° scapular upward rotation. Upward rotation requires serratus anterior (intact long thoracic n.) + trapezius (upper + lower). Loss of either = impaired overhead function.

Deltoid pulls humerus superiorly during abduction. The rotator cuff (esp. infra, teres minor, subscapularis) compresses and depresses the humeral head, preventing superior migration. Together they produce a net upward arc of motion.

Though these muscles produce opposing linear forces, no net linear translation occurs. The result is pure upward rotation of the scapula — essential for full overhead reach. Loss of any one = disrupted SHR.
OH ROM Breakdown — All Contributors
Full overhead motion (180°) requires contribution from: SC joint (elevation + posterior rotation), AC joint (upward rotation + posterior tilt), ST joint (60° upward rotation total), GH joint (~120°), and thoracic spine (extension). All must function for normal overhead reach.
Subacromial Space
Contents · Impingement · Clinical
Contents
•Supraspinatus tendon (most vulnerable)
•Subacromial bursa (subdeltoid bursa)
•Long head of biceps brachii tendon
•Subcoracoid + subcoracoid bursa
Impingement
The gap between the humeral head and coracoacromial arch narrows, compressing contents. Pain typically occurs between 60°–120° of abduction — the "painful arc." Chronic irritation → bursitis → tendon thickening → further impingement cycle.
Space Maintained By
Rotator cuff compressive force (joint centering), adequate scapular upward rotation + posterior tilt, normal GH arthrokinematics, and absence of bursal hypertrophy.

What's Required for Normal SHR
1.Osteokinematics — adequate ROM at GH, ST, AC, and SC joints
2.Arthrokinematics — appropriate roll/glide at each joint (especially GH posterior glide during flexion)
3.Force Couples — coordinated activation of upper trap + lower trap + serratus anterior for upward scapular rotation
Assessment Protocol
Ask patient to perform shoulder flexion and abduction. Perform ~5 controlled reps of each. Motion should be initiated by the humerus first at start of range. Scapula should then smoothly coordinate upward rotation, external rotation, and posterior tilt throughout AROM. On return, scapula should smoothly coordinate the opposite motions.
Observe: Amount of AROM · Quality of AROM · Motor control · Posture · Asymmetries
