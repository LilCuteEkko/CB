Brain & CNS

Gross structure of the nervous system — CNS vs. PNS, cerebral lobes, basal ganglia, diencephalon, brainstem, cerebellum, spinal cord, glial cells, and clinical pathology. Essential neuroanatomy for clinical practice.

Organization of the Nervous System
The nervous system divides into the CNS (brain + spinal cord) and the PNS (cranial + spinal nerves). Each major CNS structure has distinct anatomy and function — injury at any level produces predictable clinical deficits.
Central Nervous System (CNS)
  • Brain
    • Cerebrum = Telencephalon + Diencephalon
    • Brainstem = Midbrain + Pons + Medulla
    • Cerebellum
  • Spinal Cord
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
  • Cranial Nerves (CN I–XII)
  • Spinal Nerves (31 pairs)
  • Somatic nervous system (voluntary)
  • Autonomic nervous system (involuntary)
Key Distinction

Myelin in CNS = oligodendrocytes. Myelin in PNS = Schwann cells. Disease targeting each produces different syndromes (MS = CNS; GBS = PNS).

Gray vs. White Matter

Gray matter = cell bodies (neurons). White matter = myelinated axons (circuitry). Function or movement = specific combination/sequence of gray matter columns.

Primary vs. Association Areas
Primary Areas

First place sensory information reaches the cortex OR where motor information exits the cortex. "First in, last out." Direct, point-to-point processing. Smaller but critical.

  • Primary Motor Cortex (Area 4) — precentral gyrus
  • Primary Somatosensory (Areas 3,1,2) — postcentral gyrus
  • Primary Visual — occipital lobe
  • Primary Auditory — temporal lobe
Association Areas

Where integration of sensory and motor information occurs. Compare and combine inputs from multiple modalities. Make up the vast majority (~75%) of the cerebral cortex. Basis of cognition — awareness, perception, thinking, knowledge, memory.

Cerebral Cortex — Five Lobes
Each lobe is named for the overlying bone. The insula is buried behind the lateral sulcus. All lobes have both primary and association areas.

Motor & Sensory Homunculi
Motor Homunculus — Area 4

Map of the precentral gyrus showing which areas of cortex control which body regions. Key features:

  • Areas with finer motor control = larger cortical representation
  • Face and hands have disproportionately large areas
  • Supplies muscles of the contralateral side (due to pyramidal decussation at medulla)
  • Right brain controls left body; left brain controls right body
Sensory Homunculus — Areas 3,1,2

Map of the postcentral gyrus showing which areas receive input from each body region. Key features:

  • More sensitive body regions = more cortical space
  • Proportional to number of receptors in that body region
  • Hands and lips have disproportionately large areas
  • CNS adapts: neuroplasticity can change homuncular maps with use/training
Lobes — Functions, Areas & Clinical Notes
Each lobe has primary and association areas. Wernicke's and Broca's areas are the two critical language centers. Memory is distributed across multiple structures.

Frontal Lobe

  • Primary Motor (Area 4) — precentral gyrus; motor homunculus; contralateral control
  • Premotor Area (Area 6) — motor association; plans movement sequences
  • Broca's Area — dominant hemisphere (L in 95%); generates motor program for larynx, tongue, lips; premotor area for language; transmits to primary motor cortex for speech output
  • Frontal Eye Field (Area 8) — voluntary eye movements
Prefrontal Cortex

Functions: Decision-making, planning, moral judgment, short-term/working memory, emotional control.
Damage: Perseveration — inability to stop doing something (sandpapering through a table; continuing a topic after conversation moves on).
Classic case: Phineas Gage — ventromedial frontal lobe destruction → personality change to irreverent, fitful behavior.

Parietal Lobe

  • Primary Somatosensory (Areas 3,1,2) — postcentral gyrus; sensory homunculus
  • Somatosensory Association Area — interpret sensory info; vast majority of cortex
  • Proprioception — position of limbs
  • Location of touch or pain
  • Shape, weight, and texture of objects
  • Wernicke's area is at the confluence of temporal, parietal, and occipital lobes
Sensory Association — Somesthetic

The somesthetic association area integrates sensory modalities — allowing you to recognize objects by touch without vision, and to know where your limbs are in space without looking. Damage → astereognosis (inability to identify objects by touch).

Temporal Lobe

  • Primary Auditory Area — input from cochlea (via CN VIII)
  • Auditory Association Area — remember names of music, identify voices
  • Wernicke's Area — left hemisphere in most people; permits recognition of spoken AND written language; creates a plan of speech; at confluence of temporal, parietal, and occipital lobes
  • Face recognition — faces recognized in temporal lobe (fusiform face area)
Language Centers — Broca vs. Wernicke

Broca's (frontal, L): generates the motor program for speech. Damage → Broca's aphasia: non-fluent, effortful speech, understands language. Wernicke's (temporal, L): comprehension of language. Damage → Wernicke's aphasia: fluent but nonsensical speech, cannot understand. Lesion to Wernicke's may allow describing a pencil but not finding the word "pencil."

Occipital Lobe

  • Primary Visual Area (Area 17) — calcarine sulcus; initial visual processing
  • Visual Association Area — identifies the things we see
  • Note: Faces are recognized in the temporal lobe, not occipital
Visual Pathway

Lateral geniculate body of thalamus → visual cortex (occipital). Thalamus is the relay point for all sensory input to cortex — vision included (CN I = olfaction is the one exception that bypasses thalamus).

Insula (Insular Cortex)

  • Location: Behind lateral sulcus — hidden, must retract temporal lobe to see
  • Gustatory cortex — taste
  • Emotional aspects of pain — the "suffering" component of pain experience
Limbic System & Memory
Limbic System Components

Loop of cortical structures surrounding deep brain

  • Hippocampus — declarative memory; required to put facts into long-term memory; lesions → inability to form NEW memories (anterograde amnesia); no influence on recalling old memories; larger in people with better memories; can change size with use
  • Amygdala — emotional memory; fear; aversive learning
  • Fornix — connects hippocampus to hypothalamus and other structures
  • Cingulate Gyrus — mood; emotional processing

Amnesia Types

Anterograde amnesia: cannot store NEW data (hippocampal damage). Retrograde amnesia: cannot remember OLD data. H.M. case study: bilateral hippocampal lesion → classic anterograde amnesia — could not form new memories but recalled pre-surgical events.

White Matter & Basal Ganglia
White matter is the wiring of the brain — myelinated axon bundles organized into three types. The basal ganglia are deep gray matter nuclei that refine motor output through a brake/disinhibition mechanism.
White Matter — Three Types
Commissures (Transverse)

Connect left ↔ right hemispheres. Site where fibers cross to the contralateral side.

  • Corpus Callosum — major interhemispheric connection; 4 regions: Rostrum (anterior-most tip) → Genu (knee; frontal fibers) → Body (main trunk) → Splenium (posterior; visual/parietal fibers)
  • Anterior Commissure
  • Hippocampal Commissure (commissure of the fornix)
Projection Fibers (Vertical)

Connect cortex ↔ lower CNS.

  • Afferent fibers — input TO cortex (sensory); e.g., visual pathway (lateral geniculate → visual cortex)
  • Efferent fibers — output FROM cortex (motor); e.g., Corticospinal Tract (CST) — from motor cortex to spinal cord anterior horn
  • Travel through internal capsule
Association Fibers (Horizontal)

Connect regions within the same hemisphere.

  • Short fibers: adjacent gyri
  • Long fibers: distant lobes
  • Fasciculus = bundle/tract of fibers (e.g., cuneate fasciculus = proprioception for C and T regions)
Basal Ganglia
Location

Deep gray matter nuclei located between the cerebral cortex and thalamus, also in midbrain and near thalamus. Surrounded by white matter (internal capsule).

Terminology

Striatum = Caudate + Putamen (input nuclei). Lentiform = Putamen + Globus Pallidus. Corpus Striatum = all three (Caudate + Putamen + Globus Pallidus).

Function — Brake/Disinhibition Hypothesis

The BG operates as a braking system on movement. At rest, the BG continuously inhibits the thalamus — suppressing unwanted movement. To produce movement, the cortex inhibits the inhibitor = disinhibition.

  • Cortical INPUT to BG = excitatory
  • BG OUTPUT → thalamus + brainstem
  • BG refines movement — inhibits "incorrect" motor activity while allowing desired movement
  • BG is unlikely to initiate movement
  • "Releasing the brake" = disinhibition → desired movement accomplished, wrong movements still inhibited
Clinical: Thalamic Drive

Parkinson's: ↑ BG inhibition → ↓ thalamic drive → hypo-motor (rigidity, bradykinesia, tremor). Huntington's: ↓ BG inhibition → ↑ thalamic drive → hyper-motor (chorea = involuntary writhing).

Diencephalon
The diencephalon contains the thalamus, hypothalamus, subthalamus, and epithalamus. It sits between the cerebral hemispheres and the brainstem — functioning as the critical relay and regulatory hub of the brain.
Thalamus — "Gateway to the Cortex"

The thalamus is a relay center that MODIFIES sensory information before projecting it to the correct cortical region. Not a passive switch — it shapes what reaches consciousness.

  • All sensory input passes through EXCEPT CN I (olfaction goes directly to cortex)
  • Input is modified at the thalamus
  • Motor signals pass through via basal ganglia → thalamus → cortex
  • Limbic system connects to thalamus → role in emotional behavior
  • Most output goes to cortex; some to hypothalamus and BG
  • Each region (nucleus) of thalamus connects to specific cortical region
Visual Pathway Example

Visual input → lateral geniculate body of thalamus → visual cortex (occipital). Demonstrates the thalamic relay for specific sensory modalities.

Hypothalamus — Homeostatic Regulator

Location: Base of diencephalon, inferior to thalamus. Influences three systems: ANS, Endocrine, Limbic.

Subthalamus

Located in close proximity to substantia nigra and red nucleus. Functionally part of the basal ganglia. Lesion → Hemiballismus — violent, involuntary flinging movements of the contralateral limbs.

Brainstem & Cerebellum
The brainstem contains critical life-support centers and is the junction point for all ascending/descending CNS pathways. The cerebellum coordinates movement and motor learning.
Brainstem — Three Vertical Divisions

Tectum (roof of midbrain) · Tegmentum (middle — CN nuclei, reticular system, other nuclei) · Basis/Base (contains major motor tracts and some nuclei)

Midbrain
  • Substantia Nigra — dopamine-containing cells; loss = Parkinson's Disease
  • Red Nucleus — motor coordination
  • Cerebral Peduncles — pathway for corticospinal motor tract
  • Superior Colliculus — visual/ocular reflexes
  • Inferior Colliculus — auditory reflexes
  • Reticular Activating System — damage → coma
Pons
  • Pathways for motor tracts
  • Attachment for superior and middle cerebellar peduncles
  • CN nuclei (CN V, VI, VII, VIII originate here)
  • Reticular system continuation
  • Respiratory regulation (apneustic + pneumotaxic centers)
Medulla Oblongata
  • Most cardiovascular and respiratory control centers
  • Attachment for inferior cerebellar peduncle
  • Pyramidal Decussation — corticospinal tract crosses midline at caudal inferior medulla → right brain controls left body
  • CN nuclei (CN IX, X, XI, XII)
Brainstem Contents Summary

Ascending/descending CNS tracts · Nuclei for CN 3–12 in brainstem · Cerebellar peduncle attachments · ANS pathways · Reticular activating system (consciousness/arousal/sleep-wake)

Reticular Activating System (RAS)
Structure

Network of neural connections in the tegmentum of the brainstem. Not a discrete structure — diffuse clusters of gray matter (nuclei) scattered throughout pons, midbrain, and medulla. Connections with hypothalamus and thalamus.

Function
  • Control of cardiovascular system
  • Control of respiratory system
  • Level of consciousness, arousal, sleep-wake cycle (reticular activating system)
  • Damage in midbrain RAS → coma
Cerebellum
Functions
  • Coordination of voluntary movement — smooth, accurate execution
  • Equilibrium — balance and postural coordination
  • Motor/Procedural Learning — "how to" memories; skill acquisition
Clinical Note

Cerebellar damage → ataxia (incoordination), dysmetria (over/undershoot), nystagmus, intention tremor, dysdiadochokinesia. Ipsilateral signs (unlike cortical lesions which are contralateral).

Cerebellar Peduncles

White matter connections between cerebellum and brainstem (bidirectional):

  • Superior peduncle — attaches to midbrain; primarily efferent (output)
  • Middle peduncle — attaches to pons; primarily afferent (input from cortex)
  • Inferior peduncle — attaches to medulla; mixed (spinocerebellar tracts)

Vermis = midline structure of cerebellum; connects left and right cerebellar hemispheres

Spinal Cord
The spinal cord extends from the medulla to approximately T12–L1 (conus medullaris). It transmits sensory information superiorly and motor commands inferiorly, and serves as the pathway for reflex arcs.
Gross Anatomy
  • Conus Medullaris — tapered inferior end of spinal cord; terminates at T12–L1
  • Cauda Equina — bundle of nerve roots descending below L1 ("horse's tail")
  • Filum Terminale — fibrous extension anchoring spinal cord to coccyx
  • Spinal cord injury is identified by spinal segmental level, NOT bony vertebral level

Lumbar Puncture & Epidural
Lumbar Puncture (Spinal Tap)

Access CSF from subarachnoid space. Insert needle at L3–4 or L4–5 intervertebral space (below conus medullaris at L1 — safe, no cord). Needle inserted until small "pop" indicates penetration of ligamentum flavum. Assess for CSF return.

Epidural

Epidural needle placed in epidural space (between dura and bone). Local anesthetic bathes the nerve roots. Used for pain management (labor, post-surgical). Does not penetrate dura — different from spinal tap which enters subarachnoid space.

PNS — Nerve Root to Peripheral Nerve
The pathway from spinal cord to peripheral nerve: nerve roots → plexus → peripheral nerve. This architecture explains why nerve root injuries differ from peripheral nerve injuries in pattern.
Nerve Root Anatomy
  • Dorsal Nerve Root — afferent (sensory); cell bodies in dorsal root ganglion
  • Ventral Nerve Root — efferent (motor); cell bodies in ventral horn
  • Dorsal Ramus — posterior branch; supplies deep muscles + skin of back
  • Ventral Ramus — anterior branch; supplies limbs and anterior/lateral trunk; forms plexuses
  • Nerve root exits through intervertebral foramen
Pathway: Root → Plexus → Peripheral Nerve

Ventral rami combine to form nerve plexuses (cervical, brachial, lumbar, sacral), which then reorganize into named peripheral nerves. This is why one peripheral nerve carries fibers from multiple spinal levels.

Clinical Consequence

A nerve root lesion = dermatomal/myotomal pattern. A peripheral nerve lesion = cutaneous territory of that nerve (crosses multiple dermatomes). Distinguishing the two localizes the pathology to either spine or limb.

Cells of the Nervous System
The nervous system contains two major cell types: neurons (functional conductors) and glial cells (support). Glial cells vastly outnumber neurons — and most CNS pathology involves disruption of one or both cell types.
Neurons
  • 10% of cells by number; 50% by volume
  • "Electrical" conductors of action potentials
  • Parts: Dendrites → Soma (cell body) → Axon Hillock → Axon
  • Most CNS neurons are post-mitotic — cannot regenerate after injury (unlike PNS)
Glial Cells
  • 90% of cells by number
  • "Connective tissue" of the nervous system
  • Five major types: Astrocytes, Oligodendrocytes, Schwann cells, Ependymal cells, Microglia
Glial Cell Types

Astrocytes (Astroglia)

Most abundantBlood-Brain Barrier▼
  • Most abundant glial cell in CNS
  • Maintain neural spatial relationships
  • Induce formation of blood-brain barrier
  • Chemical protection of neurons
  • Control interstitial environment
  • Absorb and degrade glutamate and GABA (neurotransmitters) — prevent excitotoxicity
  • Control K⁺, Na⁺, and CO₂ levels around neurons

Oligodendrocytes (CNS) / Schwann Cells (PNS)

MyelinMS / GBS▼

Oligodendrocytes — CNS

  • Myelinate multiple axons in the CNS
  • Demyelinating CNS diseases attack oligodendrocytes
  • Multiple Sclerosis (MS) — classic oligodendrocyte demyelination
  • ALS — also involves oligodendrocyte damage

Schwann Cells — PNS

  • Myelinate portions of individual axons in PNS
  • Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) — autoimmune attack on Schwann cells
  • Unlike oligodendrocytes, Schwann cells can support PNS regeneration

Ependymal Cells (Ependymoglia)

CSF▼
  • Line the ventricles of the brain and central canal of spinal cord
  • Choroid plexus ependymal cells — secrete CSF
  • CSF provides buoyancy, shock absorption, and nutrient transport for the CNS

Microglial Cells

Immune▼
  • Phagocytosis of degenerative debris in CNS
  • Immune response of the CNS
  • Smallest glial cell
  • Activated in response to injury, infection, or neurodegenerative disease
Clinical Pathology
Key neurological conditions — understand the anatomical basis of each disorder and the clinical presentation pattern. Each maps directly to a specific structure or pathway.
Basal Ganglia · Dopamine

Parkinson's Disease

Pathology

Loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra → loss of dopamine input to striatum → BG remains in inhibitory state → decreased thalamic drive → hypo-motor symptoms.

Clinical Presentation (TRAP)

Tremor (resting, pill-rolling) · Rigidity · Akinesia/Bradykinesia · Postural instability. Kinesia paradoxa: can suddenly move normally with visual cues (e.g., stepping over lines on floor).

Basal Ganglia · Caudate Nucleus

Huntington's Disease

Pathology

Autosomal dominant. Caudate nucleus and BG global atrophy → loss of BG inhibition → increased thalamic drive → hyper-motor symptoms. Caudate nuclei appear flattened/atrophied on coronal section; ventricles dilated.

Clinical Presentation

Chorea — involuntary, writhing, dance-like movements. Progressive cognitive and psychiatric decline. HD = Caudate nuclei and BG damage. Fatal; no cure. Genetic testing available.

Global · Cortical Atrophy

Alzheimer's Disease

Pathology

Meninges wrinkling, widened sulci, diffuse cortical atrophy. Loss of cholinergic neurons (nucleus basalis of Meynert). Amyloid plaques + neurofibrillary tangles (tau protein). Global cerebral atrophy.

Clinical Presentation

Progressive memory loss (hippocampal involvement early), behavioral changes, language impairment, loss of ADL independence. Most common dementia. Risk increases with age.

CNS Demyelination · Oligodendrocytes

Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

Pathology

Autoimmune demyelination of oligodendrocytes → irregular demyelinating plaques in white matter (periventricular distribution on MRI). Sclerosis = hardening/scar tissue at plaque sites.

Clinical Presentation

Variable depending on lesion location. Common: optic neuritis, limb weakness/spasticity, sensory deficits, ataxia, fatigue, bladder dysfunction. Relapsing-remitting most common course. Heat intolerance (Uhthoff's phenomenon).

PNS Demyelination · Schwann Cells

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)

Pathology

Autoimmune demyelination of Schwann cells in PNS. Self-limiting. Related to autoimmune dysfunction, surgical complication, some vaccines, Hodgkin's disease, some drug reactions.

Clinical Presentation

Motor and/or sensory dysfunction begins in extremities and moves proximally (ascending paralysis). May progress to respiratory failure requiring ventilatory support. Function usually restored within weeks to months. Also known as Landry paralysis.

Subthalamic Nucleus

Hemiballismus

Pathology

Lesion to the subthalamic nucleus (functionally part of BG). Loss of subthalamic regulation of BG output.

Clinical Presentation

Violent, involuntary flinging/ballistic movements of the contralateral limbs (proximal > distal). Unilateral. Often caused by stroke to subthalamic nucleus. Can be treated with BG-modulating medications or DBS.

Name the 4 regions of the corpus callosum in order from anterior to posterior.
tap
Rostrum (anterior-most tip) → Genu ("knee"; frontal lobe fibers) → Body (main trunk) → Splenium (posterior; carries visual and parietal fibers). All connect left ↔ right hemispheres.
What are the 3 white matter fiber types and what does each connect?
tap
Commissures: L ↔ R hemispheres (corpus callosum, anterior commissure, hippocampal commissure). Projection fibers: cortex ↔ lower CNS (afferent = sensory in; efferent = CST out; travel through internal capsule). Association fibers: regions within same hemisphere.
What is the pathway from spinal cord to peripheral nerve, and why does it matter clinically?
tap
Nerve roots → ventral rami → plexus → peripheral nerve. Each peripheral nerve carries fibers from multiple spinal levels. So nerve root injury = dermatomal/myotomal pattern; peripheral nerve injury = cutaneous territory of that nerve crossing multiple dermatomes. Pattern localizes the lesion.
What is the Reticular Activating System and what happens if it's damaged?
tap
A diffuse network in the tegmentum of the brainstem (pons, midbrain, medulla) connecting to hypothalamus and thalamus. Controls sleep/wake cycle, arousal, and level of consciousness. Damage in the midbrain RAS → coma. Also controls cardiovascular and respiratory centers.
Dorsal horn vs. ventral horn — which is sensory, which is motor, and where are the cell bodies?
tap
Dorsal horn = sensory/afferent; cell bodies in dorsal root ganglion. Ventral horn = somatic motor/efferent; cell bodies in the ventral horn itself. Lateral horn = ANS/visceral motor. Dorsal columns carry sensory ascending; descending tracts carry motor commands from cortex and brainstem.

CN I — Olfaction. All other sensory input passes through the thalamus first before reaching the cortex. Olfactory fibers project directly to the olfactory cortex (piriform cortex) without a thalamic relay.
What is the difference between Broca's aphasia and Wernicke's aphasia?
tap
Broca's (frontal, Area 44/45): non-fluent speech, effortful, understands language — production deficit. Wernicke's (temporal, Area 22): fluent but meaningless speech, cannot understand — comprehension deficit. Both in left hemisphere in 95% of people.
Name the 4 memory types and the brain structure responsible for each.
tap
Declarative (facts) → Hippocampus. Procedural/Motor learning → Cerebellum. Working/Short-term → Prefrontal Cortex. Emotional/Fear → Amygdala.
What is perseveration and what structure's damage causes it?
tap
Inability to stop doing something — e.g., sandpapering through a table, or continuing to talk about a topic after conversation has moved on. Caused by damage to the prefrontal cortex (executive functions, decision-making, emotional control).
Explain the basal ganglia brake/disinhibition hypothesis.
tap
BG continuously inhibits the thalamus (brake = no movement). To move, the cortex inhibits the BG's inhibitor = disinhibition → thalamus is released → motor cortex activates → desired movement. BG refines movement; doesn't initiate it.
What is the corpus striatum and its components?
tap
Corpus Striatum = Caudate + Putamen + Globus Pallidus. Striatum (input) = Caudate + Putamen. Lentiform = Putamen + Globus Pallidus. Globus Pallidus = BG output nucleus. Caudate/Putamen = receive cortical excitatory input.
Where does the corticospinal tract cross midline and what does this mean clinically?
tap
Pyramidal decussation at the caudal/inferior medulla oblongata. This is why the right cerebral cortex controls the left side of the body and vice versa. Stroke in left motor cortex → right-sided weakness/paralysis.
Which glial cell myelinates in the CNS vs. PNS, and what disease attacks each?
tap
CNS: Oligodendrocytes (myelinate multiple axons) — attacked in MS/ALS. PNS: Schwann cells (myelinate individual axon segments) — attacked in GBS. Key: one oligodendrocyte wraps many axons; one Schwann cell wraps one axon segment.
Distinguish anterograde from retrograde amnesia and which structure causes each.
tap
Anterograde amnesia: cannot store NEW memories — caused by hippocampal damage (H.M. case study). Retrograde amnesia: cannot recall OLD memories. Hippocampus required for encoding new declarative memories into long-term storage, but not for retrieving old ones.
What are the three hypothalamic system influences and give one example of each?
tap
ANS: posterior hypothalamus → sympathetic; anterior → parasympathetic. Endocrine: produces ADH (vasopressin), regulates anterior pituitary hormone release. Limbic: emotional expression (rage, fear, pleasure) via limbic connections. Also regulates circadian rhythm (suprachiasmatic nucleus).
What is the clinical difference between nerve root injury and peripheral nerve injury?
tap
Nerve root: weakness in myotome, sensory in dermatome, ↓ DTR of that segment. Peripheral nerve: weakness in specific muscles of that nerve, sensory in cutaneous territory of nerve, DTR usually spared. Pattern of deficit localizes the lesion.
What is hemiballismus and what structure is damaged?
tap
Violent, involuntary flinging movements of contralateral limbs. Caused by lesion to the subthalamic nucleus (functionally part of BG). Often from stroke. Loss of subthalamic regulation → BG output disinhibited → excessive motor activity.
Where does the spinal cord end and what structures are below?
tap
Conus medullaris at T12–L1. Below: cauda equina (bundle of nerve roots descending within the dural sac). Filum terminale anchors cord to coccyx. Lumbar puncture done at L3–4 or L4–5 — below the conus, only nerve roots present = safe.
What are the three cerebellar functions and which one makes it clinically unique?
tap
(1) Coordination of voluntary movement, (2) Equilibrium, (3) Motor/procedural learning. Unique: cerebellar lesions produce ipsilateral signs (same side as lesion) — opposite of cortical lesions which are contralateral. Classic: ataxia, dysmetria, intention tremor.
Why does GBS present with ascending paralysis and what is its prognosis?

GBS is autoimmune demyelination of Schwann cells in PNS. Starts in extremities (longest nerves affected first) and moves proximally — ascending pattern. Self-limiting — function usually restored within weeks to months. Can cause respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation during acute phase.
What are the three white matter fiber types in the brain?
tap
Commissures: transverse connections between hemispheres (corpus callosum, anterior commissure). Projection fibers: connect cortex to/from lower CNS (afferent = sensory in; efferent = CST out). Association fibers: connect regions within the same hemisphere.
