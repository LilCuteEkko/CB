Counting Spinous Processes
Start at C7 (vertebra prominens). In thoracic region: SPs are long and angled inferiorly — the palpable SP is 1-2 levels below the actual vertebral body. In lumbar region: SPs are horizontal — what you feel is the actual level. Lumbar puncture: L3-L4 or L4-L5 space (safely below conus medullaris).
Thoracolumbar Fascia
A thick, multi-layered fascial envelope that invests the deep back muscles. Attaches to the spinous processes and transverse processes of the thoracic and lumbar regions, forming a posterior band of connective tissue.
Muscle Origins
•Latissimus dorsi — major extrinsic back muscle origin
•Transversus abdominis — wraps around laterally
•Internal oblique — attaches to posterior layer
Clinical Relevance
The TLF plays a role in load transfer across the lumbopelvic region. Tension from lat, TrA, and internal oblique creates a posterior "tension band" — important in core stability.
TLF disruption (surgery, trauma) → loss of lumbar spine stiffness and stability. Theorized role in LBP via altered proprioception and load transfer.
Intervertebral Disc (IVD)
Annulus Fibrosus + Nucleus Pulposus
Annulus Fibrosus
•Concentric layers of collagen fibers (type I collagen predominant)
•Fibers obliquely oriented — alternate directions layer by layer ("crosswoven effect")
•Provides tensile strength — resists rotation, flexion, extension, and compression
•Anchors to bone and cartilaginous endplates of vertebral bodies
•Outer layers blend with ALL and PLL
•Outer layers have innervation (sinuvertebral nerve) — potential pain source
Nucleus Pulposus
•Proteoglycans (aggrecan) attract and bind water — hydrophilic
•Not a "jelly donut" — gradual transition from annulus at margins
•Contains fine collagen fibers in loose, random arrangement
•With aging: proteoglycan loss → decreased water content → disc height loss
•Acts as a hydraulic cushion — distributes compressive loads radially
Disc Pathology
Annular fissures = discogenic pain (even without herniation). Protrusion/herniation: nucleus material pushes through annular fibers → possible nerve root compression → radicular pain (myotomal weakness, dermatomal numbness). Most herniations: posterolateral (PLL is thinnest here). L4-L5 and L5-S1 most common levels. C5-C6 and C6-C7 most common in cervical spine. Degenerative disc disease: cascade of height loss → increased facet load → instability → osteophyte formation.
Facet (Zygapophyseal) Joints
Plane synovial joints formed between the superior articular process of the lower vertebra and the inferior articular process of the upper vertebra. Surrounded by loose articular capsule. Permit gliding movement. Orientation varies by spinal region — critical determinant of motion direction.

Clinical Relevance
Innervated by dorsal primary rami (DPR) from the segment AND one level above AND below — explains why local anesthetic blocks of 2 medial branch nerves are needed to denervate one facet joint. Facet joint pain: unilateral back pain, worse with extension and rotation, may refer to buttock/thigh (not below knee). Medial branch block used diagnostically and therapeutically. Facet OA is extremely common — accounts for 15-45% of chronic LBP.

Contents
•Vertebral artery — forms a large loop as it passes from C1 transverse foramen to foramen magnum; extremely vulnerable to compression with upper cervical manipulation in rotation
•Suboccipital nerve (C1 DPR) — purely motor; innervates all four suboccipital muscles; no sensory distribution

Vertebral Artery Caution
The vertebral artery forms a large "S-curve" loop in the suboccipital triangle between C1 and the foramen magnum. Sustained end-range cervical rotation compresses the contralateral vertebral artery here. This is the primary anatomical basis for pre-manipulation VBI screening. Upper cervical manipulation in patients with atherosclerosis, hypermobility, or RA (odontoid erosion) carries risk.

Clinical Applications
•Rectus capitis posterior minor has a connective tissue bridge to the posterior dura — postulated mechanism for suboccipital pain causing dural tension and headache
•Suboccipital muscles are densest in muscle spindles in the body — critical proprioceptive role (gaze stabilization, cervicogenic dizziness)
•Whiplash: suboccipital muscles are injured — explains cervicogenic headache and dizziness post-MVA
•Suboccipital release techniques: manual therapy targeting these muscles for cervicogenic headache — high-level evidence for cervicogenic headache treatment
•Greater occipital nerve (C2 DPR) passes through/adjacent to semispinalis capitis — entrapment = occipital neuralgia

4 Limiters of ROM
1. IVD elasticity and compressibility — disc height ratio determines mobility; C and L > T
2. Orientation of facet joints — lumbar facets guide flexion/extension; thoracic allow rotation
3. Tension of facet joint capsules — capsular tightness limits end-range motion; capsular pattern
4. Resistance of ligaments and musculature — passive (ligaments) and active (muscle) restraints

Upper Cervical Mechanics
O-C1-C2 — Special Rules
•C0-C1 (Atlanto-occipital): Primary flex/ext (~15°). Minimal rotation. No disc. Superior facets of atlas are concave — "yes joint" nodding motion.
•C1-C2 (Atlantoaxial): ~50% of total cervical rotation occurs here (~45° per side). Minimal flex/ext. The dens acts as pivot. As C1 rotates on C2, the facet shape causes screw-down motion — head descends 2–3mm during rotation.
•Alar ligament: limits axial rotation at C1-C2. Sharp rotation force (MVA, manipulation) can tear alar ligaments → upper cervical instability.
•Transverse ligament integrity: tested with Sharp-Purser test or alar ligament stress tests. Rheumatoid arthritis erodes the dens — ADI (atlanto-dens interval) >3mm adults = instability.
Upper Cervical Instability Red Flags
5 D's and 3 N's for VBI. Additionally: Down syndrome (atlantoaxial instability), RA (odontoid erosion), Os odontoideum, Grisel's syndrome (post-pharyngeal infection torticollis).
Sharp-Purser test, alar ligament stress tests. Imaging: ADI >3mm on flexion X-ray, or >7mm in children = unstable.

Coupling Rules
Cervical: LF + IPSILATERAL rotation (SPs rotate contralateral/convex). Lumbar: LF + CONTRALATERAL rotation (SPs rotate ipsilateral/concave). Thoracic: transitions between patterns — upper like cervical, lower like lumbar.
Key Facts
No disc between C0-C1 or C1-C2 · Spinal cord ends L1-L2 · Suboccipital nerve (C1) = motor only · Thoracolumbar fascia = origin for lat dorsi + TrA + IO · Multifidus inserts mamillary processes · Ligamentum flavum = high elastin (allows smooth flexion)
