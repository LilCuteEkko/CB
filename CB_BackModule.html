Back & Vertebral Column

The vertebral column is the body’s central pillar — it protects the spinal cord, supports the head and trunk, anchors the ribs, and serves as the attachment point for hundreds of muscles. Its 4 curvatures balance load while 23 intervertebral discs absorb shock and allow motion. The spine must solve an impossible engineering problem: be rigid enough to support vertical load yet flexible enough to bend, twist, and rotate. Understanding how it does this — through the three-joint complex, facet orientation, and core stabilization — is fundamental to treating the most common reason patients seek PT: back pain.

Clinical Example 1 — Disc Herniation with Centralization

38-year-old office worker with 2 weeks of right-sided low back pain radiating to the posterior thigh and lateral calf (L5 dermatome). Worse with sitting and forward bending. Positive SLR at 45° on the right. Weak right EHL (L5 myotome). During repeated extension in standing: pain moves from the calf → thigh → centralizes to the low back. Clinical reasoning: L4–L5 disc herniation compressing the right L5 nerve root. Centralization with extension = excellent prognostic sign (McKenzie derangement). Treatment: directional preference exercises (prone press-ups → standing extension), avoid flexion activities, nerve mobilization once acute irritation settles, motor control training for multifidus/TrA. 90% resolve conservatively in 6–12 weeks. Surgical referral if: progressive neurological deficit, cauda equina signs, or failure of 6–12 weeks conservative care.

Clinical Example 2 — Neurogenic vs Vascular Claudication

72-year-old male with bilateral leg pain and heaviness after walking 200 meters. Scenario A: Pain relieved by sitting down (flexion), worse going downhill, leans on shopping cart. Normal pedal pulses. Scenario B: Pain relieved by standing still (rest), worse going uphill, diminished pedal pulses, skin changes. Clinical reasoning: Scenario A = neurogenic claudication (lumbar stenosis). Flexion opens the spinal canal. "Shopping cart sign" classic. Treatment: flexion-based exercises, cycling, aquatic therapy. Scenario B = vascular claudication (PAD). Rest relieves ischemia regardless of position. Refer to vascular surgery. Distinguish by: flexion relief vs rest relief, pulse quality, downhill vs uphill, skin changes. Getting this wrong sends a vascular patient to spine PT instead of a vascular surgeon.

Intervertebral Disc

Annulus fibrosus: concentric rings of type I collagen (outer) and type II collagen (inner). Resists tension. Outer 1/3 innervated (sinuvertebral nerve) — disc tears here cause pain. Nucleus pulposus: gel-like center rich in proteoglycans (aggrecan attracts/binds water). Resists compression. Highest water content in the morning (disc imbibes fluid overnight), loses height during the day (creep). Cartilaginous endplates: hyaline cartilage on vertebral body surfaces. Primary route for disc nutrition (avascular disc depends on diffusion through endplates). Endplate damage = disc degeneration cascade. No disc between C0–C1 or C1–C2.

Spinal Joints & Ligaments

Facet (Zygapophyseal) Joints

▼
Facet Joint Anatomy

Synovial plane joints between superior and inferior articular processes of adjacent vertebrae. Lined with hyaline cartilage, contain synovial fluid, enclosed in a joint capsule. Facet orientation determines available motion — this is the most important concept in spinal biomechanics. Facets are richly innervated by the medial branch of the dorsal ramus (2 levels: same level + one above) — target for facet blocks and radiofrequency ablation for facet-mediated pain. Capsular pattern: equal limitation of extension and lateral flexion in all directions.

Back Muscles — OINA Reference
Organized by layer and function. The erector spinae provide gross movement; the transversospinalis group provides segmental stabilization and fine-tuning. Multifidus is the most clinically important spinal muscle.

Erector Spinae — 3 Columns (“I Love Spine”)

▼
Mnemonic: I Love Spine (lateral → medial)

Iliocostalis (lateral) → Longissimus (intermediate) → Spinalis (medial). All innervated by dorsal primary rami. Bilateral: extends spine. Unilateral: ipsilateral lateral flexion. The primary gross extensors of the vertebral column.

Deep Local Stabilizers — Core

▼
The Core Stability System

True “core” stability comes from 4 structures working as a pressure canister: Transversus abdominis (TrA) — wraps like a corset, increases intra-abdominal pressure, fires feedforward in healthy individuals. Multifidus — posterior segmental stabilizer, atrophies rapidly with LBP. Pelvic floor muscles — bottom of the canister, co-activates with TrA. Diaphragm — top of the canister, must maintain respiratory function while stabilizing. Loss of feedforward activation of TrA and multifidus is the hallmark finding in chronic LBP patients.

Coupled Motion
Fryette’s Laws of Spinal Motion

Law I (Neutral/Type I): When the spine is in neutral (no flexion or extension), sidebending and rotation occur to OPPOSITE sides. Group coupling — involves multiple segments. Law II (Non-neutral/Type II): When the spine is flexed or extended (non-neutral), sidebending and rotation occur to the SAME side. Single segment coupling. Law III: Motion introduced in one plane will reduce available motion in the other two planes. Clinical application: Type I dysfunctions are typically adaptive (compensation). Type II dysfunctions are typically traumatic (localized). These laws guide manual therapy assessment and treatment direction.

Lumbar Coupled Motion

In the lumbar spine, the coupling pattern is debated, but clinically: the lumbar facets (sagittal orientation) strongly limit rotation. During lateral flexion, the lumbar vertebrae tend to rotate to the opposite side in neutral (Type I) and to the same side in flexed/extended positions (Type II). The SIJ couples with lumbar motion — lateral flexion of the trunk creates SIJ torsion on the weight-bearing side.

Red Flags — Immediate Referral

Cauda equina signs (saddle anesthesia, bladder dysfunction) • Progressive neurological deficit • Bilateral neurological signs • History of cancer with new spine pain (metastasis) • Fever + spine pain (infection/abscess) • Trauma + pain (fracture) • Night pain unrelieved by rest (tumor) • Weight loss + fatigue + spine pain • Age <20 or >50 with new onset spine pain without clear mechanical cause

What is the ligamentum flavum, and why does it contribute to spinal stenosis in this scenario?
tap
Connects adjacent laminae. ~80% elastin (highest of any ligament) — allows smooth flexion without buckling into canal. With aging, it hypertrophies and fibroses (elastin replaced by collagen), thickening into the posterior canal, especially during extension. Major contributor to lumbar spinal stenosis.
Why do lumbar disc herniations occur posterolaterally in this scenario?
tap
The PLL narrows as it descends (hourglass shape) — thinnest and weakest at L4–L5. The posterolateral annulus is also thinner than the posterior midline. The combination of a weak PLL and thin annulus posterolaterally creates the path of least resistance for nucleus pulposus herniation.
Distinguish neurogenic claudication from vascular claudication.
tap
Neurogenic: bilateral LE pain with walking, relieved by flexion (sitting, leaning on cart — “shopping cart sign”), worse going downhill (extension), normal pulses. Vascular: calf cramping with walking, relieved by rest (standing still), worse uphill, diminished pulses, skin/nail changes.
What is multifidus and why is it the most important spinal muscle clinically in this scenario?
tap
Deepest transversospinalis muscle. Segmental (spans 2–4 levels). Atrophies within DAYS of acute LBP and does NOT spontaneously recover even after pain resolves. Loss of feedforward activation is the hallmark of chronic LBP. Must be specifically retrained with motor control exercises. Inserts on mammillary processes in lumbar spine.
Client has saddle anesthesia, urinary retention, and bilateral LE weakness. Diagnosis and action?
tap
Cauda equina syndrome. MC cause: massive central disc herniation compressing nerve roots below L1–L2. SURGICAL EMERGENCY — decompression within 24–48 hours. LMN signs (flaccid, areflexic) because it’s nerve roots, not cord. Delay = permanent neurologic deficit including bowel/bladder dysfunction.
Fryette’s Law I vs Law II — when does sidebending and rotation go to the same vs opposite sides in this scenario?
tap
Law I (neutral): sidebend and rotation go to OPPOSITE sides. Group coupling, multiple segments. Law II (non-neutral / flexed or extended): sidebend and rotation go to the SAME side. Single segment. Law I = adaptive dysfunction. Law II = traumatic dysfunction.
