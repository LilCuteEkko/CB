Abdomen & Thorax

Gross anatomy of the abdominal and thoracic regions — surface anatomy, abdominal wall muscles, peritoneum, viscera, diaphragm, thoracic wall, lungs, and heart. Essential regional anatomy for differential diagnosis and clinical reasoning.

Neurovascular Plane

All abdominal nerves (thoracoabdominal, subcostal, iliohypogastric, ilioinguinal) travel from posterolateral → anterior within the neurovascular plane between the internal oblique and transversus abdominis. Analogous to nerves running between internal oblique and transverse abdominis — same principle as the intercostal space in the thorax.

Muscles of the Abdominal Wall
Five muscles form the anterolateral abdominal wall. They function together to support viscera, increase intra-abdominal pressure, move the trunk, and protect against blows. The 4 "F"s: fluids, flatulence, feces, and fetuses.

Rectus Abdominis (RA)

VerticalT7–12▼

Semilunar line = lateral edge of RA. Arcuate line = ~1/3 distance umbilicus to pubis; below it all aponeuroses pass anterior to RA

Rectus Sheath — Above vs. Below Arcuate Line

Above arcuate line: Anterior = EO aponeurosis + anterior IO aponeurosis. Posterior = posterior IO aponeurosis + TA aponeurosis.
Below arcuate line: ALL three aponeuroses pass anterior to RA. Transversalis fascia alone passes posterior.

Strong expandable support (organ movement) · Protects viscera from trauma · Compresses contents for defecation/urination/birth (4 F's: fluids, flatulence, feces, fetuses) · Increases intra-abdominal pressure (back support) · Moves trunk + maintains posture

Anterolateral Abdominal Wall

Three flat muscles form the lateral wall; rectus abdominis forms the anterior wall. These muscles protect viscera, compress abdominal contents (defecation, urination, birth), increase intra-abdominal pressure (back support), and move the trunk. Fiber direction mnemonic: External oblique = “hands in pockets” (inferior & medial). Internal oblique = “hands on chest” (superior & medial). Transversus = horizontal.

Diaphragm

Dome-shaped musculotendinous partition separating thoracic and abdominal cavities. The primary muscle of respiration. Innervated by phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5) — “C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive.” Irritation of diaphragm refers pain to C3–C5 dermatomes → shoulder pain (heart attack → L shoulder; gallbladder → R shoulder).

Inguinal Region
The inguinal canal is a clinically critical passageway through the abdominal wall. ~75% of all hernias occur in the groin. Understanding the anatomy explains why hernias occur and how to distinguish types.
Inguinal Ligament
  • Spans ASIS → pubic tubercle
  • Thickening of the inferior edge of the External Oblique aponeurosis — folds back on itself
  • Forms the floor of the inguinal canal

Female

Deep Inguinal Ring

Lateral opening of the canal. Outpouching of the transversalis fascia — fascia continues into canal around spermatic cord. Located lateral to inferior epigastric vessels.

Superficial Inguinal Ring

Medial opening. Slit-like opening through the fibers of the external oblique aponeurosis. Lateral crus attaches to pubic tubercle; medial crus to pubic crest.

Hesselbach's Triangle — Direct Hernia Site

Medial border: Lateral margin of rectus sheath. Superolateral border: Inferior epigastric vessels. Inferior border: Inguinal ligament. Direct hernias protrude through the weak transversalis fascia within this triangle. Corona Mortis: abnormal anastomosis between inferior epigastric artery (external iliac branch) and obturator artery — if cut during hernia repair, retracts into pelvis causing severe blood loss.

Hernia Severity

Reducible: contents can be pushed back. Incarcerated (irreducible): contents trapped — cannot reduce. Strangulated: blood supply compromised → ischemia → necrosis. SURGICAL EMERGENCY — gangrene and perforation can occur within hours.

Hesselbach’s (Inguinal) Triangle — Where Direct Hernias Occur

Three borders: (1) Rectus abdominis (medial edge of rectus sheath). (2) Inferior epigastric vessels (lateral). (3) Inguinal ligament (inferior). The triangle marks the weak area in the posterior wall of the inguinal canal — direct hernias push through here.

MALT — Inguinal Canal Walls Mnemonic

M = Muscle = Roof (internal oblique + transversus abdominis arch over the canal). A = Aponeurosis = Anterior wall (external oblique aponeurosis + internal oblique laterally). L = Ligament = Floor (inguinal ligament trough). T = Tendon = Posterior wall (transversalis fascia + conjoint tendon medially).

Two rings: Deep inguinal ring (lateral; outpouching of transversalis fascia) → Superficial inguinal ring (medial; opening in external oblique aponeurosis). Cremasteric reflex: stroke inner thigh → cremaster contracts → ipsilateral testicle elevates (L1–L2 reflex arc via genitofemoral nerve).

Thoracic Diaphragm
The primary muscle of breathing — a dome-shaped musculotendinous partition separating thoracic and abdominal cavities. Contraction flattens the dome → increases thoracic volume → inspiration. Relaxation restores dome (elastic recoil) → expiration.
Structure — Two Portions

1. Peripheral muscular portion — three parts:

  • Sternal — attaches to xiphoid process
  • Costal — attaches to internal surfaces of inferior 6 costal cartilages/ribs
  • Lumbar — arises from L1–3 and two aponeurotic arches (medial + lateral arcuate ligaments)

2. Central aponeurotic portion (central tendon) — no bony attachments; caval foramen perforates through it

Dome Position

During expiration: right dome → level of 5th rib; left dome → 5th intercostal space (heart pushes left dome down slightly). During inspiration both domes descend.

Arcuate Ligaments
  • Median Arcuate Ligament — unites R/L crura; forms arch of aortic hiatus
  • Medial Arcuate Ligament — thickening of fascia of psoas major
  • Lateral Arcuate Ligament — thickening of fascia of quadratus lumborum; QL fixes 12th rib to give diaphragm a stable base
Nerve Supply

Motor + central sensory: Phrenic nerves (C3–C5) — "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive." Peripheral diaphragm sensory: lower 6–7 intercostal nerves + subcostal nerve.

Three Diaphragmatic Apertures
2 vascular + 1 gastrointestinal. Level of each is critical — remember the rule: 8, 10, 12 (caval at T8, esophageal at T10, aortic at T12).

Referred Diaphragm Pain — Clinical Pearl

Anything irritating the diaphragm sends signals via the phrenic nerve (C3–5) → brain maps it to those C3–5 dermatomes = shoulder.
Heart attack — heart rests on diaphragm → left shoulder/arm pain.
Liver/Gall bladder disease — right lobe of liver under right diaphragm → right shoulder pain.
Splenic rupture — left diaphragm irritation → left shoulder pain (Kehr's sign).

Abdominal Viscera
Understand each organ's function, blood supply, and clinical significance. The liver and kidneys are the most clinically tested. Know the abdominal aorta branches in order.

Esophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine

GI Tract▼

Esophagus

  • Delivers food to stomach via peristalsis
  • Enters abdomen through esophageal hiatus (T10)
  • Retroperitoneal

Stomach

  • Acidic (HCl) + pepsin (proteolytic)
  • Mechanical + chemical digestion
  • Produces intrinsic factor → absorbs vitamin B12
  • Intraperitoneal; in LUQ

Small Intestine (~25 feet long)

  • Alkaline — neutralizes stomach acid; site of most digestion and absorption
  • Absorbs: free fatty acids, amino acids, monosaccharides
  • Duodenum — "U"-shaped; receives bile duct (from liver) + pancreatic duct; flow controlled by Sphincter of Oddi
  • Jejunum — most absorption
  • Ileum — leads to cecum via ileocecal junction

Large Intestine

Absorption + Storage▼
  • Functions: absorbs water; stores feces until defecation; gut bacteria produce Vitamin K (used by liver to make clotting factors)
  • Cecum → Appendix → Ascending Colon → Transverse Colon → Descending Colon → Sigmoid Colon → Rectum
  • Ascending + descending colon = secondarily retroperitoneal
  • Transverse + sigmoid colon = intraperitoneal

Liver

Largest glandRUQ▼

Dual Circulation

  • 80% from Hepatic Portal Vein (from small intestine + stomach)
  • 20% from Hepatic Artery
  • Portal Triad: Portal Vein + Hepatic Artery + Bile Duct (at periphery of lobule)
  • Hepatic veins drain into IVC

  • Converts ammonia → urea

Pancreas & Gall Bladder & Spleen

Accessory Organs▼

Pancreas

  • Endocrine: insulin + glucagon → regulate blood glucose
  • Exocrine: pancreatic juice (alkaline) + digestive enzymes → pancreatic duct → duodenum via Sphincter of Oddi
  • Head = retroperitoneal; tail = intraperitoneal

Gall Bladder

  • Stores and concentrates bile
  • Releases bile: cystic duct → bile duct → duodenum
  • Emulsifies fats for absorption

Spleen

  • Destroys old RBCs + platelets
  • Recycles iron + globin
  • Reservoir of platelets
  • Intraperitoneal; LUQ

Kidneys & Adrenal Glands

Retroperitoneal▼

Kidneys — "Filter everything, reabsorb the good stuff"

  • Filtration — passive; blood pressure driven; non-selective (everything but proteins/RBCs)
  • Reabsorption — active; selective; nutrients returned to blood
  • Secretion — active; wastes added to filtrate for excretion
  • Produces erythropoietin — stimulates RBC formation (EPO doping in athletes)
  • Internal structure: cortex → medulla → renal pyramid → renal papilla → minor calyx → major calyx → renal pelvis → ureter

Adrenal Glands — Superior pole of each kidney

  • Adrenal Cortex: secretes corticosteroids + androgens; aldosterone → kidney retains Na/water
  • Adrenal Medulla: modified sympathetic ganglion; secretes epinephrine + norepinephrine (fight-or-flight: ↑BP, ↑HR, bronchodilation, ↓GI flow, ↑muscle blood flow)

Referred Visceral Pain
Mechanism

Internal organs lack specific sensory fibers with dermatomal distribution. They respond only to stretch and chemical irritation. Pain impulses travel via splanchnic nerves → spinal segments → poorly localized. Brain maps them to the dermatomes at the level the afferent fibers enter the cord — hence referred pain.

Visceral vs. Parietal Peritoneum Pain

Visceral peritoneum: insensitive to touch/heat/cold/laceration; sensitive to stretch + chemical irritation; pain poorly localized to midline along sympathetic fibers.
Parietal peritoneum: same supply as wall lining; pain well localized (except central diaphragm → referred to C3–5 = shoulder).

Liver — Functions, Dual Circulation & Portal Triad

Largest gland in bodyDual blood supply: 80% portal vein + 20% hepatic arteryPortal triad: portal vein + hepatic artery + bile duct▼
Metabolic Functions

• Produces Vitamin K–dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X) — liver failure = coagulopathy
• Detoxifies chemicals and drugs (cytochrome P450 system)
• Converts ammonia → urea (liver failure = hyperammonemia → hepatic encephalopathy)
• Produces albumin (major plasma protein — maintains oncotic pressure; low albumin → edema/ascites)
• Produces bile → bile duct → gallbladder (storage) → duodenum (emulsifies fats for absorption)
• Stores glycogen (glucose reserve); gluconeogenesis
• Produces cholesterol and triglycerides
• Breaks down hemoglobin → bilirubin → conjugated in liver → excreted with bile into duodenum → gives feces its brown color. Elevated serum bilirubin = jaundice (yellowing of skin/sclera)

Dual Circulation & Portal Triad

~80% of hepatic blood comes from the hepatic portal vein (nutrient-rich but deoxygenated blood from the GI tract — stomach, intestines, spleen, pancreas). ~20% from the hepatic artery (oxygenated blood from celiac trunk/aorta).

Portal triad (found at each liver lobule): (1) Portal vein branch, (2) Hepatic artery branch, (3) Bile duct. Blood from both sources mixes in sinusoids → processed by hepatocytes → drains into hepatic veins → IVC.

Portal hypertension: obstruction of portal flow (cirrhosis) → increased pressure → portocaval anastomoses open → esophageal varices (bleed), caput medusae (periumbilical veins), hemorrhoids (rectal veins).

Kidneys, Adrenals & Abdominal Aorta Branches

Kidneys: filtration + reabsorption + secretionAdrenal cortex = corticosteroids; medulla = epi/NECeliac trunk → SMA → IMA (midgut watershed)▼
Kidney Functions

Three processes: (1) Filtration (non-selective, driven by blood pressure — filters everything except formed elements and large proteins). (2) Reabsorption (selective active transport — nutrients returned to blood from filtrate). (3) Secretion (selective active transport — additional wastes moved from blood into filtrate). Also produces erythropoietin (EPO) — stimulates RBC formation in bone marrow. Renal failure → anemia (no EPO). Retroperitoneal, T12–L3 level.

Adrenal Glands (on superior pole of each kidney)

Cortex (outer): secretes corticosteroids (cortisol = stress response, aldosterone = Na/H₂O retention → BP regulation) and androgens. Medulla (inner): modified sympathetic ganglion — secretes epinephrine & norepinephrine directly into blood (↑ HR, ↑ BP, bronchodilation, ↓ GI blood flow). Pheochromocytoma: adrenal medulla tumor → episodic severe hypertension.

Abdominal Aorta — Major Branches (Superior → Inferior)

The descending abdominal aorta gives off branches in this order:

• Celiac trunk (T12) → left gastric a., splenic a., common hepatic a. — supplies: liver, spleen, stomach, proximal duodenum, pancreas
• Superior mesenteric artery (SMA) (L1) → supplies: distal duodenum, jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon, proximal 2/3 transverse colon (“midgut”)
• Renal arteries (L1–L2) → kidneys (receive ~25% of cardiac output)
• Gonadal arteries (ovarian/testicular)
• Lumbar arteries (4 pairs — direct branches)
• Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA) (L3) → supplies: distal 1/3 transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, upper rectum (“hindgut”)
• Aorta bifurcates into R/L common iliac arteries at L4

Watershed zone: splenic flexure (SMA/IMA junction) — vulnerable to ischemia during hypotension → ischemic colitis.

Why Visceral Pain Refers

Internal organs lack precise dermatomal sensory fibers. Visceral pain afferents travel with splanchnic nerves and enter the spinal cord at the same segmental level as somatic (skin/muscle) afferents. The brain misinterprets visceral signals as coming from the skin/muscles of that dermatome — because somatic pain from those dermatomes is far more common. This is like getting a phone call from an unknown number but your brain assumes it’s from the contact whose name matches that area code.

Understanding peritoneal relationships is essential for understanding organ mobility, surgical access, infection spread, and referred pain patterns.
Mesentery

Double layer of peritoneum — the neurovascular highway to an organ that has invaginated into peritoneum. Carries blood vessels, nerves, and lymphatics to intraperitoneal organs.

Peritoneal Structures

Mesentery: double layer of peritoneum that suspends an organ from the body wall and acts as a neurovascular highway — carries arteries, veins, lymphatics, and nerves to the organ. Think of it as the “umbilical cord” for each organ.

Greater omentum: large double-layered peritoneal apron that hangs from the greater curvature of the stomach, drapes over the intestines, and folds back up to attach to the transverse colon. Functions as the “abdominal policeman” — migrates to sites of infection/inflammation to wall them off. Rich in fat and immune cells.

Lesser omentum: double layer connecting the lesser curvature of the stomach and proximal duodenum to the liver. Contains the portal triad (hepatic artery, portal vein, bile duct) in its free edge (hepatoduodenal ligament). Pringle maneuver: clamping the hepatoduodenal ligament to control liver hemorrhage.

Intraperitoneal vs Retroperitoneal vs Infraperitoneal

Intraperitoneal: organ nearly completely covered by visceral peritoneum (NOT literally “inside” the peritoneum). Has a mesentery. Organs: stomach, 1st part of duodenum, jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, upper 1/3 rectum, liver, spleen. In women: uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries.

Retroperitoneal: organs posterior to the peritoneum; peritoneum covers only their anterior surface.

Thoracic Wall
The thoracic cage protects thoracic contents, facilitates breathing (anchor for diaphragm, negative intrapleural pressure −5 mmHg), and provides attachment for upper extremity and neck muscles. Intrapleural pressure is always slightly negative, keeping lungs inflated.

Fiber Direction Rule — Intercostals mimic obliques

External intercostals = same fiber direction as External Oblique (inferior + medial). Internal intercostals = same fiber direction as Internal Oblique. This helps recall their actions: EO + EIC = elevation during inspiration; IO + IIC = depression during expiration.

Intercostal Space — Neurovascular Plane
VAN Order in Costal Groove

Intercostal structures run in the costal groove on the inferior border of each rib. Order from superior to inferior:
Vein → Artery → Nerve (VAN)

Clinical: Needle/Tube Placement

Always insert needles (thoracentesis, chest tube) just superior to the rib below — avoids NVB in costal groove. Going too high = hitting VAN bundle of rib above.

Vascular Supply
  • Posterior intercostal arteries — direct branches of thoracic aorta (ribs 3–11); subclavian for 1–2
  • Anterior intercostal arteries — branches of internal thoracic artery
  • Internal thoracic artery — branch of subclavian; continues as superior epigastric artery
  • Azygous venous system — posterior intercostal veins drain here → SVC
Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

Compression of neurovascular structures (brachial plexus C8-T1, subclavian artery/vein) at the thoracic outlet — between clavicle, 1st rib, and scalene muscles. Presents with arm pain, paresthesia, weakness, vascular compromise. Scalene muscle hypertrophy or cervical rib are common causes.

Thoracic Viscera — Heart & Lungs
The mediastinum contains the heart, great vessels, trachea, esophagus, and thoracic duct. The lungs occupy the lateral thoracic cavities. Pleura reduces friction and bonds lung to chest wall.

Pericardium & Coronary Arteries
Pericardium — Two Layers

Fibrous pericardium: outer; dense irregular CT; anchors heart. Serous pericardium: visceral (on heart surface = epicardium) + parietal; double layer of epithelial tissue; contains pericardial fluid to reduce friction.

Coronary Arteries

First branches off the aorta — just superior to the aortic semilunar valve (when valve opens during systole, blood enters coronary ostia). Left and right coronary arteries supply the myocardium. CAD = narrowing of coronary arteries → MI.

Cardiac Circulation Summary
Blood Flow Pathway

Systemic veins → R atrium → tricuspid → R ventricle → pulmonic valve → pulmonary trunk → pulmonary arteries → lungs (O₂/CO₂ exchange) → pulmonary veins → L atrium → mitral → L ventricle → aortic valve → aorta → systemic circulation

Thoracic Osteology — Ribs, Sternum & Costovertebral Joints

Ribs (12 Pairs)

Typical ribs (3–9): head with 2 facets (articulate with 2 adjacent vertebral bodies + intervening disc), neck, tubercle (articulates with TP of same-numbered vertebra), body (shaft) with costal groove (intercostal VAN runs here — Vein, Artery, Nerve from superior to inferior). Atypical: Rib 1 (single facet, shortest, broadest, scalene tubercle), Rib 2 (single facet on head), Ribs 11–12 (single facet, no tubercle, no sternal attachment).

True ribs (1–7): attach directly to sternum via costal cartilage. False ribs (8–10): attach indirectly (costal cartilage connects to rib above). Floating ribs (11–12): no sternal attachment. Costal cartilage = hyaline cartilage connecting ribs to sternum.

Sternum & Sternal Joints

Manubrium: articulates with clavicle (SC joint) and rib 1. Manubriosternal joint (angle of Louis) = fibrous joint between manubrium and body; landmark for rib 2 and sternal angle (T4–T5 level — aortic arch, tracheal bifurcation, thoracic duct crossing). Body: articulates with ribs 2–6 (rib 7 at xiphosternal joint). Xiphoid process: often fuses by age 40; landmark for diaphragm level. Xiphosternal joint = fibrous joint at rib 7.

Costovertebral Joints (2 per rib, ribs 2–10)

Costocorporeal (head of rib): rib head articulates with vertebral body/bodies — synovial plane joint. Costotransverse: rib tubercle articulates with TP of same-numbered vertebra — synovial plane joint. Only ribs 1–10 (ribs 11–12 lack costotransverse joints). These joints allow the “bucket handle” (lower ribs) and “pump handle” (upper ribs) motions during breathing.

Cardiac Conduction System

SA node (right atrium, near SVC junction) — “pacemaker” (intrinsic rate 60–100 bpm) → impulse spreads across atria → AV node (interatrial septum, near coronary sinus) — slows conduction to allow atrial contraction before ventricular (intrinsic rate 40–60 bpm) → Bundle of His (interventricular septum) → R/L bundle branches → Purkinje fibers (rapid spread through ventricular myocardium — intrinsic rate 20–40 bpm).

Blood supply to conduction: SA node = RCA (~60%), LCx (~40%). AV node = RCA (~80%). Bundle of His = LAD. This is why inferior MI (RCA) can cause heart blocks (AV node ischemia).

Autonomic innervation: Sympathetic (T1–T4) → ↑ HR, ↑ contractility. Parasympathetic (CN X/vagus) → ↓ HR, ↓ AV conduction. Vagal tone predominates at rest (why resting HR is 60–100, not the SA node’s intrinsic ~100 bpm).

What is the arcuate line and what changes below it in the rectus sheath?
tap
~1/3 of the way from umbilicus to pubis. Below it: ALL three aponeuroses (EO, IO, TA) pass anterior to rectus abdominis; only transversalis fascia remains posterior. Above it: normal anterior + posterior rectus sheath.

Direct vs. Indirect inguinal hernia — how do you distinguish them?
tap
Indirect: lateral to inferior epigastric artery; enters deep ring; traverses entire canal; often enters scrotum; congenital; most common. Direct: medial to inferior epigastric artery; through Hesselbach's triangle (transversalis fascia weakness); acquired; rarely enters scrotum.
What are the 3 diaphragmatic apertures, their levels, and what passes through each?
tap
T8 — Caval foramen: IVC (most anterior/superior; through central tendon). T10 — Esophageal hiatus: esophagus + vagus nerves. T12 — Aortic hiatus: aorta + thoracic duct (most posterior/inferior; between crura). Memory: 8, 10, 12.
Why does liver/gallbladder disease cause right shoulder pain?
tap
Liver and gallbladder irritate the right hemidiaphragm → pain signals travel via right phrenic nerve (C3–5) → brain maps it to C3–5 dermatomes = right shoulder. Same mechanism: heart attack on left diaphragm → left shoulder/arm pain.
What are the 3 renal processes and what drives each?
tap
Filtration: passive, blood pressure–driven, non-selective (everything except proteins/RBCs filtered). Reabsorption: active transport, selective, returns nutrients to blood. Secretion: active transport, wastes added to filtrate for excretion. + Produces erythropoietin.
What does SAD PUCKER stand for?
tap
Suprarenal · Aorta/IVC · Duodenum · Pancreas · Ureters · Colon (ascending + descending) · Kidneys · Esophagus · Rectum. All retroperitoneal. Note: pancreatic tail is intraperitoneal.
What is the liver's dual blood supply and what percentage comes from each?
tap
80% from Hepatic Portal Vein (from small intestine + stomach — nutrient-rich but low O₂). 20% from Hepatic Artery (oxygenated). Portal triad = portal vein + hepatic artery + bile duct at periphery of lobule. Fats are an exception — enter lymphatics not portal vein.
What is the neurovascular plane of the abdomen and what travels in it?
tap
The plane between the internal oblique and transversus abdominis. Carries: thoracoabdominal nerves (T7–11), subcostal nerve (T12), iliohypogastric (L1), ilioinguinal (L1), and corresponding blood vessels. Nerves travel from posterolateral → anterior in this plane.
True, false, and floating ribs — which numbers are which?
tap
True (vertebrocostal) 1–7: own costal cartilage direct to sternum. False (vertebrochondral) 8–10: costal cartilage to rib above (indirect). Floating 11–12: no sternal attachment at all. Typical ribs = 3–9; atypical = 1, 2, 10, 11, 12.
Where do you insert a needle for thoracentesis and why?
tap
Insert just superior to the lower rib (i.e., along the upper edge of the rib below). The VAN bundle (Vein, Artery, Nerve) runs in the costal groove on the inferior border of each rib — inserting below the groove avoids the neurovascular bundle.
Right lung vs. left lung — lobes and why right bronchus matters clinically.
tap
Right: 3 lobes (upper/middle/lower). Left: 2 lobes (upper/lower; cardiac notch). Right main bronchus is straighter, wider, and shorter than left → aspirated foreign bodies and aspirated fluids (during intubation) tend to enter the right lung.
Name the 4 heart valves, their types, and their positions.
tap
AV valves (prevent backflow from ventricle to atrium): Tricuspid (R atria → R ventricle, 3 cusps) · Mitral/Bicuspid (L atria → L ventricle, 2 cusps). Semilunar valves (prevent backflow from great vessels): Pulmonic (R ventricle → pulmonary trunk) · Aortic (L ventricle → aorta).
Where do the coronary arteries originate and what do they supply?
tap
First branches of the aorta — ostia located just superior to the aortic semilunar valve. They supply the myocardium (heart muscle) with oxygenated blood. Left and right coronary arteries. Coronary artery disease → myocardial infarction when supply is compromised.
What is the cremasteric reflex and what does it test?
tap
Lightly stroke inner thigh superior → inferior → scrotum rises on that side (cremaster muscle contracts). Tests integrity of L1–2 (genitofemoral nerve, genital branch). Absent in upper motor neuron lesions. Also useful to test in acute scrotal pain — absent = testicular torsion until proven otherwise.
What are the abdominal aorta branches in order from superior to inferior?
tap
Celiac trunk (liver, spleen, stomach) → Superior mesenteric A. (small intestine) → Renal A. (kidneys) → Gonadal A. (ovaries/testes) → Inferior mesenteric A. (large intestine/sigmoid) → splits into common iliac arteries at L4.
