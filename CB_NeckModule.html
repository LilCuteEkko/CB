Cervical Spine & Neck

The neck is one of the most complex anatomical regions — it contains the airway, esophagus, major blood vessels to the brain, the cervical spinal cord, thyroid and parathyroid glands, lymph nodes, and 20+ muscles, all packed into a small space with minimal bony protection. It must support the ~5 kg head while allowing the greatest mobility of any spinal region. For PTs, the neck is a crossroads: cervical dysfunction can cause headaches, shoulder pain, arm symptoms, dizziness, and difficulty swallowing. This module covers surface anatomy, fascia layers, muscles with OINA, cervical triangles, nerve supply, vasculature, and visceral structures.

Red Flags — Cervical Lymph Nodes

Hard, non-tender, non-mobile nodes that don’t resolve in 4–6 weeks = possible malignancy. Tender, mobile nodes = reactive (infection). Supraclavicular nodes (Virchow’s on left) = particularly concerning for thoracic/abdominal malignancy via thoracic duct drainage.

Cervical Fascia Layers
The cervical fascia creates compartments that direct the spread of infection and guide surgical planes. Understanding these layers explains why retropharyngeal abscess can track to the mediastinum.

Superficial Cervical Fascia

▼
Description

Thin layer of subcutaneous connective tissue between skin and deep cervical fascia. Contains the platysma muscle, cutaneous nerves, superficial veins (external jugular), and superficial lymph nodes.

Investing (Superficial Layer of Deep) Fascia

▼
Description

Most superficial of the deep cervical fascia. Splits to invest both SCM and trapezius — creating “Four Corners” of fascial investment. Forms the roof of the posterior and anterior triangles. Splits again to invest the parotid and submandibular glands.

Pretracheal Fascia

▼
Description

Two portions: (1) Muscular layer — invests the infrahyoid strap muscles. (2) Visceral layer — invests the larynx, trachea, and thyroid gland. Creates the anterior visceral compartment — all structures within it move together during swallowing.

Prevertebral Fascia

▼
Description

Encloses the vertebral column and associated muscles (scalenes, deep back muscles). Extends laterally into the axilla as the axillary sheath, enclosing the brachial plexus and axillary vessels. Forms the posterior wall of the retropharyngeal space.

Carotid Sheath

▼
Description

Condensation of all three deep fascial layers surrounding the carotid vessels, IJV, and vagus nerve. Extends from base of skull to root of neck. Contents: CCA, ICA, IJV, CN X (vagus), deep cervical lymph nodes. Clinical: Carotid sinus at CCA bifurcation is a baroreceptor — electrotherapy here contraindicated (reflex bradycardia).

Key Concept

Scalene Muscles

▼
Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

The brachial plexus and subclavian artery pass between the anterior and middle scalenes (interscalene triangle). Hypertrophy or spasm of scalenes can compress these structures → TOS (neurogenic or vascular). The subclavian vein passes ANTERIOR to the anterior scalene. Cervical rib (0.5–1% of population) increases TOS risk.

Posterior Triangle
Borders & Contents

Borders: Posterior SCM (anterior), anterior trapezius (posterior), middle 1/3 clavicle (inferior). Floor = prevertebral fascia over scalenes, levator scapulae, splenius capitis. Key contents: CN XI (spinal accessory nerve — crosses superficially, vulnerable to biopsy injury), external jugular vein, cervical plexus cutaneous branches, brachial plexus trunks (emerge between scalenes), subclavian artery (3rd part), transverse cervical and suprascapular arteries.

CN XI in Posterior Triangle

The spinal accessory nerve crosses the posterior triangle superficially. It enters from deep to SCM (which it innervates) and crosses to reach the trapezius. This superficial course makes it vulnerable during lymph node biopsy or surgery in the posterior triangle. Injury → trapezius palsy: shoulder droop, inability to fully abduct arm above 90°, scapular winging (lateral type).

The cervical plexus (C1–C4), brachial plexus (C5–T1), and cranial nerves all pass through the neck. The phrenic nerve (C3–C5) is the most clinically critical cervical nerve.

Cervical Vasculature
The arterial supply to the neck is asymmetric. Left CCA and left subclavian arise directly from the aortic arch. On the right, the brachiocephalic trunk bifurcates into right CCA and right subclavian.
Arterial Supply
Common Carotid Artery (CCA)

Bifurcates at C3–C4 level (upper thyroid cartilage) into: Internal carotid artery (ICA) — ascends to brain via carotid canal; NO branches in the neck; joins Circle of Willis. Carotid sinus (baroreceptor) at origin. External carotid artery (ECA) — 8 branches: Superior thyroid, Lingual, Facial, Ascending pharyngeal, Occipital, Posterior auricular, Maxillary, Superficial temporal.

Subclavian Artery — Key Branches

Vertebral artery — enters transverse foramen at C6, ascends to C1, loops at C1, enters foramen magnum, R+L form basilar artery. Thyrocervical trunk — inferior thyroid a., transverse cervical a., suprascapular a. Internal thoracic artery. Costocervical trunk — deep cervical a. + superior intercostal a.

Visceral Structures of the Neck
The neck contains critical endocrine, respiratory, digestive, and phonation structures. Understanding their anatomy explains surgical risks and clinical presentations.

Thyroid Gland

▼
Anatomy

2 lobes connected by isthmus. Anterolateral to trachea/larynx. Isthmus anterior to 2nd–3rd tracheal rings. Highly vascular: superior thyroid artery (ECA) + inferior thyroid artery (thyrocervical trunk).

Function

Secretes T3 and T4 (regulate metabolism, growth, temperature). Calcitonin (reduces serum calcium, minor role).

Clinical

Hypothyroidism: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, depression. Hyperthyroidism: weight loss, heat intolerance, tachycardia, exophthalmos (Graves’). Surgery risks: recurrent laryngeal nerve (voice) + parathyroid glands (calcium).

Parathyroid Glands

▼
Anatomy & Function

Four small glands on posterior thyroid (2 superior, 2 inferior). Secrete PTH — increases serum calcium via bone resorption, renal reabsorption, GI absorption (vitamin D activation).

Clinical

Accidental removal during thyroidectomy = hypocalcemia (tetany, Chvostek’s sign, Trousseau’s sign). Hyperparathyroidism: “bones, stones, groans, moans” (bone pain, kidney stones, GI symptoms, neuropsychiatric).

Larynx

▼
Anatomy

Connects pharynx to trachea. Cartilages: thyroid (largest), cricoid (only complete ring), epiglottis, arytenoids (vocal cord tension). Voice production via vocal cord vibration. Airway protection during swallowing.

Innervation & Clinical

Superior laryngeal N (CN X): internal branch = sensory above cords; external branch = motor to cricothyroid. Recurrent laryngeal N (CN X) = all intrinsic muscles EXCEPT cricothyroid. Left RLN loops under aortic arch (longer, more vulnerable). Injury → vocal cord paralysis, hoarseness, aspiration. Bilateral injury = airway emergency.

Pharynx, Trachea & Esophagus

▼
Pharynx

3 sections: nasopharynx, oropharynx, laryngopharynx. Innervation: CN X via pharyngeal plexus. Swallowing: 3 phases (oral, pharyngeal, esophageal). Killian’s dehiscence → Zenker’s diverticulum.

Trachea

Begins at C6 (inferior cricoid). C-shaped rings (open posteriorly = trachealis muscle). Bifurcates at T4–T5 (carina). Right bronchus straighter → aspiration more likely to right lung.

Esophagus

Begins at C6 (posterior to trachea). Upper 1/3 = striated, middle = mixed, lower 1/3 = smooth muscle. Esophageal hiatus at T10. GERD: LES dysfunction. Dysphagia assessment critical in stroke/SCI/Parkinson’s.

Clinical Examples
Applied clinical scenarios demonstrating examination findings, clinical reasoning, and treatment decision-making.
Clinical Example 1 — Torticollis

28-year-old woke with left neck pain, head tilted LEFT, face rotated RIGHT. Clinical reasoning: Left SCM spasm (ipsilateral lateral flexion + contralateral rotation). Treatment: heat, gentle AROM, muscle energy technique. Rule out meningitis if fever.

Clinical Example 2 — Cervical Lymph Node Red Flag

During neck assessment, firm, non-tender, fixed 2 cm node in left supraclavicular fossa, present months. Clinical reasoning: Virchow’s node = RED FLAG for malignancy via thoracic duct. Urgent physician referral.

A 48-year-old patient reports torticollis — head tilted left, face rotated right. Which muscle is shortened and what innervates it?
tap
Left SCM is shortened. SCM causes ipsilateral lateral flexion + contralateral rotation. Innervated by CN XI (motor) and C2–C3 (proprioception).
Left recurrent laryngeal nerve is cut during thyroidectomy. List the expected deficits?
tap
RLN innervates ALL intrinsic laryngeal muscles except cricothyroid. Unilateral injury: hoarseness, weak/breathy voice, aspiration risk. Left RLN loops under aortic arch — longer and more vulnerable. Bilateral = airway emergency (cords paralyze in adduction).
Which infrahyoid muscle is NOT innervated by the ansa cervicalis in this scenario?
tap
Thyrohyoid — innervated by C1 fibers traveling along CN XII. All others (sternohyoid, sternothyroid, omohyoid) = ansa cervicalis (C1–C3).
A nerve is damaged during posterior triangle lymph node biopsy. The patient has shoulder drop and can’t ABduct above 90°. What nerve?
tap
CN XI (spinal accessory) — crosses posterior triangle superficially to reach trapezius. Injury = trapezius palsy: shoulder droop, limited arm elevation, lateral scapular winging.
Detail the asymmetry in origin of the common carotid arteries.
tap
Left CCA arises DIRECTLY from aortic arch. Right CCA arises from brachiocephalic trunk (1st branch of aortic arch) which also gives off right subclavian. Left RLN loops under aortic arch — aortic pathology can cause hoarseness.
Name the contents of the carotid sheath and why is it a contraindication zone for electrotherapy?
tap
Contents: CCA, ICA, IJV, CN X (vagus), deep cervical lymph nodes. Carotid sinus (baroreceptor) at CCA bifurcation — electrotherapy stimulation can trigger reflex bradycardia and hypotension (vasovagal).
What is the clinical significance of a palpable left supraclavicular lymph node in this scenario?
tap
Virchow’s node / Troisier’s sign = red flag for abdominal/thoracic malignancy (classically gastric cancer). Thoracic duct drains on the left — cancer cells metastasize via thoracic duct to this node. Requires urgent investigation.
