Diabetic Foot — Assessment, Risk & Ulcer Prevention
20.3 million (9.4%) have diabetes, 100 million are pre-diabetic, 7.2 million are undiagnosed. 20–25% of hospital admissions for diabetes are foot-related. 15% will develop LE ulcers. 67,000 LE amputations/year (60% are diabetes-related). 80% of diabetic amputations are PREVENTABLE.

Consequences of Diabetes & IWGDF Risk Classification

Diabetes Consequences for the Foot

Atherosclerosis: Poor oxygenation of tissue (feet/hands have smaller vessels = worse symptoms). Peripheral neuropathy: Motor loss (toe deformities, muscle imbalance, intrinsic minus foot, pes cavus, prominent MTHs, claw/hammer toes), sensory loss (insensate feet fail to respond to prolonged pressure), autonomic loss (loss of moisture regulation, hair/nail growth, skin integrity). Insulin-related: Deterioration of cartilage, increased collagen cross-bridges. Neuropathic fractures: Decreased tolerance for WB stress. Decreased foot adaptation on uneven surfaces. Deformities and abnormal biomechanics change pressure distribution → discomfort → callus → ulceration.

Charcot Foot

Affects persons with peripheral neuropathy, especially diabetes. Makes bone weak → fractures. Joints can dislocate. S/S: Redness, warmth, swelling, strong pedal pulse, joint instability, loss of sensation, subluxation, rocker-bottom deformity. May NOT be painful — person keeps walking, which makes it worse. No single diagnostic test — MRI is best. Suspect if neuropathic patient presents with sudden onset of localized swelling, warmth, and erythema WITHOUT an open wound. Treatment: total contact cast for offloading.

Ulcer Prevention — Patient Education

Key prevention facts: When inflammatory changes (heat/swelling) persist from 1 day to the next, breakdown was prevented by discontinuing repeated stress. Breakdown also prevented by decreasing pressure per repetition OR reducing number of repetitions. Patient education: Don't walk barefoot. Inspect feet regularly. Inspect footwear before putting on (check for objects inside). Trim toenails regularly. No chemicals or plasters on calluses. No heaters or hot water bottles. Use stockings without seams. Glucose control. Weight management. Stop smoking. Stay active. Footwear characteristics for high-risk patients: Snug heel (prevent pistoning), wide toe box (accommodate deformities), deep toe box, fashionable enough to wear. Insurance coverage: 1 pair custom/extra-depth shoes per year, 2 pairs insoles per year (3 if non-custom shoes), shoe modifications (rocker sole, flares).

Lower Extremity Orthotics — AFOs, KAFOs & FES
Orthotics compensate for impairments by controlling, supporting, or immobilizing body segments. Key concepts: pronation leads to knee valgus, lateral patella tracking, and hip IR. No posting for patients without sensation. No AFOs for fixed deformities. Can only unload one compartment at a time.

Plantarflexion-Knee Extension Couple

For any rigid object making contact with the ground, if weight vector and GRF vector are offset, a net moment is induced. With an AFO: The tibia is controlled from advancing over the foot, keeping the knee in extension during gait. This is why both SAFO and GRAFO work for crouch gait — both prevent anterior tibial translation. Heel wedge = increases anterior tibial translation (NOT what you want for crouch gait).

AFO Drawbacks & Contraindications

Drawbacks: Poor air circulation (uncomfortable in hot weather). Shoes need to be larger. Weight changes affect fit. Don't address hemiparetic gait abnormalities at hip/knee. Contraindications: Fixed deformities (AFO cannot correct what is structurally fixed). Knee orthotics: Can only unload one compartment. Lateral joint collapse = valgus (knock-kneed). Medial joint collapse = varus (bow-legged). KAFO: Donning = seated, knee extended, leg elevated. Do NOT grab onto halo uprights (spinal orthosis).

FES & WalkAide — Functional Electrical Stimulation

Training effect = improvements persist with WA offNo pacemakers, no pregnancy▼
WalkAide Footdrop Stimulator

Tilt sensor or foot sensor triggers stimulation during swing phase to activate dorsiflexors and correct foot drop. Pros: Increased mobility, decreased effort, safety, ease of use, greater independence, freedom, confidence. Cons: Positioning/electrode problems, skin rashes/irritation, difficulty wearing short skirts/shorts, prototype deficiencies. "Training effect": Strengthening of muscles and neural connections — walking speed increases even with WA turned OFF. Limitation vs AFO: WalkAide does NOT provide mediolateral stability during stance. A hinged AFO + stimulator combination showed highest speed improvements.

FES Contraindications & Precautions

Contraindications: Pacemakers or ICDs. Pregnancy (trunk/pelvis/abdomen/lumbar/hip region). Electrodes near neck, phrenic nerve, or urinary bladder stimulators. Areas of known PVD. Eyes, gonads. Active osteomyelitis. Areas of hemorrhage. Precautions: Patients without intact sensation. Patients who cannot communicate. Impaired cognition. Cardiac dysfunction. Epilepsy/seizures.

Spinal Orthoses — Cervical, Thoracic & Lumbar
Spinal orthoses restrict motion, provide support, and protect healing structures. Selection depends on the level and type of injury, surgical status, and required motion restriction. Never grab onto halo uprights.

Patient Education for Spinal Orthoses

Donning/doffing: Log roll for application in bed. Maintain spinal alignment during all transitions. Skin checks: Inspect under brace BID for redness, breakdown, pressure areas. Hygiene: Clean liner/pads regularly. No lotions under brace (can cause maceration). Activity: Follow weight-lifting restrictions. No bending, twisting, or lifting beyond prescribed limits. Wear during all upright activities unless told otherwise. Duration: Typically 6–12 weeks depending on healing. Progressive weaning per physician orders.

Amputation Levels — Indications, Advantages & Rehab
67,000 LE amputations per year. 60% are diabetes-related. The goal of amputation surgery is to preserve the longest functional residual limb while removing all nonviable tissue. Level selection depends on vascular status, healing potential, and functional goals.

IPOP (Immediate Post-Op Prosthesis)

Functions: (1) Protects residual limb against bumping/falls. (2) Maintains knee extension to prevent contractures. (3) Reduces edema. (4) Removable for wound inspection and bandage change. (5) Reduces recovery time. Pre-prosthetic care: Edema management (shrinker socks, elastic wraps — figure-8 pattern, NOT circular), desensitization (tapping, massage, texture exposure), ROM maintenance (prevent hip flexion/abduction contracture for AKA, knee flexion contracture for BKA), strengthening (hip extensors/abductors for AKA, quad/hamstrings for BKA), transfers and mobility with AD.

Prosthetic Components — Sockets, Feet, Knees & K-Levels
Prosthetic prescription is based on the patient's functional level (K-level), residual limb characteristics, activity goals, and medical status. Component selection directly affects gait quality, energy expenditure, and patient satisfaction.

Suspension Systems (Transtibial)

Roll-on liner: Pins (most common) or suction/elevated vacuum. Suspension sleeves: Neoprene or gel sleeve over socket brim. Anatomical: Supracondylar or supramalleolar (socket trim lines provide suspension). Cuff/belt: Strap or waist belt (less common, older technology). Temporary vs definitive socket: Temporary = allows for volume changes during early post-op period. Definitive = fabricated when volume stabilizes (typically 3–6 months post-amp).

SACH Foot Deviations

Too soft heel: Compressive heel reduces flexion moment, shifts GRF anterior → can cause excessive extension moment at knee. Too hard heel: Stiff heels create dangerous knee flexion moment. Must be calibrated to patient weight and activity level.

Microprocessor Knee Key Facts

Functional level 3–4. On-board computer analyzes gait 50 times per second. Continuous hydraulic adjustments ensure stability, security, and efficiency. Stance stability engaged/disengaged as necessary. Does NOT help sit-to-stand. DOES help stand-to-sit and stumble recovery. Requires regular charging and maintenance.

Knee Comparison: Polycentric vs Single Axis

Polycentric: Inherent stability at heel strike. Easy to initiate swing phase. Tend to be heavier. Increased maintenance. Good for K2–K3. Single axis: Less moving parts, simple design, low fabrication costs. Less stable at heel strike. One axis to absorb gait stress. Hydraulic vs Stance Control: Hydraulic (K3–4) provides resistance to flex/ext during swing, variable cadence, heavier, requires maintenance. Stance control (K1–3) provides added stability during missteps, braking mechanism locks during stance, most popular, lightweight. Brake will weaken and knee will fail without proper adjustments and maintenance.

Outcome Measures for K-Level Justification

To justify component level to insurance: TUG, Berg Balance, Amputee Mobility Predictor (AMPpro and AMPnoPro), Basic Amputee Mobility Score (BAMPS), Locomotor Capability Index 5 (LCI), 2-Minute and 6-Minute Walk Test, Plus-M. Must demonstrate functional level warrants the prescribed component. K3–4 components (hydraulic/microprocessor) require documented evidence of community/variable cadence ambulation ability.

Prosthetic Gait — Alignment, Deviations & 6 Determinants
Dynamic alignment is observed during ambulation. The goal: controlled foot flat, heel raise control, terminal impact dampening, smooth transitions, appropriate response to cadence changes, and vertical pylon at mid-stance.

6 Determinants of Effective Energy Use in Walking

(1) Pelvic rotation. (2) Pelvic tilt (list). (3) Knee flexion at mid-stance. (4) Foot and ankle motion. (5) Knee motion. (6) Lateral pelvic displacement. All work to minimize vertical and lateral COM displacement — reducing energy cost. Amputees lose several of these determinants depending on amputation level and prosthetic design.

Dynamic Alignment Checklist

Observe during ambulation: Controlled foot flat (no foot slap). Heel rise control (no premature rise). Terminal impact dampening (no audible slam into extension). Smooth transitions between stance and swing. Appropriate response to cadence changes. Vertical pylon at mid-stance. Symmetrical step length and timing. Adequate toe clearance during swing. Level pelvis (no excessive drop or hike). Smooth COM trajectory (minimal vertical and lateral displacement).

Patient with diabetes presents with red, warm, swollen foot, strong pedal pulse, and loss of sensation. What are you thinking?
tap
Charcot foot (diabetic neuropathic osteoarthropathy). Characteristics: decreased sensitivity (neuropathy), joint collapse and bone weakening, can result from minor trauma. Strong pedal pulse distinguishes from PVD (which would have decreased/absent pulses). Redness, warmth, and swelling in a neuropathic patient WITHOUT an open wound = suspect Charcot. May not be painful — patient keeps walking, making it worse. Rocker-bottom deformity can develop. No single diagnostic test — MRI is best. >2° temperature change = neuropathic fracture. 4+ bounding pulse = possible Charcot or aneurysm. Treatment: Total contact cast for offloading. Do NOT allow continued unprotected WB.
What's the difference between a SAFO and GRAFO, and when does each work for crouch gait?
tap
SAFO: Posterior shell with anterior strap. Prevents both excessive DF and PF. GRAFO: Anterior shell. Prevents excessive DF. Sets ankle in slightly more PF position which may place knee in more extension. For crouch gait: Both work through the plantarflexion-knee extension couple — the AFO prevents anterior tibial translation, keeping the knee in extension during stance. Research (Ries 2019): No significant difference between SAFO and GRAFO for correcting crouch gait in children with CP. Factors affecting outcomes: crouch severity, knee flexion contracture, DF in stance, and age. Key rule: No AFOs for fixed deformities. A heel wedge INCREASES anterior tibial translation (opposite of what you want).
AKA patient with circumducted gait and forward flexed trunk. What are the prosthetic and patient causes?
tap
Circumduction causes — Prosthetic: Prosthesis too long, excessive knee friction, inadequate suspension (prosthesis dropping during swing). Patient: Hip abductor substitution, habit, fear of catching toe, locked prosthetic knee. Forward trunk lean — Prosthetic: Socket aligned too far anteriorly, inadequate knee friction/stability. Patient: Weak hip extensors, fear of knee buckling, habit from pre-prosthetic period. For this specific case: She has a manually locking knee maintained in locked position — this CAUSES the circumduction (she swings the entire extended leg around). A hydraulic knee (K3-4) would allow swing-phase flexion and eliminate the need for circumduction. Justify to insurance using TUG, Berg, AMPpro, 2MWT/6MWT, Plus-M. Her unequal stride length (short sound side) is compensatory for the locked prosthetic knee.
What monofilament finding indicates loss of protective sensation, and why does it matter for footwear prescription?
tap
5.07 monofilament (10g): Inability to sense this = positive loss of protective sensation = leading cause of foot ulceration in diabetes. Scale: 4.17 = normal. 5.07 = protective sensation threshold. 6.10 = loss of protective sensation (OR = 3.44). 128-Hz tuning fork is actually more sensitive to onset of neuropathic changes than monofilament. Biothesiometer >25V has OR of 7.95. Why it matters for footwear: Loss of protective sensation + foot deformity = IWGDF Risk Level 2 (OR 2.25) → needs depth shoes with custom molded insoles and rigid rocker sole. Add history of ulcer/amputation = Risk Level 3 (OR 6.67) → needs the above PLUS possibly an AFO. No posting for patients without sensation — they can't feel pressure changes from posts and could develop ulcers. ABI <0.9 = refer to MD for PVD evaluation. ABI >1.2 in DM = calcified vessels, ABI is useless, refer.
Why is a Syme's amputation NOT appropriate for a diabetic patient, and what's the first choice instead?
tap
Syme's (ankle disarticulation) is NOT for diabetic patients because they can't adequately care for it — healing depends on good blood supply to the skin flap, and diabetic patients have compromised vasculature. Additionally, fat pad migration is common (especially in heavy patients), which can necessitate revision to a higher amputation level. Transtibial (BKA) is the 1st choice for diabetic patients. Tibia transected 10–12cm below tibial tuberosity (absolute minimum = below the tibial tuberosity because of quad attachment). 70–100% of unilateral BKAs are functionally ambulatory. Only 10–20% increase in energy cost. Contraindicated if: Fixed knee flexion contracture >15° (can't fit prosthetic). Gangrenous/infectious process extending close to tibial tuberosity. Energy comparison: TMA = 10%, Syme's = 10%, BKA = 10–20%, Knee disarticulation = 40–60%, AKA = 65–100%. Every level higher = exponentially more energy.
