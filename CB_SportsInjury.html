Soft Tissue Injury & Healing
NCAA data (1988–2004): 50% of all injuries are lower extremity. 15% are lateral ankle sprains. Concussion and ACL rupture frequencies increased by 7% over the sample period. Understanding tissue healing phases is fundamental to every sports rehab decision — you cannot progress rehab faster than biology allows.

Key Tissue Healing Concept

Injury → Pain → Altered Motor Control. Back pain in youth = facet, tissue, or disc-related. Hamstrings may try to stabilize → possible LBP and hamstring tightness (hamstrings compensating for multifidus insufficiency). An athlete placed on the disability list if they can't play in 3 straight days. Athletes with a history of acute LBI shut off significantly fewer muscles and did so with delayed timing even when symptom-free — they believed they had regained preinjury function but had altered neuromuscular patterns.

Motor Control & Pain Theory — Moving Differently in Pain

▼
Vicious Cycle Theory (Old)

Muscle activity increases stereotypically in pain → sustained activity → ischemia → accumulation of algesic agents → more pain. Increased muscle spindle sensitivity via Group III/IV nociceptive afferents. Problem: doesn't match clinical observations.

Pain Adaptation Theory (Old)

Activity of painful muscles DECREASES. Activity of antagonist muscles INCREASES. This reduces amplitude, velocity, and force of painful movement. Problem: mechanisms poorly defined.

New Theory (Hodges) — 5 Factors

(1) Involves redistribution of activity within AND between muscles. (2) Changes mechanical behavior (modified movement and stiffness). (3) Leads to protection from further pain/injury or threatened pain/injury. (4) Not explained by simple excitability changes — involves multiple levels of the motor system (complementary, additive, competitive). (5) Short-term benefit but potential long-term consequences: increased load, decreased movement, and decreased variability. Clinical implication: Athletes who recover from injury may continue to demonstrate altered motor patterns even when pain-free. The injured athletes believed they had regained preinjury function — but they hadn't. This is why return-to-sport testing must assess MOVEMENT QUALITY, not just strength and ROM.

Acceleration & Deceleration Injury Mechanisms

Acceleration injuries: Hamstring strains (late swing phase of sprinting), groin strains (rapid push-off), Achilles rupture (explosive push-off), metatarsal stress fractures (sudden training volume increase). Deceleration injuries: ACL tears (70% non-contact — landing, cutting, pivoting), patellar tendinopathy (eccentric landing loads), tibial stress fractures, anterior ankle impingement. Combined: Concussion (rapid acceleration/deceleration of brain), cervical spine injuries (whiplash mechanism), acromioclavicular joint sprains (direct or indirect force). Key principle: Deceleration injuries tend to involve eccentric failure of the musculotendinous unit or ligamentous structures that cannot absorb the imposed forces.

Shoulder in Sports — Red Flags, Diagnosis & RTC Protocol
The shoulder produces the highest angular velocities of any human movement and places tremendous stress on the shoulder complex. RTC tears affect at least 10% of people over 60. 250,000 repairs per year in the US. Overall return to sport: 84.7%. Return at equivalent level: 65.9%.

Shoulder Red & Yellow Flags in Athletes

▼
Red Flags Requiring Referral

Pain reduced with sleeping ON shoulder: 60–70% of all pts with shoulder pathology — this is common, not a red flag. No difficulty sleeping: 75–92% of shoulder patients report difficulty getting to sleep or waking during the night. RED FLAGS: Pain NOT alleviated by rest. Insidious onset AND pain that cannot be reproduced. Systemic signs/symptoms. Differential diagnosis: Kehr Sign (L shoulder pain from spleen injury + hypotension), cardiovascular (L shoulder referred from heart — includes HTN, heart disease, DM, hyperlipidemia), gallstones (R scapula referred pain — >40, overweight, female), cancer (constitutional symptoms — if hx of cancer, ALWAYS rule out).

RTC Tear Protocol — From Injury to Return to Play

▼
Mechanism & Medical Management

MOI: FOOSH (59%), forced ER of abducted UE, stress lifting, traumatic anterior dislocation. Summary: compressive/tensile stress (anterior humeral translation + resisting IR forces), instability of inert tissues, and trauma. SITS: Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, Subscapularis. Medical management: Rest, activity modification, NSAIDs. Corticosteroid injection (short-term relief, facilitates rehab). PRP and hyaluronic acid as alternatives. Surgical: Open repair (large/complex tears), all-arthroscopic repair, mini-open repair (3–5cm incision). Complications: nerve injury, infection, deltoid detachment, stiffness, re-tear.

PT Intervention Progression

Phase 1 (Protective): PROM into extension, flexion, IR/ER. Pain management (STM, cold, heat, TENS). Phase 2 (Active-Assisted): Wand/PVC pipe exercises into extension, flexion, IR/ER. Postural training (retraction, extension, IR/ER with bands, 90/90 ER/IR, thoracic extension). Phase 3 (Strengthening): Rows, bicep curls, banded shoulder flexion, mid rows. Phase 4 (Stability/Sport-Specific): Kettlebell swings, body blade scaption, serratus anterior press-up with perturbation, overhead exercises. Decreasing soft tissue tightness: STM, PNF stretching, joint mobilization. Optimizing ROM: PROM with joint mobilization.

Return to Play — The 3 P's

Performance: Restoration of function through sport-specific training. Plyometrics, agility drills, end-range proprioceptive exercises, perturbation drills. Focus on stability and maintaining end-range stability. Practice: Gradual progression via sequentially increasing time, intensity, and reps during practice. Play: Unrestricted participation following successful practice progression — competition at 100% effort. Discharge criteria: (1) Appropriate healing time. (2) Completed formal rehab. (3) Full sport-specific pain-free ROM. (4) Excellent stability with no pain during special tests. (5) Strength meeting specific criterion. (6) Satisfactory functional test scores. (7) Sport-specific testing passed. (8) Acceptable patient-reported subjective score. (9) No kinesiophobia (fear of movement).

Knee & ACL — Reconstruction, Rehab & Evidence
ACL rupture frequencies have increased 7% over the NCAA study period. The female athlete is 2–8x more likely to tear their ACL. After ACLR: 63% returned to pre-injury level, 44% returned to competitive sport, and 20–30% experience re-tear. Altered neuromuscular control persists ≥2 years post-reconstruction.

ACL Injury Mechanics, Risk Factors & Rehab Timeline

▼
Non-Contact ACL Tear Mechanism

70% of ACL tears are non-contact. Classic mechanism: deceleration + cutting/pivoting with knee near full extension, knee valgus collapse, tibial internal rotation, and foot planted. Landing from a jump with poor neuromuscular control. Female risk factors: Wider Q-angle, increased knee valgus, ligament laxity (hormonal), decreased hamstring-to-quad ratio, decreased neuromuscular control, smaller ACL cross-section, narrower intercondylar notch. Modifiable factors (basis of prevention programs): Neuromuscular control, landing mechanics, hip-core strength, hamstring strength.

ACL Rehab Critical Timeline

Week 0–2: PRICE, quad activation, patellar mobilization, PROM toward full extension (most important early goal). Week 2–6: Progressive WB, CKC exercises (wall slides, mini squats), stationary bike. Week 4–6: THIS IS THE WEAKEST POINT. Graft is undergoing ligamentization — strongest early post-op, then weakens as native ligament tissue is replaced before remodeling into functional graft. Protect during this window. Month 3–6: Progressive strengthening, neuromuscular training, proprioception. Begin running protocol ~4 months if criteria met. Month 6–9: Sport-specific training, agility, plyometrics. Month 9–12: Return-to-sport testing. Limb symmetry index ≥90% for quad, hamstring, hop tests.

Evidence — Post-ACLR Findings

Ardern et al. (BJSM 2011): 63% returned to pre-injury level. 44% returned to competitive sport. 20–30% retear rate. Benjaminse et al. (2015): Female athletes who returned to sport after ACLR continued to demonstrate biomechanical limb asymmetry ≥2 years post-reconstruction during landing. This asymmetry predicts the 2nd tear. Altered neuromuscular control of the hip and knee during dynamic landing persists long after clinical discharge. Implication: Time-based return-to-sport is insufficient. Criteria-based testing (LSI ≥90%, hop tests, movement quality) is essential.

ACL Prevention Programs & Unique Knee Injuries

▼
ACL Injury Prevention Programs

Programs like FIFA 11+, PEP Program, and Sportsmetrics have shown 50–70% reduction in ACL injury rates when properly implemented. Key components: (1) Neuromuscular warm-up. (2) Landing mechanics training (soft landing, knee over toe, avoid valgus). (3) Plyometric training (proper deceleration). (4) Core and hip strengthening. (5) Balance and proprioception. (6) Hamstring strengthening (Nordic curls). Must be performed ≥2–3x/week to be effective. Compliance is the biggest challenge.

FMS & SFMA — Movement Screening Systems
Motor Control = ability to regulate and direct mechanisms essential to movement. Consider Task (mobility, stability, manipulation, variable), Individual (action, ROM, perceptions, feedback, cognition, fear avoidance), and Environment (regulated vs chaotic). The SFMA is a "GPS for rehab" — it identifies the root cause of movement dysfunction.

5 Principles & FMS vs SFMA

▼
5 Principles of Functional Movement Systems

(1) Body weight movement patterns should NOT provoke pain. (2) Gross limitations of fundamental movement patterns, even if pain-free, can cause compensation and substitution. (3) Fundamental patterns should be symmetrical. (4) Fundamental movement capability should precede performance-based capability. (5) Fundamental movement capability should precede complex movement activity or complex skill training. Additional: Pain with movement = reported, managed, diagnosed, and treated by medical professional. Manage movement pattern limitations and asymmetries (mobility and stability) BEFORE applying significant volume of fitness, performance, or sports training.

FMS (Functional Movement Screen)

Screens movement RISK as it pertains to exercise, sport, and increased activity. 7 tests: Deep Squat, Hurdle Step, Inline Lunge, Shoulder Mobility, Active SLR, Trunk Stability Push-Up, Rotary Stability. Scoring: 3 = complete and consistent. 2 = completed with compensation/faulty form. 1 = incomplete. 0 = pain. Score ≤14 = increased injury risk. Asymmetry between sides is a key finding.

SFMA (Selective Functional Movement Assessment)

Helps separate pain-provoking from dysfunctional patterns. 7 top-tier assessments: Cervical Spine, Upper Extremity, Multisegmental Flexion, Multisegmental Extension, Multisegmental Rotation, Single Leg Stance, Overhead Deep Squat. Scoring: FN (Functional Non-painful) — leave alone. FP (Functional Painful) — treat pain. DN (Dysfunctional Non-painful) — START HERE. DP (Dysfunctional Painful) — treat both. Breakout: Systematically breaks the movement into parts → arrive at diagnosis: TED (Tissue Extensibility Dysfunction), JMD (Joint Mobility Dysfunction), or Motor Control Problem (teach them how to move differently).

Baseball & Softball — Pitching Mechanics & Injuries

Softball Windmill Pitch (6 Phases)

Key differences from baseball: Flat surface (no mound — no gravity assistance), shorter distance (12.2m vs 18.4m), no pitch count (1,500–2,000 in 3 days). Distraction force and torque at shoulder/elbow = SIMILAR to baseball. Phase 1 (Wind-up): IR of throwing arm, weight to ipsilateral leg. Phase 2 (6→3 o'clock): Contralateral stride leg in hip flexion/knee extension. Glutes stabilize pelvis. Phase 3 (3→12 — Top of Backswing): Max ER position = full cocking. Post deltoid, infraspinatus, teres minor active. Phase 4 (12→9): Stride 60–70% height. "Leaping" to get closer to batter. Glutes, scapular stabilizers, biceps. Phase 5 (9→release): Pec major, subscapularis, serratus anterior IR and adduct humerus. Biceps activity greatest. At release: glutes and triceps peak. Phase 6 (Follow-through): Abrupt stop by stride leg (high GRF). Greatest stress = acceleration phase (SB) vs deceleration phase (BB).

BB vs SB Biomechanical Comparison

Similarities: Subscapularis and pectoralis major stabilize. Serratus anterior stabilizes humeral head. Pecs generate power. Increased torque for IR. Increased distractive forces at shoulder and elbow. Differences: SB greatest stress during acceleration, BB during deceleration. SB power from IR, BB from adduction. SB humerus adducted, BB abducted. Axillary artery thrombosis (hyperabduction syndrome): Artery compressed by pec minor during excessive ABER + extension. Symptoms: claudication, rapid fatigue, decreased distal pulses, pallor, decreased skin temp.

Swimming — 40–91% Prevalence of Shoulder Pain

400K competitive swimmers. Prevalence: 40–91%, increases with years of participation (15% elite swimmers have bilateral pain). 8,000–20,000 revolutions/week. 500,000 stroke revolutions/season. Impingement in Swimming

Freestyle swimmers have shoulder impingement for about 25% of stroke time. Increased risk if: (1) More IR during pull phase (high elbow pull — mechanical advantage for propulsion but puts structures at risk), (2) Late initiation of ER during recovery, (3) Decreased upward scapular rotation. A larger scapular tilt angle REDUCES impingement risk. Etiology: Overuse, GH laxity, muscle imbalances/inflexibility, poor stroke mechanics. Physical exam findings: Tight pec minor, mid-trap weakness, poor trunk endurance.

Running Injuries — Prevention & Conditioning
Injury rates in runners: 25–65% (college athletes 51%, military 20–50%). Yearly incidence of long-distance running injuries: 19.4–79.3%. Etiologies are multifactorial — training, anatomical, and biomechanical. History of previous injury is the strongest predictor of subsequent injury.

Risk Factors, Biomechanics & Common Running Injuries

▼
Training Factors

Excessive distance or intensity. Rapid increases in weekly mileage (>10%/week rule). Surface and shoe selection. Stretching before running may increase injury incidence (Hreljac) — conflicting evidence. Higher mileage per week in males = risk factor. But increased training miles per week appears PROTECTIVE against knee injuries.

Anatomical Factors

Arch height (high = rigid, low = hypermobile). Degree of pronation/supination. Genu valgum or varum. Rearfoot varus. Leg length discrepancies. Muscular weakness (hip abductors, glutes, core).

Biomechanical Factors

Magnitude of impact forces. Rate of impact loading. Magnitude of active push-off forces. Magnitude and rate of foot pronation. Midfoot rotation vs tibial internal rotation — the femur or tibia can rotate OR the foot can compensate.

Running Shoe Classification

Board Lasted: Rigid platform, most stable but uncommon now. Slip Lasted: No board — most flexible (seam down middle of footbed). Combination: Board in rearfoot, slip in forefoot (control + flexibility). Strobel Lasted: Most common today — thin EVA sheet glued to midsole, upper stitched around perimeter. Less flexible than slip, not as rigid as board.

Exercise Science — Bioenergetics, Hormones & Programming
Understanding the physiological basis of exercise allows PTs to design optimal training programs for injury prevention, rehabilitation, and performance. The hormonal response to exercise directly affects recovery, adaptation, and return-to-sport timelines.

Hormonal Response to Exercise

Fight response (exercise/stress): Increases in HR, BP, cortisol, epinephrine, testosterone. Adrenal glands release cortisol during fight/workout — tells brain to be in fight mode. Testosterone: Increases with resistance training (especially heavy compound lifts). Important for muscle protein synthesis and recovery. Cortisol: Catabolic hormone — necessary short-term but chronic elevation leads to muscle breakdown, immune suppression, and impaired recovery. Balance: Ventral vagal = calm, "life is good." Dorsal vagal (too long) = hormone dump → hypervigilant → depressive. Sympathetic = fight or flight. Inter-set rest periods (Schoenfeld): Longer rest periods (2–3+ min) allow greater volume and strength gains. Short rest (<60 sec) may maximize metabolic stress and hypertrophy in certain protocols.

The Female Athlete Triad

Three interconnected conditions: (1) Disordered eating / low energy availability (caloric intake insufficient for exercise demands). (2) Menstrual dysfunction / amenorrhea (absent or irregular periods from hormonal disruption). (3) Low bone mineral density / osteoporosis (increased stress fracture risk). Now recognized as a spectrum (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport — RED-S) affecting both males and females. Screening: Ask about menstrual history, dietary habits, stress fracture history. Management: Multidisciplinary (physician, dietitian, psychologist, PT). Increase caloric intake, reduce training load if needed, address bone health.

Programming Principles for Return to Sport

Concurrent training: Combining aerobic and resistance training. Meta-analysis shows it can be effective but may attenuate strength/power gains if aerobic volume is too high. Periodization: Systematic planning of training variation (volume, intensity, frequency) to optimize performance and recovery. Linear (progressive overload) vs undulating (daily/weekly variation). Sport-specific principle: Training must mimic the metabolic, biomechanical, and neuromuscular demands of the sport. A distance runner needs aerobic programming; a football lineman needs phosphagen system training.

Why is the 4–6 week mark the most dangerous period after ACL reconstruction?
tap
Ligamentization. The graft is strongest immediately after surgery (it's a strong tendon). At 4–6 weeks, the graft undergoes avascular necrosis and revascularization — the original tendon tissue is being replaced by ligament tissue. During this transition, the graft is at its weakest point. Progressive loading must be carefully managed during this window. The graft then gradually remodels and strengthens over 6–12+ months. This is why return to sport is 9–12 months — the biology, not the patient's feelings, determines the timeline. Additionally, 63% return to pre-injury level, 44% to competitive sport, and 20–30% will retear. Limb asymmetry during landing persists ≥2 years post-ACLR and predicts second tears.
What is the biggest difference between the baseball overhead pitch and the softball windmill pitch in terms of injury risk?
tap
Greatest stress location is different: Baseball = deceleration phase (posterior cuff and scapular stabilizers are MOST active here, eccentric overload). Softball = acceleration phase (pec major, subscapularis, and serratus doing maximal work). Other differences: BB pitches from 10-inch elevated mound (gravity assist) while SB pitches from flat surface (abrupt stop by stride leg = high GRF). SB has no pitch count (1,500–2,000 pitches in 3 days). SB distance is shorter (12.2m vs 18.4m). Power generation: SB from IR, BB from adduction. Humerus: SB adducted, BB abducted. However: Distraction force at the shoulder and torque at the shoulder/elbow are SIMILAR between the two. Both require subscapularis and pec major for stabilization, serratus for humeral head stabilization.
What is the SFMA and how does DN (Dysfunctional Non-painful) guide your treatment?
tap
SFMA = Selective Functional Movement Assessment — "GPS for rehab." It systematically breaks movement patterns into parts to arrive at a diagnosis: Tissue Extensibility Dysfunction (TED), Joint Mobility Dysfunction (JMD), or Motor Control Problem. DN (Dysfunctional Non-painful) = START HERE. Pain isn't the root cause of this dysfunction. The movement is limited but doesn't hurt — meaning it's a mobility, stability, or motor control issue that exists independently of the painful pattern. Treating DN first often resolves compensations that are causing pain elsewhere. Scoring: FN = leave alone. FP = treat pain source. DN = treat dysfunction first. DP = treat both pain and dysfunction. 5 Principles: Pain-free patterns should not provoke pain. Limitations cause compensation. Patterns should be symmetrical. Fundamental capability before performance. Movement competency before training volume.
Swimmer with bilateral shoulder pain, 15 years of competitive swimming, pain at 76% of training sessions. What are you looking for?
tap
Risk factors for this age/exposure: Hours/year, years of exposure, and decreased side bridge endurance (masters category 23–76). With 15 years of competitive swimming at 8,000–20,000 revolutions/week (500,000/season), this is classic overuse impingement. Exam focus: Pec minor tightness (VERY common in swimmers), mid-trap weakness, trunk endurance (prone and side bridge), scapular dyskinesis, GH IR deficit, serratus anterior strength. Biomechanics to assess: Hand entry position (altered = decreased deltoid/UT/rhomboid activation), elbow exit (altered = increased infraspinatus with decreased subscapularis). Freestyle swimmers experience impingement during 25% of stroke time. Risk increases with: more IR during pull phase (high elbow pull), late ER initiation during recovery, and decreased upward scapular rotation. Treatment: Address pec minor flexibility, strengthen mid/lower traps and serratus, improve trunk endurance, stroke mechanics modification.
What are the 9 return-to-play criteria for shoulder injuries and what are the 3 P's?
tap
9 RTP Criteria: (1) Appropriate healing time. (2) Completed formal rehab program. (3) Full sport-specific pain-free ROM. (4) Excellent stability with no pain during special tests. (5) Strength meeting specific participation criterion. (6) Satisfactory functional test scores. (7) Sport-specific testing passed. (8) Acceptable patient-reported subjective score. (9) No kinesiophobia (fear of movement). The 3 P's: Performance = restoration of function through sport-specific training (plyometrics, agility, end-range proprioception, perturbation drills in clinic). Practice = gradual progression via sequentially increasing time, intensity, and reps during practice. Play = unrestricted participation at 100% effort in competition, following successful practice progression. Overall RTC stats: 84.7% return to sport, 65.9% at equivalent level, 49.9% of professional/competitive at same level.
What does Hodges' new motor control theory say about athletes who "feel recovered" after injury?
tap
They may feel recovered but still have altered motor patterns. Hodges' 5 factors: (1) Activity redistributes within AND between muscles. (2) Mechanical behavior changes (modified movement, stiffness). (3) Changes are protective (short-term benefit). (4) Multiple levels of the motor system are involved (not simple excitability changes). (5) Short-term benefit but long-term consequences: increased load, decreased movement, decreased variability. Evidence: Athletes with a history of acute LBI shut off significantly fewer muscles and did so with delayed timing — even when SYMPTOM-FREE. They believed they had regained preinjury function but had not. Clinical implication: Return-to-sport testing must assess MOVEMENT QUALITY, not just strength, ROM, and pain. This is why the FMS/SFMA, hop tests, and movement analysis are essential — an athlete who "feels fine" may still be compensating in ways that set them up for re-injury.
