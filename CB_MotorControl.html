Motor Control Across the Lifespan · Neurorehabilitation

Theory of Motor Control — Clinical Application

Motor control is the science of how the nervous system organizes, coordinates, and adapts movement across tasks and environments. This is everything for PT — it explains why patients move the way they do after neurological injury, what recovery actually looks like, and why specific interventions work. This module covers the I-T-E framework, all five theories, motor learning principles, neurofacilitation approaches, task-oriented methods, stroke rehabilitation, postural control across the lifespan, and every functional outcome measure you need in clinic.

Foundations of Motor Control
APTA Vision: "Transforming society by optimizing movement to improve the human experience." Motor control is the science that underpins this mission. The I-T-E framework organizes every clinical encounter: who is the patient (Individual), what are they doing (Task), and where are they doing it (Environment)?
Core Definitions
Motor Control

"The ability to regulate the mechanisms required to generate movement to meet the demands of a given task in a specific environment." (Shumway-Cook & Woollacott 2011) Field definition: "the nature of movement and how movement is controlled." You are a movement scientist.

Motor Learning

"The acquisition and/or modification of skilled action." Involves practice and experience leading to relatively permanent changes in the capability to generate skilled actions. Performance = temporary changes in motor behavior. Learning = permanent structural/synaptic changes.

Motor Development

"The evolution of changes in motor behavior occurring as a result of growth, maturation, and experience across a lifespan." In pediatrics: work to gain a skill. In adults: work to return a skill. This distinction shapes how you frame goals, activities, and prognosis.

The I-T-E Framework
Individual — Who?

Action (motor/neuromuscular system), Perception (sensory/perceptual system — integration of sensory input into meaningful information), Cognition (attention, planning, motivation, emotion — provides the goal and intent). All three must be assessed and addressed. A patient with intact motor function but impaired cognition cannot perform a complex functional task.

Task — What?

Stability (maintaining posture/balance), Mobility (moving through space — ADLs, transfers, walking), Manipulation (UE demands + increased cognitive load). Also: open vs. closed task characteristics. Design principle: simple → complex, closed → open, stability → mobility → manipulation.

Environment — Where?

Closed/Predictable: fixed, stable — floor doesn't change, patient controls pace. Open/Unpredictable: constantly changing — busy hallway, uneven terrain. Regulatory factors: directly constrain movement (floor surface, cup shape). Non-regulatory factors: background noise, distractions — don't directly constrain but affect performance.

Task Taxonomy — The 2x2 Grid
Closed / Predictable EnvironmentOpen / Unpredictable Environment
StabilitySitting on chair at rest; standing at sink; holding a railingStanding on rocker board; perturbations; seated on moving bus
MobilityWalking on flat empty hallway; running on treadmill; climbing fixed stairsWalking in crowded mall; crossing busy street; gait over uneven terrain

Design Principle

Progression always: simple → complex, closed → open, stability → mobility. A patient who can sit safely in a quiet PT gym cannot yet navigate a busy SNF hallway. These are different tasks in different environments and must be trained separately. Ecological validity = the patient's real-world environment.

Sensory Systems in Motor Control
Three Balance Sensory Inputs
  • Visual system — eyes, horizon reference
  • Vestibular system — inner ear, acceleration/head position
  • Somatosensation — tactile, proprioception, joint position

Cerebellum = automatic response/fine tuner/adapter. Basal ganglia = memory-dependent motor patterns. These systems don't operate independently — damage to one forces reliance on the others (sensory reweighting). Loss of reweighting capacity = major fall risk factor in aging and neurological patients.

Cognition's Role
Cognitive Systems

Attention, planning, problem solving, motivation, and emotion underlie intentions and goals. Cognition provides the "why" behind movement. Cognitive load directly affects movement quality: dual-tasking (cognitive + motor) degrades both in neurological patients, aging adults, and healthy novice learners.

Clinical Gap

The gap between clinic performance and real-world function is the central problem motor control science helps solve. A patient who can walk perfectly in your gym will fall at home if you haven't trained them in their actual environment with its actual demands. Always ask: does this task reflect their real life?

Five Theories of Motor Control
Theories provide frameworks for interpreting movement and guidelines for clinical practice. They are dynamic — evolving as science advances. Each theory has elements that remain clinically relevant today. Understanding limitations is as important as the core ideas, because limitations reveal which approach emerged as a correction.

1. Reflex Theory — Sherrington (early 1900s)

Reflexes = building blocksStimulus → ResponseCannot explain spontaneous movement▼
Core Idea

Reflexes are the fundamental building blocks of complex behavior. All movement is ultimately a chain of reflexes triggered by sensory input. Complex voluntary movement = integrated chains of simple reflexes. The nervous system operates as a stimulus-response machine.

Clinical Application

Neurofacilitation approaches built on this: sensory input (brushing, icing, tapping) to elicit or inhibit movement. Using primitive reflexes to facilitate movement in early recovery. Reflex testing remains a core part of the neuro exam today.

Limitations
  • Cannot explain spontaneous movement without sensory trigger
  • Cannot explain speed of rapid movement — feedback loops too slow
  • Cannot explain novel movement in novel environments
  • Deafferented animals still move — reflex chains aren't the whole story
  • Ignores motivation, context, and anticipation

2. Hierarchical Theory — Jackson (late 1800s), expanded mid-20th century

Higher centers control lowerCortex → Brainstem → SCNot strictly top-down▼
Core Idea

The nervous system is organized hierarchically — higher centers control lower. Cortex controls brainstem which controls spinal cord. Development follows cephalocaudal and proximal-to-distal progression, with cortical control gradually suppressing primitive reflexes.

Clinical Application

Explains why primitive reflexes re-emerge after cortical damage. Explains developmental sequence. NDT/Bobath used this concept. Still foundational for UMN vs LMN lesion understanding.

Limitations
  • Movement is NOT strictly top-down — spinal CPGs generate coordinated movement independently
  • Cerebellum and basal ganglia modulate without being "higher" or "lower"
  • Does not explain bidirectional communication among levels
  • Too rigid to explain adaptability and context-dependence

3. Motor Programming Theory — Schmidt (1970s)

Central motor programs (GMP)Open-loop controlSchema TheoryUnderweights feedback▼
Core Idea

"Control of movement is managed by a motor program that organizes, initiates, and carries out intended action." GMP (Generalized Motor Program) = stored abstract representation of a movement class; contains rules for spatial/temporal patterns of muscle activity. Movement can be executed without sensory feedback (open-loop control).

Schmidt's Schema Theory (1975)

A schema = set of rules stored in memory following multiple presentations. GMP stores the rules; short-term memory stores: initial conditions, parameters used, KR received, sensory consequences. Variability of practice strengthens schema — you get better at lifting ANY unknown weight, not just trained ones. Best support for variable practice schedules.

Open vs. Closed Loop

Open-loop: pre-programmed command; no real-time feedback correction; fast ballistic movements (throwing, tennis serve). Closed-loop: continuous sensory feedback to compare actual vs. desired position; slow controlled learning tasks (tracing a star). Open-loop control ⁄ open environment (separate concepts).

Clinical Application

Explains why variable practice leads to better generalization. Mental practice activates same GMPs as physical practice — strong evidence for stroke rehab. Design variable practice schedules for patients who need to function in multiple environments.

Limitations
  • Cannot explain all novel movements — programs can't be stored for every possibility
  • Underestimates role of ongoing sensory feedback
  • Same program produces different movement in different environments — theory can't explain this

4. Systems / Dynamic Systems Theory — Bernstein (1967), Shumway-Cook (2007)

Multiple interacting systemsInternal + external forcesTask-centeredFoundation of modern PT▼
Core Idea

"Movement is controlled through many interacting systems including internal and external forces acting on the body." No single system is in charge. Movement emerges from cooperation of multiple subsystems (neurological, MSK, cardiopulmonary, sensory, cognitive). Development is nonlinear. Foundation for the task-oriented approach and I-T-E framework.

Bernstein's Degrees of Freedom Problem

The body has far more DOF than needed for any task — the CNS must constrain unnecessary DOF. Stage 1: Freeze DOF (co-contraction, minimal joints — stiff but manageable). Stage 2: Release DOF (more joints, better coordination). Stage 3: Exploit DOF (use ALL DOF including gravitational/momentum forces — expert efficiency). See Stages tab for full breakdown.

Shumway-Cook's Task-Centered Approach

Movement organized around a goal (task) in a specific environment. Assessment and treatment focus on functional tasks, not isolated muscles. Interventions must vary both task AND environment. Patient motivation and cognition are part of the system. This shifted PT from neurofacilitation to task-oriented training in the 1990s-2000s.

Why It Changed PT

Isolated muscle strengthening does not automatically improve functional gait. Practice of the TASK in the ENVIRONMENT is required. Treatment must be functional, goal-directed, and ecologically valid. This directly supports CIMT, locomotor training, and ASAP frameworks.

5. Ecological Theory — Gibson (1979), Newell (1991)

Environment drives movementAffordancesPerception-action couplingSearch strategies▼
Core Idea

"Movement is controlled by the information in our environment that is detected by our motor systems." Affordances = opportunities for action perceived directly from the environment (a chair affords sitting, a handle affords pulling). Movement = coupling of perception and action — they are inseparable. The CNS learns to detect the most relevant perceptual cues to select optimal movement strategies.

Newell's Ecological Motor Learning (1991)

Motor learning = increasing coordination between perception and action, consistent with task and environmental constraints. Core concept: search strategy — exploring all perceptual cues to find the most relevant ones for performing a task efficiently. Example: learning wheelchair propulsion by exploring perceptual cues to find optimal movement strategy.

MC vs. ML Ecological Theories

Ecological MC (Gibson): how environment provides information guiding movement in the moment. Ecological ML (Newell): how the individual learns to better detect/use environmental information over time. Same distinction as MC vs ML: MC = current movement control, ML = future capability acquisition.

Clinical Application

Training in real-world environments — PT gym doesn't provide same perceptual cues as patient's home, street, workplace. Community reintegration training is grounded here. Using both KP (during) and KR (outcome) feedback to improve perception-action pairing. Discovery learning often more effective than prescriptive instruction.

Motor Learning — Principles for Clinical Practice
Every PT session is a motor learning session. How you structure practice, deliver feedback, and sequence tasks has a direct, measurable effect on whether patients retain and generalize what they learn. The research on this is strong — apply it deliberately.
Forms of Learning
Nondeclarative (Implicit)

More reflexive, automatic, habitual. Requires frequent repetition. Performed unconsciously once established. Types: Habituation (decreased response to repeated non-painful stimulus), Sensitization (increased response to threatening stimulus), Classical conditioning (pairing stimuli), Operant conditioning (trial-and-error), Procedural learning (brushing teeth, driving — performed without thinking). Most functional motor skills in rehab become procedural with sufficient practice.

Declarative (Explicit)

Knowledge that can be consciously recalled — requires awareness, attention, reflection. Facts and events. Neurological insight: Alzheimer's patients lose declarative memory but often retain procedural skills. Early-stage learning requires explicit instruction; automaticity (implicit) develops with practice.

Feedback — The Therapist's Most Powerful Tool
Intrinsic Feedback

Information the patient receives naturally from sensory systems during and after movement — proprioception, vision, vestibular, touch. Cannot be directly controlled by clinician but can be manipulated: remove visual cues to force proprioceptive reliance, or block sensation to reduce overreliance.

Extrinsic Feedback — PT's Role

KP (Knowledge of Performance) — information about movement quality/pattern DURING movement ("your trunk is rotating too much"). KR (Knowledge of Results) — information about outcome AFTER movement ("you reached 8 inches today"). Best practice: give KR every 5 trials (Schmidt & Lee, 2005) — allows self-correction and prevents feedback dependency.

Feedback Dependency Trap

Giving feedback after every rep improves session performance but degrades learning. Fading feedback (reducing frequency as skill improves) produces better long-term outcomes. Goal: a patient who self-monitors and self-corrects without the PT present. That's the difference between performance and learning.

Practice Conditions — Design Is the Intervention

Blocked vs RandomMassed vs DistributedVariable vs ConstantContextual interference▼
Blocked vs. Random Practice

Blocked: same task repeated before moving to next (10 STS, then 10 step-ups). Better session performance, WORSE retention. Random: different tasks in random order (STS, door, step-up, STS...). Harder, performs worse in session, significantly BETTER for retention and real-world generalization. This is the contextual interference effect — cognitive challenge of switching tasks enhances memory encoding. Clinical application: random practice for intermediate to late-stage rehab; some blocked practice acceptable for early-stage (patient needs to understand the task first).

Massed vs. Distributed Practice

Massed: more practice time than rest within session. Higher volume. May fatigue patient but maximizes repetitions. Distributed: equal or more rest than practice. Better tolerated for patients with limited endurance (MS, post-stroke). Generally produces better learning when patient fatigue is a factor. Clinical judgment required based on patient capacity and diagnosis.

Variable vs. Constant Practice

Constant: same task, same parameters each time. Builds a specific skill — limited generalization. Variable: same task class, different parameters (different cup sizes, surfaces, speeds). Strengthens the schema (Schmidt) — better generalization to novel situations. Variable practice is most effective for long-term learning, especially for patients who function in multiple environments (home, community, workplace).

Part vs. Whole Practice

Whole practice: practice entire task as one unit — best when parts depend on each other (gait, rolling). Part practice: break into components and practice separately before combining — best for complex tasks with one particularly difficult component. Example: practice weight shift component of STS before adding the standing phase for patient with severe knee instability.

Mental Practice

Cognitive rehearsal of a physical skill without overt movement. Activates same GMPs and cortical regions as physical practice. Evidence supports mental + physical > physical alone, especially post-stroke. Use when physical attempt is not yet possible (post-surgical restrictions, severe weakness). Have patient mentally rehearse a transfer before attempting it.

Stages of Motor Learning
Three major frameworks describe progression from novice to expert. Each stage has distinct characteristics and requires different feedback strategies and practice designs. In neurorehab, the patient's current stage tells you exactly how much instruction, demonstration, and feedback to provide.
Fitts & Posner — 3 Stage Model (1967)
Stage 1 — Cognitive

Understanding the task. HIGH attention demand. Slow, variable, error-prone. Cannot dual-task at all. PT role: active instructor. "What do I need to do?"

Stage 2 — Associative

Refining movement. Fewer errors, smoother. Moderate attention. Beginning self-correction. PT role: facilitator. "How do I do this better?"

Stage 3 — Autonomous

Automatic. LOW attention demand. Can dual-task (talk while walking). Fast, consistent, adaptable. Minimal feedback needed. PT role: challenge creator. "I do this without thinking."

Bernstein's 3 Stage Model — Degrees of Freedom
Stage 1 — Freeze DOF

Minimize joints involved. Co-contraction locks unused joints. Reasonably accurate but very inefficient. Cannot adapt to environmental changes. Note: co-contraction also seen in CP, dystonia — don't confuse pathological tone with early-learning co-contraction.

Stage 2 — Release DOF

Allow more joints to participate. Begin coordinating between joints. Movement becomes more efficient and adaptable. Example: wrist now flex/extends during hammering rather than being held rigid.

Stage 3 — Exploit DOF

All DOF used optimally. Now exploits passive/reactive forces (gravity, momentum, elastic storage). Energy-efficient and context-adaptive. Expert tennis serve uses trunk and leg recoil — no joint wasted.

Gentile's 2 Stage Model
Stage 1 — Getting the Idea

Understand the movement goal AND distinguish regulatory from non-regulatory environmental features. Which features matter (floor surface, cup shape) vs. irrelevant (background noise, color). First successful attempt — the "aha" moment. Most instruction and modeling happens here.

Stage 2 — Fixation / Diversification

Fixation (closed tasks): refine and make consistent — practice same movement until reliable. Example: household transfers. Diversification (open tasks): adapt across varying conditions — community ambulation in varied environments. Most real-world tasks require BOTH.

ASAP Language Principle

ASAP (Accelerated Skill Acquisition Program) emphasizes empowering language as an intervention: "How can you make this harder now?" Patients given timed gait feedback and encouragement walked significantly faster at discharge AND 3 months later vs. controls. For practice to affect motor learning, it must have purpose and goal-directedness — repetition without purpose is exercise, not learning.

Neuroplasticity & Recovery of Function
Neuroplasticity is the biological basis for motor learning and neurological rehabilitation. The brain is not fixed after injury — it reorganizes, forms new connections, and recruits adjacent cortex in response to active, goal-directed practice. Understanding the mechanisms and the role of aerobic exercise gives you the rationale for every evidence-based neurorehab intervention.
Neuroplasticity — Mechanisms
Definition

"Facilitated by actively attending and adapting to functional tasks (goal-oriented)" as opposed to training muscle activation patterns in isolation. Plasticity = the ability to show modification. Short-term changes = enhanced synaptic efficacies. On a continuum, these lead to relatively permanent structural changes.

Short-Term Plasticity

Enhanced synaptic efficacy — more neurotransmitter release, increased receptor sensitivity, increased firing rates. Occurs rapidly with practice. Underlies performance improvements within a single session. Without continued practice, these changes are not sustained.

Long-Term Structural Plasticity

Axon sprouting, synaptogenesis, dendritic branching, cortical map reorganization. Requires sustained active goal-directed practice over time. Use-dependent — cortex representing most-practiced movements expands. Disuse causes cortical shrinkage. Learned nonuse: affected limb avoided because success rate is low → cortical representation shrinks → limb less usable → more avoidance. CIMT is designed to break this cycle.

Recovery vs. Compensation
Recovery

Achieving function through original processes or previously-used mechanisms. True neural recovery — restoring the original movement strategy. Walking with normal gait pattern; using affected limb for reaching. This is the goal — but not always achievable depending on lesion size/site and time since injury.

Compensation

Substituting for impairments using an assistive device or atypical motor patterns. Allows function but has costs: energy inefficiency, long-term MSK consequences, risk of learned nonuse. PTs must weigh when to encourage compensation (safety, function) vs. push toward true recovery.

Factors Affecting Recovery
  • Age — younger brain has greater neuroplasticity
  • Genetics — BDNF Val66Met polymorphism affects BDNF-dependent learning
  • Lesion size and site — larger/deeper lesions = worse prognosis
  • Pre-injury experience and activity level — higher brain reserve is protective
  • Quality and intensity of post-injury rehabilitation — more right-task practice = better outcomes

BDNF, Aerobic Exercise & Neuroplasticity

BDNF = growth factor for learningAerobic primes motor learning>30 min | ~70% HRmax | 4x/week▼
What Is BDNF?

Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor. Promotes neuronal survival, growth, differentiation, and synaptic plasticity. Necessary for long-term potentiation (the cellular mechanism underlying learning). Bidirectionally transported across the blood-brain barrier. BDNF peaks 10–60 minutes following aerobic exercise in humans.

Aerobic Exercise BEFORE Practice

High-intensity cycling (3 x 3 min, above ventilatory threshold) immediately BEFORE motor task practice enhanced retention at 1 AND 7 days vs. no prime. Mechanism: elevated BDNF → facilitates detection and encoding of task-relevant info during subsequent practice → enhanced motor memory consolidation.

Aerobic Exercise AFTER Practice

Also enhances retention. Mechanism: facilitates motor memory consolidation processes (via BDNF-mediated synaptic strengthening). Both timing windows are clinically viable — "prime then practice" or "practice then consolidate."

Optimal Prescription (Mang) for Neuroplasticity
  • Duration: >30 minutes per session
  • Intensity: ~70% of heart rate maximum
  • Frequency: 4 days per week
  • Mode: combination aerobic + resistance
  • Cycling and high-intensity intervals well-supported
Stroke Aerobic Guidelines (Billinger)

Minimum: 20 minutes moderate intensity aerobic exercise, 3 days per week. Intensity: >30% heart rate reserve. Benefits stop when exercise stops — must be ongoing. Early mobility (acute) prevents deconditioning and learned nonuse and improves discharge disposition.

SNF Clinical Application

Start neurorehab session with 10–20 min aerobic activity (seated bike, arm ergometer, supported treadmill) before task-specific practice. This is especially relevant in SNF and outpatient. Even low-intensity movement before motor tasks may produce priming effects. The "exercise-then-practice" sequence has a growing evidence base.

Neurofacilitation Approaches
Neurofacilitation emerged from reflex and hierarchical theories — using sensory input to facilitate desired movement and inhibit abnormal patterns. Though task-oriented approaches now dominate the evidence base, these techniques remain clinically valuable especially in early-stage neurological rehab. Key principle: Facilitation = increase patient's ability to move. Inhibition = decrease use of abnormal movement patterns.

Brunnstrom — Stages of Stroke Recovery & Abnormal Synergies

Stroke-specificAbnormal synergies7 StagesOverflow / Raimiste's▼
Context

Anna Signe Sofia Brunnstrom — Swedish physiotherapist. Programme designed specifically for stroke recovery and resultant abnormal synergies. Most applicable to stroke; possibly applied to TBI if abnormal synergies are present.

Synergy Defined

Normal synergy: muscles contract in coupled sequence for efficient movement. Abnormal synergy: obligatory, stereotyped patterns that prevent selective isolated joint control. After stroke, attempting to move one joint fires the whole synergy — patient cannot isolate elbow flexion without shoulder ABD, forearm sup, and wrist flexion all activating simultaneously.

UE Flexion Synergy

Scap retraction & elevation, shoulder ABD & ER, elbow flexion (dominant), forearm supination, wrist flexion, finger/thumb flexion & adduction. Elbow flexion dominates — locks arm against body in flexed, IR position.

UE Extension Synergy

Scap protraction & depression, shoulder ADD & IR (dominant), elbow extension, forearm pronation (dominant), wrist flex or ext, finger/thumb flexion & adduction.

LE Flexion Synergy

Pelvis elevation & retraction, hip flexion (dominant), knee flexion, ankle DF & inversion, toe dorsiflexion. Hip flexion dominates — shortens leg for swing clearance.

LE Extension Synergy

Hip extension & ADD (dominant), knee extension (dominant), ankle PF & inversion, toe PF. This is the stance phase pattern — helps weight-bearing but causes circumduction gait and toe drag during swing.

Overflow / Raimiste's Phenomenon

Resistance to the uninvolved side facilitates movement on the involved side. Mechanism: irradiation of neural activity across the midline. Example: resist hip ABD on uninvolved LE → involved LE also abducts. Used in Stages 1–2 to elicit initial AROM on the affected side.

Rood — Sensory Facilitation and Inhibition

PT & OT dual credential5 key techniquesPurposeful movement3–5 reps to facilitate▼
Overview

Margaret S. Rood — both PT and OT. Used sensory techniques to facilitate or inhibit movement with intent of purposeful movement. Typically: 3–5 reps to facilitate voluntary contractions; 10–20 minutes for neutral warmth. Always note effect on agonist vs. antagonist — same technique applied differently produces different results.

Brushing (Fast)

FACILITATES. Fast brush over muscle belly to excite the targeted muscle group. Also used to promote static holding of extensors for postural control.

Icing

Quick ice: FACILITATES — brief cold application excites muscle via thermal stimulus. Prolonged icing: INHIBITS — vasoconstriction, decreased nerve conduction velocity, reduced movement. Clinical rule: short cold exposure facilitates; extended cold inhibits.

Tapping (over muscle belly or tendon)

FACILITATES. Light repetitive percussion activates the stretch reflex to elicit contraction. Applied directly to the target muscle. Useful for activating weak or hypoactive muscles.

Deep Pressure

INHIBITS. Sustained deep pressure stimulates the GTO → autogenic inhibition → muscle relaxes. Holding the tendon firmly can relieve spasms. Also used for calming effects in sensory processing dysfunction. Pressure over the thenar eminence is key to releasing the fisted hand.

Neutral Warmth

INHIBITS. Wrapping body part (or whole body) in blanket at body temperature — 10–20 minutes. Produces calming/inhibitory effects via sustained warmth. Used to decrease overall muscle tone and spasticity before movement activities. Different from therapeutic heat — neutral warmth is about sustained body-temperature contact, not external heat.

Ayres — Sensory Integration (SI)

Jean Ayres, 1960s. SI theory: brain must register, modulate, and discriminate info from all sensory systems to produce purposeful movement. 8 senses: tactile, auditory, visual, taste, smell, proprioception, vestibular, interoception. Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) = sensory input enters brain but not organized into appropriate responses. Gravitational insecurity = purely vestibular problem. Tactile defensiveness = heightened touch sensitivity. Primarily pediatric and ABI populations.

PNF — Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation (Knott, Voss, Kabat)

Diagonal patternsD1/D2 UE complete detailFacilitation & inhibitionIrradiation / overflow▼
D1 UE — Flexion

Shoulder: Flexion – Adduction – External Rotation; forearm supinated; fingers flexed. Resembles combing hair or pulling a seatbelt across body. Hand placement: right hand — palmar surface cupped over dorsal-ulnar aspect of patient's right hand/wrist. Left hand — posterior-lateral arm surface to control IR and proximal components.

D1 UE — Extension

Shoulder: Extension – Abduction – Internal Rotation; forearm pronated; fingers extended. Resembles pushing away or throwing to the side.

D2 UE — Flexion

Shoulder: Flexion – Abduction – External Rotation; forearm supinated; fingers extended; elbow extension pattern. Resembles drawing a sword or raising arm overhead. Hand placement: left hand — palmar surface cupped over dorsal-radial aspect of patient's right hand/wrist. Right hand — anterior-lateral arm surface to control rotation and proximal components.

D2 UE — Extension

Shoulder: Extension – Adduction – Internal Rotation; forearm pronated; fingers flexed. Resembles putting sword back or reaching down and across.

LE Patterns (overview)

D1 LE Flexion: Hip flex-ADD-ER; knee flex; ankle DF + inversion; toe extension. D1 LE Extension: Hip ext-ABD-IR; knee ext; ankle PF + eversion; toe flex. D2 LE Flexion: Hip flex-ABD-IR; knee flex; ankle DF + eversion; toe extension. D2 LE Extension: Hip ext-ADD-ER; knee ext; ankle PF + inversion; toe flex.

Facilitation Uses

Apply resistance in direction of desired movement — strengthen weak muscles, improve coordination. Irradiation (Raimiste's): strong resistance on stronger muscle groups overflows to weaker ones. Used for LE strengthening in gait retraining, UE strengthening post-stroke. Rhythmic initiation (passive → active → resisted) for hypoactive patients.

Inhibition Uses

Contract-relax / Hold-relax: apply resistance to spastic muscle group → patient contracts maximally → relax → GTO autogenic inhibition → passive stretch into new range. Used before functional activities in high-tone patients (stroke, CP). Slow rhythmic movement through diagonal pattern (antagonist direction to spastic synergy) also inhibitory.

NDT / Bobath — Neurodevelopmental Technique

Karel & Berta Bobath (1940s)Handling & elongationAll mobility is multiplanarThenar handshake▼
Core Concepts

Feedback AND feed-forward control. Posture is initiated from the base of support. ALL MOBILITY IS MULTIPLANAR. Weight shift = COG moving off the BOS = elongation on the weight-bearing side. Controlled asymmetry is always multiplanar. Fixation patterns develop to compensate for inadequate motor control.

Elongation Principle

Weight shift → elongation on the WB side (lateral trunk flexion of loaded side). This frontal plane control unlocks transverse plane mobility = greater freedom of movement. Elongation is the key to unlocking tone and facilitating isolated movement in neurological patients.

Handling Rules

Always a facilitating hand (provides sensory input, alters tone) and a guiding hand (directs movement). Critical: NEVER pull or push. Hands GUIDE — not force. Use deep tactile and proprioceptive input. Move WITH the patient — feel the weight shift in your own body. Patient should not actively help you move them (passive → active progression).

Tone Reduction Sequence

Rotation at the proximal joint with ER component > IR. As tone reduces, DOF for the limb increases. Work proximal to distal: proximal shoulder → proximal elbow → wrist → thenar eminence.

The Thenar Handshake

Apply pressure with your thumb into patient's thenar eminence while rotating at the wrist (supination/pronation). As thenar eminence relaxes, fingers automatically release from flexion. Take up the new open space with your hand as you apply deep pressure with more of your hand. The goal is never to pry fingers apart — this stimulates protective grasp reflex. Final position: patient's hand open with your thenar eminence contacting theirs.

After Opening the Hand

Place open hand on surface and weight bear using elongation strategies. Clean and dry skin. Consider splinting or cone use. Work with OT when applicable. Fisted position + moisture = maceration + infection risk — critical in your SNF patients. Document skin condition at each visit.

Task-Oriented & Modern Approaches to Neurorehabilitation
Task-oriented approaches emerged as the evidence base shifted away from neurofacilitation toward functional, goal-directed, neuroplasticity-driven interventions. Core insight: practicing the task in the environment where it will be performed produces the best outcomes. The PT's role shifts from facilitator-of-movement to coach of functional performance. Currently, there is greater emphasis on explicitly training function and less on inhibiting reflexes and retraining normal patterns of movement.

Carr & Shepherd — Motor Relearning Programme (MRP)

Movement Science-Based (MSB)Anticipatory postural controlKinematics of normal movementMotor learning applied▼
Framework

Motor Relearning Programme (MRP): "PT training of motor control based on understanding of the kinematics and kinetics of normal movement, motor control processes, and motor learning." Sensory input modulates movement. Anticipatory feedback and postural control are central. Motor learning principles explicitly applied — practice design, feedback scheduling, variable practice all built in.

Movement Science-Based (MSB)

Grounded in biomechanics and motor control science rather than traditional neurofacilitation theory. Normal movement kinematics (joint angles, muscle timing, forces) are the reference standard. Treatment involves analysis of deviations from normal movement and targeted practice to restore them. Emphasizes patient understanding of the goal, not just following instruction.

Tasks Addressed

Functional mobility skills: bed mobility, sitting (scooting, edge sitting), transfers, standing/stepping, walking, stair climbing. For each task, PT analyzes which components are missing or deviated and designs specific practice targeting those components.

ASAP — Accelerated Skill Acquisition Program (Winstein)

Hybrid combination of task-specific + skill-based + impairment-mitigating training with embedded patient-centered motivational enhancements. Three domains: Skill (motor learning + self-management), Capacity (impairment mitigation), Motivation (intrinsic drive). Empowering language is a core component — "How can you make this harder now?" Patients with timed gait feedback and encouragement walked significantly faster at discharge and 3 months later. Language specificity is an intervention.

CIMT — Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy

Force use of affected limb90% waking hours constraintShaping — multi-trial practiceReverses learned nonuse▼
Core Mechanism

Forced use of the hemiparetic UE (mitt/sling on unaffected UE for 90% of waking hours) combined with intensive massed practice of functional activities (shaping — graded task difficulty, multi-trial practice). Directly targets learned nonuse: after stroke, affected limb avoided due to low success rate → cortical map shrinks → limb less capable → more avoidance. CIMT reverses this by forcing cortical map expansion through use.

Candidate Criteria

Strong evidence for mild-moderate UE hemiparesis. Minimum criteria: 10° active wrist extension + 10° active finger extension, adequate cognition, no severe balance impairment (removing unaffected UE = fall risk). Not appropriate for Stage 1–2 Brunnstrom (flaccid/minimal movement). Intensive protocol: 6 hrs/day for 10 consecutive weekdays. Modified CIMT (mCIMT) for less intensive schedule.

Contraindications and Limitations
  • Severe balance impairment — removing unaffected UE support creates fall risk
  • Significant cognitive impairment — cannot self-monitor or follow safety precautions
  • Severe UE spasticity (paradoxically) — affected limb too rigid to benefit from forced use
  • Acute phase — most appropriate for subacute/chronic stroke
  • Shaping requires skilled therapist supervision — not a home program alone

Locomotor Training — Body Weight Support + CPG

Central Pattern GeneratorBWS treadmill trainingSpeed drives CPG inputOverground generalization▼
The CPG

Central Pattern Generator (CPG): spinal cord interneuron networks that generate rhythmic, coordinated stepping movements without requiring voluntary cortical input. In lower mammals (cats), the spinal CPG produces stepping even after complete spinal cord transection when hindlimbs are supported over a treadmill. In humans, the CPG is less dominant but still contributes to gait — locomotor training aims to activate and strengthen it through repetitive, correctly-timed sensory input.

BWS Protocol

Patient supported in harness over treadmill (reducing gravitational load). Therapists manually facilitate limb movement timing and patterning. Speed is key — treadmill speed drives rhythmic sensory input to CPG. Start with higher BWS (30–40%) and faster speeds as tolerated; progressively reduce BWS and increase challenge. Used for: stroke, incomplete SCI, TBI, MS, CP.

Previously Part-Whole, Now Task-Specific

Earlier approach trained gait components separately (part practice). Current evidence favors whole-task practice with CPG activation via BWS + treadmill — provides the complete sensory experience of walking rather than isolated component training. Functional, goal-directed, ecologically valid.

Overground vs. Treadmill

Both have supporting evidence. Treadmill/BWS: high step volume, controlled speed, CPG activation, BWS option. Overground: ecological validity, community integration. Combination likely optimal — use treadmill/BWS for high-volume CPG training, transition to overground in varied environments for generalization. Goal is always community-level ambulation.

Postural Control, Motor Development & Aging
Postural control is the foundation for every functional movement. In pediatrics, it develops through a predictable sequence driven by primitive reflexes, righting reactions, and equilibrium reactions. In adults, postural control declines with aging through multisystem degradation. In neurological patients, it is disrupted in ways that predict fall risk, functional limitation, and quality of life.

Reflexes, Righting Reactions & Equilibrium Reactions

Primitive = innate, always stimulus-responseRighting = gravity-driven, visionEquilibrium = sensory integration▼
Primitive Reflexes

Without volitional control; ALWAYS in response to stimuli; initially protective; integrate with neural maturation. Primitive reflexes NEVER truly disappear — inhibited by cortical maturation and re-emerge after cortical damage (stroke, TBI). In adults with neurological injury, returning primitive reflexes indicate loss of cortical inhibitory control.

Righting Reactions

Driven by vision and gravity. Develop as a result of positions AGAINST GRAVITY. Orient the head and body to the vertical (upright) position. Allow rolling and transitions from lying to sitting — patient "finds upright" after displacement. Emerge after primitive reflex integration; bridge to equilibrium reactions. Also driven by labyrinthine and visual input — require intact vestibular and visual systems.

Equilibrium Reactions

Protective; driven by sensory integration to environment; response to mobility. Required for functional balance. Include three postural strategies: Ankle strategy (small perturbation on firm surface — distal to proximal; sway at ankle to return COG), Hip strategy (larger perturbation or compliant surface — trunk flexes/extends quickly; proximal to distal), Stepping strategy (COG displaced outside BOS — must step to prevent fall). Normal individuals use all three selectively. Neurological patients often stuck in hip/stepping strategy even for small perturbations.

Motor Development — Lifespan Sequence

Rolling → walkingNo creeping = LD riskPositions of play matterPeds vs adult PT▼
Developmental Sequence

Rolling → Sit to quadruped → Tall kneeling → Half kneel to stand → Cruising → Walking. Each stage builds specific postural control, strength, and motor planning. Creeping (hands-and-knees): all planes of motion simultaneously — the only full-body, multiplanar, reciprocal coordination pattern in development. No creeping is correlated with learning disorders. Cruising: frontal plane ABD/ADD — the only time frontal plane lower extremity work occurs in development.

Positions of Play

Infants develop motor skills based on positions they're placed in and encouraged to explore. Prone: head control, neck extension, shoulder girdle strength. Supine: reaching, ATNR integration, bilateral coordination. Seated: trunk control, visual-motor coordination. Each position provides a different postural challenge. Tummy time is medically recommended precisely because prone play drives critical motor development.

Key Developmental Age Milestones

0–2 months: newborn. 0–1 year: infant — primitive reflex integration. 1 year: hand preference (NOT dominance), destruction over construction, knows body parts. 1–3 years: toddler — increased strength/coordination, gait components refined, curiosity. 4–7 years: bilateral coordination, gait toward adult pattern, feedback > feed-forward learning, balance for game play. 10–18: adolescent. Development depends on practice, exploration, experience.

Peds vs. Adult PT

Peds: working to GAIN or acquire a skill (development). Adult: working to RETURN a skill or function (recovery/compensation). Peds evaluation adds: developmental milestone assessment, primitive reflex testing, play-based observation, normative vs. criterion-referenced comparison. Both share: I-T-E framework, functional task focus, family/caregiver education, motivational strategies.

Pediatric Outcome Measures

PDMS-2: 0–6 yrs, norm-referenced, 6 subtests (reflexes, stationary, locomotion, object manipulation, grasping, visual-motor integration). Bot-2: 4–21 yrs, norm-referenced, identifies mild-moderate motor deficits, requires cognition. HELP: birth–36 months, gross/fine motor checklist, criterion-referenced. GMFM: cerebral palsy specific, 5 areas, no standardized criterion-reference. GMFCS Levels 1–5 classify CP functional mobility level.

Aging & Postural Control

Multisystem declineSensory reweighting impairedmCTSIB — 30 sec gold standardExercise is the intervention▼
Age-Related Changes Affecting Postural Control
  • Visual: ↓ acuity, ↓ contrast sensitivity, ↓ dark adaptation, ↓ depth perception
  • Vestibular: ↓ hair cell numbers, ↓ VOR gain, ↓ ability to resolve sensory conflict
  • Somatosensory: ↓ peripheral nerve conduction; vibration is the first sensation to go in peripheral neuropathy/diabetes; ↓ plantar sensation
  • Musculoskeletal: sarcopenia; ↓ ankle DF strength and ROM; ↓ Type II fiber recruitment speed
  • Cognitive: ↓ dual-task ability; ↓ attention allocation to balance; slower reaction times
  • CNS processing: slower integration; less ability to reweight sensory systems when one is unreliable
mCTSIB — Modified Clinical Test for Sensory Interaction & Balance

4 conditions testing VSR (vestibulospinal reflex). Each condition: 30 seconds. Condition 1: Firm, Eyes Open — all sensory systems available. Condition 2: Firm, Eyes Closed — removes vision. Condition 3: Foam, Eyes Open — reduces somatosensory. Condition 4: Foam, Eyes Closed — removes vision AND reduces somatosensory — tests VESTIBULAR in isolation. Gold standard: 30 seconds per condition. Failure pattern reveals which system is impaired or over-relied upon. Document: sway pattern, strategy used (ankle vs hip vs stepping), need for assist.

Sensory Reweighting

Normal balance requires selecting the most reliable sensory input for the current environment. Example: on foam (somatosensory reduced) → rely more on visual and vestibular. In fog (visual reduced) → rely more on somatosensory and vestibular. Elderly and neurological patients lose this flexibility — over-rely on one system or cannot switch when one is removed. mCTSIB systematically tests each combination to identify the impaired system.

Stroke — Motor Control Perspective
795,000 strokes per year in the US. 4th leading cause of death. Leading cause of long-term disability. 87% ischemic. In your SNF you will treat more stroke patients than almost any other neurological diagnosis. Motor control science provides the framework for understanding what the patient lost, why they move the way they do, and what will drive recovery.
Demographics & Emergency Management
Key Statistics
  • 795,000 per year; 4th leading cause of death in US
  • Leading cause of long-term disability; kills 1 per 4 minutes
  • 610,000 first strokes; 185,000 recurrent; 1/4 had previous stroke
  • 87% ischemic; 13% hemorrhagic
Ischemic Stroke — tPA

Tissue Plasminogen Activator (tPA) = "clot buster." Window: 3–4.5 hours from symptom onset. Restores blood flow. Contraindicated in hemorrhagic stroke — worsens bleeding. Post-acute meds: heparin, Coumadin, Eliquis, Plavix (anticoagulants). Aspirin to decrease cerebral edema.

Hemorrhagic Stroke

Blood is toxic to brain tissue — kills cells directly. Goal: reduce ICP ASAP. Surgical evacuation of blood, reduce compression, intracranial blood clot removal. NO tPA. Medical management critical before PT begins safely.

Phases of Rehabilitation
Acute Phase

Early mobility is critical. Prevent bed rest deconditioning. Prevent learned nonuse. Functional mobility training, ADLs. Discharge disposition determination. Even rolling in bed and sitting at edge begins the neuroplasticity process.

Sub-Acute (SNF / Inpatient Rehab)

Inpatient rehab: 3 hrs rehab, 2+ disciplines, 6 days/week. SNF: less intensive but sustained. Decrease time from onset to admission = increase functional outcomes. This is where you see most stroke patients. Brunnstrom staging guides intervention selection.

Chronic (>6 months post CVA)

Transition to outpatient neuro rehab should occur BEFORE 6 months. Goals: higher function, return to home/work/community. Ongoing neuroplasticity possible years post-stroke. CIMT evidence exists for chronic stroke. Aerobic exercise and task-specific practice continue to produce gains even years later.

Stroke by Vascular Territory & Hemisphere

ACA / MCA / PCALeft = aphasia, apraxiaRight = neglect, pusherLobes and syndromes▼
ACA (Anterior Cerebral Artery)

Medial region: lower body areas of both motor and sensory cortex (LE more affected than UE), cingulate cortex, prefrontal/orbitofrontal cortex. Internal capsule and basal ganglia perforators. Clinical: contralateral LE > UE weakness; transcortical motor aphasia; echolalia possible; executive dysfunction.

MCA (Middle Cerebral Artery)

Lateral region: arm/face areas of motor and sensory cortex (UE + face more affected), dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, association areas, part of optic tract (Meyer's loop). Most common stroke territory. L MCA: right hemiplegia, Broca's/Wernicke's/global aphasia. R MCA: left hemiplegia, left hemineglect, pusher syndrome.

PCA (Posterior Cerebral Artery)

Occipital lobe (primary vision), inferior temporal lobe (ventral visual pathway — form and color), perforating branches to diencephalon, internal capsule, midbrain. Clinical: homonymous hemianopsia; visual agnosia; cortical blindness (bilateral); memory deficits (temporal lobe).

Left Hemisphere

Right-sided hemiplegia/hemiparesis. Aphasia (Broca's = nonfluent, expressive; Wernicke's = fluent but nonsensical; global = neither). Apraxia = cannot plan/perform motor task despite intact motor system. Right visual field cut. Right sensory loss.

Right Hemisphere

Left-sided hemiplegia. Left hemineglect — spatial attention deficit, not visual deficit. Visual field cut left. Motor impersistence. Anosognosia (denies deficit). Pusher syndrome. Left gaze preference (eyes deviate right, toward lesion). Right hemisphere processes spatial attention for BOTH hemifields — that's why left neglect is so profound and right hemisphere lesion rarely causes right neglect.

Critical Distinction: Hemineglect vs. Hemianopsia

Hemianopsia = visual field cut — the patient CANNOT SEE stimuli in the affected hemifield (visual pathway damage). Hemineglect = attention deficit — the patient CAN see the stimuli but has lost the will or motivation to attend to them. They ignore or disown the left limbs and left space. Not non-compliance. Clinically: a patient who doesn't dress the left side of their body, doesn't eat food on the left side of the plate, or turns only to the right during conversation — this is neglect.

Clinical Syndromes & Special Presentations

Locked-in syndromeWallenberg syndromePusher syndromeAphasia types▼
Locked-In Syndrome

Basilar artery thrombosis — bilateral infarction of ventral pons. CATASTROPHIC. Sudden onset → acute progressive hemiparesis → tetraplegia → bulbar paralysis (CN 5–12) → mutism. CANNOT MOVE OR SPEAK BUT IS ALERT, ORIENTED, AND FEELS EVERYTHING. Only vertical eye movements and blinking are preserved. Communication via eye blinks. Requires specialized team care.

Lateral Medullary / Wallenberg Syndrome

Ipsilateral lesion. Clinical features: (-) pain and temperature in IPSILATERAL face; ataxia in limbs and gait; vertigo, N&V; nystagmus; Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhydrosis); contralateral pain/temp loss over body; hoarse voice; dysphagia. Dysphagia → aspiration → pneumonia risk is a critical safety concern.

Pusher Syndrome

Patient actively pushes toward the paretic/hemiplegic side. Driven by distorted perception of upright — they perceive their tilted body as vertical. Combats therapist attempts to correct alignment. Cannot use visual feedback to self-correct because visual body schema is also disrupted. Treatment: heavy reliance on verbal feedback, mirror, environmental vertical cues. Prognosis: typically resolves, but significantly slows rehab progress.

Tone Terminology for Stroke

Flaccidity: nothing there, just hanging. Complete loss of muscle tone. Stage 1 Brunnstrom. Hypotonia: weak, reduced tone — more than flaccidity. Normal tone. Spasticity: velocity-dependent increased resistance. UMN. Rigidity: non-velocity-dependent resistance. Extrapyramidal (basal ganglia). Clonus: rhythmic oscillatory contraction — sustained stretch triggers repeated reflex contractions. Severe UMN lesions.

Hidden Stroke Risks for Women
  • Oral contraceptives: even low-estrogen pills — 2x risk; higher with other risk factors
  • Migraine with aura: up to 10x risk; combined with OCPs → both hemorrhagic AND ischemic risk
  • Hormone replacement therapy: slight increased risk
  • History of preeclampsia/eclampsia: increased HTN and stroke risk 1–30 years post-delivery
  • Autoimmune illness: diabetes, lupus
  • Clotting issues: LE DVT, multiple miscarriages, Livedo Reticularis (purplish net-like mottled skin pattern from vascular spasms)

Key Rules

Count 1,1000–2,1000 for standardized velocity. ONE AND DONE — test becomes inaccurate when repeated multiple times in one session (repeated stretching reduces tone → false low scores). PT hand position: one hand under mid-thigh; other in U-shape over ankle. LE: quads into flexion, hamstrings into extension. Document bilaterally.

Why Extension Synergy Produces Circumduction

LE extension synergy (hip ADD + knee ext + ankle PF + inversion) locks leg in extension during stance — helpful for weight-bearing. During swing phase, patient cannot selectively flex hip/knee/ankle (flexion synergy would fire the whole pattern). Result: patient hikes the hip (pelvis elevation) and swings the stiff leg outward (circumduction) to clear the ground. Ankle PF also prevents dorsiflexion → foot drop and toe drag.

Vaulting

Patient rises on toes of the UNAFFECTED leg during swing of affected leg — compensating for inability to shorten the affected leg during swing. Energy-costly, asymmetric step length. Goal: reduce vaulting by improving swing-phase limb shortening (hip/knee flexion, ankle DF) on affected side.

Key Intervention Exercises
  • "Pump the water" — rhythmic DF/PF to break ankle synergy and facilitate ankle movement
  • Facilitating knee flexion/extension — selective knee control isolated from LE extension synergy
  • Tall kneeling to sit — eccentric knee flexion control; critical for stepping
  • Focus on eccentric quad control — the missing component in circumduction gait

What is the best definition of motor control? What are the three factors that influence movement organization?
tap
Motor Control: "The ability to regulate the mechanisms required to generate movement to meet the demands of a given task in a specific environment." (Shumway-Cook & Woollacott). Three factors: Individual (Who — action, perception, cognition), Task (What — stability, mobility, manipulation; open vs. closed), Environment (Where — regulatory vs. non-regulatory; closed predictable vs. open unpredictable). APTA Vision: "Transforming society by optimizing movement to improve the human experience." You are a movement scientist.
Compare the five theories of motor control: core idea, key limitation, and clinical contribution.
tap
Reflex (Sherrington): reflexes = building blocks; can't explain spontaneous movement; contributed sensory facilitation techniques. Hierarchical (Jackson): higher controls lower; spinal CPGs work independently; contributed UMN/LMN framework, developmental sequence. Motor Programming (Schmidt): GMPs + open-loop; ignores ongoing feedback; contributed mental practice, schema, variable practice. Systems/Dynamic (Bernstein/Shumway-Cook): emergent from multiple subsystems; complex to apply; contributed task-oriented approach, I-T-E. Ecological (Gibson/Newell): affordances and perception-action coupling; underestimates CNS; contributed real-world training, discovery learning.
What is the difference between performance and learning? Why does giving KR after every trial hurt long-term learning?
tap
Performance = temporary changes in motor behavior (visible during session). Learning = relatively permanent changes in capability to generate skilled actions — observable in retention and transfer tests, not acquisition. Giving KR after every trial: patient uses feedback as a crutch → high session performance but poor retention. Best practice: KR every 5 trials (Schmidt & Lee, 2005). Fading frequency as skill improves produces better long-term outcomes. Goal: self-monitoring, self-correcting patient who doesn't need the PT present.
Explain Bernstein's 3-stage DOF model and the clinical relevance of co-contraction.
tap
Stage 1 — Freeze DOF: minimal joints, co-contraction stabilizes unused joints. Stiff, reasonably accurate, inefficient. Stage 2 — Release DOF: allow more joints, coordinate between them, improves efficiency. Stage 3 — Exploit DOF: all DOF used optimally including passive forces (gravity, momentum) — expert movement. Co-contraction clinical note: normal in Stage 1 of learning — don't pathologize it in a patient learning a new skill. Also a characteristic of neurological pathology (CP, dystonia) where it does NOT diminish with practice as skill is acquired.
What are the Brunnstrom 7 stages and the abnormal UE and LE synergy patterns?
tap
Stages: (1) Flaccidity — PROM, positioning; (2) Spasticity begins, minimal vol mvt — initiate AROM, overflow; (3) Spasticity peaks, synergies controlled — neuroplasticity focus; (4) Spasticity decreases, combine synergies — tabletop, gait; (5) Complex movement returns — fine motor, advanced gait; (6) Spasticity gone, isolated mvts; (7) Normal. UE Flexion synergy: scap retract/elev, shoulder ABD+ER, elbow flex (dominant), forearm sup, wrist/finger flex. LE Extension synergy: hip ext+ADD (dominant), knee ext (dominant), ankle PF+inv, toe PF — this is what causes circumduction and toe drag in gait.
What is BDNF, how does aerobic exercise affect it, and what is the optimal prescription for neuroplasticity?
tap
BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) = growth factor for neuronal survival, differentiation, synaptic plasticity, and LTP (the cellular mechanism of learning). Bidirectionally crosses BBB. Peaks 10–60 min post aerobic exercise. Before practice: facilitates encoding of task-relevant info. After practice: facilitates motor memory consolidation. Both timing windows work. Optimal Rx (Mang): >30 min/session, ~70% HRmax, 4 days/week, aerobic + resistance. Stroke minimum (Billinger): 20 min moderate intensity, 3x/week, >30% HRR. Benefits stop when exercise stops. SNF application: seated bike or arm ergometer BEFORE task-specific practice.
Describe Rood's 5 sensory techniques — does each facilitate or inhibit, and why?
tap
Fast brushing: FACILITATES — excites muscle group; also used for static extensor holding. Quick icing: FACILITATES — thermal stimulus excites muscle. Prolonged icing: INHIBITS — vasoconstriction, decreased nerve conduction, reduced movement. Tapping (belly or tendon): FACILITATES — activates stretch reflex. Deep pressure: INHIBITS — GTO activation → autogenic inhibition → muscle relaxes; releases spasms; thenar handshake. Neutral warmth: INHIBITS — 10–20 min body-temp wrap calms and decreases overall tone before functional activities. Protocol: 3–5 reps to facilitate voluntary contractions.
What are the PNF D1 and D2 UE flexion patterns with hand placements? How is PNF used for spasticity inhibition?
tap
D1 UE Flexion: Shoulder Flex-Add-ER; forearm sup; fingers flex. (Seatbelt). RH: palmar over dorsal-ulnar wrist/fingers. LH: posterior-lateral arm to control IR. D2 UE Flexion: Shoulder Flex-Abd-ER; forearm sup; fingers extended; elbow extension pattern. (Draw sword). LH: palmar over dorsal-radial wrist/fingers. RH: anterior-lateral arm to control rotation. Spasticity inhibition via PNF: Contract-relax — resist the SPASTIC muscle group → patient contracts maximally → relax → GTO fires → autogenic inhibition → tone reduces → passive stretch into new range. Slow rhythmic movement through the diagonal in the ANTAGONIST direction to the spastic synergy is also inhibitory.
What is NDT elongation and what are the thenar handshake steps for releasing a fisted hand?
tap
Elongation: weight shift = COG off BOS = elongation on WB side (lateral trunk flexion of loaded side). This frontal plane control unlocks transverse plane mobility. ALL MOBILITY IS MULTIPLANAR. Always: facilitating hand + guiding hand. NEVER pull or push. Move WITH patient — feel WS in your own body. Thenar handshake: (1) Apply pressure with your thumb into patient's thenar eminence. (2) Rotate at wrist (sup/pronation). (3) As thenar eminence relaxes, fingers automatically release from flexion. (4) Take up the new open space with more of your hand. (5) Never pry fingers — stimulates protective grasp reflex. After: WB open hand on surface, clean/dry skin, consider splinting or OT referral.
What are the 4 mCTSIB conditions, what does each test, and what is the gold standard?
tap
mCTSIB tests VSR (vestibulospinal reflex). 4 conditions, 30 seconds each. Condition 1: Firm surface, Eyes Open — baseline, all systems available. Condition 2: Firm, Eyes Closed — removes vision; tests somatosensory + vestibular. Condition 3: Foam, Eyes Open — reduces somatosensory; tests vision + vestibular. Condition 4: Foam, Eyes Closed — removes vision AND reduces somatosensory; tests VESTIBULAR in isolation. Gold standard: 30 seconds per condition without losing balance. Failure pattern reveals impaired system. Document: sway pattern, strategy used (ankle vs hip vs stepping), need for assist.
Modified Ashworth Scale — all 6 grades and key administration rules.
tap
Grades: 0 = no tone; 1 = slight catch and release; 1+ = catch + minimal resistance through <1/2 ROM; 2 = marked through most ROM (still movable); 3 = considerable (passive movement difficult); 4 = rigid. Rules: Count 1,1000–2,1000 for standardized velocity. ONE AND DONE — repeated testing gives false-low scores (repeated stretching reduces tone temporarily). PT hand position: one hand under mid-thigh, other in U-shape over ankle. LE: quads into flexion, hamstrings into extension. Document bilaterally — compare affected vs. unaffected. Berg <40 in your SNF patient = 100% fall risk.
What TUG cutoff indicates fall risk for: normal adult, older stroke, frail elderly, Parkinson's, Hip OA, vestibular?
tap
Normal adult: >13.5 sec (CDC: >12). Older stroke: >14 sec. Older adults in fall clinics: >15 sec. Frail elderly: >32.6 sec. Parkinson's: >11.5 sec. Hip OA: >7.95 sec. Vestibular disorders: >10–11.1 sec. LE amputees: >19 sec. Clinical note: TUG is a composite task — a slow score tells you there is a problem but not WHY. Combine with observational gait analysis and balance testing to identify the specific impairments driving the result.
What is CIMT? Who is a good candidate, and what is the mechanism for why it works?
tap
CIMT: forced use of hemiparetic UE by constraining unaffected UE (mitt 90% waking hours) + intensive massed practice of functional activities (shaping — graded difficulty, multi-trial). Mechanism: targets learned nonuse — affected limb avoided → cortical map shrinks → less capable → more avoidance. CIMT forces cortical map expansion via use. Candidate: 10° active wrist extension + 10° active finger extension, adequate cognition, no severe balance impairment. NOT for Stage 1–2 Brunnstrom. Strong evidence for mild-moderate chronic UE hemiparesis. Intensive protocol: 6 hrs/day x 10 consecutive weekdays. mCIMT for less intensive schedule.
Why does R MCA stroke cause left hemineglect while L MCA stroke rarely causes right neglect?
tap
The RIGHT parietal lobe controls spatial attention for BOTH right AND left hemifields. The LEFT parietal lobe controls attention primarily for the right hemifield only. Therefore: Right parietal damage → left hemineglect (right hemisphere no longer attends to left space). Left parietal damage → right parietal takes over spatial processing for both sides → no significant neglect. This asymmetry is why left-sided neglect from right hemisphere stroke is profound and persistent. Critical distinction: hemineglect = ATTENTION deficit; hemianopsia = VISUAL FIELD deficit. Patient who ignores left side of plate, left limbs, left space — this is neglect, not non-compliance.
Blocked vs. random practice — which produces better session performance? Which produces better retention? Why?
tap
Blocked practice: same task repeated before moving to next. Better session performance. WORSE retention — less cognitive engagement = weaker memory traces. Random practice: different tasks in random order. Worse session performance. BETTER retention and transfer — the contextual interference effect. Each task switch forces the learner to reconstruct the motor plan, elaborating it more deeply in memory. Clinical application: early-stage patients may benefit from some blocked practice (cognitive stage — needs to understand the task first). Intermediate/late stage: shift to random practice for retention. Variable practice + random order = best long-term learning outcomes.
What is the CPG, and how does body weight support treadmill training exploit it for gait retraining?
tap
CPG (Central Pattern Generator): spinal cord interneuron networks that generate rhythmic, coordinated stepping without voluntary cortical input. Cats with complete SCI still step on treadmill when hindlimbs are supported — driven by repetitive sensory input (rhythmic load + limb position) activating spinal circuits. In humans, CPG contributes to gait but is less dominant. BWS Treadmill Training: harness over treadmill; therapist manually facilitates limb timing; treadmill speed provides rhythmic sensory input activating CPG. Start with 30–40% BWS; progressively reduce. Speed is key — drives sensory input. Transition to overground for generalization. Evidence strongest for chronic incomplete SCI and subacute stroke.
What is ASAP and what is the evidence for language specificity in stroke rehabilitation?
tap
ASAP (Accelerated Skill Acquisition Program, Winstein): hybrid task-specific + impairment-mitigating training with patient-centered motivational enhancements. Three domains: Skill (motor learning + self-management), Capacity (impairment mitigation), Motivation (intrinsic drive). Evidence: patients given daily timed gait feedback and encouragement walked significantly faster at discharge AND 3 months later vs. controls. Empowering language: "How can you make this harder now?" "Active people like you usually do very well on this task." For practice to affect motor learning: purpose and goal-directedness are required — repetition without purpose is exercise, not learning. Language IS a clinical intervention.
What is the developmental sequence in NDT, and what does each transition train?
tap
Rolling → Sit to quadruped → Tall kneeling → Half kneel to stand → Cruising → Walking. Rolling: trunk rotation, upper/lower body dissociation. Sit to quadruped: weight shift in sitting, hip extension, transition through asymmetric WB. Tall kneeling: hip extension + stability without knee; frontal plane WS. Half kneel → stand: hip and knee extension through full range; step-to-stand component. Cruising: frontal plane ABD/ADD — the only developmental position that trains frontal plane LE work; lateral WS, momentum management. Walking: dynamic postural control all planes, reciprocal arm swing, CPG activation. Each transition usable therapeutically for patients at corresponding motor recovery level.
What primitive reflexes persist into adulthood after cortical damage? What does their return indicate?
tap
Primitive reflexes are inhibited (not eliminated) by cortical maturation. After cortical damage (stroke, TBI), lower centers are released from cortical inhibition — primitive reflexes re-emerge. Returning in adults with CNS damage: ATNR (head rotation → asymmetric arm posturing), grasp reflex (palm touch → involuntary grip), Babinski (plantar stroke → toe extension + fan — always pathological in adults), Moro-like startle response. Frontal release signs (excessive blinking, grope, grasp, palmomental) indicate frontal lobe dysfunction. Their return indicates the cortex can no longer inhibit lower centers — tells you where the lesion is (cortical/subcortical UMN damage) and guides prognosis/intervention.
FIST score >41 means what clinically? MDC = ? Clinically significant change = ?
tap
FIST score >41 = indicator for discharge to HOME vs. facility (supports home discharge decision). MDC (Minimal Detectable Change) = 5 points — change of 5 or more exceeds measurement error, meaning real change has occurred. Clinically significant change = >6 points. Used for non-ambulatory post-stroke patients who cannot participate in TUG or DGI. Tests: sitting balance, sensory components, motor components, proactive (anticipatory), reactive balance, steady-state sitting. Tasks are meant to be done in order — don't skip steps. Don't repeat timed tests. Document specific tasks patient fails — those become your treatment targets.
