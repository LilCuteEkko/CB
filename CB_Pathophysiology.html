Inflammation & Tissue Healing
Every injury your patient presents with is somewhere in this sequence. Understanding the phases — and their timelines — is how you justify your clinical decisions, select your modalities, and explain your progression to the patient and to the medical team.
Foundational Definition

Inflammation is injury to vascularized tissue resulting in a coordinated, complex, and dynamic series of events designed to restore function, replace damaged tissue, and promote regeneration. It is not a problem — it is a protective mechanism. Chronic inflammation is the pathologic state where acute inflammation, tissue destruction, and healing all occur simultaneously without resolution.

The Three Phases
Phase 1 — Inflammatory

Days 0–6 (up to 7)

Vascular Response

Injury → chemical mediators released → vasoconstriction (brief, seconds to minutes) then vasodilation. Histamine (from mast cells/platelets/basophils) + bradykinin → increased vascular permeability → plasma proteins leak into tissue → edema. Hageman factor (clotting XII) → coagulation + vasoconstriction. Prostaglandins sensitize pain receptors. Cell membranes become positively charged post-injury → promotes inflammatory cell adherence to vessel walls.

Leukocyte Migration Sequence

Extravasation (neutrophils migrate vessel → tissue) → Margination (roll + adhere to vessel walls) → Pavementing (accumulation of cells) → Emigration (squeeze between endothelium into interstitial fluid). Macrophages arrive Day 2 — most effective phagocytes when oxygen is present.

Hemostatic Response — Fibrin Lattice

Platelets bind to collagen → PDGF released → fibrin + fibronectin create a fibrin lattice. This is the ONLY source of tensile strength during the inflammatory phase. Also forms temporary plug in blood/lymph vessels limiting bleeding. Hematoma = blood-filled space. Hemarthrosis = blood in joint.

Edema Types

Transudate: first edema fluid; little protein, few cells — moves by hydrostatic/osmotic forces. Exudate: protein-rich; inflammatory cells; from increased permeability. Pus (suppurative exudate): dead neutrophils + bacteria + tissue debris. Abscess: localized collection of pus walled off by leukocytes — can rupture and spread infection. Four mechanisms of vascular permeability: endothelial cell contraction, platelet adhesion and thrombosis, leukocyte-dependent endothelial injury, leaking from regenerating capillaries.

Phase 2 — Proliferative / Reparative

Days 3–20

Epithelialization

Superficial and deep wound surfaces covered by new epithelium. Provides protective barrier against fluid/electrolyte loss and infection. Begins within hours of injury in clean wounds.

Fibroplasia — Collagen Synthesis

Fibroblasts migrate to the inflamed area → synthesize Type III collagen (immature) + ground substance. Produce hyaluronic acid (GAG) → draws water → increases intracellular matrix → facilitates cellular migration. By Day 7: significant increase in collagen concentration → increased tensile strength. By Day 12: Type III replaced by Type I. By Day 21: collagen production at maximum but wound strength only 20% of normal dermis.

Granulation Tissue & Wound Contraction

Capillary budding creates new vessels + fibroblasts + collagen = granulation tissue. Limited tensile strength initially. Wound contraction: pulls edges together; begins Day 5, peaks at 2 weeks. Contractures = fixed high resistance to passive stretch from fibrosis — clinical concern especially in burns and post-surgical scars. Progressive loading at this stage stimulates organized collagen deposition.

Healing by Intention

Primary: minimal tissue loss, closed with sutures, heals without wound contraction. Secondary: significant tissue loss or contamination → contraction must occur first, more scar. Delayed primary: delayed wound closure or grafting. Angiogenesis: growth of new blood vessels (essential for healing).

Phase 3 — Maturation / Remodeling

Day 9 → up to 2 years

Collagen Maturation

Type III collagen → Type I collagen (stronger, more organized). Fibers align with lines of stress (Wolff's law equivalent for soft tissue). Fibroblast, macrophage, myofibroblast, and capillary concentrations decrease. Water content declines. Scar appears whiter. Can persist up to 1–2 years in large injuries. This is why loading during remodeling drives optimal fiber alignment — and why immobilization during this phase produces poor mechanical quality scar.

Collagen Types — Clinical Relevance

Type I: bone, skin, tendon, ligament — highest tensile strength; mature collagen. Type II: cartilage. Type III: immature; first produced after injury — less strong; replaced by Type I over time. Clinical implication: pushing too hard during proliferative phase risks disrupting immature Type III collagen. Allowing adequate time for Type I maturation = optimal functional recovery.

Chronic Inflammation

Simultaneous active inflammation + tissue destruction + healing = persistent low-grade state. Cells present: monocyte/macrophage (phagocytosis, regulates coagulation), lymphocyte (humoral + cell-mediated immune response), eosinophil (allergic + parasitic), neutrophil (acute PMN), basophil (allergic; contains histamine + heparin). Failure to resolve acute inflammation → chronic tissue damage.

Tissue-Specific Healing

Tendon Healing — Tendinitis vs. Tendinosis vs. Tendinopathy

▼
Etiology of Tendinopathy

Mechanical theory: repetitive load extending into the linear zone → microstructural damage exceeds repair. Vascular theory: poor blood supply at enthesis → avascular zone → limited healing capacity. Exercise-induced hyperthermia: temperature rise during repetitive contraction. Neurologically mediated: mast cell degranulation releases substance P and CGRP. Important: pathologically abnormal tendons are not always painful — and painful tendons may not show morphologic change.

Tendinitis vs. Tendinosis vs. Tenosynovitis

Tendinitis: outdated term — inflammatory cells are absent from load-bearing tendon after acute phase. Should be replaced with tendinosis or tendinopathy. Tendinosis: degeneration of collagen from chronic overuse; Type I → Type III; no inflammatory cells. This is why anti-inflammatories have limited value in chronic tendinopathy. Tenosynovitis: inflammation of the synovial sheath surrounding the tendon (e.g., DeQuervain's, trigger finger) — this IS inflammatory; anti-inflammatories have more rationale here.

Ligament Healing & Muscle Repair

▼
Ligament Structure & Biology

Not as richly vascularized as other tissues → slower healing → more scar. Contains C-type pain fibers + proprioceptors (mechanoreceptors). Fibroblasts are metabolically active and responsible for "ligamentization" (e.g., ACL reconstruction graft). Composition: ~70% water, ~20% Type I collagen, 5% other collagen, 3% elastin, 2% glycoprotein. Sharpey's fibers: collagen that cements ligament to bone at insertion.

Ligament Healing Phases

Bleeding and Inflammation: local pain + bleeding from torn vessels; fibrin lattice forms. Proliferation (bridging material): fibroblasts + macrophage differentiation form bridging material. Complete ruptures are fundamentally unable to regenerate normal ligament matrix — they form mechanically inferior scar. Matrix remodeling: scar matrix contracts, becomes less viscous, more scar-like with time; but always mechanically inferior to original ligament. This is why post-surgical ligament reconstruction uses graft tissue, not relying on native healing.

Muscle Injury — Where and Why

Muscle strains usually occur near the myotendinous junction (MTJ), occasionally in the muscle belly. Mechanism: tension generated exceeds tensile capacity of the weakest structural element. Localized disruption of the sarcolemma in response to excessive tension. Eccentric loading = most injurious. Active (stimulated) muscle requires significantly more force to failure than passive muscle — absorbs more energy before failure. Injuries always at distal MTJ in maximally stimulated muscle.

Muscle Repair — Satellite Cells

After injury and phagocytosis of debris: satellite cells (resident muscle stem cells) activate → transform into myoblastic cells → form myotubes → new muscle fibers. This is muscle regeneration, not scar formation — which is why muscle has better healing capacity than ligament. Satellite cell activity is enhanced by growth hormone, testosterone, and mechanical loading. Age, immobilization, and temperature all influence healing rate.

Factors Affecting Tissue Healing

Local: type/size/location of injury, infection, vascular supply, movement/pressure, temperature, topical medications, electromagnetic energy, foreign body. Systemic: age (decreased flexibility, joint mobility, collagen cross-linking), infection, metabolic status (DM = major impairment), nutrition (protein deficiency impairs collagen synthesis), hormones (cortisol is catabolic), medications (corticosteroids impair healing), fever, oxygen availability.

Infectious Disease — PT Clinical Framework
You will encounter infectious disease in every clinical setting. Your role: recognize the signs, know transmission routes, implement precautions, and know when to hold therapy. "Need to know specific bugs and their signs and symptoms" — and which ones require isolation, which allow PT, and which are a contraindication to treatment.
Signs & Symptoms of Infectious Disease
  • Fever — most characteristic systemic sign. The 102°F Rule: conditions that regularly exceed 102°F (39°C) form one category; those that don't form another. 104°F = delirium/seizure risk especially in children. Pyrogens (endotoxins + leukocyte extracts) raise the hypothalamic thermostat.
  • Chills, sweating, malaise, nausea/vomiting
  • Tachypnea, confusion, hypotension — especially in older adults, nosocomial infections, immunocompromised
  • Abscess — localized pus collection walled by leukocytes; can rupture and spread. Psoas abscess: can present as buttock/hip/thigh pain — important DDx for apparent musculoskeletal complaints
  • Rash — microbe penetrates stratum corneum and multiplies locally
  • Red streaks (lymphangitis) — hemolytic streptococcus/staphylococcus following a vein toward lymph nodes — blood poisoning signal; immediate medical referral
  • Inflamed lymph nodes — acute infection: tender, asymmetric, enlarged, matted, skin red/warm. Supraclavicular and inguinal nodes = common metastatic cancer sites — hard and fixed → medical referral
  • Joint effusion — monoarticular (one joint) bacterial/mycobacterial/fungal/viral — don't assume OA

Chain of Transmission — 6 Links (Break Any Link)

Pathogenic agent → Reservoir → Portal of exit → Mode of transmission → Portal of entry → Susceptible host. PT breaks the chain primarily at: mode of transmission (hand hygiene, PPE, barriers) and susceptible host (patient education, vaccination status awareness). Nosocomial infections: 5% of hospital admissions acquire a new infection in-hospital. Handwashing is the single most effective prevention.

VZV — Shingles (Herpes Zoster) Clinical Features

Primary VZV = chickenpox → virus remains latent in dorsal root ganglia → reactivates as shingles in ~10–20% of population. Risk: adults >50 + immunocompromised (HIV, chemo, corticosteroids, cancer). Erupts along dermatomes (typically T3–L3; T5/T6 common). Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN): pain/hyperalgesia/allodynia persisting after rash — no cure; management with antivirals, gabapentin, TCAs. Contagious from 1–2 days before rash until all lesions have crusted. "Dewdrop on a rose petal" = classic rash description.

Infectious Mononucleosis — PT Protocol

EBV (HHV-4); oral secretions, blood, transplanted organs; 4–6 week incubation. Splenic rupture = most dangerous complication (enlarged spleen from EBV). PT rule: NO contact sports for at least 1 month after symptoms resolve — not just 1 month from diagnosis. Also: aseptic meningitis, encephalitis, hemolytic anemia, Guillain-Barré as complications. Kehr's sign: left shoulder pain from phrenic nerve irritation (referred pain from ruptured spleen or free abdominal blood) = immediate medical emergency.

Herpes Simplex & PT: Herpetic Whitlow

PT can inoculate their own finger from contact with a patient's active HSV lesion → herpetic whitlow (HSV infection of finger). Always wear gloves when treating patients with oral or skin lesions. HSV-1 is also implicated in Bell's Palsy (CN7 facial nerve paralysis). Acyclovir is the treatment. Barrier precautions for any patient with active herpetic lesions — standard precautions are minimum.

Lyme Disease — 3 Stages, PT Implications

▼
Pathogen & Transmission

Tick-borne spirochete: Borrelia burgdorferi. Vector: Ixodes scapularis tick (host = white-tailed deer). Named after Lyme, Connecticut (1976). 3–32 day incubation. Tick must usually remain attached >36 hours for transmission. Can survive for years in certain body tissues (joints, nervous system).

3 Stages of Lyme Disease

Stage 1 (Early localized — days): Erythema migrans (bull's-eye rash) + flu-like symptoms. Rash resolves spontaneously in ~4 weeks without treatment. Stage 2 (Disseminated — weeks): spreads to nervous system, heart, joints. Neurologic: aseptic meningitis, headache, stiff neck, Bell's palsy (cranial neuropathy), radiculopathies → may improve/resolve even without treatment. Cardiac: conduction abnormalities. Stage 3 (Late persistent — months to years): ~60% untreated develop intermittent Lyme arthritis; predominantly large joints especially knees; monoarticular or oligoarticular (NOT bilateral — distinguishes from RA).

PT Differential — Lyme vs. Other Arthritis

Lyme arthritis: monoarticular/oligoarticular; does NOT affect joints bilaterally (though sides may alternate); large joints (knee most common); history of tick exposure/travel to endemic area; responds to antibiotics (doxycycline/amoxicillin 14–21 days). RA: bilateral symmetric small joints; chronic; autoimmune. If you see a patient with apparent "knee OA" or unexplained mono-articular arthritis + possible tick exposure → screen for Lyme. Chronic Lyme arthritis = most widely recognized consequence of untreated Lyme in the US.

PT Precautions During Active Infection

During active Stage 2/3 Lyme: joint protection during active arthritis flares; neural tension techniques cautiously if radiculopathy present; fatigue management is essential (significant fatigue is common); avoid aggressive exercise during active systemic stage. After antibiotic treatment: progressive return to function. Always screen: unexplained mono/oligoarticular arthritis + constitutional symptoms (fever, fatigue) → referral before aggressive PT intervention.

Bloodborne Pathogens, Influenza & Nosocomial Infections

▼
Bloodborne Pathogens — HBV, HCV, HIV

Hepatitis B (HBV): transmitted via percutaneous injury or direct contact with blood/body fluids. Preexposure vaccination strongly recommended for all HCWs. Hepatitis C (HCV): needlestick highest risk; 6–7 week incubation; 75%+ develop chronic HCV; no vaccine available → strict standard precautions essential. HIV: standard precautions; needlestick risk ~0.3%; bloodborne; no cure but antiretroviral therapy allows normal lifespan. All three require: gloves for any contact with blood/body fluids, proper sharps disposal, immediate incident reporting for exposure.

Influenza — Vaccine & PT

Types A, B, C; Type A = most serious (H1N1, H5N1). Symptoms: fever, headache, fatigue from inflammatory cytokines (interferon, TNF). Vaccination recommended for: age >50, chronic heart/lung disease, diabetes, renal dysfunction, immunosuppression, pregnancy, HCWs. Antivirals (oseltamivir) reduce duration/severity IF started within first 48 hours of symptoms. Cytokine storm = proposed mechanism of 1918 pandemic lethality.

Pseudomonas — Most Common Nosocomial Pneumonia

Opportunistic pathogen; thrives on moist environmental surfaces (swimming pools, whirlpool tubs, respiratory therapy equipment, flowers, endoscopes). Invades blood vessels → vascular thrombosis. Produces protective proteoglycan shield → evades immune system. Clinical clue: fruity/sweet grape-like odor with blue-green exudate from wounds. Signs of bacteremia: fever, tachypnea, tachycardia, hypotension, delirium, renal failure, ARDS. PT source: traced to HCW hands and nails → strict hand hygiene. Immunocompromised (cystic fibrosis, neutropenic, AIDS) are highest risk.

Nosocomial Infections — PT Role

5% of hospital patients without prior infection will acquire one during hospitalization. Handwashing = most effective prevention — must be done even when gloves are used. Alcohol-based hand rub can replace handwashing UNLESS hands are visibly soiled. PT-specific risks: pool/whirlpool → Pseudomonas; equipment sharing → MRSA/C. diff; therapeutic touch → surface contamination. C. difficile: alcohol hand rubs do NOT kill C. diff spores — must use soap and water. Contact precautions + gown and gloves for C. diff patients.

Endocrine Dysfunction — PT Relevance
Endocrine disorders present to PT disguised as musculoskeletal complaints. CTS in both hands = think endocrine. Proximal muscle weakness + fatigue despite rest = thyroid or parathyroid. Bilateral flexor tenosynovitis = hypothyroidism. Pathologic fractures + kyphosis = hyperparathyroidism. Know the MSK presentations of each gland — they will appear in your clinic without a known endocrine diagnosis.
MSK Signs & Symptoms of Endocrine Disease — Screening Flags

Hand stiffness + bilateral CTS — think: hypothyroidism, diabetes, acromegaly, pregnancy. Proximal muscle weakness + atrophy + fatigue that persists despite rest — thyroid/parathyroid disease, acromegaly, Cushing's, DM, osteomalacia. Flexor tenosynovitis with stiffness (bilateral) — hallmark of hypothyroidism. Arthralgias of small hand joints — endocrine/metabolic disease. Pathologic fractures + thoracic kyphosis + vertebral compression fractures — hyperparathyroidism. Bilateral CTS — distinguishing feature from overuse CTS (which is usually unilateral).

The HPA Axis & Stress Response
Hypothalamus — Master Integrative Center
HPA Axis — Hierarchy

Hypothalamus (main integrative center for endocrine + ANS) → connects to anterior pituitary → controls anterior and posterior pituitary. Negative feedback loops: simple (e.g., low serum Ca²⁺ stimulates PTH, high inhibits) or complex (e.g., injury → CRH → ACTH → cortisol → suppresses HPA axis). Steroid therapy inhibits HPA axis by suppressing its own secretion — explains why chronic corticosteroid users cannot mount a stress response (Addisonian crisis risk).

Neuroendocrine Response to Stress — 6 Hormones

Catecholamines (epinephrine/norepinephrine): ↑ HR and contractility, peripheral vasoconstriction, ↑ blood glucose (glycogenolysis), ↑ fat catabolism — prepare body for fight/flight. Cortisol: regulates protein/CHO/lipid metabolism; ↑ blood glucose; weight gain; mood swings; higher in morning. Endorphins: modulate pain perception; raise pain threshold; produce sedation/euphoria — "body's heroin." Growth Hormone: stimulates skeletal + visceral growth; protein/CHO/fat metabolism; ↑ with stress. Prolactin: stimulates breast tissue; ↑ with stress. Testosterone: regulates sex characteristics; muscle mass; ↓ after stressful stimuli.

Cortisol — Elevated vs. Deficient
Elevated Cortisol Signs (Cushing's)

Abdominal weight gain with thin arms/legs (truncal obesity) · Puffy, flushed face (moon face) · Buffalo hump · Mood swings · Anxiety · Fatigue/poor sleep · Reddish stretch marks (striae) · Acne/skin changes · Irregular periods · Higher susceptibility to infection · High blood pressure · Generalized muscle weakness · Marked osteoporosis (worsened by Ca²⁺ loss in urine). PT: exercise with caution (fragile bones); skin integrity monitoring; fall risk.

Cortisol Deficiency (Addison's)

Profound fatigue, weight loss, hypotension, anorexia, N/V, bronze skin hyperpigmentation (elevated ACTH + MSH). Adrenal crisis (Addisonian crisis): severe abdominal/low back/leg pain; severe vomiting, diarrhea, hypotension; rapidly progresses to hypovolemic shock → renal shutdown. Triggers: surgery, pregnancy, accident, trauma, infection, salt loss from diaphoresis, missed steroid doses. Aquatic PT is contraindicated in Addison's disease — heat/humidity requires more cortisol for BP regulation; body cannot compensate.

Thyroid Dysfunction — Hyper vs. Hypo

▼
Hyperthyroidism — Graves Disease

Autoimmune; excess T3/T4 → accelerated metabolism. Signs: tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, heat intolerance, weight loss despite increased appetite, diarrhea, exophthalmos (eye protrusion — Graves-specific), goiter, sweating, anxiety, insomnia, proximal muscle weakness (thyrotoxic myopathy). Thyroid storm: life-threatening extreme hyperthyroidism → extreme tachycardia, hyperthermia (>106°F), agitation, coma — medical emergency. PT: monitor HR and BP; high resting HR may prevent exercise; watch for palpitations during activity; fall risk from muscle weakness.

Thyroid Cancer — PT Red Flag

Hard, painless nodule or multinodular gland. 4 types: Papillary (most common, slow-growing), Follicular, Medullary (C cells, MEN2 syndrome), Anaplastic (rare, fast-growing, poorly differentiated). PT flag: patient presents with dysphagia, hoarseness, dyspnea AND you find asymptomatic nodule or unusual neck swelling → immediate medical referral. Papillary and follicular together = ~90% of thyroid cancers; generally treatable.

Hypothyroidism — MSK Presentations

Deficient T3/T4 → slowed metabolism. Signs: fatigue, cold sensitivity, weight gain (10–15 lbs fluid retention), forgetfulness, depression, dry skin/hair, constipation, bradycardia, hoarseness, thick tongue. Musculoskeletal: bilateral CTS (endocrine CTS most often bilateral → distinguishes from overuse); flexor tenosynovitis with stiffness; myxedema (non-pitting boggy edema especially around eyes/hands/feet); inflammatory arthritis indistinguishable from RA; upper eyelid droop. Rhabdomyolysis warning during exercise — CK levels may be elevated at rest; aggressive exercise can precipitate muscle breakdown.

Hashimoto's Thyroiditis — Most Common Hypothyroidism

Women > men (10:1); ages 30–50; autoimmune (genetic predisposition HLA-DR3). Autoimmune destruction of thyroid → decreased T3/T4 → elevated TSH → compensatory goiter → eventually hypothyroidism develops. Most common cause of hypothyroidism in women over 50. May be hyperthyroid transiently early on (gland hyperfunction as it's being destroyed) before becoming hypothyroid. Difficulty swallowing possible from goiter compression.

Exercise & Hypothyroidism

Activity intolerance, weakness, apathy. Exercise improves lipid profile (hypothyroidism raises LDL/triglycerides). Exercise helps constipation from slowed peristalsis. Rhabdomyolysis risk: warn about excessive or sudden high-intensity exercise — CK may already be elevated; progression must be gradual. Medications (levothyroxine) if overdosed can cause atrial fibrillation or osteoporosis — monitor for cardiac symptoms and fall risk.

Parathyroid — Calcium Regulation & PT Bone Implications

▼
Hyperparathyroidism — Bone PT Implications

Excess PTH → increases Ca²⁺ + phosphorus release from bone (demineralization) + Ca²⁺ absorption in GI + Ca²⁺ reabsorption in kidneys. Result: hypercalcemia + bone demineralization → pathologic fractures, thoracic kyphosis, vertebral compression fractures. Nephrocalcinosis: calcium phosphate deposits in renal tubules. PT precautions: early ambulation essential (weight-bearing speeds recalcification); lung auscultation for pulmonary edema (if receiving large volumes saline); watch for digitalis toxicity (elevated Ca²⁺ + digitalis = arrhythmia risk); proceed with extreme caution for UE exercises post-parathyroidectomy (avoid disturbing surgical site).

Post-Parathyroidectomy PT

Semi-Fowler positioning; support head and neck to decrease edema. Observe for signs of tetany: Trousseau sign (carpal spasm from BP cuff inflation), Chvostek sign (facial nerve hyperirritability — tap facial nerve → facial muscle twitching), tingling in hands and mouth, increased neuromuscular irritability. Tetany = hypocalcemia from rapid calcium drop post-surgery. Also monitor for gout or pseudogout (crystal deposition from calcium disruption).

Hypoparathyroidism — Most Common Cause

Most common cause: iatrogenic — accidental removal of parathyroid glands during thyroidectomy or anterior neck surgery. Also: infarction from inadequate blood supply during surgery. Idiopathic (autoimmune) more common in children. Low PTH → reduced bone resorption → serum Ca²⁺ falls → severe neuromuscular irritability. High serum phosphate (less excreted by kidneys without PTH). Calcification may form in eyes and basal ganglia chronically.

Tetany — Clinical Signs

Acute tetany from hypocalcemia: tingling in fingertips → around mouth → feet → spreads to painful muscle tension, spasm, grimacing → laryngospasm → arrhythmia. Trousseau sign: inflate BP cuff above systolic → carpal spasm within 3 minutes = positive (hypocalcemia). Chvostek sign: tap facial nerve at angle of jaw → ipsilateral facial muscle twitching = positive. These are the two physical exam signs of hypocalcemia PT must know.

Conn Syndrome — PT Implications

Primary aldosteronism: aldosterone excess → sodium/water retention + potassium excretion → hypokalemia. PT implications: observe for signs of tetany; hypokalemia-induced cardiac dysrhythmia (most dangerous PT concern — hypokalemia causes arrhythmia → monitor HR rhythm during exercise); paresthesia; muscle weakness. Acute care vs. outpatient settings differ in monitoring capability.

Pituitary Disorders — Gigantism, Acromegaly, Cushing's, SIADH

▼
Gigantism vs. Acromegaly

Gigantism: GH excess BEFORE epiphyseal closure → overgrowth of long bones → increased bone thickness + soft tissue hypertrophy → up to 9 feet in height; develops in children. Acromegaly: GH excess AFTER epiphyseal closure → enlargement of face/jaw/hands/feet + coarsened features; slow development in adults. Both from hypersecretion of GH (usually pituitary adenoma). Ortho PT implications for acromegaly: arthritis, OA of spine, CTS in 50% of people with acromegaly, thoracic/lumbar pain, increased mortality with elevated GH/IGF-1. Screen ANY acromegaly patient for weakness, joint mobility changes, poor exercise tolerance.

Post-Op Pituitary Surgery (Hypophysectomy)

Ambulation and exercise encouraged within 24 hours. Coughing, sneezing, and blowing the nose are CONTRAINDICATED after surgery — increases intracranial pressure, risk of CSF leak. Deep breathing exercises ARE encouraged. Visual field changes if tumor compressed optic nerve → stay within patient's visual field during therapy. Watch for unexpected mood changes (hormone fluctuations post-op).

Cushing's Syndrome — PT Management

Hypercortisolism (from pituitary adenoma → Cushing's disease, or from exogenous steroids → Cushing's syndrome). Signs: protuberant abdomen, poor wound healing, skin thinning, generalized muscle weakness, marked osteoporosis (worsened by Ca²⁺ loss in urine), moon face, buffalo hump, thin arms/legs with central obesity. PT goal: promote joint mobility, strength, function. Anyone with Cushing's = screen for weakness, joint mobility loss, poor exercise tolerance + fall risk. Osteoporosis precautions apply. Wound healing monitoring.

Diabetes Insipidus vs. SIADH

Diabetes insipidus (DI): ADH deficiency → excretion of large amounts of dilute urine (no glucose — distinguishes from DM). Caused by hypothalamic/neurohypophyseal injury. SIADH: excessive ADH → water intoxication from fluid retention → sudden weight gain, fluid retention, decreased urination, hyponatremia. Most common cause: ectopic ADH from malignancy (oat cell/small cell lung cancer). Also: pulmonary disorders, CNS disorders, medications, stress/trauma, cancer. DI treated with vasopressin → excess vasopressin → can cause SIADH. Monitor for sudden weight gain or fluid retention changes in cancer patients.

GI System — Referred Pain, Red Flags & PT Management
The GI system refers pain to the musculoskeletal system constantly — and PT is often the first to catch it. Low back pain, hip pain, shoulder pain, and midthoracic pain can all be visceral referrals from the GI tract. Know the patterns. Know the red flags. Know when your patient needs a physician, not another lumbar mobilization.
GI Referred Pain Patterns — PT Must Know
Kehr's Sign — Left Shoulder Pain = Ruptured Spleen

Kehr's sign: acute left shoulder pain when lying down with legs elevated, caused by blood or irritants in the peritoneal cavity irritating the phrenic nerve → referred to left shoulder tip. Classic sign of ruptured spleen. Also seen in: free air in abdominal cavity, stomach ulcer, diverticulitis, laparoscopy. PT application: patient with EBV/mono, trauma, left shoulder pain with no MSK explanation → check for spleen tenderness + refer immediately. This is not a shoulder problem.

Other GI Referred Pain Patterns

Peptic ulcer (posterior wall): thoracolumbar pain + back pain + right shoulder pain. GI bleed: left shoulder pain (Kehr's sign). Acute ulcer: thoracolumbar pain. Referred pain occurs because visceral afferents share spinal levels with somatic afferents. The brain misattributes the source. Always screen any patient with unexplained spinal/shoulder pain for GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, altered bowel habits, blood in stool).

Psoas Abscess — PT Masquerader

GI infection/inflammation can create a psoas abscess confined to the psoas fascia that occasionally extends to the buttock, hip, and thigh. Patient presents with apparent hip flexor or groin pain. Red flags: pain not responding to MSK treatment, constitutional symptoms (fever, malaise, weight loss), history of Crohn's disease, diverticulitis, or immunocompromise. Screen with: fever, WBC, activity-related abdominal pain. Refer for imaging.

GI Red Flags & Vital Signs in PT
GI Bleed — Recognize the Signs

Coffee-ground emesis: upper GI bleed slowed/stopped; hemoglobin converted to hematin by gastric acid. Hematemesis: vomiting fresh red blood = active upper GI bleed. Melena: black, tarry, sticky stools = upper GI bleed. Hematochezia: bright red blood from rectum = lower GI bleed (or very rapid upper GI bleed). PT vital sign thresholds for suspected GI bleed: SBP <100 mmHg + pulse >100 bpm + ≥10 mmHg drop in diastolic BP → hold PT, notify MD immediately.

PUD — PT Management Cues

Peptic ulcer disease: burning/gnawing/cramping/aching pain near xiphoid, occurring in waves. Back pain or midthoracic pain (posterior wall perforation). Right upper quadrant and right shoulder pain. Additional history: pain relieved by antacids, worsened by NSAIDs/alcohol, vomiting, coffee-ground or bright-red hematemesis. PT consideration: NSAIDs (commonly prescribed post-MSK injury) → gastric mucosal erosion. 50% of long-term NSAID users have gastritis — monitor for GI symptoms.

Appendicitis — McBurney's Point

Peak incidence: males in 2nd–3rd decade. Classic sequence: epigastric/periumbilical pain → anorexia → N/V → low-grade fever → pain migrates to right lower quadrant. McBurney's point: 1.5–2 inches superomedial to the anterior superior iliac spine (2/3 way from umbilicus to ASIS). Positive test = pain in RLQ with palpation and rebound tenderness (worse when pressure released). Aggravating factors: coughing, walking, laughing, bending — any increase in IAP. PT must screen any patient with apparent hip flexor or RLQ pain: any unilateral RLQ pain + fever + nausea = immediate referral.

Crohn's Disease — PT Musculoskeletal Screening

Terminal ileum involvement → periumbilical pain referred to low back. Psoas abscess from Crohn's = low back/hip/SI pain that mimics MSK pathology. PT screening rule: any client with low back, hip, or sacroiliac pain of unknown origin → ask about accompanying intestinal symptoms (diarrhea, blood in stool, abdominal cramping, recent weight loss). IBD patients may have personality susceptibility to emotional stress that precipitates symptoms — biopsychosocial approach essential.

GERD — PT Positioning Considerations

LES (lower esophageal sphincter) weakness → acid reflux into esophagus. PT positioning: avoid supine for GERD patients during exercise — increases reflux; avoid exercises that increase intraabdominal pressure (IAP) in supine. Hiatal hernia: avoid all supine exercises + any exercises that increase IAP. Left side down positioning reduces GERD (stomach anatomy). Barrett's esophagus (from chronic GERD): 30–40× risk of esophageal adenocarcinoma — weight management and exercise reduce recurrence risk. GERD aggravated by strenuous exercise (inhibits gastric emptying).

Esophageal Varices — PT Precautions

Dilated veins in lower esophagus from portal hypertension (usually cirrhosis). Primary PT concern: avoid causing rupture. Avoid activities that increase IAP. No breath holding (Valsalva). Observe for behavioral/personality changes, stupor, lethargy, hallucinations = hepatic encephalopathy → medical emergency. Assess for fluid retention (ascites). Handle all transfers gently — any increase in abdominal pressure could rupture varices → massive bleed.

PT Clinical Decision Cases — Lab Value Application
Case 1: WBC 22,000 + Temp 102.7°F → Hold PT

74yo woman with pneumonia, sepsis, acute renal failure, COPD, HTN, CAD, DM. WBC 22,000 (normal 5,000–10,000) + temperature 102.7°F. Decision: Do NOT treat. WBC is significantly elevated suggesting active infection; temperature confirms systemic febrile response — body is mobilizing immune response. Contact physician; educate patient on clinical reasoning; document in chart. Resume PT when WBC trending down + fever resolved + patient hemodynamically stable.

Case 2: Sodium 112 mEq/L → Recognize Hyponatremia

87yo male, altered mental status, receiving IV saline. CT head negative. Na⁺ = 112 mEq/L (normal 136–145). Clinical significance: severe hyponatremia. Expected symptoms: stupor, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, lethargy, limp muscles, decreased tendon reflexes, orthostatic hypotension, seizures, stomach cramping. PT: hold exercise; immediate medical notification; IV saline correction underway; monitor mental status carefully — confusion may impair patient cooperation and safety. Return to PT when Na⁺ normalizes and mental status improves.

Case 3: Glucose 60 During Exercise → Hypoglycemia Protocol

65yo s/p TKA, HTN, IDDM, SVT. Pre-therapy glucose: 110 mg/dL. After 15 min cycling: dizziness + diaphoresis + PR 107 + glucose 60 mg/dL. Decision: Stop exercise immediately. Glucose 60 = hypoglycemia. Steps: (1) Stop all activity. (2) Provide 15g fast-acting carbohydrate. (3) Have patient sit/lie. (4) Recheck glucose in 15 minutes. (5) Do not resume therapy until glucose ≥80 mg/dL and patient asymptomatic. (6) Document and notify nursing. This is why all DM patients on insulin/sulfonylureas MUST carry glucose source to every PT session.

Case 4: Cancer Patient — Lab Values Before Starting

62yo woman, COPD + Stage IIb NSCLC, 1 month post-radiation + 2 weeks post-chemo. "Overwhelming weakness." Labs to check BEFORE PT: Hemoglobin (27–31 pg reference), WBC/ANC (neutropenia = infection risk), Hematocrit (37–47%), Platelet count (150,000–400,000/mm³). Post-chemo: all likely suppressed. If ANC <500 = room only, strict precautions, mask, no pool. If Hgb <8 = light ADLs only. If platelets <20,000 = no exercise. Follow-up: Hgb and Hct are most important to track throughout care — they determine exercise tolerance.

Case 5: Post-Op TKR, CAD History — Pre-PT Lab Review

78yo bilateral TKR, PMH: HTN, CAD s/p angioplasty + stenting. No labs available POD 0. What I want to know: Hemoglobin (surgery + anesthesia → blood loss → anemia); CK-MB (cardiac marker — perioperative MI risk with CAD history); CPK (overall muscle/organ damage marker). POD 1 without CBC: watch for pallor, tachycardia, fatigue out of proportion, dizziness — all suggest hemoglobin drop. For HTN history: HDL, LDL, triglycerides guide exercise safety. Vital signs: pre/during/post exercise — document any significant changes.

What is the ONLY source of tensile strength during the inflammatory phase of healing?
tap
Fibrin lattice — formed when fibrin + fibronectin create a temporary scaffolding at the wound. Platelets bind to collagen → PDGF released → fibrin lattice forms. This is the wound's only mechanical strength during Days 0–6. Clinical implication: do NOT stress the wound during this phase — the fibrin lattice is fragile. No other structure provides tensile strength until fibroblasts synthesize Type III collagen (Day 7+). This is why PRICEMM and protection are essential in the first week.
Type III → Type I collagen: when does this happen and why does it matter?
tap
Type III (immature) collagen is produced from Day 5 onward in the proliferative phase. By Day 12: Type III begins converting to Type I. By Day 21: collagen production at maximum but wound is only 20% of normal strength. Maturation phase: Type III → Type I continues for up to 2 years. Type I is stronger and aligns along lines of stress (Wolff's law equivalent). Clinical implication: progressive loading during maturation drives optimal Type I alignment — immobilization during this phase = mechanically inferior scar. This is why early mobilization and graded loading are the evidence-based standard.
Infectious mononucleosis — what is the most dangerous complication and what is the PT rule?
tap
Splenic rupture — most dangerous complication of EBV infectious mononucleosis (HHV-4). Enlarged spleen from EBV becomes vulnerable to rupture with trauma or contact sports. PT rule: No contact sports for at least 1 month after symptoms resolve (not from diagnosis — from resolution). Also: Kehr's sign (left shoulder pain when lying with legs elevated) = ruptured spleen → immediate medical emergency. Complications also include: aseptic meningitis, encephalitis, hemolytic anemia, aplastic anemia, Guillain-Barré. Monospot test = positive heterophil antibodies. Prognosis excellent with rest + supportive care.
Bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome — what endocrine conditions should you screen for?
tap
Bilateral CTS is a classic flag for endocrine disease (vs. unilateral which is more typical of overuse): Hypothyroidism (most common endocrine cause; also bilateral flexor tenosynovitis + stiffness), Acromegaly (GH excess → soft tissue overgrowth; CTS in 50% of acromegaly patients), Diabetes mellitus, Pregnancy (fluid retention). Screen by asking: fatigue disproportionate to activity, weight gain/loss, cold/heat intolerance, changes in bowel habits, hair/skin changes, menstrual irregularities. Bilaterality of CTS = the distinguishing feature that prompts endocrine screening.
Kehr's sign — what is it and what does it indicate?
tap
Kehr's sign: acute left shoulder pain (at the tip) when lying down with legs elevated, caused by blood or irritants in the peritoneal cavity irritating the phrenic nerve (C3–C5) → referred pain to left shoulder. Classic indicator of ruptured spleen. Also seen with: free air in peritoneum, stomach ulcer, diverticulitis, laparoscopy, any left-sided peritoneal irritant. PT application: patient with apparent left shoulder pain + history of EBV/mono, abdominal trauma, or anticoagulation → check for abdominal tenderness + refer immediately. This is a medical emergency, not a shoulder problem. Do not apply manual therapy to the left shoulder.
Adrenal crisis (Addisonian crisis) — what triggers it and why is aquatic PT contraindicated in Addison's disease?
tap
Adrenal crisis triggers: surgery, pregnancy, accident, trauma, infection, salt loss from profuse sweating (diaphoresis), missed steroid doses in someone with chronic adrenal insufficiency. Clinical: severe abdominal/low back/leg pain, vomiting, diarrhea, hypotension → progresses to hypovolemic shock → renal shutdown. Aquatic PT contraindicated because heat + humidity demands the body produce more cortisol to regulate BP and cool the body — but Addison's patients cannot produce adequate cortisol → blood vessels cannot respond appropriately → blood pressure drops → cannot thermoregulate. Standard PT in a cool, controlled environment with careful vital sign monitoring is appropriate.
WBC count — when do you hold PT, and what is the ANC threshold for room-only precautions?
tap
Hold PT if WBC >12,000–14,000 with active fever and signs of systemic infection — body is fighting; exercise is an additional stressor. WBC >20,000 = significant infection → hold + notify MD. ANC (Absolute Neutrophil Count) <500/mm³ = severe neutropenia → room-only precautions: strict hand hygiene, mask, gown, no pool or gym, no fresh flowers or fruit (pathogen sources), no visitors with colds. If ANC <500 + fever ≥38.3°C = febrile neutropenia = medical emergency → hold PT immediately, notify nursing. ANC 500–1,000 = proceed with caution + mask. ANC >1,000 = standard precautions generally sufficient.
Lyme disease — Stage 2 neurological manifestations and how Lyme arthritis differs from RA.
tap
Stage 2 neurological manifestations (weeks after tick bite): aseptic meningitis (headache, stiff neck, difficulty concentrating), cranial neuropathies especially Bell's palsy (facial nerve/CN7), radiculopathies — may improve even without treatment. Also: cardiac conduction abnormalities. Lyme arthritis vs. RA: Lyme = monoarticular or oligoarticular; does NOT affect joints bilaterally (sides may alternate); large joints (knee most common); responds to antibiotics (doxycycline 14–21 days); associated with endemic tick exposure. RA = bilateral symmetric small joints; chronic autoimmune; does not respond to antibiotics. Any unexplained mono/oligoarticular arthritis with tick exposure history → screen for Lyme before attributing to OA or RA.
Tendinopathy vs. tendinitis — why does this distinction matter clinically?
tap
The term "tendinitis" implies acute inflammation — but inflammatory cells are absent from load-bearing tendons after the first few days of injury. In chronic tendinopathy, the pathology is degeneration (Type I → Type III collagen, neovascularization, neurogenic mediators) — not inflammation. Clinical implication: NSAIDs, corticosteroid injections, and ice have limited therapeutic rationale for chronic tendinopathy — they target a mechanism that isn't present. Loading is the treatment — progressive tendon loading reorganizes and matures collagen (Type III → Type I). Tenosynovitis (e.g., DeQuervain's, trigger finger) IS inflammatory (synovial sheath) — anti-inflammatories and rest DO have rationale here. Knowing the difference changes your treatment.
McBurney's point — where is it and what does a positive finding mean?
tap
McBurney's point location: 1.5–2 inches superomedial to the anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) — approximately 2/3 of the way from the umbilicus to the ASIS. Positive finding: pain at RLQ with palpation + rebound tenderness (pain worse when pressure suddenly released) = appendicitis until proven otherwise. Also: patient lies with hip flexed (reduces stretch on inflamed peritoneum), aggravated by coughing/walking/laughing (increased IAP). Classic sequence: periumbilical pain → anorexia/N/V → pain migrates to RLQ → low-grade fever. PT application: screen any patient with unilateral hip flexor or RLQ pain + constitutional symptoms → immediate medical referral.
