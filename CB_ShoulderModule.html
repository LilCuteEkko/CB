Shoulder & MSK Foundations

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body — a ball-and-socket design that sacrifices bony stability for range of motion.

Shoulder Complex — Anatomy & Kinematics
The shoulder complex is the most mobile joint in the body, sacrificing stability for range of motion. Four joints work together as a functional unit. Stability depends primarily on dynamic muscular control — the rotator cuff — rather than bony or ligamentous constraint.
Four Joints of the Shoulder Complex
Glenohumeral (GH) Joint

Type: Ball and socket (multiaxial, synovial)
Convex: Humeral head (large) on Concave: Glenoid fossa (shallow, small) Glenoid covers only ~25–30% of humeral head at any given position Glenoid labrum deepens fossa by 50% and increases contact area CPP: Full abduction + external rotation (ABER)
OPP: ~55° abduction, 30° horizontal adduction (resting) Least stable major joint in the body → most commonly dislocated

Scapulohumeral Rhythm
2:1 Ratio — The Fundamental Rule

For every 3° of arm elevation, 2° occur at the GH joint and 1° occurs at the scapulothoracic joint (upward rotation). This 2:1 GH:ST ratio is maintained throughout elevation above 30°.

180° Total Arm Elevation

120° at GH (glenohumeral) + 60° scapular upward rotation (ST) = 180° total. The scapula must upwardly rotate to keep the glenoid under the humeral head throughout elevation.

Clinical Significance

Loss of scapular upward rotation (serratus anterior/lower trapezius weakness) → humeral head cannot clear subacromial space → impingement. Scapular dyskinesis = disrupted 2:1 rhythm = compressive loading on rotator cuff.

Rotator Cuff — Structure & Function
Primary Function — Depression of Humeral Head

The rotator cuff's most critical role is compressing and depressing the humeral head into the glenoid fossa during deltoid-driven elevation. Without this dynamic stabilization, the deltoid would simply shear the humeral head superiorly → subacromial impingement. The cuff creates a force couple with the deltoid.

Subacromial Space

Normal width ~9-10mm. Reduced by: arm elevation (narrowed at 60-120°), GH IR, scapular dyskinesis, hooked acromion (Type II/III), supraspinatus tendon hypertrophy. <7mm = impingement range.

Shoulder Special Tests
Special tests are only performed when they will help arrive at a diagnosis. Provocation tests reproduce familiar pain. Clearing tests rule out a structure. Facilitation tests assess neuromuscular control. Always confirm/negate your hypothesis — don't test randomly.
Using Sensitivity & Specificity

High sensitivity (Sen) → good at ruling OUT (SnNOut: if Sensitive test is Negative → rule Out). High specificity (Spec) → good at ruling IN (SpPin: if Specific test is Positive → rule In). Spec = proportion of true negatives correctly identified. Sen = proportion of true positives correctly identified.

Scapular Stability Tests (Facilitation)
Scapular Assistance Test (SAT)

Examiner places one hand on inferior medial scapula, other on superior scapular base → provides upward rotation assistance as patient actively elevates arm in scapular or sagittal plane (Kibler).

Positive = Decreased pain or increased ROM

Indicates possible subacromial impingement driven by scapular dyskinesis. Reliability: kappa 0.53–0.62; agreement 77–91% for flexion/scapular plane. Guides treatment toward scapular stabilizer strengthening.

Scapular Retraction Test (SRT)

Cross-hand technique — examiner manually retracts scapula while patient performs a previously painful or impossible movement (Kibler). Can be performed with 90° abduction during ER/IR.

Positive = Symptoms relieved

Indicates posterior impingement or RC pathology. Restoration of subacromial space via improved scapular position. Positive SRT = scapular retractors are priority treatment targets (rhomboids, middle trapezius).

Flip Sign

Resisted ER with shoulder at 0° abduction (Kelley). Positive = medial border of scapula "flips" away from thorax → indicates decreased scapular stability. Consider spinal accessory nerve palsy (trapezius) or severe serratus anterior weakness.

Tissue Healing Rates
Healing timelines drive clinical decision-making — they determine when to progress loading, when to expect a patient to plateau, and how to set realistic expectations. These ranges represent typical healing in healthy tissue; comorbidities (diabetes, smoking, immunosuppression, malnutrition) extend all timelines significantly.
Critical Clinical Principle

Tissue healing provides a biological ceiling — you cannot progress faster than tissue biology allows. Symptoms improving before tissue heals = risk of re-injury. Tissue healed before symptoms resolve = psychosocial factors driving disability. Both require different interventions.

Phases of Tissue Healing
Phase 1 — Inflammatory

Day 0–5 (up to 7)

Vascular response, cellular recruitment. Cardinal signs: redness, heat, swelling, pain, loss of function. Purpose: clean up damaged tissue, initiate repair. PRICE/POLICE principles. Active ROM within pain-free limits acceptable. Avoid aggressive loading.

Phase 2 — Proliferative

Day 5 – Week 3–6

Collagen synthesis (Type III initially). Granulation tissue. Fibroblast activity. Progressive loading stimulates organized collagen deposition. ROM restoration priority. Begin gentle strengthening as tolerated. Scar tissue pliable at this stage.

Phase 3 — Remodeling

Week 3 – 2 years

Type III collagen → Type I (stronger). Collagen fibers align with lines of stress (Wolf's law for soft tissue). Progressive loading essential for optimal alignment. Tissue not back to full strength for months. Return to sport criteria based on tissue timelines + functional testing.

Graft Ligamentization — ACL Example
Ligamentization Process

ACL graft goes through necrosis → revascularization → remodeling. Graft is actually weakest at 6-8 weeks (necrosis) then slowly strengthens. Return to sport <9 months increases re-tear risk. Ligament graft healing: 2 months → 2 years for full maturation.

Articular Cartilage — Unique Challenge

Articular cartilage is avascular → poor healing capacity. Repair procedures (microfracture, MACI, allograft) depend on fibrocartilage in-growth. Healing takes 2-3 months to 2 years. Cannot tolerate full loading until adequate maturation — drives long post-op timelines.

Shoulder Rehabilitation — Exercise Progression by Healing Stage
Match exercise selection to the biological stage of healing. Loading too early risks re-injury; loading too late results in disuse atrophy and prolonged disability. These stages apply across shoulder diagnoses — adjust based on post-op protocol, irritability, and patient response.
Stage 1 — Inflammatory / Acute

Days 1–4  ·  Protect + Pain Control + Gentle Motion

PROM & AAROM

Distal driver AAROM — opposite hand drives involved arm into flexion, extension, and rotation; patient relaxes shoulder musculature Proximal driver AAROM — clinician or patient guides motion from the proximal humerus Stability with ROM — wand, physioball, wall, self-assist, or PT-assisted in sitting; gentle guided elevation in pain-free range

Isometrics

Typically initiated at 90° pushing against a wall — multiple planes: Flex, Ext, IR, ER Sub-maximal intensity (20–40% MVC) — not a strengthening dose; used for pain modulation and early neuromuscular activation

Isometrics — Wall-Based (Flex / Ext / IR / ER)

Position: Start at 90° shoulder position, arm pressing gently against a wall Directions: Flexion (anterior wall press), Extension (posterior wall press), IR (medial forearm press), ER (lateral forearm press) Sub-maximal effort — no joint movement; pain modulation goal; activates RC co-contraction without mechanical stress to healing tissue Progress resistance gradually as pain allows; isometrics are the bridge from complete rest to active loading

Additional

Pendulum exercises — gravity-distracted GH joint; Codman's; passive distraction via body sway Gear shift exercise — supine; elbow bent; small pain-free GH rotations in gravity-eliminated position Scapular squeezes — retraction; activates mid/lower trap without GH loading; safe in all acute presentations

Resisted pulling / Rows (H, M, L) — with and without kinetic chain involvement Row with posterior or rotational step — add head rotation for cervical integration 3D bicep curl — diagonal plane; integrates forearm, elbow, shoulder IR / ER with band — light resistance; sub-pain threshold; RC co-contraction

Clinical Note

Light resistance at this stage is not primarily a strengthening dose — it encourages RC co-contraction and neuromuscular re-education. Pain should not increase during or after exercise. Scar tissue is pliable and responsive to movement in this window.

Stage 3 — Remodeling

Days 21–60  ·  Progressive Loading — Strength & Function

Primary Goal

Type III collagen → Type I conversion is driven by mechanical stress. Progressive loading along functional lines is essential for optimal collagen fiber alignment. This is the window where strength training has maximal biological impact on tissue quality.

Progress load, speed, and range — systematically advance all three variables; do not plateau on just one Resisted pushing — push-up progressions, chest press, wall push-up → floor push-up → weighted; bench press for strength athletes Resisted lifting and chopping — diagonal UE patterns; woodchops; functional reaching patterns PNF patterns (D1, D2) — proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation; diagonal patterns integrate multiple muscle groups; D2 flexion (reaching up and out) mimics throwing/overhead tasks

Progression Variables

Increase load OR speed OR range — not all three simultaneously. Monitor for symptom flare 24–48 hours post-exercise. If soreness resolves within 24 hours = appropriate loading. If persists >48 hours = reduce intensity.

Stage 4 — Maturation

Day 60 — up to 2 years  ·  Power, Agility & Sport-Specific

Primary Goal

Develop power (rate of force development), reactive stability, and sport/task-specific neuromuscular patterns. Prerequisites: adequate strength (see UE plyometric criteria), full ROM, no pain at rest or with functional activity.

Plyometric & Agility Exercises

Trampoline throws — bilateral or unilateral medicine ball throws into a small trampoline; reactive catch + immediate throw; trains SSC Up and overs — ball thrown up and over an obstacle or into a target; full elevation arc; trains eccentric deceleration Clap push-ups — explosive concentric push phase + airborne + catch; maximal SSC demand; requires full strength baseline Body blade with EOR — blade oscillation at end-of-range position; trains proprioception + RC stabilization under vibration at challenging position Kettlebell swings — hip-driven; trains kinetic chain power transmission; RC stabilizes at end range; integrates entire chain

Prerequisites Before Stage 4

Must meet UE plyometric criteria (see EBP Interventions tab): MMT ≥4/5, isokinetic ≥80% bilateral, CKCUEST within 20% normative. Do not progress to plyometrics without adequate strength foundation — risk of re-injury is high.

Fibromyalgia

▼
What Is It

Widespread pain across multiple body areas. Abnormal neurological response to stimuli — central sensitization and altered pain processing. Specific diagnosis criteria (historical: tender point testing; current: widespread pain index + symptom severity scale). Distinguish from myofascial pain syndrome (more localized, trigger points). Female > male. Biopsychosocial components essential.

Presentation

Widespread pain · Fatigue · Sleep hygiene disruption (non-restorative sleep) · "Fibro fog" — concentration and memory difficulties · GI symptoms (IBS overlap common) · Biopsychosocial contributors: anxiety, depression, trauma history, poor coping strategies.

PT Management

Education — pain neuroscience education (PNE) is a cornerstone: pain as a brain output, not tissue damage signal.
Graded Motor Imagery: bilateral movement, mirror therapy, 2-point discrimination, explicit motor imagery.
Desensitization techniques.Exercise: non-impact cardio (aerobic exercise is Level 1 evidence for fibromyalgia — reduces central sensitization), aquatics (warm water, gravity-eliminated), AROM, weight-bearing. Goal: increase activity tolerance gradually without symptom flares.

Clinical Caution

Do NOT apply standard musculoskeletal treatment protocols — aggressive exercise, heavy manual therapy, or high-load interventions will trigger flares and worsen sensitization. Graded, low-intensity exposure is the key. Coordinate with physician (medications: duloxetine, pregabalin, milnacipran are FDA-approved for fibromyalgia).

Rhythmic Stabilization

▼

Target Impairments

Technique

Patient holds a position while therapist applies alternating forces in multiple directions — requiring isometric co-contraction of all surrounding muscles. No movement occurs. Positions progress: 0° → 45° → 90° ABD in scapular plane. Can be done in weight-bearing (quadruped, side-lying) or non-WB.

Remodeling phase, after basic strength established. Essential for instability cases, post-surgical shoulder (SLAP repair, Bankart, RCR). Excellent bridge between strength exercise and functional training.

EBP — Tendinopathy Loading: Eccentric vs. Concentric vs. HSR
Evidence from Couppé, Svensson, Silbernagel, Langberg, Magnusson (JOSPT 2015;45:853-863). Applies to all tendinopathies: Achilles, patellar, lateral epicondylalgia, RC tendinopathy.
Tendinopathy — What We Now Know
NOT Inflammatory — "Tendinitis" is Outdated

Tendinopathy tissue shows: collagen degeneration + necrosis + neovascularization + irregular fiber structure + increased ground substance. No elevated inflammatory markers after exercise. The preferred term is tendinopathy or tendinosis — not tendinitis. This changes treatment: anti-inflammatories address the wrong mechanism for chronic presentations.

Loading Is the Treatment Mechanism

Tendon fibroblasts respond to mechanical strain: adequate loading = increased collagen synthesis + blood flow → adaptation. Depriving tendon of strain leads to degeneration and cell death. The question is not whether to load but how to load optimally.

Eccentric Is NOT Superior to Other Loading

No convincing clinical evidence that isolated eccentric loading produces better outcomes than concentric or heavy slow resistance training at comparable loads. Multiple high-quality studies: eccentric = concentric = combined loading for pain and function outcomes. The key variable is load magnitude and duration, not the direction of movement.

Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) Training — Superior Biochemical Effects
What HSR Adds

HSR = high load, low speed, few reps (e.g., 3-4 reps at ~70-85% 1RM). When compared head-to-head with eccentric protocol: similar pain and function improvements, but HSR produced additional biochemical benefits (increased collagen content, reduced glycation) and structural changes not seen with eccentric-only training.

Clinical Implication

The exclusive focus on eccentric loading has overshadowed other effective approaches. A progressive loading continuum is more appropriate: begin with tolerable loads (isometric for pain control → isotonic → HSR → sport-specific). Load magnitude matters; mode matters less.

Load Progression Principle

Current evidence supports: Isometric (pain control, early phase; 5×45 sec at 70% MVC) → Isotonic slow/concentric-eccentric (proliferative phase) → HSR or eccentric (remodeling, strength) → Plyometric/SSC (power, sport-specific). The tendon needs progressively increasing load to stimulate collagen adaptation.

EBP — UE Plyometric Exercise: Science & Criteria
From Davies, Riemann, Manske (IJSPT 2015;10:760). Plyometrics are the missing link between strength and return to sport. They train power — the rate of force development — which is distinct from strength alone.
Phase 1: Eccentric Pre-Stretch

Loads muscle spindle, series elastic components (SEC), and parallel elastic components (PEC). Three stretch variables: magnitude, rate, and duration. Rate is most critical — faster stretch = stronger neurological signal from muscle spindle = greater concentric response. SEC stores energy as potential kinetic energy.

Phase 2: Amortization ("Time to Rebound")

Time from eccentric cessation to concentric onset. The shorter the amortization, the more power produced (stored energy used efficiently). Long amortization = energy wasted as heat, stretch reflex not activated. Primary goal of plyometric training: decrease time to rebound.

Phase 3: Concentric Shortening

Power production phase. SEC recoil contributes 70–75% of concentric force increase. RC is 55–60% fast-twitch fibers (Lovering) — FT fibers most affected in RC injuries. Plyometrics selectively recruit FT fibers. Must be performed at ≥80–100% MVC to recruit FT fibers needed for power development.

Contraindications to UE Plyometrics

Pain at the injury/surgical site Inflammation — acute or subacute Acute or subacute sprains or strainsJoint instability (e.g., uncleared GH instability) Post-op soft tissue limitations (healing structure at risk)
Most important: insufficient foundational strength — if strength criteria not met, coordination and motor control are not present for high-demand plyometrics

Recovery Between Sessions

Recommended: 48–72 hours between plyometric sessions. Terminate session if movement quality deteriorates (fatigue = injury risk). Quality > quantity — intensity must be at ≥80% MVC to recruit the fast-twitch fibers that produce power.

Impairment-Based Interventions — Shoulder
Match the exercise to the specific impairment identified in the examination. This is how evidence-based exercise prescription works in practice: examination findings drive exercise selection, not diagnosis alone. The same "shoulder pain" diagnosis may require completely different exercises based on which specific impairment is present.
Clinical Reasoning Sequence

Pec Minor Length / Tightness

▼
Clinical Relevance

Pec minor tightness causes scapular anterior tipping and downward rotation → reduces subacromial space → secondary impingement. Common in desk workers, forward head posture, overhead athletes. Identified on examination by palpation of pec minor tightness and observation of anterior scapular tipping at rest.

Doorway stretch — bilateral pec minor/major stretch in doorway; elbow at 90°; lean through opening Wall Ball stretch — foam roller/ball behind shoulder blade; unilateral pec stretch with trunk rotation away T-extension over wall ball — supine over roller at mid-thoracic; arms in T-position; gravity-assisted extension + pec stretch Manual seated stretch — clinician applies posterior directed force to coracoid process while patient retracts; direct stretch

Key Teaching Cue

Patient should feel stretch across front of chest and anterior shoulder — not in the bicipital groove or deltoid. If they feel it in the wrong spot, adjust arm position. Thoracic extension component is critical; do not let the lumbar spine hyperextend to compensate.

Thoracic Spine Hypomobility

▼
Why Thoracic Mobility Matters for Shoulder

Thoracic hypomobility in extension and rotation forces the shoulder to compensate — reducing total available arm elevation and increasing scapular elevation with reaching. Research demonstrates that thoracic manipulation can immediately increase shoulder flexion ROM. Address the thorax before assuming shoulder-specific limitation.

90/90 (Book Openers) — sidelying; knees at 90°; top arm rotates open following hand; thoracic rotation emphasis Quadruped short lever arm openers — quadruped; hand behind head; thoracic rotation opening with short lever arm Prayer stretch — quadruped; sit back onto heels; arms extended; thoracic extension + shoulder flexion Wall ball / Foam roller — supine over roller at targeted thoracic level; gravity-assisted extension; segmental mobilization Standing thoracic openers — hands behind head; seated or standing; elbows open laterally; thoracic extension Thread the needle — quadruped; one arm threads under body rotating thorax; alternates sides

Progression

Begin with foam roller (passive) to identify mobility restrictions. Progress to active rotational exercises (book openers, thread the needle). Add resistance and speed for sport-specific overhead athletes. Target the segment(s) identified as most restricted on assessment — not the entire thoracic spine uniformly.

Posterior GH Capsule Tightness (GIRD)

▼
GIRD — Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit

Loss of GH IR compared to contralateral side (>20° = clinically significant). Posterior capsule tightness forces the humeral head anterosuperior during elevation → secondary impingement and posterior labral stress. Common in baseball pitchers and other throwers due to repetitive overhead stress. Distinguish posterior capsule from posterior RC tightness.

Posterior-Superior Capsule

Posterior glide with IR — mobilization: posterior-superior glide with passive IR; improves IR via direct joint mob Towel stretch — standing; hand behind back; opposite hand pulls via towel; graduated IR stretch

Posterior-Inferior Capsule

Posterior glide ABD → ADD — mobilization: posterior glide with shoulder in progression from ABD toward ADD Sleeper stretch — sidelying on involved shoulder; shoulder at 90° ABD; forearm passively rotated into IR by opposite hand; gravity-assisted; first-line for GIRD

Sleeper Stretch Evidence

The sleeper stretch is supported by multiple studies for improving GH IR ROM in overhead athletes. Perform at 90° ABD, gently push forearm into IR — stop at first onset of posterior shoulder discomfort (not pain). Hold 30 seconds, 3-5 reps. Avoid when posterior labral pathology is suspected (can compress posteriorly). Cross-body stretch is an alternative with less posterior capsule stress.

Serratus Anterior Weakness / Deactivation

▼
Clinical Significance

SA is the primary driver of scapular upward rotation and protraction. Weakness causes medial scapular winging, loss of upward rotation, and impaired scapulohumeral rhythm. SA also co-activates with subscapularis — synergistic relationship important for GH stability. Innervated by long thoracic nerve (C5-C7).

Target Exercises

Push-up plus — standard push-up + final protraction at top; SA + subscapularis co-activation; highest SA EMG activation120° SA punch — standing/seated; arm punches forward/up to 120° flex; "throwing a slow punch at the ceiling" Dynamic hug — resistance band; bilateral shoulder flex/horizontal ADD with protraction; SA + subscapularis

Progression

Start: Wall push-up plus (least load) → Knee push-up plus → Full push-up plus → Unstable surface push-up plus. For acute/early phase: wall slides with protraction (low load, pain-free ROM). For later phase: plank with protraction, single-arm push-up plus variations. The "plus" component (final protraction) is the key SA-activating element — do not skip it.

EMG Benchmark

Push-up plus produces ~60-70% MVIC SA activation. 120° SA punch: ~50-60%. Both are adequate for strengthening. Lower-level exercises (wall slides, prone protraction) at ~20-30% MVIC are better for early neuromuscular re-education without overloading.

Lower Trapezius Weakness & UT:LT Ratio Correction

▼
Upper Trap : Lower Trap Imbalance

In shoulder impingement, the UT:LT ratio is typically elevated (upper trap over-dominant, lower trap inhibited). This creates excessive scapular elevation with arm raising instead of upward rotation. Goal is to preferentially activate lower trap while minimizing upper trap contribution. Key principle: prone exercises preferentially bias lower trap over upper trap.

SA (protraction/upward rotation) + LT (depression/upward rotation) together produce pure scapular upward rotation without elevation. This is the key therapeutic target for restoring scapulohumeral rhythm.

Multidirectional Instability (MDI)

▼
MDI — Clinical Presentation

GH instability in multiple directions (anterior, posterior, inferior). Positive sulcus sign. Often in younger patients with hyperlaxity (Ehlers-Danlos), gymnasts, swimmers. Key treatment: dynamic stabilization via rotator cuff co-contraction — conservative management is first-line for MDI. Surgery only after 6+ months of dedicated rehab failure.

Side-plank — lateral core stability + GH compression loading; builds foundation for proximal stability Banded lat pull-down — resistance band overhead; GH compression + scapular depression; builds inferior stability Turkish get-up (TGU) — comprehensive whole-body stability exercise; challenges GH in multiple positions; excellent for MDI once basic stability established Resisted ER walkouts — band around wrists; maintain shoulder position while walking sideways; builds rotator cuff endurance for dynamic stability

Progression Principles for MDI

Start: stable surface, gravity-eliminated or low-load positions (sidelying ER, rhythmic stabilization). Progress: closed kinetic chain (push-up, plank variations, quadruped). Progress: unstable surfaces (Bosu, foam pad). Progress: dynamic loads and speed (Turkish get-up, overhead carries, resisted ER walkouts). Emphasize proprioception throughout.

Kinetic Chain Integration
The shoulder does not operate in isolation. During overhead reaching and throwing, 50-55% of total kinetic energy comes from the legs and trunk. Impairments in distal segments increase demands on the shoulder. Assess and treat the full kinetic chain.
Activation Sequence for Shoulder Elevation

Proximal to distal loading — bottom-up sequence

(1)Soleus — ankle stability, foundation of upright posture (2)TFL — hip stabilization, pelvic control (3)Hamstrings / Gluteals — hip extension force generation (4)Erector Spinae — trunk extension, thoracic extension for shoulder clearance (5)Deltoid + RC — shoulder elevation driven by proximal stability

Clinical Application

A pitcher with hip weakness will compensate at the shoulder. An office worker with collapsed foot arch will have altered trunk stability. Address the chain — not just the painful segment.

Post-Operative Shoulder Rehabilitation
Understanding surgical procedures drives appropriate post-op rehab. The precautions, timelines, and goals differ substantially by procedure and by tear/repair size. Always defer to the surgeon's specific protocol — these are general frameworks.
Universal Post-Op Principles

Surgical goals: Restore "nearly normal" anatomy and biomechanics · Decrease long-term pain · Improve long-term function · Promote joint health. Never outpace the biology — tissue healing timelines constrain progression regardless of how the patient feels. A repaired tendon at 12 weeks is still at ~25% of normal tensile strength.

Type II SLAP Repair

▼
Patient Population & Indications

Younger individuals, overhead athletes (baseball, tennis, swimming). Indicated after failure of conservative interventions and inability to return to play. Superior labrum anchored to glenoid via suture anchors.

Post-Op Precautions

Sling: 4–6 weeks.
ROM: staged per surgeon's protocol.
Isolated biceps contraction: minimum 6 weeks restriction (surgeon-dependent) — risk of "peel-back" of biceps-labral complex if loaded early. Conservative with biceps overload for minimum 6 months total.

Rehab Big Picture

Initial: cervical, elbow, wrist/hand ROM. Avoid positions placing tension on biceps. Scapular kinematics first — periscapular activation before GH loading. ROM staged over first 6 weeks.

Return to Activity Timeline

Golf/Return to work: 3–4 months. Throwing: 6+ months (with interval throwing program).

Complications

Re-tear (peel-back of biceps-labral complex) · Infection (monitor wound) · Post-op stiffness/adhesive capsulitis (especially with comorbidities or complex surgical approach)

Anterior Capsular Shift / Capsulorrhaphy (Bankart Repair)

▼
Indication

History of recurrent anterior/inferior shoulder dislocations, typically with concomitant pathology (RC tear, labral pathology: SLAP vs. Bankart, bony pathology: Hill-Sachs lesion). Arthroscopic reduction of GH ligament complex volume.

Post-Op Precautions

Sling: 6 weeks. ROM: staged per surgeon 6–12 weeks. Must avoid positions that stress the anterior capsule — avoid combined ABD + ER (the "at-risk" position) until cleared.

Rehab Big Picture

Initial: cervical, elbow, wrist/hand ROM. Scapular kinematics before GH loading. Milestone before functional training: full AROM + symmetrical strength + no scapular dyskinesis. All three must be met.

Return to Activity Timeline

Return to work: 4–6 months. Return to sport: 6+ months. Contact sports and overhead sports toward the longer end.

Subacromial Decompression (SAD)

▼
Indication & Procedure

Failed conservative management for subacromial impingement syndrome. Secondary to anatomic factors: bone spur, hooked acromion (Type II/III). Arthroscopic resection of the undersurface of the acromion and/or CA ligament to expand the subacromial space.

Key Clinical Point: No Anatomic Repair

Unlike RCR or SLAP repair, there is no healing structure to protect. ROM and strengthening can progress as tolerated based on pain and irritability rather than tissue healing timelines. This is the most aggressive early progression of all shoulder procedures.

Rehab Big Picture

Sling: primarily for comfort, not structural protection. 5–10 lb lifting restriction. Early pain management (ice, modalities, gentle AROM). Maintain cervical, elbow, wrist mobility. Regain full shoulder ROM as tolerated. Normalize scapulohumeral kinematics. Progress strengthening based on pain irritability and patient goals.

Rotator Cuff Repair (RCR)

▼
Indication & Procedure

Failed conservative management (medications, injection, rest, PT). Age, pain irritability, response to PT, function level, anatomy, comorbidities all factor in. Primarily arthroscopic — tendon reattached to humeral head via suture anchors. Subacromial space inspected + debrided simultaneously.

Critical Precautions

Sling with ABD pillow: 6 weeks (maintains repair in position of least tension).
Lifting restriction: 5–10 lbs minimum 3 months.Avoid cyclic loading: up to 12 weeks (repetitive low-load movement can disrupt healing). ROM depends on tear size: small tear ~6 weeks staged; massive repair ~6-8 weeks staged. PROM → AAROM → AROM → strengthening.

⚠ CRITICAL FACT — Tensile Strength

At 12 weeks post-op, the RTC is at only 25% of NORMAL tensile strength. Progression of strengthening must be very gradual. Early aggressive loading risks repair failure. Scapular stabilizers and deltoid are strengthened first, before loaded RTC exercises.

Return to Activity

Return to function: 4–6 months. Return to sport: 7–9 months (depending on patient goals and surgeon clearance).

Signs of Repair Failure

Shrug sign with shoulder flex/ABD · External rotation weakness or lag sign · Significant increase in ER ROM (tendon no longer restraining) · Large-massive tear failure: typically <12 weeks · Small-medium failure: <6 months

ORIF — Proximal Humerus Fracture

▼
Patient Population & Procedure

Elderly population. Common comorbidities (osteoporosis, DM, cardiovascular disease). Neer Classification: based on number of "parts" involved (greater/lesser tuberosity, humeral head, shaft). Imaging critical: XR, CT, MRI. ORIF: incision, fracture manually reduced, metal plate + surgical screws fixated.

Post-Op Precautions

Sling: 6–8 weeks. Restricted WB, AROM, and lifting restrictions are patient-specific based on fracture type, structures involved, and soft tissue/nerve/vascular integrity.

Rehab Big Picture

Patient-specific based on fracture location, tissue quality, comorbidities. Initial: joint protection, optimize cervical/elbow/wrist/hand ROM. Restore GH ROM safely per surgeon guidelines. Normalize scapulohumeral kinematics before advancing strengthening. Prioritize function.

Complications

Adhesive capsulitis (delay in PT, complexity of approach, comorbidities) · Subacromial impingement (altered kinematics, decreased mobility, hardware) · Infection · Hardware failure · AVN of humeral head (risk ↑ with # of parts) · Malalignment

Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (TSA)

▼
Indication

Advanced GH pathology with persistent pain and loss of function after failure of conservative interventions. Conditions: OA, RA, RC tear arthropathy, osteonecrosis, fracture. Must have adequate RC integrity — this differentiates from RTSA. Goals: resurfacing for pain, soft tissue balancing for stability, motion restoration for function.

Post-Op Precautions & Rehab

Sling: 4–6 weeks (with or without ABD pillow based on surgery). Rehab: optimize cervical/elbow/wrist/hand ROM → restore GH ROM → normalize scapulohumeral kinematics → progressive strengthening. Avoid aggressive passive ER stretching early (risks subscapularis repair if performed).

Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (RTSA)

▼
Key Distinction from TSA

Indicated when the RC is deficient (massive RC tear, RC tear arthropathy, failed TSA). Reverses the ball-and-socket: glenoid becomes the ball, humerus becomes the socket. This moves the center of rotation medially and inferiorly → increases deltoid moment arm → allows anterior deltoid to substitute for the non-functional RC for shoulder elevation.

Post-Op Precautions

Sling: 4–6 weeks (with or without ABD pillow). No reaching behind back OR across body: 6–12 weeks (surgeon-dependent — protects the posterior soft tissue repair).

Rehab Big Picture

Primary goal: deltoid + scapular function. Anterior deltoid essential for elevation (80–130° active elevation expected). If teres minor intact: max 15° active ER initially. PROM in scapular plane (flexion + ER initially; avoid hyperextension throughout; initiate IR when cleared). Avoid hyper-extension throughout. Realistic goal-setting critical.

Return to Activity Timeline

Recovery: 1–2 years. Low-impact sports under surgeon discretion: 4–6 months. Patients must understand this is a long-term commitment.

Complications

Acromial stress fracture (insidious onset; rapid AROM decline; acromion pain + tenderness; pain with deltoid MMT) · Scapular notching (erosion of scapular neck from humeral cup in ADDuction) · Dislocation (typically requires MOI) · Instability · Infection · Persistent pain · Poorly controlled swelling

Outcome Measures & Clinical Interview Tools
Outcome measures provide objective, reproducible data to track patient progress, demonstrate treatment effectiveness, and guide clinical decisions. SINSS and LOCIDAA are structured frameworks for the patient interview that ensure you gather all relevant information systematically.

Used to assess the patient's current status and guide clinical decision-making. Rate each component and use findings to determine appropriate treatment intensity and progression.

What is the intensity of the problem? Rate using 0–10 VAS or NRS.

Rate as

Minimal (0–3) · Moderate (4–6) · Severe (7–10)

High severity → lower treatment intensity initially. Match exercise load to severity.

Reflects the tissue's ability to handle physical stress. Related to tissue status and degree of inflammation.

How long until symptoms are provoked? How long do symptoms last? How long until symptoms are relieved?

Rate as

Min: provoked with significant effort, resolves quickly · Mod: provoked with moderate effort, slow resolution · Severe: provoked easily, does not resolve

Classification of tissue involved in the condition.

Bone Muscle Ligament Joint structure Nerve

Guides which special tests to perform and how to load in treatment.

S — Stage

Acute / Subacute / Chronic

Improving — appropriate loading, continue progression Stable — reassess: is treatment sufficient? Is diagnosis correct? Worsening — red flag consideration, referral, de-escalate treatment intensity

Manual Therapy — Shoulder & Upper Extremity
Manual therapy is selected based on identified impairments, not diagnosis. The clinical sequence: identify impairment (subjective + objective exam) → select technique → perform litmus test (re-test the impairment) → apply technique → re-test → adjust. If the re-test does not improve, modify the technique or the direction before abandoning it.
Mobilization with Movement (MWM) — Mulligan Concept
Definition (Mulligan, NAGS/SNAGS/MWMs, 6th Ed.)

MWM: "A sustained passive accessory joint mobilization applied by a therapist, with concurrent active pain-free physiological movement to end range performed by the patient."

Key Principles

Goal = abolition of symptoms — not just reduction. If pain persists during the MWM, it is either the wrong technique, wrong direction, or wrong dosage.
Constant therapist-patient communication — verbal and non-verbal. The patient must report any pain provocation immediately and the technique must be modified.
Litmus test — always establish a functional movement test (e.g., active shoulder flexion ROM) before the MWM. Re-test immediately after to confirm improvement. Failure to improve on the litmus test = modify the technique (direction, force, position) OR MWM is not indicated for this patient at this time When abolition achieved with overpressure from therapist: progress to 3 sets × 10 reps for neuromuscular re-education and lasting effect MWMs can be converted to self-MWMs (SMWM) for home exercise once the correct direction is established

Contraindications to Manual Therapy (Including MWM)

Uncleared fracture or suspected fracture in the region Acute inflammatory arthritis flare (RA, gout — active joint inflammation) Joint infection (septic arthritis) Malignancy in the region Unhealed surgical repair (e.g., acute post-op RCR — do not mobilize into restricted direction) Severe osteoporosis with high fracture risk Vascular compromise / DVT in the region Hypermobility / instability without stabilization component

Always

Screen for red flags before any manual technique. The Canadian C-Spine Rule must be applied before cervical manual therapy. Do not mobilize an uncleared post-traumatic spine.

UE MWM Techniques

MWM #1 — Shoulder Flexion, Scaption, or Abduction (with Load)

▼

Indication

Pain-limited or ROM-limited shoulder flexion, scaption, or abduction. The accessory glide corrects a positional fault at the GH joint that is maintaining the restriction or pain.

Technique

Patient standing or seated; holding a 3–5 lb weight in the involved hand Therapist applies a sustained accessory glide to the humeral head (typically a posterolateral or inferior direction — trial and adjust to find pain-free direction) While maintaining the sustained glide, patient actively elevates the arm through flexion, scaption, or abduction to end range Movement must be completely pain-free — if pain present, adjust glide direction The weight adds traction and facilitates the Mulligan effect

Litmus Test

Measure active ROM (flexion or abduction) before and after. Expect immediate improvement in both ROM and pain. If no change → adjust glide direction (try more inferior, more posterior, or lateral) → if still no change → MWM not indicated.

Dosage

When pain-free motion achieved: 3 sets × 10 reps with overpressure at end range. Progress to self-MWM (patient uses belt or opposite hand to sustain glide while self-elevating). Include in HEP once direction is established.

Common Error

Performing MWM if any pain reproduced during technique. Stop immediately, adjust direction. The MWM must be completely pain-free — this is non-negotiable by Mulligan's definition.

MWM #2 — Supine Shoulder Flexion or Abduction with Scapular Pinning

▼

Indication

Restricted or painful shoulder flexion/abduction where scapular stabilization (pinning) is needed to isolate the GH contribution and apply the accessory glide more precisely. Useful when standing MWM is not achieving adequate effect or when scapular dyskinesis is a contributing factor.

Technique

Patient supine Therapist stabilizes (pins) the scapula against the thorax with one hand — preventing excessive scapular elevation or tipping during elevation Other hand applies sustained accessory GH glide (typically posterolateral or inferior) Patient actively elevates arm into flexion or abduction while therapist maintains both the scapular pin and the GH glide Movement must be completely pain-free throughout

Clinical Advantage of Supine Position

Gravity-eliminated starting position reduces the baseline demand on the RC and periscapular muscles, allowing the therapist to apply the accessory glide more selectively. Also allows greater therapist body weight assistance for patients requiring more force. Better for irritable presentations where standing loads are too provocative.

Scapular Pinning Rationale

In patients with scapular dyskinesis, the scapula compensates early during elevation (premature elevation or tipping). Pinning prevents this compensation and forces the motion to occur at the GH joint — where the impairment resides. This isolates the technique effect.

MWM #3 — GH Internal Rotation MWM

▼

Indication

Restricted or painful GH IR — common in overhead athletes (GIRD), post-operative shoulder (after RCR, SLAP repair, anterior capsular shift), and adhesive capsulitis. Particularly effective when passive IR stretch alone is insufficient or provocative.

Technique

Patient positioned with shoulder at 90° abduction Therapist applies a sustained accessory glide to the humeral head — typically a posterior and/or inferior direction to decompress the posterior capsule While maintaining the glide, patient actively rotates into IR (or therapist passively moves through IR) to end range Movement must be completely pain-free Standing IR MWM is an alternative: patient positions arm in IR starting position; therapist applies accessory glide while patient actively rotates further into IR

Litmus Test for IR MWM

Measure passive or active GH IR ROM (in degrees) before and after. Expect immediate increase in pain-free IR range. If no improvement: try adjusting glide direction (more posterior, more inferior, or combined). If no change in any direction → consider sleeper stretch or Grade III–IV posterior GH joint mobilization instead.

Relationship to GIRD Treatment

GIRD (>20° IR deficit vs. contralateral): MWM GH IR is a clinician-applied technique to immediately increase IR ROM. Sleeper stretch is the patient-performed HEP equivalent. Both target the posterior GH capsule. MWM tends to produce more immediate ROM gains; sleeper stretch maintains them over time. Use both.

Impairment-Based Manual Therapy Toolbox
Select technique based on identified impairment. Establish a litmus test before every technique. Re-test after to confirm effect. Always have 2–3 technique options per impairment — if the first does not work, use an alternative.

Scapular Dyskinesia + Restricted Flexion / Abduction

▼

Technique Options

MWM Scapular Repositioning — therapist manually corrects scapular position (retraction + depression) while patient elevates arm; tests if scapular position is driving the impairment CARS with Manual Tracking — Controlled Articular Rotations: therapist guides the shoulder through its full active range with hands-on tracking of scapular and GH motion; neuromotor re-education + ROM Subscapularis Release — direct myofascial release to subscapularis; reduces anterior GH tightness that limits upward rotation Pin and Stretch — Latissimus Dorsi — tissue is pinned, then stretched through full shoulder elevation; addresses lat tightness that restricts elevation Pin and Stretch — Subscapularis — tissue pinned, shoulder moved into ER; direct stretch to subscapularis Soft Tissue Mobilization — general periscapular STM: upper trap, levator scapulae, rhomboids, posterior deltoid

Active shoulder flexion or abduction ROM + pain rating. After MWM Scapular Repositioning: if ROM improves immediately → confirm scapular position is the primary driver → treat scapular stabilizers (SA + LT) as priority. If no improvement → GH capsule or soft tissue restriction more likely.

Clinical Reasoning

The Scapular Assistance Test (SAT) in the objective exam is the diagnostic equivalent of MWM Scapular Repositioning. A positive SAT tells you manual scapular assistance improves motion → the manual therapy approach should include scapular repositioning techniques + scapular stabilizer exercise. Always pair manual therapy with targeted exercise.

▼

⚠ Post-Op Caution — Anterior Capsular Repair

After anterior capsular shift or Bankart repair: do NOT aggressively mobilize into ER in early post-op phase. The anterior capsule was intentionally tightened. Follow surgeon's staged ROM protocol. ER restriction early post-op is expected and protective — not a mobilization target.

More Emphasis on Self-Mobilization

For ER restriction not related to surgical repair: active range of motion and self-mobilization techniques (doorway ER stretch, supine wand ER) often as effective as passive techniques. Therapist-applied manual ER mobilization is a complement, not the primary treatment.

▼

Technique Options

Why Thoracic Manual Therapy Affects the Shoulder

Thoracic manipulation can immediately increase shoulder flexion ROM by 10–20° — before any shoulder-specific technique is applied. This is because thoracic extension and rotation are prerequisite for full scapular upward rotation and shoulder elevation. Always assess and treat thoracic mobility before assuming the restriction is purely at the GH joint.

Litmus Test for Thoracic Techniques

Measure active shoulder flexion ROM before and after thoracic mobilization. A meaningful increase (+10°) confirms thoracic mobility was a significant contributor. Then decide: continue thoracic → or add GH techniques for the remaining restriction.

What is scapulohumeral rhythm and why does it matter clinically in this scenario?
tap
2:1 GH:ST ratio — for every 3° of arm elevation, 2° at GH and 1° at scapulothoracic (upward rotation). 180° total = 120° GH + 60° ST. Loss of scapular upward rotation (serratus/lower trap weakness) → humeral head cannot clear subacromial space → impingement. Disrupted rhythm = scapular dyskinesis.
List the 4 rotator cuff muscles, their actions, and nerve supply?

MWM — definition, goal, and what to do if pain is provoked during the technique.
