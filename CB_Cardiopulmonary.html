Oxygen Transport System
Every patient is a cardiovascular and pulmonary patient. The heart and lungs contribute to or impair the ability to exercise. CV and pulmonary disease are top 3 causes of death and disability. Primary impairments (asthma, COPD, CHF) or secondary impairments (CVA, SCI, fractures, surgery, burns) both impact the O₂ transport chain.

Ventilation, Respiration & the O₂ Chain

Ventilation ≠ RespirationAerobic = 19× more efficient▼
Key Distinction

Ventilation = movement of air between the atmosphere and the lungs (breathing). Respiration = exchange of O₂ and CO₂ between the lungs and the blood (gas exchange). If ventilation is impaired, respiration will be impaired.

O₂ Transport Chain
  1. Ventilation — movement of gas into and out of lungs
  2. Distribution — air traveling to areas within the lungs
  3. Diffusion — movement of gases between alveoli and blood
  4. Perfusion — blood pushed through pulmonary capillaries by the heart
  5. Circulation — movement of blood from pulmonary capillaries through the body
Aerobic vs Anaerobic Metabolism

Aerobic: Requires O₂, uses carbs/fats/proteins, occurs in mitochondria, produces H₂O + CO₂, yields ~38 ATP/glucose molecule. Anaerobic: No O₂ required, uses only carbs, occurs in cytoplasm, produces lactic acid, yields ~2 ATP/glucose. Aerobic is ~19× more efficient.

Aerobic vs Anaerobic Tissues

Aerobic cells: Heart (highest resting O₂ consumption at 9.7 mL/100g/min), CNS, PNS, skeletal muscle. Anaerobic: Connective tissue (bone, cartilage, RBCs), skeletal muscle (can do both). Cardiac muscle consumes the most O₂ at rest — this is why cardiac ischemia is so damaging.

Minute Ventilation & Dead Space

Minute ventilation = VT × RR = 500 mL × 12 = 6 L/min. Exercise can reach 70 L/min. Anatomical dead space (conducting airways, no gas exchange) ≈ body weight in lbs in mL (e.g., 150 lb person = ~150 mL dead space). So of 500 mL tidal volume, only ~350 mL is available for alveolar ventilation. Critical: A neuro patient with TV of 200 mL has only 50 mL for gas exchange — this is why shallow breathing is so dangerous.

Ventilation-Perfusion (V/Q) Ratio

Normal V = 4 L/min, Q = 5 L/min, ratio ≈ 0.8. High V/Q = "wasted" or dead-space ventilation (air reaching areas with poor blood flow, e.g., pulmonary embolism). Low V/Q = blood "shunted" past areas with no gas exchange (e.g., atelectasis, pneumonia). Both impair O₂ transport.

Hypoxemia — Signs, Symptoms & Consequences

PaO₂ < 55 mmHgLong-term → Cor Pulmonale▼
Signs & Symptoms of Hypoxemia
  • Headache, SOB, breathlessness, palpitations, angina
  • Restlessness, tremors, bluish skin/fingernails/lips (cyanosis)
  • Reverse dyspnea: false SOB that improves with exertion and worsens with rest
  • PaO₂ < 55 mmHg → compensatory ↑ minute ventilation, ↓ PaCO₂, peripheral vasodilation, ↑ HR, ↑ CO
Severe Hypoxemia

Impaired judgment → progressive loss of cognitive function → progressive loss of motor function → loss of consciousness. Commonly seen: post-op, chronic lung disease, ARDS, pneumonia.

Long-Term Hypoxemia Cascade

Chronic hypoxemia → pulmonary hypertension → ↑ right side workload → right ventricular dysfunction → cor pulmonale (right-sided heart failure from pulmonary disease). Cor pulmonale with chronic hypoxemia = poor prognosis (32–100% increase in mortality).

Oxygen Toxicity & O₂ Tank Calculations

FiO₂ > 50% prolonged = toxicTank math for PT▼
Signs of O₂ Toxicity

Substernal chest pain, dry cough, dyspnea, pleuritic pain with inspiration, nasal stuffiness/congestion, sore throat. Occurs with prolonged exposure to high FiO₂ concentrations.

O₂ Prescription for PT

PT writes: "SpO₂ > ____% for PT intervention" (fill in target). This allows the PT to titrate O₂ flow rate during therapy to maintain the prescribed saturation level.

Tank Time Calculation

Formula: Tank duration (min) = (PSI × tank factor) ÷ flow rate (L/min). For a standard E-tank (factor = 0.28): if PSI = 1000, flow = 4 L/min → (1000 × 0.28) / 4 = 70 minutes. Always subtract 200 PSI as safety reserve before calculating. So with 200 PSI buffer: (800 × 0.28) / 4 = 56 minutes of usable time.

Cardiac Pathophysiology
Understanding cardiac pathology is essential for every PT — 62% of patients in an outpatient orthopedic practice have CVD. During MSK exams, determine if CVD exists and if risk factors are present, especially before aerobic exercise. Risk of cardiac event during exercise is higher in patients just starting exercise programs.

Primary HTN (90–95%)

Modifiable: high sodium, obesity, DM, hypercholesterolemia. Non-modifiable: family history, age >55, gender (M <55, F >55), ethnicity.

Pathogenesis

↑ peripheral resistance from arteriole narrowing is the #1 characteristic. Sympathetic: NE/Epi → vasoconstriction + ↑ CO. Renin-angiotensin: ↓ renal blood flow → renin → angiotensin → vasoconstriction + aldosterone → Na⁺/H₂O retention. Prolonged HTN → elastic tissue replaced by fibrous collagen → arteriosclerosis.

Disease Risk by BMI & Waist Circumference

Waist M ≤40" / F ≤35": Overweight (25–29.9) = Increased risk; Class I obesity (30–34.9) = High; Class II (35–39.9) = Very High; Class III (≥40) = Extremely High. Waist M >40" / F >35": All categories shift one level higher.

Atherosclerosis & Coronary Artery Disease

Plaque = smooth muscle + lipids + ECMThrombus = complete occlusion▼
Atherosclerosis

Thickening and loss of elasticity of arterial walls with intimal lesions (atheromas/plaques) that protrude into and obstruct vascular lumina. Three components: smooth muscle cells + macrophages + leukocytes; ECM with collagen and elastic fibers; intra/extracellular lipids. Foam cells = large lipid-laden cells from monocytes. Primarily affects elastic arteries and large/medium muscular arteries.

Clinical Consequences

Focal rupture/ulceration → thrombus formation. Hemorrhage within plaque → expansion or rupture. Complete thrombotic occlusion → MI, cerebral infarction. Aneurysm from plaque pressure or ischemic atrophy of media. Leads to: CAD/CHD/IHD, PVD (gangrene of LEs), stroke.

Angina Scale

0 = No angina. 1 = Light, barely noticeable. 2 = Moderate, bothersome. 3 = Severe, very uncomfortable. 4 = Most pain ever experienced.

Heart Failure — Left vs Right, Stages & Classes

LHF = pulmonary edemaRHF = peripheral edema, JVDStage D = LVAD/transplant▼
Left-Sided Heart Failure

LV can't maintain normal CO → blood backs up into lungs → pulmonary edema (dyspnea, orthopnea, crackles/rales, arrhythmia). Causes: CAD, MI, long-standing HTN, dilated cardiomyopathy. Systolic HF: Can't pump hard enough (low EF, e.g., 40/100 mL = 40%). Diastolic HF: Can't fill enough (stiff ventricle, reduced preload). Long-standing HTN → concentric hypertrophy → less room for blood → ↑ O₂ demand → squeezes coronaries → weaker contraction.

Right-Sided Heart Failure

Usually caused by advanced LHF → blood backs up into lungs → ↑ pulmonary artery pressure → harder for RV → biventricular HF. Or from chronic lung disease (cor pulmonale): hypoxia → pulmonary arteriole constriction → ↑ pulmonary BP → RV hypertrophy/failure. Signs: JVD, hepatosplenomegaly (pain), ascites, pitting edema (gravity-dependent pooling). Prolonged congestion → cardiac cirrhosis → liver failure.

CHF Treatment

ACE inhibitors (gold standard for CHF) → dilate blood vessels. Diuretics → reduce fluid buildup. Positive inotropes (digoxin) → improve contractility. Stage D → VAD (bridge to transplant or destination therapy) or transplant. S3 heart sound in older adults indicates presence of CHF ("ventricular gallop" — sounds like "Kentucky").

Pitting Edema Scale

1+: Barely perceptible, immediate rebound, <2mm. 2+: Easily identifiable, rebounds in 15 sec, 2–4mm. 3+: Rebounds in 15–30 sec, 5–7mm. 4+: Rebound >30 sec, >7mm.

Lymphedema — Primary vs Secondary

10% Primary / 90% SecondaryStage 3 = Elephantiasis▼
Primary Lymphedema (10%)

Congenital malformation or impairment of lymphatics. LE most often affected. Milroy's disease begins in infancy with abnormal lymph node formation.

Secondary Lymphedema (90%)

Acquired disruption/damage. Causes: venous insufficiency, filariasis, cancer treatment (most common in developed countries), obesity, trauma, IV drug abuse.

Staging

Stage 0: No visible lymphedema. Stage 1: Reversible with elevation and rest, no secondary skin changes. Stage 2: Spontaneously irreversible, possible infections, some skin changes. Stage 3: Elephantiasis — many infections, fibrotic skin changes.

Respiratory Pathophysiology
Respiratory diseases fall into three main categories: infectious (URI, pneumonia, TB), obstructive (COPD, asthma), and restrictive (fibrosis, chest wall). Vascular disorders (PE, pulmonary edema) and expansion disorders (atelectasis, pneumothorax, pleural effusion) also impact the respiratory system.

COPD — Emphysema & Chronic Bronchitis

Pink Puffer = EmphysemaBlue Bloater = Chronic Bronchitis▼
COPD Overview

Group of lung diseases that block airflow. Combination of emphysema + chronic bronchitis. Damage can't be reversed. Symptoms: SOB, wheezing, chronic cough. Treatment: rescue inhalers, inhaled/oral steroids. Typical PFT: low FEV1, low FEV1/FVC, high residual volume.

Emphysema — "Pink Puffer"

Alveoli damaged → walls weaken and rupture → larger air spaces instead of many small ones → decreased surface area for gas exchange. Presentation: thin, breathing fast, pink skin, pursed lip breathing, tripod position, uses accessory muscles. Barrel chest → heart sounds very distant.

Chronic Bronchitis — "Blue Bloater"

Inflammation/swelling of bronchial tubes → mucus buildup → decreased perfusion. Presentation: bluish skin/lips, deeper breaths but poor O₂ intake, frequent productive cough, wheezing, crackles, accessory muscle use.

Sputum Evaluation

Clear/white: Normal or viral. Yellow: Infection (WBCs present). Green: Chronic infection or Pseudomonas. Rust/brown: Pneumonia, old blood. Pink/frothy: Pulmonary edema. Red/bloody (hemoptysis): TB, cancer, PE. Always document color, consistency, amount, and odor.

Fremitus

Vibration felt through chest wall while patient says "99." ↑ Fremitus: consolidation (pneumonia) — solid tissue transmits vibrations better. ↓ Fremitus: air/fluid between lung and chest wall (effusion, pneumothorax, emphysema).

Electrocardiogram (ECG)
ECG represents the electrical activity of the heart only — no information about mechanical events. Provides data on HR, rhythm, ischemia/infarction, drug and electrolyte abnormalities. PTs must recognize irregular or lethal rhythms and identify ischemia vs infarction.

ECG Paper

Records at 25 mm/sec. Small box = 0.04 sec. Large box (5 small) = 0.2 sec. 15 large boxes = 3 seconds. 30 large boxes = 6 seconds.

Calculating HR from ECG

Method 1: Count QRS complexes in 6 seconds (30 large boxes), multiply by 10. Example: 7 QRS in 6 seconds = HR of 70 bpm. Method 2: 300 ÷ number of large boxes between R-R intervals.

ST Segment — Critical for PT

Deviation ≥0.5mm from baseline = abnormal. ST elevation = myocardial infarction (tissue death). ST depression = ischemia (tissue starving). >2mm ST depression during exercise = TERMINATE THE TEST immediately.

Lead Placement — 5-Lead

RA = White (right arm), LA = Black (left arm), RL = Green (right leg), LL = Red (left leg), V1 = Brown (chest). Memory: "Smoke over fire" (black over red), "White to the RIGHT" (white over green), "Brown is ground" (chest). 12-lead used when ischemia/infarction suspected — 12 views of heart.

Hypomagnesemia Cascade

Low Mg²⁺ → PVC → V-tach → V-fib. This electrolyte pathway is high-yield — always check Mg levels when you see ventricular ectopy.

Stepped-Care Approach to HTN

Step 1: Mild HTN — monotherapy (diuretic, BB, ACE, or CCB). Step 2: Add 2nd drug from different class. Step 3: Add 3rd agent from unused class. Step 4: Add 4th from different class. Angina treatment: nitrates + BB + CCB.

Antiarrhythmic Classes

Class I: Na⁺ channel blockers (normalize Na entry). Class II: Beta blockers (↓ sympathetic effects, control AV node). Class III: Prolong repolarization/refractory period, e.g., Amiodarone (Pacerone) — pulmonary toxicity, liver damage. Class IV: CCBs (↓ SA discharge, ↓ AV conduction).

Cardiopulmonary Evaluation
The CP evaluation follows the same patient management model but with specific emphasis on chart review for cardiac history, lab values, medications, and O₂ therapy. The patient interview is critical — if you appear hurried or distracted, the therapeutic relationship suffers.

Peripheral Vascular Disease (PVD/PAD)

Signs & Symptoms — The 6 P's

Pulselessness, Paralysis, Paresthesia, Pain, Pallor, Poikilothermia (temperature changes). Also: intermittent claudication (aching/cramping in legs with walking, relieved by rest), numbness/tingling/weakness, burning/aching in feet at rest, non-healing sores, cold legs/feet, skin color changes, hair loss on legs.

Arterial vs Venous Insufficiency Signs

Arterial: Less hair, dry skin, color changes, ↓ temperature, pain with elevation. Venous: Medial location, hemosiderin staining, more drainage, glossy skin, edema. Risk factors for PVD: obesity, diabetes, smoking, CHF, sedentary lifestyle.

Claudication Scale

Grade 1: Definite discomfort/pain but initial or modest level. Grade 2: Moderate pain from which you can be distracted. Grade 3: Intense pain you cannot be distracted from. Grade 4: Unbearable pain — stop walking, rest, resume when able.

Pulmonary Interventions
O₂ transport is limited in patients with ineffective cough or impaired mucociliary clearance. Retained secretions or mucus plugs interfere with gas exchange. Secretions must be mobilized from small peripheral airways to larger central airways where coughing can remove them.

Postural Drainage

Passive technique placing pt in positions that allow gravity to assist drainage from bronchopulmonary tree. Priority: treat most affected lung segment first. Encourage deep breathing and coughing. Positions may be modified for precautions.

Contraindications to Postural Drainage
  • ICP >20 mmHg, unstable head/neck injury
  • Active hemorrhage with hemodynamic instability
  • Recent spinal surgery or acute spinal injury
  • Active hemoptysis, empyema, bronchopleural fistula
  • Pulmonary edema from heart failure, large pleural effusions, PE
  • Older/confused/anxious patients, rib fracture ± flail chest
Trendelenburg Contraindications

ICP concerns, uncontrolled HTN, distended abdomen, esophageal surgery, recent hemoptysis from lung carcinoma, uncontrolled airway at risk for aspiration.

Contraindications to Chest Wall Manipulation (in addition to above)
  • Subcutaneous emphysema, recent epidural/spinal anesthesia
  • Recent skin grafts/flaps on thorax, recently placed pacemaker
  • Suspected pulmonary TB, lung contusion, bronchospasm
  • Osteomyelitis of ribs, osteoporosis, coagulopathy
  • Chest wall pain complaint
Effective Cough — 4 Stages

(1) Inspire enough volume. (2) Close glottis to build positive intrathoracic pressure. (3) Active contraction of abdominals and intercostals. (4) Open glottis → explosive expulsion of air and secretions.

Exercise Prescription & Cardiac Rehabilitation
Most clients will not know they have CP dysfunction. 45% of clinicians agree measuring HR and BP is essential, yet 38% never measured HR and 43% never measured BP in new patients. Risk of cardiac events is higher when just starting exercise. Chance of death from exercise: 6 per 100K in middle-aged men.

Criteria for Termination of Exercise
  • Severe fatigue, patient wants to stop
  • Signs of poor perfusion: ataxia, N/V, lightheadedness, pallor, cyanosis
  • SpO₂ < 90%
  • Chest pain, peripheral ischemia claudication, >8/10 VAS
  • SBP > 250 mmHg or DBP > 115 mmHg
  • Drop in SBP > 10 mmHg (cardiac tamponade concern)
  • HR not increasing with activity
  • SOB/dyspnea, monitoring equipment failure

Karvonen's Formula — Target HR Calculation

Target HR = (Max HR – Resting HR) × % + Resting HR. % = 40–70% of heart rate reserve. Example: Resting HR = 80, Max HR = 172. Target = (172–80) × 0.40 + 80 = 117 bpm (starting intensity for first 1–2 weeks). Progress gradually as aerobic capacity develops.

Phase II Progression Example

Week 1–2: Treadmill 0% grade × 3.0 mph, HR 117 × 20 min. Week 2–4: 0% × 3.5 mph, HR 126 × 30 min. Week 4–6: 2% × 3.5 mph, HR 136 × 40 min. Week 6–8: 4% × 3.8 mph, HR 144 × 45 min.

Factors Making Pt Inappropriate for Cardiac Rehab

Overt CHF, unstable/resting angina, hemodynamic instability (near syncope), serious arrhythmias, conduction defects, impaired function of other organ systems, uncontrolled HTN (>200 mmHg systolic), other precluding diseases.

Mechanical Ventilation & Transplant
Mechanical ventilation provides positive pressure to inflate the lungs during acute illness, serious trauma, or exacerbation/progression of chronic illness. PTs work with ventilated patients and must understand modes, settings, weaning signs, and transplant considerations.

Indications & Types of Mechanical Ventilation

Indications for Mechanical Ventilation
  • Apnea (temporary stoppage of breathing)
  • Acute hypercapnia not quickly reversed with standard treatment
  • PaO₂ < 50 mmHg with supplemental O₂
  • RR > 30 breaths/min
  • Vital capacity < 10 L/min
  • Protection of airway
  • Reversal of respiratory muscle fatigue
Invasive

Intubation: Artificial airway into trachea. For upper airway obstruction, inability to protect lower airways from aspiration, inability to clear secretions. Cuff leak suspected if patient can phonate. Tracheostomy: When >1–2 weeks of intubation needed. Reduced laryngeal injury, improved oral comfort, feasible oral feeding and vocalization.

Non-Invasive

Interface connects patient to ventilator without artificial airway. CPAP = continuous positive airway pressure. Used during weaning and for obstructive sleep apnea (5+ episodes of apnea lasting ≥10 sec each within 1 hour while sleeping).

LVAD & Heart/Lung Transplant

LVAD = no pulse (continuous)No CPR with LVADTransplant = denervated heart▼
Ventricular Assist Device (VAD)

For Stage D heart failure (LVEF <20%, 6–12 month prognosis). Bridge to transplant (BTT), bridge to candidacy (↓ BMI, cancer-free period), or destination therapy (DT). Continuous LVAD = no palpable pulse. No swimming/baths (shower only). No CPR with LVAD. Patient carries battery/controller in black bag. 2× 6MWT distance improvement. Energy conservation techniques important.

Heart Transplant — Post-Op PT

Denervated heart = no parasympathetic output. Higher resting HR (90–110 bpm). HR response to exercise is delayed (relies on circulating catecholamines, not vagal tone). Requires gradual warm-up and extended cool-down. Therapy initiated 12–24 hours post-op. Focus: gas exchange, airway clearance, pain, mobility. Progress: breathing exercises → coughing → bed mobility → ROM → seated exercises → ambulation (10–15 min continuous) → stationary cycle → stairs. Sternotomy precautions apply.

Allograft Rejection Signs

Malaise, reduced exercise tolerance, low-grade fever, ventricular dysrhythmias, hypotension with activity. Endomyocardial biopsy at 7–10 days.

Lung Resection Techniques

Wedge resection: Small sample/wedge. Segmentectomy: Entire bronchopulmonary segment (larger than wedge). Lobectomy: Entire lobe. Bronchoplastic/sleeve resection: Entire lobe + portion of mainstem bronchus. Pneumonectomy: Entire lung ± surrounding pleura.

Review Cards — Cardiopulmonary

What is the difference between ventilation and respiration, and why does it matter?
tap
Ventilation = mechanical movement of air in/out of lungs (breathing). Respiration = gas exchange (O₂/CO₂) between lungs and blood. If ventilation is impaired, respiration WILL be impaired. This is why PTs focus on breathing techniques, positioning, and airway clearance — improving ventilation directly improves gas exchange. Aerobic metabolism (requires O₂) is 19× more efficient than anaerobic — every effort to optimize O₂ transport matters.
How do you differentiate obstructive from restrictive lung disease on PFTs?
tap
Obstructive: FEV1 drops dramatically but FVC stays normal → FEV1/FVC ratio <0.8 (e.g., 1.0/4.0 = 25%). Air goes in but can't get out. Examples: COPD, asthma, CF. Restrictive: Both FEV1 and FVC decrease proportionally → ratio stays normal or slightly ↑ (e.g., 2.5/3.0 = 83%). Lungs can't expand. Examples: pulmonary fibrosis, scoliosis, obesity, ALS. The ratio is the key discriminator.
What are the signs of right-sided vs left-sided heart failure, and how does one cause the other?
tap
LHF: Blood backs up into lungs → pulmonary edema (dyspnea, orthopnea, crackles, S3 sound). RHF: Blood backs up into body → JVD, hepatosplenomegaly, ascites, peripheral pitting edema. LHF → ↑ pulmonary pressure → RV works harder → biventricular failure. Or chronic lung disease → cor pulmonale → RHF directly. S3 in elderly = CHF. ACE inhibitors = gold standard treatment.
What are the 4 phases of cardiac rehab and when does the patient transition between them?
tap
Phase I (inpatient): ICU → step-down, ~5 days. MET 1–4. Self-care eval, monitored mobility. Graduates with LLGXT. Phase II (outpatient monitored): Hospital, 3×/week × 12 weeks (insurance covers). Monitored: ECG telemetry, BP, HR, Borg. Karvonen's formula for target HR. Cool-down critical — cardiac events occur when too short. Phase III (community): YMCA/gym, lower monitoring. Self-monitoring. Phase IV: Lifelong independent exercise. Key: intensity is the crucial element — calculated from LLGXT data.
What is the significance of >2mm ST depression on ECG during exercise?
tap
Immediately terminate the exercise test. ST depression = myocardial ischemia (tissue starving for O₂). >2mm is significant and indicates the heart is not receiving adequate blood flow during exertion. ST elevation = more serious — indicates infarction (tissue death). Normal ST segment is isoelectric (flat at baseline). Any deviation ≥0.5mm from baseline is abnormal. This is the single most important ECG finding for PTs conducting or monitoring exercise.
Why is A-fib dangerous and what should the PT monitor?
tap
A-fib: Multiple ectopic foci in atria → erratic quivering → no real P-waves, irregular R-R. No true atrial contraction = no "atrial kick" = less ventricular filling. Not immediately life-threatening if ventricular response <100 at rest. But 30% of A-fib patients develop emboli → most are on anticoagulant therapy. PT must monitor: ventricular rate during exercise (if response >100 = concern for ↓ CO), INR levels (bleeding risk during therapy), signs of embolic events (sudden neuro changes).
How does beta blocker use affect your exercise prescription?
tap
Beta blockers ↓ HR and ↓ force of contraction → the patient's HR will NOT rise normally with exercise. You cannot use standard age-predicted max HR formulas. Use RPE instead of HR to guide intensity. Non-selective BBs can cause bronchoconstriction in asthma. BBs may mask signs of hypoglycemia in diabetic patients — tachycardia and tremors won't appear. Watch for excessive HR depression and orthostatic hypotension. Always check med list before setting HR targets.
What is the hypomagnesemia cardiac cascade?
tap
Low Mg²⁺ → PVC → V-tach → V-fib. This is a high-yield electrolyte-cardiac connection. Normal Mg: 1.2–1.9 mEq/L. Causes of low Mg: decreased intake/absorption, vitamin D deficiency, impaired renal absorption, hyperthyroidism, metabolic acidosis. Conversely, hypermagnesemia causes heart block and hypotension. Always check Mg levels when you see PVCs or ventricular ectopy on telemetry. Similarly: hypokalemia (K⁺ <2.5) can cause cardiac arrest; hyperphosphatemia can cause heart block, ventricular dysrhythmia, and asystole.
What are the key contraindications to postural drainage and percussion?
tap
Postural drainage: ICP >20 mmHg, unstable head/neck injury, active hemorrhage, recent spinal surgery, active hemoptysis, empyema, bronchopleural fistula, pulm edema from HF, large pleural effusion, PE, rib fracture ± flail chest. Trendelenburg specifically: uncontrolled HTN, distended abdomen, esophageal surgery, uncontrolled airway. Chest wall manipulation additionally: subcutaneous emphysema, recent pacemaker, suspected TB, bronchospasm, osteoporosis (big one for SNF populations), coagulopathy, chest wall pain.
What are the criteria for terminating exercise testing or activity?
tap
Stop immediately for: Severe fatigue or patient request. Signs of poor perfusion (ataxia, N/V, pallor, cyanosis). SpO₂ <90%. Chest pain or angina. Peripheral ischemia/claudication. VAS >8/10. BP changes: SBP >250, DBP >115, or drop in SBP >10 mmHg (drop may indicate cardiac tamponade). HR not increasing with activity (chronotropic incompetence). SOB/dyspnea. Equipment failure. Key: SBP dropping with increased activity is a red flag — the heart can't keep up with demand.
What is unique about exercising a heart transplant patient?
tap
Denervated heart = no parasympathetic (vagal) innervation. Consequences: higher resting HR (90–110 bpm). HR increase with exercise is delayed because it depends on circulating catecholamines rather than neural response. This means: gradual warm-up essential (heart needs time to respond), extended cool-down required (HR stays elevated longer). Therapy starts 12–24 hrs post-op. Sternotomy precautions apply. Monitor closely for rejection signs: malaise, ↓ exercise tolerance, low-grade fever, ventricular dysrhythmias, hypotension with activity. No CPR if LVAD was used as bridge.
How does exercise affect Type 2 DM at the cellular level, and what's the exercise prescription?
tap
Muscle contraction recruits GLUT4 transporters → uptake glucose from blood to working muscle for 48 hours post-exercise. Long-term training ↑ number of transporters and glucose uptake. This is why aerobic exercise should be no >48 hrs apart. Exercise can TREAT and even PREVENT Type 2 DM: augments insulin sensitivity, ↓ pancreas burden, ↓ HTN, improves lipids, improves body composition, decreases death from DM by >50%. Rx: 40% VO₂max → 50–70%. 10–15 min → 30–60 min. Non-WB preferred (neuropathy). Resistance 2–3×/week.
