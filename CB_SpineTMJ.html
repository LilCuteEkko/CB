TMJ — Temporomandibular Joint & Headache DDx

The TMJ is the most frequently used joint in the body — activated over 2,000 times daily for talking, chewing, swallowing, and yawning. It is unique: covered by fibrocartilage (not hyaline), divided by an articular disc into 2 compartments, and obligated to move bilaterally in synchrony. TMD affects 5–12% of the population and frequently coexists with cervical dysfunction through the trigeminocervical complex. PTs are first-line providers for TMD. This module covers TMJ anatomy, muscles of mastication with pain referral patterns, kinematics, disc displacement, TMD examination, headache differential diagnosis, and evidence-based management.

Patient A: clicking at ~25mm opening that has been present for 6 months. Opening = 42mm. Reciprocal click (opening + closing). No pain with chewing. Patient B: clicking suddenly STOPPED 2 weeks ago. Opening = 20mm. Jaw deflects to the right. No click. Pain with chewing on right side. Interpretation: Patient A = DDWR (disc displacement with reduction). The click IS the disc reducing — it pops back into place. Near-normal function. Treatment: controlled rotation exercises, patient education (not harmful, often self-limiting). Patient B = DDWOR (disc displacement without reduction) — the former clicking disc is now STUCK anteriorly. The disc can't reduce anymore, blocking translation → limited opening <25mm. Jaw deflects TOWARD the locked side. Treatment: joint mobilization (anterior glide to create space), mandibular distraction, gentle sustained opening exercises. May need advanced imaging if no improvement in 4–6 weeks.

Patient reports unilateral right-sided headache, temporal region, 3×/week for 2 months. How do you differentiate? Check 1 — Side-locked? Yes, always right = could be cervicogenic or TMD (migraine can shift sides).
Check 2 — Neck triggers? Cervical rotation restricted right, pain reproduced with right rotation = cervicogenic features.
Check 3 — FRT: Positive right (loss of 15° rotation compared to left). SN 90%, SP 88% for cervicogenic HA.
Check 4 — TMJ screen: No jaw clicking, no pain with chewing, TMJ palpation non-tender.
Check 5 — SNOOP red flags? No systemic symptoms, no neuro signs, gradual onset, age 38, no pattern change.
Conclusion: Cervicogenic headache. FRT positive + side-locked + neck trigger + restricted cervical rotation. Treatment: C1–C2 mobilization, deep neck flexor training (Jull protocol), postural correction.

TMJ Anatomy
The TMJ is a modified synovial hinge joint with translation. It is unique among joints: covered by fibrocartilage (not hyaline), divided into 2 compartments by an articular disc, and used ~2,000 times/day (talking, chewing, swallowing, yawning). 5–12% of the population has TMD requiring treatment; >1/3 will experience at least one TMD sign/symptom in their lifetime.

Trigeminocervical Complex

The TMJ sits at the convergence of the trigeminal nerve (CN V) and upper cervical nerve roots (C1–C4) in the brainstem/upper spinal cord. Afferent input from the TMJ, face, teeth, dura, and cervical spine all converge on the same second-order neurons in the trigeminocervical nucleus. This explains why: TMD causes headache, neck pain causes facial pain, and cervicogenic headache mimics migraine. Sensitization at this convergence zone creates nociplastic pain (centrally mediated hyperalgesia and allodynia). Always screen the cervical spine in TMD patients and vice versa.

Muscles of Mastication & Pain Referral Patterns
All muscles of mastication are innervated by CN V3 (mandibular division of trigeminal). Pain referral from masticatory muscles is the most common source of orofacial pain. 85% of TMD patients have referred craniofacial pain on palpation (Wright, 2000). Knowing the referral patterns is essential for accurate diagnosis.

Accessory Muscles & Suprahyoid Depressors

▼
Jaw Openers (Mandibular Depressors)

The lateral pterygoid is the primary opener. It is assisted by the suprahyoid muscles WHEN the infrahyoids fix the hyoid bone: digastric (anterior belly CN V3, posterior belly CN VII), mylohyoid (CN V3), geniohyoid (C1 via CN XII). Without infrahyoid fixation of the hyoid, the suprahyoids elevate the hyoid instead of depressing the mandible.

Other Referral Sources to Consider

Trapezius trigger points refer to the temple and behind the ear (mimics TMD).
SCM trigger points refer to the forehead, periorbital area, and ear.
Suboccipital muscles refer to the occiput and temple. Always palpate cervical musculature in TMD patients — referred craniofacial pain from upper trapezius is the single most common non-masticatory trigger point finding in TMD populations.

Clinical Rule

Ipsilateral pain with jaw movement = muscular origin. The painful muscle is on the SAME side as the movement direction. Contralateral pain = joint origin. The painful joint is on the OPPOSITE side (being compressed/stretched). Example: pain on the right with right lateral excursion = right muscular. Pain on the LEFT with right lateral excursion = left TMJ articular.

TMJ Kinematics & Biomechanics
The TMJ is the only joint in the body that MUST move bilaterally and synchronously. Movement at one TMJ obligates movement at the other. Dysfunction at one joint immediately affects the contralateral side.

Deviation vs Deflection

Deviation (S-curve): jaw moves AWAY from midline during opening then returns to midline by end of movement. Indicates disc displacement with reduction (disc recaptures mid-opening).
Deflection (C-curve): jaw moves away from midline and stays deviated at end of movement. Indicates: hypomobility on the deflection side (joint can’t translate), disc displacement without reduction (locked), or unilateral muscle spasm/contracture. The jaw deflects TOWARD the restricted/locked side.

Arthrokinematics Summary

Inferior compartment (discomandibular): rotation (spin) between condyle and disc.
Superior compartment (discotemporal): translation (glide) between disc and temporal bone. The disc moves WITH the condyle during translation (pulled by lateral pterygoid attachment). Disc displacement = disc no longer moves synchronously with the condyle. Convex condyle on concave fossa: roll and glide occur in opposite directions (condyle rolls posteriorly while gliding anteriorly during opening).

Temporomandibular Disorders (TMD)
TMD is classified into 2 main categories: myogenous (muscle origin — 65% of TMD) and arthrogenous (joint origin — 35%). Use the Diagnostic Criteria for TMD (DC/TMD) framework for standardized classification.

Arthrogenous TMD — Disc Displacement

▼
Disc Displacement WITH Reduction (DDWR)
Mechanism

Disc is displaced anteriorly at rest. During opening, the condyle translates forward and the disc “pops” back into normal position (reduces). On closing, disc displaces anteriorly again. Reciprocal click: opening click (reduction) + closing click (re-displacement). The disc is recapturable.

Presentation

Click/pop with opening (usually ~25mm when translation begins). May have closing click. Opening may be near-normal range. Jaw may deviate toward affected side then correct (S-curve deviation). Intermittent locking possible.

Disc Displacement WITHOUT Reduction (DDWOR)
Mechanism

Disc is displaced anteriorly and does NOT recapture during opening. The disc stays anterior — acts as a physical block. “Closed lock.” No click (disc doesn’t reduce). Often preceded by history of DDWR (clicking that suddenly stopped).

Presentation

Limited opening <25mm (can’t translate past the stuck disc). Jaw deflects (C-curve) TOWARD affected side. No click (vs DDWR which has click). Significant functional limitation — difficulty eating. Pain in retrodiscal tissue (condyle compresses bilaminar zone).

Other Arthrogenous TMD

Synovitis/capsulitis: inflammation of synovium/capsule. Pain at rest, worse at end-range.
Retrodiscitis: inflammation of retrodiscal tissue from disc displacement or trauma. Severe pain, jaw deviates AWAY from painful side at rest.
OA: crepitus (grinding), morning stiffness, reduced ROM.
RA: bilateral TMJ involvement, morning stiffness >30 min, progressive joint destruction.
Dislocation: condyle translates anterior to eminence and cannot return. Jaw locked OPEN (vs closed lock = DDWOR). Manual reduction required.

DC/TMD Diagnostic Criteria (2014)

Standardized diagnostic system replacing the old RDC/TMD. Axis I: physical diagnosis (myalgia, arthralgia, disc displacement with/without reduction, degenerative joint disease, subluxation, headache attributed to TMD). Axis II: psychosocial assessment (pain catastrophizing, depression, anxiety, functional limitation). Both axes must be assessed — central sensitization and psychosocial factors are major contributors to chronicity in TMD.

Headache Red Flags — Refer Immediately

“SNOOP”: Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, cancer history).
Neurological signs (confusion, seizures, focal deficits, papilledema).
Onset sudden (“thunderclap” = worst headache ever = SAH until proven otherwise).
Older age (>50 with new-onset headache = temporal arteritis, mass).
Pattern change (headache character fundamentally different from prior). Any of these = immediate medical referral before PT treatment.

Evidence-Based PT Management of TMD
PT is first-line conservative treatment for TMD. Strong evidence for manual therapy + exercise + patient education. Always address both TMJ and cervical spine.

Manual Therapy

▼
TMJ Techniques

Joint mobilization: start with external techniques (closed mouth) before progressing to intraoral. Inferior glide (distraction) through molars is the most common but should NOT be first-line (requires pre-positioned opening which stretches capsule). Start with anterior/posterior/lateral glides externally.
Disc recapture maneuver (DDWR): protract mandible past click point, then open/close from protruded position.
Cervical/thoracic manipulation: address concurrent cervical dysfunction (76% of TMD patients). Thoracic manipulation is safe and effective adjunct.

Exercise & Neuromuscular Re-education

▼
Key Exercises

Controlled opening: tongue on palate, open only in rotation range (0–25mm) to retrain motor control without aggravating translation.
Rhythmic stabilization: gentle isometric resistance in all directions with jaw slightly open.
6×6 protocol (Rocabado): (1) Tongue rest position (tongue on palate, teeth apart, breathe through nose), (2) Controlled rotation opening, (3) Rhythmic stabilization, (4) Axial extension (chin tuck), (5) Distraction/midline opening, (6) Mandibular stabilization.
Postural correction: forward head posture correction, scapular retraction, deep neck flexor training (same exercises as cervicogenic headache management).

Patient Education & Behavioral Modifications

▼
Essential Education Points

Resting jaw position: lips together, teeth apart, tongue on palate. Patients should maintain this throughout the day.
Habit awareness: identify and reduce parafunctional habits (clenching, grinding, nail biting, gum chewing, pen chewing, leaning chin on hand).
Soft food diet during acute phase (avoid chewy/hard foods, cut food into small pieces, don’t bite into apples).
Sleep position: avoid prone (forces lateral cervical rotation + jaw compression).
Stress management: bruxism and clenching increase with stress. Relaxation techniques, jaw awareness cues (phone alarm “unclench” reminders).
Refer to dentist for occlusal splint/night guard if bruxism confirmed.

Individual has clicking that occurs at ~25mm opening, then again on closing. Diagnosis?
tap
Disc displacement WITH reduction (DDWR). Reciprocal click: opening click = disc reduces (condyle recaptures disc during translation at ~25mm). Closing click = disc re-displaces anteriorly. Click = reduction. If clicking suddenly stops + limited opening → progressed to DDWOR.
Individual’s jaw deflects to the RIGHT during opening and stays deviated. Opening = 22mm. Diagnosis?
tap
Disc displacement WITHOUT reduction (DDWOR) on the RIGHT. Deflection (C-curve) = jaw deviates toward the restricted side and stays there. <25mm opening = rotation only, no translation (disc blocking). No click. “Closed lock.” Right TMJ cannot translate past the anteriorly displaced disc.
Why do TMD patients often have headaches and neck pain simultaneously in this scenario?
tap
Trigeminocervical complex. Afferent input from TMJ (CN V), face, teeth, dura, and cervical spine (C1–C4) all converge on the same second-order neurons in the brainstem. Sensitization at this convergence creates cross-referral: TMD → headache, neck pain → facial pain. 76% of TMD patients have concurrent cervical dysfunction.
Individual has unilateral side-locked headache triggered by neck movement. FRT positive. What headache type?
tap
Cervicogenic headache. Side-locked (doesn’t shift sides), triggered by neck movement/posture, FRT positive (SN 90%, SP 88%). Treatment: cervical manipulation + deep neck flexor training (Jull 2002 RCT). Not migraine (would shift sides, have photophobia/phonophobia/nausea).
Masseter trigger points — where does the pain refer in this scenario?
tap
Lower teeth, lateral face, ear, periorbital region. Masseter is the most common source of referred jaw pain. Easily palpable externally over angle of jaw. Compare: temporalis refers to upper teeth + temple + behind eye. Lateral pterygoid refers preauricular + deep behind eye. Medial pterygoid refers to tongue + throat + ear.
List the SNOOP headache red flags?
tap
S = Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, cancer). N = Neurological signs (confusion, seizures, focal deficits). O = Onset sudden (“thunderclap” = SAH). O = Older age (>50 new onset = temporal arteritis, mass). P = Pattern change. Any SNOOP = immediate medical referral before PT treatment.
What is the resting jaw position you should teach every TMD patient in this scenario?
tap
Lips together, teeth APART, tongue resting on palate. This is the true resting position — teeth should not be in contact except during chewing and swallowing (~20 min/day). Teeth clenching = sustained masseter/temporalis contraction = fatigue, trigger points, disc loading. Teach as a habit — phone reminders (“unclench”) help.
