Therapeutic Exercise & Rehab Principles

The foundational framework for how PT prescribes, progresses, and justifies exercise. Movement variables, rehabilitation staging, resistance training dosing, plyometrics, balance and fall prevention, soft tissue mobilization, Janda syndromes, and functional testing — this is how you build a plan of care.

The 10 Movement Variables
This is the language of exercise prescription. Use these 10 variables to describe any exercise with precision, communicate with colleagues, and systematically progress or regress any movement. Without this framework, you're guessing. With it, every exercise choice has a rationale you can explain and defend.

Complete Exercise Description — Standard Lunge

Action: Anterior lunge · Environment: Flat floor · Position: Standing · Driver: Foot (anterior) · Direction: Anterior · Height: Floor level · Distance: Mid range · Load: Bodyweight · Rate: Slow, controlled · Duration: 3 × 10 each leg.

Full description: "Slow, bodyweight anterior lunge with anterior foot driver at floor level to mid range for 3 × 10 repetitions each leg. Target: neuromotor control, quadriceps, hamstrings, gluteals, and hip flexor stretch for the stance limb."

Regression (Easier)

Change Environment: Step behind to a chair for balance assist.
Change Distance: Short range step → only slight knee bend.
Change Rate: Very slow with 3-second hold at bottom.
Change Driver: Static lunge (stationary) rather than dynamic stepping.
Change Load: Hands on wall. Goal: reduce proprioceptive demand, balance requirement, and hip flexor length tension.

Progression (Harder)

Change Environment: BOSU or foam pad under front foot.
Change Load: Add dumbbells or medicine ball.
Change Direction: Add torso rotation to opposite knee at bottom (frontal + transverse plane).
Change Height: Step up onto 12-inch box.
Change Rate: Explosive push-back to standing.
Change Driver: Overhead reach with MB at bottom position. Goal: increase multi-planar demand, proprioceptive challenge, and functional power.

Driver Is the Most Powerful Variable

Changing the driver changes which muscle is being primarily loaded and challenged. In plank: elbow driver = anterior chain stability. Knee driver with rotation = oblique + hip flexor challenge. Foot driver with leg lift = posterior chain + gluteal activation. Same exercise, completely different targets — just by changing the driver.

Muscle Contraction Types

Concentric: active shortening of muscle to produce force (e.g., bicep curl upward phase).
Eccentric: active lengthening while producing force — generates 10–40% MORE force than concentric, most injurious, DOMS source (e.g., curl downward phase, landing from jump).
Isometric: muscle produces force without changing length (e.g., wall sit, plank).
Isotonic: muscle changes length under load — encompasses both concentric and eccentric phases.
Hierarchy of force production: Eccentric > Isometric > Concentric (weakest).

Programming Order — Compound Before Isolation

Multi-joint, large muscle group exercises (squat, deadlift, press, row) should always precede single-joint, smaller muscle exercises (leg extension, bicep curl). Rationale: compound exercises require maximal neural recruitment and CNS output — performing them when fatigued risks poor motor patterns and injury. Complete your squats before your leg extensions.

Rehabilitation Staging Framework
Rehab is not random exercise selection — it's a staged, evidence-informed progression from pain management to performance. Every clinical decision maps back to where the patient is in healing and the priority of rehabilitation. This framework is how you justify your POC, answer your clinical instructor's questions, and guide patients through recovery.
The 3 R's of Rehabilitation

1 — Relieve

Pain management, edema control, protection of injured tissue. Patient cannot build on anything if they're in too much pain to engage.

2 — Restore

ROM, flexibility, strength, neuromuscular control, endurance. The fundamental qualities of movement that were lost or disrupted.

3 — Return to Function

Work, sport, ADLs, performance. Task-specific training that replicates the demands of the patient's life. The goal of everything that came before.

Priority of Rehabilitation — 6-Step Hierarchy

(1) Address reason for abnormal movement/dysfunction/pain — you cannot build strength on a dysfunctional movement pattern (2) Flexibility and ROM — adequate range is required for safe loading (3) Strength and Endurance — once movement quality and range are present (4) Balance and Coordination — neuromuscular control for functional tasks (5) Function — task-specific training (stairs, gait, reaching, lifting)
(6) Performance — sport-specific, power, speed, agility

Clinical Application

You don't skip steps. A patient who can't achieve adequate hip flexion range (Step 2) should not be working on single-leg squat strength (Step 3) in that motion. Patients often want to jump to function before the foundation is built — your job is to explain why sequencing matters.

Active Rest Concept

Active rest = after a tough training session or during recovery days, low-intensity movement (swimming, light cycling, walking) keeps blood flowing, maintains mobility, and speeds recovery — superior to passive rest (sitting still). Distinct from cool-down: can occur 24 hours later. Maintains cardiovascular output while facilitating tissue repair.

Progression & Overtraining Prevention
2–10% Progression Rule

When a patient can comfortably perform the current workload for 1–2 extra reps over the desired number on 2 consecutive sessions, increase load by 2–10%. This prevents overtraining and drives progressive overload safely. More aggressive increases → higher injury risk. Patience in progression = better long-term outcomes.

Overtraining Signs

Persistent fatigue despite rest, performance plateau or decline, mood disturbance (anxiety/depression/irritability), sleep disruption, increased injury frequency, resting HR elevation. Prevention: avoid dramatic volume increases; build in deload weeks; adequate sleep and nutrition. Not just for athletes — SNF patients can overtrain too.

Benefits of Resistance Training

Combats sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) Increases bone mineral density (osteoporosis prevention) Reduces cardiovascular risk (↓BP, ↓body fat, ↑HDL) Reduces fall risk Decreases anxiety and depression Slows degeneration of joints Improves quality of life and independence

Plyometrics — Science, Staging & Clinical Application
Plyometrics are the bridge between strength and performance — the missing link between rehabilitation and return to sport. Davies, Riemann & Manske (IJSPT 2015). Used in the terminal phases of rehab to develop power, train fast-twitch fibers, and prepare tissues for the actual demands of sport, work, and daily life. Not for everyone — but for those who need explosive movement, non-negotiable.
The 3 Phases of a Plyometric Movement
Phase 1 — Eccentric Pre-Stretch (Loading)

Also called: readiness, pre-loading, preparatory, counter-movement, potentiation phase. The eccentric motion stretches the muscle spindle + non-contractile tissue (Series Elastic Component [SEC] + Parallel Elastic Component [PEC]). This is the neurophysiological-biomechanical priming. Three stretch variables determine stored energy: magnitude, rate, and duration of stretch. Manipulate any of these = different power output. Higher rate of stretch = stronger spindle signal = greater efferent response.

Phase 2 — Amortization (Time to Rebound)

The transition from eccentric pre-stretch cessation to onset of concentric contraction. Also called: electromechanical delay phase. This is the key to plyometric performance. Shorter amortization = more efficient energy transfer = greater power. If amortization is too long: stored energy dissipates as heat, stretch reflex is not activated, concentric output is diminished. Primary goal of plyometric training: decrease time to rebound.

Phase 3 — Concentric Shortening (Power Output)

The resultant power production phase. Uses elastic recoil from SEC + PEC + the augmented concentric contraction from spindle activation. The SEC accounts for 70–75% of concentric force increase in plyometrics — making elastic energy storage the most critical mechanism. Elftman hierarchy: Eccentric > Isometric > Concentric (weakest alone). Plyometrics amplify concentric output by leveraging the pre-stretch.

Physiological & Neurophysiological Basis
Muscle Fiber Recruitment — Size Principle

ST (Type I) fibers recruited at sub-maximal intensity. FT IIa fibers recruited at ~30–80% maximal intensity. FT IIa + IIb at ~70–80%+ intensity. Plyometrics must be performed above 80% MVC to recruit fast-twitch fibers critical for power. FT fibers respond better to high-speed, small-amplitude pre-stretch. Quality of movement > quantity. If technique deteriorates, stop — fatigue has defeated the training purpose.

GTO Desensitization

The Golgi Tendon Organ (GTO) is a protective reflex that prevents over-contraction. One of the primary neurological goals of plyometric training is to desensitize the GTO — allowing muscles to generate higher forces without the protective inhibition firing. This increases neuromuscular coordination and makes movements more automatic (neural efficiency). The training effect on the nervous system predominates in weeks 1–8; hypertrophic changes begin after several additional weeks.

DOMS from Plyometrics — Patient Education

Eccentric contractions generate 10–40% more force than concentric → microtrauma to SEC/PEC → release of hydroxyproline → noxious stimulus = DOMS. DOMS will always occur in unaccustomed eccentric exercise. Resolves in 7–10 days. Trained athletes experience less due to repeated bout effect. Always educate patients starting plyometrics that DOMS is normal, expected, and self-limiting — not a sign of injury.

Contraindications & Pre-Training Requirements
Contraindications to Plyometrics
Absolute Contraindications

Pain · Active inflammation · Acute or sub-acute sprains · Acute or sub-acute strains · Joint instability · Soft tissue limitations from postoperative conditions. Most important: insufficient foundational strength or training base — if an athlete cannot meet minimum strength criteria, the coordination and motor control are not present to safely perform plyometrics.

Relative Contraindications (LE Specific)

Joint pathology (cartilage + ligament injury, arthritis, bone bruises, muscle-tendon injuries) until tissue healing has sufficiently occurred to tolerate repetitive, rapid, high compressive and shear forces. The clinician must judge tissue readiness — no hard timeline, requires clinical judgment.

Programming Variables & SAID Principle

▼
Training Variables to Manipulate

Neuromuscular overload: rapid change of direction, total reps/sets, ROM.
Spatial overload: exaggerated ROM → increased stretch reflex activation.
Temporal overload: executing movement as rapidly as possible — shorter amortization = more power.
Intensity: bilateral jump to box = lower; single-leg jump from box = higher.
Volume: total foot contacts per session.
Frequency: sessions per week.
Recovery: rest between sets (longer for heavy plyos) + 48–72 hrs between sessions.
Specificity: match movement patterns, angular velocities, metabolic demands to athlete's sport and position.

SAID Principle — Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands

The body adapts specifically to the demands placed on it. An overhead pitcher needs shoulder internal rotation power at 5,000–7,000°/sec. A soccer player needs single-leg power and lateral cutting. A PT can't replicate these demands with traditional resistance training alone — plyometrics provide the speed, load, and pattern specificity needed. Design plyometric programs to replicate the actual movement pattern, speed, and force demands of the patient's activity. Train each movement pattern in isolation first, then integrate back into whole-movement patterns.

Technique — The Non-Negotiable

Poor technique during plyometrics reinforces faulty motor patterns — possibly the same patterns that caused injury. Never allow bad form. Feedback: verbal, mirror, video. When technique deteriorates: stop immediately. Goal: increase volume while maintaining excellent technique. If athlete cannot hold form for the prescribed volume, reduce volume until they can. The quality of the movement IS the training stimulus.

Balance, Fall Prevention & Neuromotor Training
Falls are the leading cause of injury-related mortality in adults 65+. Every 15 minutes an older adult is treated in the ED for a fall. 1 in 3 Americans aged 65+ falls every year. The most important message: falls are largely preventable and are NOT a normal result of aging. PT-directed exercise programs reduce fall rates by 35–40%. This is one of PT's most evidence-based interventions.
Risk Factors for Falls
Patient Risk Factors

Age ≥65 years Fear of falling — may stop activities → deconditioning → more weakness → higher fall risk (self-fulfilling cycle)
Medications: 4+ medications = high fall risk; any med causing dizziness; medication changes increase risk; use one pharmacy; annual medication review Poor vision: annual eye exams after 50; multifocal lenses blur vision looking down at steps; cataracts → correcting them reduces fall risk Lower body weakness Poor balance Physical inactivity Chronic disease: DM, arthritis, stroke, Parkinson's, MS, depression, COPD, chronic pain

Environmental Hazards — Home Assessment

Loose throw rugs · poor lighting · slippery bathtubs/showers · clutter in walkways · loose electrical cords · unstable stools or chairs · hazardous steps or curbs without handrails. PT role: screen for these in discharge planning, home health, and patient education. Home modification reduces fall risk independently of exercise.

What Is Balance?
Definition + Sensory Systems

The ability to control and maintain the body's position as it moves through space. Requires coordination of 3 sensory systems: Visual (environmental reference), Somatosensory/Proprioceptive (body position, surface feedback), Vestibular (inner ear, head position and motion).
Balance is a skill — a learned motor pattern. Patients can keep it or relearn it by PRACTICING IT.

3 Motor Strategies for Postural Control

Ankle strategy: small perturbations; muscle activation distal → proximal (tibialis anterior/gastroc activate first).
Hip strategy: rapid or large perturbations; hip flexion/extension to return CoM over BoS; activation proximal → distal (hip flexors first).
Stepping strategy: when CoM moves outside BoS and ankle/hip are insufficient — rapid step to widen base. PT trains all three — matching strategy to perturbation magnitude.

Fear of Falling — Vicious Cycle

Fear → activity avoidance → loss of confidence → inactivity → muscle weakness → decreased proprioception → social isolation → depression → functional decline → higher fall risk → more fear. Breaking the cycle: graded exposure to balance challenges, SMART goals, success experiences, group programs for social support.

Evidence-Based Fall Prevention Programs
Otago Exercise Program

Series of 17 strength and balance exercises delivered by a PT in the home. Reduces falls 35–40% in frail older adults. Individualized, home-based, PT-directed. Exercises progress over time. Includes walking program. Evidence base: multiple RCTs (Campbell et al.). Appropriate for: frail, homebound older adults with fall history or high fall risk.

Matter of Balance

8-week structured group intervention. Includes exercises to increase strength and balance. Also has cognitive/educational component — addresses fear of falling, self-efficacy, behavioral change. Community-based setting. Lay-leader delivery model (not PT-directed but PT can facilitate). Evidence: reduces fear and fall rate in community-dwelling older adults.

Tai Chi

Traditional Chinese slow movement exercise program. 4 main styles: Yang (most common, large gentle movements), Chen, Wu, Sun. Does NOT include educational component. Evidence: reduces fall risk in community-dwelling older adults; improves balance, coordination, and lower-extremity strength. Safe for most older adults; recommended 2–3×/week. No equipment needed.

For patients who avoid activity due to fear: graded exposure hierarchy from safest (sitting balance tasks) → standing at counter → standing unsupported → stepping → walking. Build confidence through successful completion of challenging tasks. Patient-centered goal setting essential — what activities do they want to return to? Work backward from that.

Janda Syndromes — Upper & Lower Crossed
Vladimir Janda identified predictable patterns of muscle imbalance that develop from our modern posture and movement habits. These patterns create the most common presentations in PT: neck pain, shoulder dysfunction, low back pain, and hip problems. Understanding which muscles are tight and inhibited — and in what pattern — drives your entire exercise selection.
Upper Crossed Syndrome (UCS)

Common pattern in: desk workers, students, phone users, anyone with prolonged forward head/rounded shoulder posture. Results in cervical and thoracic dysfunction, shoulder impingement, and headaches.

Soft Tissue Mobilization
Soft tissue mobilization (STM) is a manual therapy intervention targeting muscles, fascia, tendons, ligaments, and scar tissue. It's adjunctive — it prepares tissue for exercise, reduces tone in hypertonic structures, and mobilizes scar. It does NOT replace exercise. Knowing when to use it, how to apply it, and when NOT to use it is as important as the technique itself.
Absolute Contraindications

STM is absolutely contraindicated in any of the following — do not proceed without physician clearance or alternative approach:

Bacterial infection at or near treatment site Malignancy (cancer) at treatment site Systemic or localized infection Sutures over the area (wait for wound healing) Recent fracture Cellulitis Febrile state (fever) Hematoma (organized blood clot — do not disperse) Acute circulatory condition (DVT, arterial insufficiency) Open wound at treatment site Osteomyelitis Advanced diabetes (impaired healing, neuropathy) Hypersensitivity of the skin Inappropriate end feel: spasm, empty, or bony Pain unrelieved by rest (suggests systemic/visceral origin)

Relative Contraindications — Proceed with Caution

Joint effusion or inflammation — avoid direct compression over distended joint; work proximal and distal Rheumatoid arthritis — avoid during flare; gentle grade I–II effleurage tolerated in remission Presence of neurological signs — proceed with extreme caution; neural tension may worsen with deep tissue work Osteoporosis — avoid compressive techniques; light effleurage only Hypermobility — STM to already-lax tissue can worsen instability; stabilization exercises priority Pregnancy — certain areas contraindicated (lumbar, sacral, medial ankle); use caution Dizziness — assess before cervical STM; may be vertebrobasilar insufficiency

Application Parameters

Type: effleurage, deep friction, myofascial release, pin-and-stretch, scar mobilization, trigger point release.
Duration: technique- and patient-specific; typically 5–15 min.
Force: progress from superficial to deep; always reassess.
Patient/client position: supported, relaxed, tissue accessible. Drape appropriately. Lotion or oil appropriate for most techniques except IASTM.

STM Techniques — What They Are and When to Use
Effleurage (Grades 1–3)

Long, gliding strokes from distal to proximal. Grade 1: very light, superficial — lymphatic and venous drainage, edema reduction, Phase 1 of healing. Grade 2: moderate pressure — increases circulation, reduces tone. Grade 3: deeper, sustained pressure — deeper muscle layers. Start here in acute phase as "appetizers" before any deeper technique.

Myofascial Release / Deep Technique

Sustained pressure with slow, progressive tissue engagement — follows tissue resistance (not force through it). Duration: 60–120 seconds per site until tissue release felt (softening, "give"). Used for fascial restrictions, chronic tightness, scar adhesions. Key: "meet and melt" — you wait for the tissue to release, not force it. Fibroblastic and remodeling phases.

Pin and Stretch with Oscillation

Therapist pins the soft tissue at a specific point (cross-fiber or along fiber) while the patient or therapist moves the joint through range — creating relative movement between the tissue and the pin point. Breaks adhesions, restores tissue extensibility, highly effective for muscle belly restrictions. The oscillation component adds neurophysiological input.

Scar Mobilization

After wound closure and epithelialization (typically 3+ weeks post-surgery or injury): direct pressure and multi-directional mobilization of scar tissue. Goals: restore tissue mobility, prevent adhesion formation between tissue planes, address hypersensitivity (desensitization), improve cosmesis. Patient should also perform self-massage between sessions.

Trigger Point Release

Sustained ischemic pressure on a hyperirritable spot within a taut band of skeletal muscle → reproduction of referred pain pattern → release. Hold until pain reduces 50–70%. Then gentle stretch. Address contributing factors (posture, repetitive strain, Janda patterns). Not appropriate over bone, nerve, or in absolute contraindication areas.

STM Clinical Reasoning — When to Use What
Acute Phase (Days 1–4): Light Only

Effleurage grades 1–3 for edema/pain management. Gentle MWM if tolerated. No deep tissue, no pin-and-stretch, no aggressive compression. Fibrin lattice is fragile — you cannot disrupt Phase 1 healing tissue with deep work. Light touch is therapeutic; deep pressure resets the inflammatory clock.

Fibroblastic Phase (Days 5–21): Graded Loading

Begin deeper soft tissue work — middle of range, fascial ranges. Soft tissue combined with joint mobilization. Lines of stress (load along collagen fiber direction). Grade III–IV joint mobs. Pin-and-stretch to begin restoring tissue extensibility. Soft tissue on tension AND off tension.

Remodeling + Maturation: Full STM Toolbox

Full myofascial release, deep technique, scar mobilization, trigger point release all appropriate. Increased pressure in specific tracts. Fascial consideration for the entire kinetic chain. By maturation phase: little to no manual therapy needed — the tissue has remodeled with appropriate loading; exercise is now the primary driver.

Summary Principle — STM Sets Up Exercise

Educate → Drape → Apply STM (with understanding of tissue properties) → Check in with patient → Educate again (why this, what you felt, what to expect). STM is not the treatment — it's the warm-up for the treatment (exercise). A patient who walks out of PT having only received passive manual therapy has been undertreated.

Functional Screening Tests
CKCUEST — Closed Kinetic Chain UE Stability Test

2 lines of tape 36 inches apart. Athlete in push-up position. One hand crosses body to touch opposite tape, returns. Count touches in 15 seconds. Average 3 trials. Normative: Males = 21 touches, Females = 23 touches. Pass = within 20% of normative data. Used as UE plyometric readiness criterion.

Vail Squat Test — LE Endurance + Mechanics

Repetitive single-leg squats × 3 minutes. Each minute: score 5 criteria (1 point each): (1) Knee flexion 30–60°; (2) No dynamic knee valgus; (3) No knee locking on extension; (4) Knee not past toe during flexion; (5) Upright trunk. Failure = 3 consecutive reps with uncorrected error OR unable to complete time = lose that point. Max = 15 points.

5-10-5 Pro Agility Test

Start at center cone. Sprint 5 yards to one side (hand touches line), sprint 10 yards to opposite side (hand touches), sprint back 5 yards through center. Time stops when athlete runs through center cone. 2 reps each direction; average. Compare to gender/age/sport norms or pre-injury level. Tests acceleration, deceleration, direction change — essential for cutting sports.

Knee-to-Wall Distance (Dorsiflexion)

Toes against wall → bend knee forward to touch wall. If achieved: move foot back 1–2 cm per attempt until heel lifts. Record distance when heel contact maintained. Compare side-to-side. Clinical relevance: restricted ankle dorsiflexion compensated by knee valgus, excessive trunk lean, or premature heel rise in squat — all increases ACL and knee overload risk.

Core Stability
The core is the lumbo-pelvic-hip (LPH) complex — the center of gravity and movement center of the body. Without adequate core control, every movement up and down the kinetic chain is compromised. Core stability is not just about abs — it's the coordinated activation of global (phasic, power) and local (tonic, stabilizing) muscles working synergistically to produce, reduce, and transfer force safely.
Core Anatomy — Global vs. Local Muscles
Global (Dynamic, Phasic, Power-Producing)

Larger torque-producing muscles that work in bursts (phasic activation). Drive movement, produce gross stability. Key global muscles: Rectus abdominis, external oblique, thoracic portion of lumbar iliocostalis. These are the muscles you see working in a sit-up or trunk rotation — high force output, but cannot maintain sustained stability alone.

Local (Postural, Tonic, Segmental Stabilizers)

Smaller, deep muscles that work continuously (tonic activation) to provide segmental stability and control inter-vertebral motion. Key local muscles: Lumbar multifidi, transverse abdominis (TA), psoas major, quadratus lumborum, lumbar parts of iliocostalis and longissimus, diaphragm, posterior fibers of internal obliques, pelvic floor. These are the "deep core" — what you activate with the TA brace. Without them, load is transferred directly to passive structures (discs, ligaments).

Diaphragm & Pelvic Floor — The Roof and Floor

The diaphragm is the roof of the core. Contraction increases intraabdominal pressure (IAP) — essential for spinal stability. Poor ventilation (weak diaphragm) = increased compressive force on the lumbar spine. The pelvic floor is the base. When diaphragm, TA, pelvic floor, and multifidi all co-contract, IAP rises and the spine is protected during loading. This is why breathing pattern and diaphragmatic control matter for every core exercise.

Psoas — The Critical Interface

Main structure that may inhibit core activation. In CKC (closed kinetic chain): psoas is a lumbar stabilizer. In OKC: it's a hip flexor. Reciprocal inhibition: tight or dominant psoas → inhibited gluteus maximus. Synergistic dominance during hip extension: when glutes are inhibited, hamstrings and lumbar erectors compensate → LBP. Psoas also synergizes with erector spinae, multifidi, and deep abdominal wall to balance anterior shear at the lumbar spine.

Core Function, Dysfunction & Assessment
Synergistic Kinetic Chain Function

Core: produces force, reduces force, dynamically stabilizes against abnormal force (e.g., getting pushed). Good posture = maximal neuromuscular efficiency: normal length-tension relationships, normal force-couple relationships, normal arthrokinematics. Tight hip flexors → weaker glutes and altered stability down the chain. Transformational zone (LPH complex): where compensation and pain commonly occur — needs control.

Dysfunction Cascade

Core inefficiency → inability to respond to demands → repetitive microtrauma → biomechanical fault → injury. Compensation from other muscles alters arthrokinematics → joint wear. Bad core = altered arthrokinematics throughout kinetic chain. Tightness in one area (e.g., tight hip flexors) creates imbalance → longer, weaker abs and glutes → tight erector spinae (LCS pattern). Strong, stable core = good impact on peripheral joints.

Core Assessment Battery

Endurance tests: Prone plank (bridge), side plank, abdominal curl (torso flexor), superman (torso extensor), single-leg squat.
Neuromotor endurance: Abdominal control blood pressure cuff test (checks TA activation); leg lowering — observe LB posture/stability during descent.
Strength and power: Straight-leg lowering with velocity; backwards overhead MB jump and throw (eccentric control).

Core Training Progression Principles

Slow → fast · Simple → complex · Stable → unstable · Low force → higher force · General → specific · Correct execution → increase intensity. Foundation: muscular activation of TA and multifidus with normal breathing must be mastered before any dynamic loading. Patient education essential — patient must understand and visualize deep core activation. Time under tension improves intramuscular coordination and static/dynamic stabilization.

Core Training Program Variables & Clinical Guidelines
Program Variation Variables

Plane of motion (sagittal → frontal → transverse) · ROM (partial → full) · Loading (BW → resistance) · Body position (supine → sitting → standing → single-leg) · Speed (slow → fast) · Amount of feedback (contact → verbal cue → no cue) · Duration and frequency. Challenge the core in all three planes — sagittal-plane-only training (crunches/planks alone) creates an incomplete program.

Guidelines for Core Training

Systematic, progressive, and functional. Begin with the most challenging task the patient can perform with correct technique. Provide a proprioceptive-rich environment — multiple ranges, speeds, loads. Add UE and LE movements to challenge the patient. Proximal stability before distal mobility — the core must stabilize before the extremities can move with power.

Core Stability → Rehab of Peripheral Joints

Strong stable core improves neuromuscular efficiency throughout the kinetic chain. Every extremity movement originates at the core. A patient with a weak core doing shoulder exercises is moving a proximal unstable platform — the shoulder cannot achieve optimal mechanics. Similarly, a patient with LCS doing knee strengthening cannot achieve proper hip mechanics during squat or lunge patterns. Assess and address the core in every orthopedic patient.

McGill Big 3 — Evidence-Based Core Protocol

Stuart McGill's research-supported minimally loading core stability exercises: (1) Modified curl-up (activates RA without flexion stress) · (2) Side plank (activates QL, obliques, TA with lateral stability) · (3) Bird dog (activates multifidi and contralateral EM with minimal disc compression). These three exercises minimize spinal load while maximizing stability activation. Appropriate for patients with disc pathology, acute LBP, and as a foundation for all core progressions.

Aerobic Training & Energy Systems
Aerobic exercise is one of the most powerful medicines available — reducing mortality from all causes, improving cardiovascular and respiratory function, reducing anxiety and depression, preventing falls, and managing chronic disease. ACSM guidelines are the clinical standard. Understanding energy systems tells you why your prescription choices produce different outcomes.
ACSM Aerobic Exercise Guidelines
Weekly Minimum Recommendations

Moderate intensity: 30–60 min/day, ≥150 min/week (5 days × 30 min).
Vigorous intensity: 20–60 min/day, ≥75 min/week (3 days × 25 min). Can be done continuously or in bouts of ≥10 minutes throughout the day.
Additional health benefits at 300 min/week moderate or 150 min/week vigorous.
Resistance training: minimum 2 days/week.
Volume target: ≥500–1,000 MET-min/week ≈ 1,000 kcal/week ≈ ~10,000 steps/day.

MET Levels — Intensity Classification

Light: 2.0–2.9 METs (slow walking, light housework).
Moderate: 3.0–5.9 METs (brisk walking, cycling <10 mph, water aerobics).
Vigorous: ≥6 METs (running, swimming, fast cycling, basketball). 1 MET = 3.5 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ oxygen consumption (resting metabolic rate). Key: older adults working at the same MET level as younger adults are working at a higher %VO2max — same absolute intensity = greater relative intensity with age.

Benefits of Aerobic Exercise

Increased maximal oxygen uptake (VO2max) Decreased HR and BP at given submaximal intensity Increased exercise threshold for angina / ischemia onset Increased capillary density in skeletal muscle Higher lactate threshold (can clear lactic acid faster) Decreased anxiety and depression Improved cognitive function Reduced fall risk in older adults Decreased all-cause mortality: 30% lower relative risk with high PA levels vs. sedentary Management of T2DM, HTN, obesity, osteoporosis, CAD, COPD

Energy Systems — The 3 Pathways
Phosphagen System — 0–30 Seconds

Anaerobic. Fuel: creatine phosphate (CP). Active at the start of ALL exercise regardless of intensity. Rapidly replenishes ATP — highest power output, lowest yield. Powers the initial burst of any movement: first step off the line, first rep of a max lift. Depleted in 10–30 seconds.

Glycolysis — 30–90 Seconds

Anaerobic. Fuel: glycogen (glucose). Powers moderate-to-high intensity, short-duration exercise. Slower than phosphagen but yields more ATP. Byproduct: lactate (not lactic acid — lactate is a fuel, not a waste product). The lactate threshold = %VO2max where blood lactate begins rising above resting = endurance performance predictor. Well-trained athletes have higher lactate thresholds.

Oxidative System — >90 Seconds

Aerobic. Requires O2. Fuel: carbohydrates (higher intensity) and fats (longer duration, lower intensity). Slowest system, highest ATP yield. Powers all sustained aerobic activity. Fat provides 9 kcal/g vs. carbohydrate's 4 kcal/g — fat is more energy-dense but slower to metabolize. All three systems are ALWAYS active simultaneously — dominance shifts with intensity and duration.

Key Performance Factors

VO2max: maximal oxygen uptake — ceiling of aerobic performance capacity.
Lactate threshold: higher threshold = better endurance performance.
Exercise economy: energy cost at a given velocity — higher economy = less energy for same speed.
Fuel source: efficiency using fat as primary fuel = better ultra-endurance performance.
Fiber type: high % Type I slow-twitch = endurance advantage.

Aerobic Training Types & Program Structure
Continuous Training

20–30 min sustained aerobic activity. Reach overload by increasing duration. Most effective for improving endurance in healthy individuals. Uses submaximal oxidative energy system. Example: 30-min run at steady pace. Progress: increase time before increasing intensity.

Cross Training

Different modes of training to maintain conditioning. Switches movement patterns — reduces overuse injury risk. Effective for athletes in off-season. Example: swim instead of run. Maintains cardiovascular fitness while allowing primary sport musculature to recover.

Interval Training

Activity alternating with prescribed rest periods. Good for improving VO2max and power vs. pure endurance training. Less demanding for deconditioned patients — allows high quality work with recovery. Work interval: longer = more aerobic stress. Rest interval: replenishes ATP; can be passive or active recovery; rest must be shorter than work. Rest-to-work ratio: 1:3 for aerobic emphasis; up to 1:10 for power development.

Detraining

Significant regression occurs within 2 weeks of stopping activity. Maintaining fitness is easier than improving it. Cardiorespiratory fitness declines faster than muscular fitness. For SNF/post-surgical patients: even maintaining minimal aerobic activity (walking) during recovery preserves substantially more fitness than complete rest.

Circuit Training

Series of exercises completed in order for a specified number of rounds. Trains multiple muscle groups. Stresses multiple energy systems simultaneously. Time-efficient. Effective for general conditioning, weight management, and functional training. Can blend resistance and aerobic exercise in the same session.

Session Structure (ACSM)

Warm-up: 5–10 min — increase muscle temp, dilate blood vessels, get HR within 20 beats of target.
Conditioning: 20–60 min — the workout.
Cool-down: 5–10 min — prevent blood pooling, prevent fainting, enhance recovery, prevent cardiac issues.
Stretching: 5–10 min. Always include all four components in a complete aerobic training session.

Ergonomics & Workplace PT
MSK injuries are the leading cause of workplace pain and disability — estimated $45–54 billion annually, 30% more lost work days, and 400,000 injuries per year. PT's role in ergonomics: education, equipment recommendations, and activity modification. The same rehab principles (Relieve, Restore, Return) apply to the occupational setting.
Ergonomic Injury Mechanisms
When Does MSK Injury Occur?

When mechanical workload > load-bearing capacity of the MSK system. Goal of ergonomic intervention: balance work requirements with workers' capacities. Not all jobs can be changed — but all workers can be trained and their workstations modified.

Occupational Risk Factors

Mechanical overload: magnitude of force applied to the body.
Duration of exposure: time + repetition frequency = repeated microtrauma accumulation.
Posture at work: how they perform tasks — sustained awkward positions are as damaging as high forces. The combination of high force + high repetition + awkward posture = highest MSK injury risk.

Types of Workplace Injuries

Acute: clear mechanism of injury (MOI), immediately painful, immediate limitation. Chronic/Cumulative: progressive onset, builds over time from repetitive microtrauma — often misattributed to "getting older" rather than occupational factors. Most common sites: low back, neck, shoulder, wrist/hand.

Intervention Approaches

Administrative controls: changing how the job is done — rotating workers, reducing exposure time, frequency, and intensity. Engineering controls: modifying the workstation physically — adjustable chairs, monitor stands, ergonomic keyboards, anti-fatigue mats, mechanical lifts. PT typically addresses both through education and workstation assessment.

Workstation Assessment & Exercise Design
Postural Assessment Protocol

Observe patient at their actual workspace. Common findings: rounded shoulders (UCS), forward head posture, elbow flexion from desk height, non-neutral spine, feet not supported. Identify 3 primary impairments, rank by importance. Common: (1) shortened pec minor, (2) weak cervical flexors, (3) weak abdominals/lower traps.

3 Workstation Adjustments for UCS Pattern

Monitor at eye level — eliminates cervical flexion from downward gaze; prevents levator scapulae and upper trap overactivation Lumbar support — restores lumbar lordosis; reduces posterior pelvic tilt and compensatory thoracic kyphosis Forearms supported (armrests or desk at correct height) — eliminates sustained shoulder elevation from unsupported arm weight Screen distance 18–24 inches; feet flat on floor; hips at 90°; elbows at ~90°

Heavy Load Lifting — Patient Education
What to Consider Before Lifting

Weight of object · Distance between body and load (lever arm — further = more spinal load) · Duration of activity · Repetitions performed. Load × distance from spine = moment arm. A 10 lb box held 2 feet away = same spinal stress as 20 lbs held 1 foot away.

Safe Lifting Principles

Lift close to body with both hands Avoid bending and rotation of trunk — use hip and knee flexion (hip hinge) Use equipment if available (cart, dolly, lift assist) Lift with a partner for heavy or awkward loads Avoid moving loads over obstacles Plan the path before lifting — no surprises mid-lift

High Force Exertion — Push/Pull & Sedentary Posture
Push/Pull Considerations

Direction of force · Distance from body · Posture during exertion · Distance covered by applied force. Principles: use both hands; avoid trunk rotation or lateral flexion during push/pull; keep force close to body; use assistive equipment. Push is safer than pull — pull creates spinal rotation forces. Pushing at waist height is biomechanically optimal.

Sedentary Behavior Risk

For average adults, 50% of waking hours involves prolonged sitting. Sedentary time independently associated with all-cause mortality, cancer, diabetes, CVD — even in people who exercise otherwise. 30% lower relative risk of all-cause mortality for high PA individuals. Recommendation: break sitting every 30–60 minutes with movement — even standing briefly or walking to the water cooler.

Occupational PT Solutions — 3 R's Applied

Relieve: ask for help; use lifts/devices; proper body mechanics education.
Restore: proper mechanics training; modify work environment; strengthen core and build muscular endurance.
Return: muscular endurance training matched to job demands; work simulation in clinic; graded return to full duties. Plus: drink more water, move more frequently, patient education on cumulative microtrauma.

ACSM resistance training prescription for hypertrophy vs. In this case, muscular endurance — load, volume, rest.
tap
Hypertrophy: Load = 70–85% 1RM (novice/intermediate), 70–100% (advanced); Volume = 1–3 sets × 8–12 reps (novice/int), 3–6 sets × 1–12 reps (advanced); Rest = 2–3 min heavy, 1–2 min lighter. Muscular Endurance: Load = <70% 1RM; Volume = 2–4 sets × 10–25 reps; Rest = 30 sec–1 min. The key difference: hypertrophy uses higher loads with moderate reps; endurance uses lower loads with higher reps and shorter rest (metabolic fatigue). Progression rule for both: increase load 2–10% when patient can perform 1–2 extra reps over goal on 2 consecutive sessions.
What is the amortization phase and why is it critical to plyometric performance in this scenario?
tap
The amortization phase is the transition time from cessation of eccentric pre-stretch to onset of concentric contraction — also called electromechanical delay or "time to rebound." It is the key to plyometric performance because: shorter amortization = more efficient elastic energy transfer from the SEC/PEC to concentric output = greater power. If amortization is too long, stored elastic energy dissipates as heat, the stretch reflex is not activated, and concentric power is reduced. Primary training goal: decrease time to rebound. This is why plyometrics must be performed with maximal intent and high intensity (>80% MVC) — slow, deliberate movements eliminate the SSC benefit entirely.

Name the LE pre-training strength requirements before starting a plyometric program?
tap
Before initiating LE plyometrics: (1) Full ROM bilaterally; (2) Strength base: full free-weight squat at 1.5–2.5× bodyweight AND/OR squat 60% bodyweight 5× in 5 seconds (Wathen criteria); (3) 30-second single-leg stance with eyes open AND closed (Voight & Tippett); (4) Single-leg half-squat with adequate control; (5) No pain at injury/surgery site; (6) Minimal to absent swelling. Most important overall contraindication: insufficient foundational strength — coordination and motor control are not present to safely perform plyometrics without adequate strength base. Adding power training on top of weakness = injury. Also: tissue healing must be respected — no plyometrics over actively inflamed joints or healing ligament/cartilage.

What is the LSI, what does it measure, and what is the passing threshold for return to sport in this scenario?
tap
Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) = (involved limb score ÷ uninvolved limb score) × 100. Measures the symmetry of performance between legs — how close the injured limb is to the healthy limb. Used primarily with the four functional hop tests: single-leg hop for distance, 6-meter timed hop, triple hop, and crossover hop. Passing threshold: ≥90% LSI for most return-to-sport decisions; some protocols require 100% for contact/collision sports. Must be combined with: isokinetic strength testing (>80% peak torque bilateral), clinical examination, and clinical judgment. An LSI of 90% means the involved limb performed at 90% of what the uninvolved limb did — this is considered adequate symmetry for most athletic activities.

tap

What is DOMS, why does it happen in plyometrics, and what do you tell your patient in this scenario?
tap
DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness) is post-exercise muscle soreness that peaks 24–72 hours after unaccustomed eccentric exercise. Mechanism: eccentric contractions generate 10–40% more force than concentric → microtrauma to the Series Elastic Component (SEC) and Parallel Elastic Component (PEC) of muscle-tendon unit → release of hydroxyproline → noxious stimulus → perceived soreness. DOMS will always occur in skeletal muscles with unaccustomed eccentric exercise. Highly trained athletes experience less due to the repeated bout effect (previous exposure reduces response). Patient education: "DOMS is normal, expected, and self-limiting — it is not an injury. It resolves in approximately 7–10 days. It indicates the training is working. Do not stop exercise because of it — continue at reduced intensity if very sore." This education is critical when starting plyometrics or any new eccentric loading program.
Identify the 4 muscle contraction types and the Elftman force-production hierarchy?
tap
Concentric: active shortening of muscle to produce force (e.g., upward phase of bicep curl). Eccentric: active lengthening while producing force — generates 10–40% more force than concentric; most injurious; DOMS source (e.g., downward phase of curl, landing from jump). Isometric: muscle produces force without changing length (e.g., wall sit, plank). Isotonic: muscle produces force while changing length — encompasses both concentric and eccentric. Elftman force-production hierarchy: Eccentric > Isometric > Concentric (weakest alone). This is why plyometrics — which use eccentric pre-stretch to amplify concentric output — generate the greatest power of any training modality. Plyometrics reverse the concentric weakness by leveraging stored elastic energy.
Lower Crossed Syndrome — which muscles are tight, which are weak, and the postural consequence in this scenario?
tap
Tight (facilitated/overactive): Rectus femoris, iliopsoas (hip flexors), lumbar extensors (erector spinae), thoracolumbar fascia. Weak (inhibited/underactive): Gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, gluteus minimus, abdominals (especially deep). Postural result: anterior pelvic tilt, increased lumbar lordosis, hip flexion bias, compensatory thoracic kyphosis. Clinical presentations: low back pain, hip impingement, SI joint dysfunction, poor gluteal activation, "butt wink" during squat, inability to hip hinge. Treatment order: release/stretch hip flexors and lumbar extensors FIRST, then activate glutes and abdominals. Strengthening weak muscles against tight antagonists creates co-contraction and reinforces dysfunction — order matters.
Priority of rehabilitation — list the 6 steps in order and explain why sequence matters.
tap
(1) Address reason for abnormal movement/dysfunction/pain — you cannot build strength on a dysfunctional pattern; identify the driver of impairment first. (2) Flexibility and ROM — adequate range is required before safely loading. (3) Strength and Endurance — once movement quality and range are present. (4) Balance and Coordination — neuromuscular control for functional tasks. (5) Function — task-specific training (stairs, gait, lifting, ADLs). (6) Performance — sport-specific, power, speed, agility. Why order matters: patients who skip steps get hurt. Loading a restricted joint damages cartilage. Strengthening without balance creates functional instability. Training performance before function produces athletes who perform in the gym but fail in their sport. This is also the sequence for clinical documentation — justify each phase transition.

In clinical practice, what does "driver" mean in exercise prescription and give 3 examples showing how it changes the exercise.
tap
The driver is the body part that receives power and transmits motion to other parts of the body — it determines primary muscle targeting and movement emphasis. Example 1 — Plank: Elbow driver (forearm plank) = anterior core stability focus. Knee driver with same-side rotation = oblique + hip flexor challenge. Foot driver (leg lift in plank) = posterior chain + gluteal activation. Same position, completely different muscles. Example 2 — Row: Elbow driver (pull elbows back) = rhomboids/middle trap emphasis. Shoulder blade driver (retract scapula first) = lower trap/serratus emphasis. Example 3 — Lunge: Anterior foot driver = quad-dominant, knee-flexion emphasis. Hip driver (push hip through) = glute-dominant, hip-extension emphasis. The driver is the most powerful single variable for changing what a familiar exercise targets — essential for tailoring exercise to specific impairments.
Plyometric progression levels — what exercises belong in each level and what determines advancement in this scenario?
tap
Level 1 (Beginner/Low): Weight-shifting ankle pops, stationary jumps, single jump in multiple planes, double-to-single leg hop, squat jumps, bilateral mini jumps, skipping. Level 2 (Intermediate/Medium): Stationary hops alternating legs (Heisman), jump and reach, lateral bounding, ankle bounces, shuffling/carioca, tuck jumps, jumping to box, zigzag jumps, in-place jumps. Core stability — what is the difference between global and local muscles, and why does it matter clinically in this scenario?
tap
Global (phasic, dynamic): larger torque-producing muscles that activate in bursts — rectus abdominis, external obliques, thoracic iliocostalis. Produce gross movement and high-force stability demands. Local (tonic, postural): smaller deep muscles that activate continuously to provide segmental stability — transverse abdominis, lumbar multifidi, diaphragm, pelvic floor, QL, psoas. Why it matters: you cannot train a stable core by doing sit-ups alone. Global muscles overpower local stabilizers when local control is deficient — like trying to stabilize a tent with ropes but no center pole. Foundation: TA and multifidi co-contraction with normal breathing must be mastered before any dynamic loading. Plank training activates both global (rectus) and local (TA); McGill Big 3 (curl-up, side plank, bird dog) provides the evidence-based minimal-load core stabilization foundation.
Three energy systems — name each, its fuel source, and its time domain.
tap
(1) Phosphagen system — fuel: creatine phosphate; time: 0–30 seconds; anaerobic; highest power output, lowest yield; active at start of ALL exercise; powers explosive bursts. (2) Glycolysis — fuel: glycogen; time: 30–90 seconds; anaerobic; moderate intensity short-duration; byproduct = lactate (which is a fuel, not a waste product); lactate threshold training improves performance. (3) Oxidative system — fuel: carbohydrates (higher intensity) and fats (longer duration); time: >90 seconds; aerobic; requires O2; slowest, highest yield. All three are always active simultaneously — dominance shifts based on intensity and duration. Clinical application: plyometrics and max strength training use phosphagen; HIIT uses glycolysis + oxidative; endurance training is purely oxidative. This determines rest periods between sets.
Ergonomics — when does MSK injury occur and what are the three types of workplace intervention in this scenario?
tap
MSK injury occurs when mechanical workload > load-bearing capacity of the MSK system. Risk factors that combine to create highest risk: high force + high repetition + awkward posture. Acute injury: clear MOI, immediate pain. Chronic/cumulative: progressive from repeated microtrauma — often misattributed to aging. Three intervention types: (1) Administrative controls — change how the job is done: worker rotation, reduce exposure time and frequency, job task modification. (2) Engineering controls — modify the workstation: adjustable chair/desk, monitor stand, mechanical lifts, ergonomic tools. (3) PT interventions — education, exercise program for identified impairments, activity modification training, body mechanics instruction. PT's role: workstation assessment → identify 3 postural impairments → modify workstation → prescribe exercise with dosage and rationale for each impairment.
