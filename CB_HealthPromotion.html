Health Promotion — The PT's Role in Prevention

Physical therapists are movement scientists and health educators — not just injury fixers. The leading causes of death and disability in the US are preventable lifestyle conditions: heart disease, obesity, diabetes, stroke, cancer. PTs see patients over extended periods, build genuine rapport, and have the clinical skills to address physical activity, nutrition, stress, and behavior change. This module covers wellness theory, behavior change models, nutrition, obesity, motivational interviewing, stress, substance use, and the evidence for exercise as medicine — all from the PT perspective.

Health Promotion Foundations
Health is not merely the absence of disease — it is a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being (WHO). The leading causes of death and disability in the US are lifestyle conditions: ischemic heart disease, stroke, obesity, diabetes, cancer, and smoking-related illness. These are preventable. PTs are uniquely positioned to lead the assault on lifestyle conditions because of their prolonged patient contact, movement science expertise, and patient trust. This is your scope — own it.
The PT's Role in Health Promotion
Why PTs Are Uniquely Positioned

PTs interact with patients over extended periods across multiple visits — a pattern of care that no other healthcare provider replicates. This creates the "teachable moment": an opportunity to address health behaviors, screen for lifestyle risks, and empower patients toward lasting change. APTA Vision: "Transforming society by optimizing movement to improve the human experience." Promoting health and wellness is not optional — it is core to the profession.

Core PT Health Promotion Competencies (Dean, 2009)
  • Smoking assessment and cessation counseling initiation
  • Basic nutritional assessment and counseling
  • Physical activity and exercise recommendations
  • Stress assessment and basic stress reduction recommendations
  • Sleep assessment and basic sleep hygiene recommendations
  • Referral to appropriate specialists when beyond PT scope
The Crisis of Lifestyle Conditions

Sedentary behaviors are linked to at least 300,000 cases of premature mortality and more than $90 billion in direct medical costs annually in the US alone. Less than 5% of Americans meet the minimum recommendation of 30 minutes of moderate physical activity per day. Only 8% of adolescents and 45% of children meet recommendations for 60 minutes per day. The US ranks 22nd out of 23 industrialized nations in "healthy" life expectancy — despite spending twice as much on healthcare as any other industrialized nation.

Epidemiology — How Population Data Drives PT Practice

Incidence vs PrevalenceDeterminants of HealthLifestyle conditionsPT consultation role▼
Epidemiology Defined

The study of the distribution and determinants of health, disease, morbidity, injuries, disability, and mortality in populations — applied to control of health problems. Epidemiology is concerned with the health of population groups, not individuals. This distinguishes epidemiological descriptions from clinical descriptions of diseases. Incidence and prevalence are its two core metrics.

Incidence vs. Prevalence

Incidence: number of NEW health events in a population exposed to that risk in a given time period. Most useful in acute disease study. Example: new flu cases per 1000 people exposed this season. Prevalence: TOTAL number of existing cases of a condition at a designated time. Most useful in chronic disease study. Example: total people living with diabetes in the US right now. PT relevance: prevalence drives your patient population; incidence tells you which conditions are emerging.

Determinants of Health — Why People Get Sick
  • Policymaking: taxes, seatbelt laws, smoking in public places, FDA regulations
  • Social factors: where we live, income level, school quality, public safety
  • Physical environment: natural/built environments, exposure to chemicals, walkability
  • Health services: access, quality, geographic availability
  • Individual behavior: lifestyle choices — the most modifiable category
  • Biology and genetics: age, BRCA1, sickle cell — least modifiable
The PT Consultation Role

"The physical therapist's expertise is needed in a consultation role to legislators, community planners, businesses, schools, and other public and private institutions particularly where a large number of people, and secondarily their families, will be reached through public health messages." (Dean, 2009) This extends PT practice far beyond the clinic — into policy, workplace wellness, school programs, and community health initiatives.

Three Levels of Intervention Required

Individual and Family: personal behavior change — exercise, nutrition, stress management, smoking cessation. Most PT interventions target this level. Community (including employers): creating environments that support healthy choices — workplace wellness programs, community exercise facilities, safe walking environments. National: government policies, laws, and programs that structure the incentive landscape — tax policy, labeling requirements, health insurance incentives. PT's lesson: individual counseling alone is insufficient if the environment undermines the behavior you're trying to promote. Consider the full picture.

The Three-Legged Stool of Overall Health

Herbert Benson's model: optimal health requires balance among three legs — (1) Surgery and procedures (invasive medical care), (2) Pharmaceuticals (medications), and (3) Self-care (lifestyle behaviors). The stool is unstable if any leg is too short or too long. The US healthcare system over-invests in the first two legs and dramatically underinvests in self-care. PTs are uniquely positioned to strengthen the self-care leg for their patients.

Holistic Health & The Mind-Body Connection

Whole person — body/mind/spiritOxidative stress + agingRelaxation responsePrevention > treatment▼
Holistic Medicine Defined

"The art and science of healing that addresses the whole person — body, mind, and spirit." Integrates conventional and alternative medicine. Emphasis on promoting optimal health. Searches for underlying cause, not just symptoms. Prevention is preferable to treatment. Patient autonomy is central — the patient is an active partner, not a passive recipient. Examples: acupuncture, herbal remedies, meditation, massage, nutrition therapy, yoga, tai chi, biofeedback, essential oils.

Oxidative Stress & Free Radicals — Accelerating Aging

Oxidative stress = imbalance between free radicals and antioxidants. Natural physiologic process occurring during respiration — forms unstable oxygen particles ("free radicals"). Damage caused by free radicals promotes: chronic disease (diabetes, CAD, hypertension, atherosclerosis) and accelerated aging. Three factors accelerate the aging process: oxidative stress, chronic inflammation, and chronic psychological stress. Aerobic exercise stimulates improved antioxidant defenses. Antioxidant foods: fruits and vegetables, whole grains, nuts and seeds.

The Relaxation Response (Benson)

"A physical state of deep rest that changes the physical and emotional responses to stress — and the opposite of the fight or flight response." Technique: concentrate and focus the mind on a single word and block all competing thoughts. Effects: decreases fatigue, pain, BP, and stress hormone levels. Improves sleep, motivation, productivity, and decision-making ability. Clinically relevant for every patient dealing with chronic pain, anxiety, or high stress loads — teach it as part of your plan of care.

Meditation

"A wakeful hypo-metabolic physiologic state." Oxygen consumption decreases during meditation, indicating low metabolic demand — deep rest at the cellular level. Many forms: transcendental meditation, mindfulness meditation, body scan, loving-kindness. Clears the mind, re-energizes, reduces cortisol levels. Evidence for meditation in: chronic pain reduction, anxiety management, blood pressure reduction, improved sleep quality. PT can recommend and briefly teach basic meditation techniques.

Mind Over Matter — The Pain-Cognition Link

Stanford University research: cognitive strategies can meaningfully reduce pain perception. Pre- and post-surgical education (explaining the procedure, what to expect, techniques to direct attention away from pain) significantly reduces pain scores and analgesic use. Hypnosis has demonstrated ability to alter vital signs. The mind IS a clinical tool — use it. Patient education, expectation-setting, and teaching attention-redirection techniques are legitimate PT interventions.

Behavior Change Models
Understanding why patients do or don't follow through on health behaviors is not guesswork — there are tested theoretical models that explain it precisely. These models help you identify WHERE a patient is in their readiness to change, WHY they're stuck, and WHICH intervention strategy will actually move them forward. Using the wrong strategy for the wrong stage is why well-intentioned advice fails.

Transtheoretical Model (TTM) — Stages of Change (Prochaska, 1979)

Most widely used health behavior model6 stages, nonlinearMatch intervention to stageMost patients are pre-action▼
Core Premise

Behavior change is a process that unfolds over time through a series of stages — not an event. Most intervention programs are action-oriented (expecting patients to just start doing something), but the majority of at-risk people are in precontemplation or contemplation. Critical assumption: specific processes should be applied at specific stages. Giving an action-stage intervention to a precontemplation patient is why advice gets ignored. The model integrates across addiction, eating, exercise, smoking cessation, and virtually any health behavior.

The 6 Stages
  • Precontemplation: Not convinced of the importance of change; no intention to act in next 6 months; behavior change not on the person's radar. "I don't have a problem."
  • Contemplation: No definite plan but intends to change within next 6 months; constantly weighs pros/cons but doesn't act; profound ambivalence. "I know I should, but..."
  • Preparation: Intends to take action within next 30 days and has taken some behavioral steps. "I signed up for a gym."
  • Action: Engaged in making changes for less than 6 months. "I've been exercising 3x/week."
  • Maintenance: Working to integrate behavior change into daily life for more than 6 months; distress is normal. "I've been active for 8 months."
  • Termination: No longer succumbs to temptation; total self-efficacy. Most relevant for addictive behaviors.
Stage-Matched PT Interventions
  • Precontemplation: Give more information about value of change; use persuasion; target perceived ability to access resources. Do NOT lecture or prescribe — they will resist.
  • Contemplation: Set a date for change; point out costs and benefits; involve family and friends; improve resource accessibility. Decisional balance exercise is powerful here.
  • Preparation: Develop a plan; set STG and LTG; establish rewards; time management skills; progressive program design. Now is when exercise prescription applies.
  • Action: Adjust to new lifestyle; manage unexpected emotional and physical reactions; goal setting; rewards; problem-solve barriers.
  • Maintenance: Continue paying attention; help through any relapse; vary behaviors (cross-train); expand social support; cultivate intrinsic motivation.
Decisional Balance

To progress from precontemplation to action, pros of changing must increase ~1.0 SD AND cons must decrease ~0.5 SD (based on 12 studies). This means: emphasize increasing the BENEFITS twice as much as reducing the costs. Ask: "What are the good things about [current behavior]?" then "What are the less good things?" After each cost identified, ask "How does this affect you?" or "Does this concern you?" — this is more powerful than confronting costs directly.

Health Belief Model (HBM) — Hochbaum et al. (1950s)

Perceived susceptibilityPerceived barriers = strongest predictorSelf-efficacy added 1988Fear-based but effective▼
Core Constructs
  • Perceived susceptibility: One's subjective perception of the risk of contracting a health condition. "Do I think I could get diabetes?" The more vulnerable a person feels, the more likely they are to act.
  • Perceived severity/seriousness: How serious the consequences of the disease are. "If I get diabetes, is it a big deal?"
  • Perceived threat: Combination of susceptibility and severity — the combined sense of danger that motivates action.
  • Perceived benefits: Belief in the efficacy of the advised action to reduce risk. "Will exercising actually prevent diabetes?"
  • Perceived barriers: Negative aspects of the health action; tangible and psychological costs. "I don't have time to exercise" = most powerful barrier.
  • Cues to action: Bodily events (symptoms) and environmental events (news stories, physician advice) that trigger action.
  • Self-efficacy: Conviction one can successfully execute the required behavior. Added in 1988 when HBM was applied to lifestyle behaviors requiring long-term change.
What Research Shows About Predictors

Perceived barriers = the single most important predictor across ALL studies and behaviors — more important than any other construct. Clinical implication: your first priority is identifying and addressing barriers, not explaining why exercise is good (they already know). Perceived susceptibility = stronger predictor of PREVENTIVE behavior than sick-role behavior. Perceived severity = least powerful predictor overall, but strongly related to sick-role behavior (once already sick, severity drives adherence). Perceived benefits = stronger predictor of sick-role behavior than preventive.

PT Application — Physical Activity in Latina Women

Qualitative study with 143 women, ages 40–79. Perceived benefits (health promotion, physical fitness, improved roles) and barriers (time constraints of women's roles, personal health, internal and external factors) function as competing elements explaining physical inactivity. Main issue = barriers; women identified benefits but still didn't exercise. Lesson: to increase physical activity, emphasize overcoming barriers and promote perceived benefits. Address the real-world constraints, not the abstract concept of health.

Social Cognitive Theory (SCT) — Bandura (1986)

Self-efficacy = central constructReciprocal determinismObservational learningMost robust behavior change theory▼
Core Premise

Behavior change is affected by interactions among the environment, personal factors (including cognitions), and attributes of the behavior itself — called reciprocal determinism. None of these operates in isolation; they constantly influence each other. A person who believes they can exercise (personal), has a park nearby (environment), and enjoys the activity (behavior factor) is far more likely to persist.

Self-Efficacy — The Most Important Prerequisite

Bandura proposed that self-efficacy is the single most important prerequisite for behavior change — it affects how much effort is invested and the level of performance attained. Three domains: (1) having tacit task knowledge and skills, (2) having an explicit sense of confidence in mobilizing motivation and cognitive resources, (3) having confidence in one's ability to execute successfully in a specific context. Self-efficacy is behavior-specific — a patient may have high self-efficacy for walking but low for swimming. Must be assessed for each target behavior.

Four Ways to Build Self-Efficacy
  • Performance accomplishments (most powerful): Repeated successful experiences. Design activities patient CAN succeed at initially — build the ladder of mastery incrementally. A patient who successfully completes 5 sit-to-stands gains confidence for 10.
  • Vicarious experience (observational learning): Watching others like themselves succeed. Patient support groups, peer role models. "If she can do it with a knee replacement, so can I."
  • Social and verbal persuasion: Encouragement from credible sources. ASAP language principle: "Active people like you usually do very well on this task." Counts for less than the first two but still matters.
  • Physiological and affective states: Ensuring correct interpretation of internal states. Teach patient that the sweating and heart rate increase during exercise is normal and healthy — not a warning sign.
Observational Learning — Modeling

People learn by watching others' behaviors and their outcomes. PT demonstration matters — showing the exercise is more effective than describing it. Also: group therapy settings allow patients to observe peers succeeding. Sharing patient success stories (with permission) is a legitimate SCT-based clinical tool. "I have another patient with a similar knee who is back to hiking after 3 months" — this is not empty encouragement, it is evidence-based vicarious learning.

Theory of Reasoned Action / Theory of Planned Behavior (TRA/TPB)

Most widely used social cognitive modelIntention → BehaviorAttitude strongest predictorPerceived behavioral control = TPB addition▼
Core Premise

Behavioral intention is the immediate determinant of behavior. All other factors that influence behavior are mediated THROUGH intention. TRA (Fishbein, 1967): intention is determined by (1) attitude toward the behavior + (2) subjective norms (social influence). TPB (Ajzen, 1986/1991): adds a third determinant — perceived behavioral control (how easy or difficult it is to perform the behavior). People are more likely to perform a behavior when they believe it is under their control.

Research Findings on Exercise (Downs & Hausenblas, 2005)
  • TRA/TPB can predict exercise intention AND behavior
  • People's intention is the strongest determinant of exercise behavior
  • Attitude most strongly influences intention — if a patient doesn't believe exercise will help them, they won't intend to do it, and they won't do it
  • Intention is a stronger predictor of exercise behavior than perceived behavioral control
  • Influence of significant others has less impact than attitude and perceived control
Cardiac Rehab Application

Study of 215 Phase II cardiac rehabilitation patients (mean age 59.52). Intention was found to be the SOLE determinant of exercise adherence. Attitude, perceived behavioral control, and subjective norm all significantly influenced intention. Clinical lesson: build the intention first. Attitude change comes from providing compelling evidence that exercise will achieve outcomes the patient values. Don't just prescribe — convince first, then prescribe.

Subjective Norms — Social Influence Matters

Normative beliefs about whether key people (spouse, family, physician, friends) approve or disapprove of the behavior + the patient's motivation to comply with those people. "My wife wants me to exercise" increases intention. "My friends think the gym is not for people like me" decreases it. PT clinical tool: identify key influencers in the patient's life and, where possible, include them in the plan. Family education is not optional — it is behavior change science.

Diffusion of Innovation Theory (Rogers, 1962) & Ecological Model

Population-level changeEarly adopters → laggardsEcological = multilevelPolicy + environment + individual▼
Diffusion of Innovation

Explains how ideas, behaviors, or innovations spread through populations over time. Aims to maximize exposure and reach of successful interventions. Used for: family planning, adoption of new medical technologies, use of new drugs, school health education programs, public health campaigns. Relevant for PTs who want to scale health promotion beyond individual patients — how do you get an entire community to adopt regular physical activity? Same principles as marketing — early adopters lead, mass adoption follows, laggards convert last.

Ecological Model — Why Individual Counseling Alone Fails

Health behavior exists in an ecological context: Personal factors (psychological, biological, developmental) → Social factors (family, friends, coworkers, neighbors) → Institutional factors (workplaces, schools, healthcare facilities) → Community factors (organization and availability of resources) → Public policy (tax incentives, laws, insurance rates). Some environments promote healthy behaviors and others restrict them. Interventions on multiple levels simultaneously are required for population-level change. PT can influence all levels — patient, family, employer, community advocate, policy voice.

Motivational Interviewing in PT Practice
Motivational Interviewing (MI) is an evidence-based, patient-centered counseling approach for facilitating behavior change by helping patients explore and resolve ambivalence. It is not advice-giving — it is a collaborative conversation that elicits the patient's own reasons to change. Less than 35% of PT students receive formal MI training, yet approximately 50% of Americans do not adhere to prescribed plans of care. These two facts are connected.
What Motivational Interviewing Is (and Isn't)
Definition

"A directive, client-centered counseling style for eliciting behavior change by helping clients explore and resolve ambivalence." Developed by Miller and Rollnick (1991) from Carl Rogers' client-centered approach. First described in literature in 1983. Originally developed for addictive behaviors — now used across all health behaviors. Key principle: motivation to change is ELICITED from the client — it is NOT imposed. The examination and resolution of ambivalence is its central purpose.

The Adherence Problem

~50% of Americans do not adhere to plans of care prescribed by an "expert" provider. Non-adherence estimates: 20–40% in acute illness, 30–60% in chronic illness, 80% in preventive care. The paternalistic approach ("do what I tell you") produces these results. Ideal patient education promotes a collaborative relationship where motivation comes from the patient rather than the clinician. PT sessions are a "teachable moment" — patients are motivated by their injury to make broader changes. Use that window.

Five Core Principles of MI
1. Express Empathy

Reflective listening — understand the patient's perspective without judgment. Ambivalence is normal and not a sign of weakness or stubbornness. "I understand that finding time to exercise is genuinely difficult with your work schedule."

2. Develop Discrepancy

Help the patient see the gap between their current behavior and their stated values or goals. "You mentioned you want to be around for your grandchildren. How does your current activity level align with that goal?" The patient, not the clinician, voices the discrepancy.

3. Roll With Resistance

Avoid arguing. Resistance is a signal to change approach. "You may be right that exercise isn't for everyone. What do you think might work for you?" Arguing creates reactance — the more you push, the more they resist.

4. Support Self-Efficacy

Reinforce the patient's belief they are capable of change. Past success stories, acknowledging small wins. "You managed your pain without medication for 2 weeks — that shows real discipline."

5. Avoid Argument; Respect Autonomy

Patients have the right to choose their own path. "It's ultimately your decision. What I can do is share what the evidence shows." Respecting autonomy paradoxically INCREASES the likelihood of behavior change.

MI Techniques — The OARS Framework & Practical Tools

Open questionsAffirmationsReflectionsSummariesChange talk▼
Open-Ended Questions (at least 50% of questions)

Open questions invite the patient to think and share rather than giving a yes/no answer. Goal: get the patient talking about WHY they might want to change, not why they can't. "What are some of the changes you've noticed since your knee started bothering you?" "What matters most to you about your recovery?" "What have you already tried?" Closed questions close conversations; open questions open them.

Affirmations — Affirm the Client's Interpretation

Acknowledge the patient's strengths, efforts, and values — but not before you know how the client feels about the behavior. Affirm what the client has said, not a generic positive statement. "You've been dealing with this pain for months and you're still showing up — that says something about your commitment." Affirmations build self-efficacy and therapeutic alliance simultaneously.

Reflective Listening — The Core Skill

Think of the question you want to ask → guess how the client might answer → say your answer out loud as a statement. Do not let your voice go up at the end (not a question — a reflection). "It sounds like you're worried that exercise might make things worse." This technique validates the patient's experience, builds rapport, and often prompts the patient to clarify or correct — moving toward self-exploration. Things that masquerade as reflective listening but aren't: advice disguised as questions ("Have you tried...?"), leading questions, premature solutions.

Summaries — Collect and Redirect

Periodically summarize what the patient has said to demonstrate you've listened AND to gently amplify the change talk (reasons to change) relative to sustain talk (reasons not to). "So on one hand, you're concerned about the time commitment and your past injuries. On the other hand, you've told me you want to be active at your grandchild's wedding and you know being stronger will help your pain. Have I got that right?" The summary ends with invitation to correct or elaborate.

Importance & Confidence Questions

"How important is it that you change your ___?" (1–10 scale). "How confident are you that you could change your ___?" (1–10 scale). Clinical use: if importance is low ← work on the discrepancy, explore values, provide information. If importance is high but confidence is low ← work on self-efficacy, break tasks into smaller steps, celebrate small wins. Both must be addressed for action to follow intention. Asking "Why not a lower number?" after they rate themselves elicits their own reasons for change — more powerful than your reasons.

The One Thing to Remember

"The goal is to have the client say OUT LOUD their reasons to change." The goal is for the client to demonstrate the motivation to change. The client uses the clinician's motivational interviewing to: experience their ambivalence, increase their motivation to change, directly face the difficult decision, and resolve ambivalence in favor of the health-promoting choice. When patients say it in their own words, they own it. You saying it doesn't matter — them saying it does.

Values Clarification & Decisional Balance in PT Practice

Identify what matters to the patientDecisional balance = pros + consAgenda-setting chart▼
Values Clarification

Help the patient identify what they truly value — then connect their health behaviors to those values. Common values: family, spirituality, independence, health, discipline, responsibility, relationships, career, financial security. "What are the most important things in your life right now?" "How does your current health affect those priorities?" The discrepancy between current behavior and stated values is the internal motivation that MI leverages — you don't need to impose external motivation when internal motivation is awakened.

Agenda-Setting Chart

Useful when there are several behavior change targets — gives the client ownership of which issue to address first. Uses illustrations and text representing issues related to the behavior or condition (e.g., heart disease risk factors). Blanks for the client to fill in other issues of importance. Let the client SELECT the issue of greatest importance — never impose the priority. This creates buy-in from the first conversation.

Decisional Balance Exercise

Guide patient through a structured exploration: "What are some of the GOOD THINGS about [current problem behavior]?" (Let them say it — don't react negatively). Then: "What are some of the LESS GOOD THINGS about [current behavior]?" After each less-good thing: "How does this affect you?" or "Does this concern you?" This draws out the internal conflict rather than having the clinician impose it. Why this works: when people articulate their own concerns, they are far more likely to act on them than when someone else articulates the concerns for them.

Clinician Behaviors Consistent With MI
  • Seeking to understand the person's frame of reference, particularly via reflective listening
  • Expressing acceptance and affirmation (not judgment)
  • Eliciting and selectively reinforcing client's own self-motivational statements
  • Monitoring client's degree of readiness to change — not jumping ahead
  • Affirming client's freedom of choice and self-direction
  • Looking in the same direction as the client (collaboration), not at the client (fixing)
Obesity, BMI & Weight Management
Obesity is both a disease and a primary risk factor for nearly every condition PTs treat. It drives musculoskeletal breakdown (knee, hip, spine), metabolic disease (T2DM, HTN, hyperlipidemia, cardiovascular disease), pulmonary dysfunction (sleep apnea, reduced lung volumes), and inflammatory conditions. It also profoundly affects exercise tolerance, rehab outcomes, and discharge planning. Understanding obesity pharmacologically, physiologically, and behaviorally is essential clinical knowledge.

BMI Limitations

BMI does not measure body fat directly. It can misclassify muscular individuals as overweight. A better clinical picture combines BMI with: waist circumference (males >102 cm, females >88 cm = high risk), body fat percentage, functional capacity, and metabolic lab values (fasting BG, HbA1c, lipid panel). For your SNF patients — BMI 35+ with multiple comorbidities requires documentation of equipment weight limits and modified positioning strategies.

Obesity Statistics & Why They Matter for PT
Current Landscape

Obesity trends have climbed consistently for decades. Over 40% of US adults are obese. Childhood obesity has tripled in the last 40 years. The US spends over $147 billion annually on obesity-related medical costs. Obesity directly causes or exacerbates: T2DM, HTN, CAD, OSA, OA, GERD, fatty liver disease, depression, musculoskeletal pain, and multiple cancers. Most of your patients are affected.

The Musculoskeletal-Obesity Link

Every pound of body weight = approximately 3 lbs of force at the knee per step. A 50-lb weight loss reduces the cumulative load on the knee joint by millions of pounds per mile walked. Obesity accelerates cartilage degradation not only mechanically but also through inflammatory pathways — adipose tissue secretes pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) that drive OA progression even in non-weight-bearing joints. For your knee and hip OA patients, weight management is a primary therapeutic intervention, not an afterthought.

Obesity & Functional Capacity in PT

Obesity reduces: aerobic capacity (VO2max reduced per kg lean body mass), ventilatory efficiency (reduced FRC, ERV in supine), exercise tolerance, balance (altered COG, reduced proprioception from plantar loading), and wound healing (adipose tissue is poorly vascularized). Obesity increases: fall risk, surgical complication rates, delayed discharge, medication dosing complexity, and equipment needs. Document these functional impacts — they justify intensity modifications in your plan of care.

Obesity Treatment — Lifestyle, Medical & Surgical Approaches

Lifestyle = first-line5% weight loss = significant health gainsBariatric surgery PT considerationsExercise + diet = best combo▼
Lifestyle Intervention — First-Line Treatment

Combination of caloric restriction + increased physical activity + behavioral modification. Even modest weight loss matters: 5–10% of body weight produces clinically significant improvements in BG, BP, lipids, sleep apnea, and joint pain. This is achievable and should be the first target. ACSM recommendation: 150–250 min/week of moderate-intensity exercise for weight loss maintenance. More is better — 250–300 min/week produces greater loss. Caloric deficit of 500–1000 kcal/day produces ~1–2 lb/week weight loss.

Exercise Type for Obesity

Aerobic exercise: most effective for caloric expenditure and metabolic health. Resistance training: builds lean mass → increases basal metabolic rate → more calories burned at rest. Combination is superior to either alone. Aquatic exercise: excellent for severe obesity — reduces joint loading while allowing significant aerobic work; improves compliance due to pain reduction. Walking: most accessible, sustainable, evidence-strong. Reduce sedentary time independently of exercise time.

Medical & Pharmacological Management

GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide/Ozempic/Wegovy, liraglutide/Saxenda): significant weight loss (15–20% body weight in trials); approved for obesity management. Side effects: nausea, GI upset, injection site reactions. PT relevance: patients may have reduced appetite and energy — monitor for inadequate caloric intake before strenuous exercise. Orlistat (Xenical): blocks fat absorption; GI side effects. Phentermine-topiramate: appetite suppressant; CNS effects including dizziness (fall risk).

Bariatric Surgery — PT Before and After

Pre-surgical PT: optimize functional capacity before surgery — better pre-op function predicts better post-op outcomes. Assess mobility, educate on post-surgical exercise. Post-surgical PT: early mobilization critical. Progressive walking program. Weight-bearing restrictions may apply (especially with total hip/knee alongside bariatric surgery). Watch for nutritional deficiencies (B12, iron, calcium, vitamin D) that affect muscle function, bone density, and nerve health. Monitor for dumping syndrome. Aquatic therapy often ideal early post-surgery for morbidly obese patients still significantly overweight.

Nutrition, Dietary Guidelines & Supplements
Nutrition is arguably the most powerful lifestyle medicine available, yet most PTs receive minimal formal training in it. You don't need to be a dietitian to understand foundational dietary principles, recognize malnutrition, and counsel patients toward evidence-based eating patterns. Poor nutrition underlies obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, wound healing failure, and muscle loss — conditions you treat daily. Know the basics cold.

2015–2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans — The Evidence Base

Follow a healthy eating patternAdded sugars <10% caloriesSaturated fat <10% caloriesSodium <2300 mg/day▼
The 5 Guidelines
  • 1. Follow a healthy eating pattern across the lifespan. All food and beverage choices matter. A healthy eating pattern at the right calorie level helps achieve and maintain healthy weight.
  • 2. Focus on variety, nutrient density, and amount. Choose a variety of nutrient-dense foods across all food groups within recommended amounts.
  • 3. Limit calories from added sugars and saturated fats; reduce sodium. Cut back on foods higher in these components.
  • 4. Shift to healthier food and beverage choices. Replace typical choices with more nutrient-dense options.
  • 5. Support healthy eating patterns for all. Everyone has a role in supporting healthy choices at every level — individual, family, community, national.
Key Quantitative Limits
  • Added sugars: <10% of daily calories — at 2000 kcal, this is <50g/day. Average American exceeds this significantly (mainly from soda, pastries, candy).
  • Saturated fats: <10% of daily calories — replace with unsaturated fats ← reduces cardiovascular risk. Sources: butter, fatty meat, full-fat dairy, coconut/palm oil.
  • Sodium: <2300 mg/day — most Americans consume >3400 mg/day. Reduction is critical for HTN management — directly relevant to PT patients on antihypertensives.
  • Alcohol: ≤1 drink/day women; ≤2 drinks/day men. Not recommended to start drinking.
A Healthy Eating Pattern Includes
  • A variety of vegetables from all subgroups: dark green, red/orange, legumes, starchy, and others
  • Fruits — especially whole fruits (not juice)
  • Grains — at least half as whole grains
  • Fat-free or low-fat dairy (milk, yogurt, cheese, or fortified soy)
  • Variety of protein foods: seafood, lean meats and poultry, eggs, legumes, nuts, seeds, soy
  • Oils (liquid at room temperature preferred over solid fats)
Nutrient-Dense vs. Empty Calorie Foods

Nutrient-dense: provide vitamins, minerals, and other beneficial substances without added solid fats, sugars, refined starches, or sodium. All vegetables, fruits, whole grains, seafood, eggs, beans, unsalted nuts, fat-free/low-fat dairy, and lean meats when minimally processed. Empty calories: provide calories with little nutritional value — soda, candy, chips, white bread, alcohol. The average American's diet is heavily skewed toward empty calories — this is what your patients are eating, and it directly affects their recovery.

Macronutrients, Micronutrients & PT-Relevant Deficiencies

Protein = muscle healingCalcium + D = bone healthPotassium underconsumedIron deficiency = fatigue▼
Protein — Most Important for PT Patients

Required for muscle repair, immune function, wound healing, enzyme production. Recommended: 0.8 g/kg/day (sedentary); 1.2–1.7 g/kg/day (active); 1.2–2.0 g/kg/day (post-surgical, elderly, wound healing). Inadequate protein → sarcopenia, impaired healing, prolonged recovery. Sources: meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, soy, nuts. Elderly SNF patients are frequently protein-deficient — document and refer to dietitian when protein intake is clearly inadequate for healing demands.

Calcium & Vitamin D — Bone Health

Calcium: 1000–1200 mg/day for adults (food first: dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, sardines). Vitamin D: 600–800 IU/day; most Americans are deficient. D3 (cholecalciferol) from sun exposure + fortified foods + supplement. Vitamin D deficiency → impaired calcium absorption → bone loss → fracture risk. Critical for osteoporosis patients and post-fracture rehab. Corticosteroid use (your COPD/rheumatology patients) depletes both — flag these patients for supplementation discussion.

Underconsumed Nutrients of Public Health Concern
  • Potassium: most Americans severely under-consume. Sources: bananas, avocado, sweet potato, white potato, white beans, salmon, spinach. Critical for BP management and preventing hypokalemia from diuretics.
  • Dietary Fiber: goal 25 g/day (women) to 38 g/day (men). Average consumption ~15 g/day. Sources: whole grains, legumes, vegetables, fruits. Fiber → BG regulation, cholesterol reduction, bowel regularity, satiety.
  • Iron: deficiency → anemia → fatigue, weakness, reduced exercise tolerance. Common in premenopausal women, vegans, elderly. Symptoms look like deconditioning — check hemoglobin if unexplained fatigue.
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: EPA and DHA from fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines). Anti-inflammatory; cardioprotective; neurological health. Most Americans deficient.
Overconsumed Dietary Components — The Real Problem

Added sugars: soda, pastries, candy, flavored yogurt, breakfast cereals — primary drivers of obesity, T2DM, metabolic syndrome, and dental disease. Sodium: processed/packaged foods are responsible for ~70% of dietary sodium — not the salt shaker. HTN patients need this perspective. Saturated fat: found in butter, full-fat dairy, fatty red meat, tropical oils — drives LDL elevation and cardiovascular risk. Refined grains: white bread, white rice, pastries — spiked BG without fiber to slow absorption.

Supplements, Herbal Remedies & the PT's Role

Self-medication is commonNutrigenomics = gene-diet interactionFDA does not regulate supplementsEducation not endorsement▼
The Supplement Reality

The supplement industry is a multi-billion dollar market with minimal FDA oversight. Supplements are NOT required to prove safety or efficacy before being sold — only manufacturers cannot claim to "cure" a disease. Many supplements have genuine evidence (omega-3s, vitamin D, magnesium, creatine). Many have limited or no evidence. Some interact with medications. Your role as a PT: educate yourself and your patients about the evidence, avoid endorsing unproven products, and screen for potentially dangerous interactions (e.g., fish oil + warfarin = increased bleeding risk).

Nutrigenomics — The Gene-Food Interface

The study of interactions between an individual's genome and their nutritional environment. Key insight: genes are not destiny — nutrition modifies the EXTENT to which different genes are expressed. The presence of a genetic variant merely connotes a predisposition; whether that predisposition manifests as disease depends on diet, lifestyle, and environment. Example: MTHFR gene variant → reduced ability to use folate → elevated homocysteine → cardiovascular and neural tube defect risk — but can be significantly modified by adequate folate and riboflavin intake. Precision nutrition is an emerging field — the future is personalized dietary recommendations based on genetic profile.

Exercise Is Medicine & Physical Activity Promotion
Harold Elrick MD first declared "Exercise is Medicine" in 1996. The ACSM has since built a global initiative around this premise. The evidence is unambiguous: regular physical activity is the single most powerful intervention for preventing and managing chronic disease, disability, and premature death. PTs are movement scientists — exercise prescription is not peripheral to your practice. It IS your practice.
Physical Activity Guidelines — Adult Recommendations
ACSM / HHS 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines
  • Aerobic activity: 150–300 min/week moderate-intensity OR 75–150 min/week vigorous-intensity OR equivalent combination
  • Muscle-strengthening: ≥2 days/week all major muscle groups
  • Older adults: add multicomponent balance training ≥3 days/week
  • Children 6–17: 60 min/day moderate-vigorous activity; vigorous ≥3 days/week; muscle/bone strengthening ≥3 days/week
  • Key insight: less than 5% of Americans meet minimum adult recommendations; only 45% of children meet recommendations for 60 min/day
PAR-Q — Physical Activity Readiness Questionnaire

Standard pre-exercise screening tool for ages 15–69 (over 69 → consult physician before starting). 7 YES/NO questions screening for cardiac symptoms, chest pain, dizziness, bone/joint problems, BP/heart medications, and other contraindications. One or more YES: talk with physician before starting. All NO: cleared to start physical activity (slowly and progressively). Valid for 12 months. Reassess if health changes. BP >144/94 at screening → physician consultation before starting. Use this as your clinical tool before prescribing any exercise program.

Stress, Mind-Body Skills & Breathing
Hans Selye MD coined "stress" in 1951. Chronic stress is not just unpleasant — it is physiologically destructive. Elevated cortisol and adrenaline over time impairs immune function, accelerates aging, drives inflammation, disrupts sleep, and worsens virtually every condition PTs treat. Teaching stress management techniques is a legitimate, evidence-based PT intervention that directly improves patient outcomes.
The Physiology of Stress
What Chronic Stress Does to the Body

Increased sympathetic NS activity → outpouring of adrenaline (epinephrine) and cortisol and other stress-related hormones. Short-term: survival function. Chronic: impairs immune system resistance (linked to poverty, bereavement, depression). #1 stressor = job stress. Stress escalated in children, teens, college students, and elderly. Associated with exposure to crime, violence, peer pressure.

Health Consequences of Chronic Stress
  • Impaired immune function → ↑infection susceptibility, ↓wound healing
  • Elevated cortisol → ↑blood glucose, ↑BP, weight gain (especially visceral fat)
  • Sleep disruption → cascades into pain sensitivity, cognitive impairment, mood disorders
  • Musculoskeletal: muscle tension, trigger points, myofascial pain, postural changes
  • Cardiovascular: ↑HR, ↑BP, ↑platelet aggregation → ↑MI and stroke risk
  • GI: IBS exacerbation, ulcer risk, bowel dysfunction
  • Mental health: anxiety, depression, burnout — depression is the #1 cause of disability worldwide
Breathing Techniques — PT's Direct Tool
Proper Breathing (Diaphragmatic)
  • Uses the diaphragm as the primary respiratory muscle
  • Inhale and exhale through nose at rest
  • Full deep breaths with complete exhalation
  • Normal adult RR: 12–18 breaths/min
  • Allows good air exchange in alveoli
  • Activates parasympathetic NS → reduces stress response
Compromised Breathing Patterns

Postural breathing (kyphosis/forward head → restricted diaphragm expansion). Shallow breathing (only upper chest). Mouth breathing (bypasses nasal filtering, humidification). Accessory muscle use (SCM, scalenes, traps — sign of chronic respiratory or stress-related dysfunction). Breath holding (common with pain and exertion). Limited air exchange → stagnant air in lung bases → ↓oxygenation. PTs treat the MSK and postural components that drive these patterns.

Breathing Technique Benefits in PT
  • Improve awareness of breathing pattern
  • Regain control when short of breath
  • Pain control during painful procedures or exercises
  • More effective breaths = better air exchange
  • Pursed lip breathing: slows exhalation, improves gas exchange in COPD
  • Diaphragmatic breathing: parasympathetic activation, stress reduction

Mind-Body Skills & Relaxation Techniques

Relaxation responseMeditationMindfulnessClinical evidence▼
The Relaxation Response (Benson)

"A physical state of deep rest that changes the physical and emotional responses to stress." Technique: concentrate and focus the mind on a single word (or phrase, breath, image) and block all competing thoughts. Achievable in 10–20 minutes. Effects: decreases fatigue, pain, BP, and stress hormone (cortisol) levels. Improves sleep, motivation, productivity, and decision-making. Directly opposes the fight-or-flight response at the neurobiological level. Evidence-based: validated in clinical populations including chronic pain, cardiac, and cancer patients. Teach it in 5 minutes. It costs nothing. It has no side effects. Use it.

Meditation — Hypo-Metabolic State

"A wakeful hypo-metabolic physiologic state." Oxygen consumption decreases during meditation — indicating low demand at the cellular level. Multiple forms: transcendental meditation, mindfulness, body scan, loving-kindness, visualization. Clear the mind, re-energize, reduce cortisol. Evidence: BP reduction, anxiety reduction, chronic pain management, immune function improvement, sleep quality. Meditation apps (Calm, Headspace, Insight Timer) make practice accessible — recommend as a home program.

Mind-Body Skills for Clinical Education

Teaching mind-body skills to students and patients produces: better connections with others and expression of feelings, improved self-awareness, skills applicable to daily situations, stress relief that practitioners can also use personally, reduced burnout in healthcare providers. Clinical application: include 2–5 minutes of breathing or mindfulness instruction in sessions for patients with chronic pain, high anxiety, or poor adherence. Studies show mind-body intervention in medical education improves empathy, resilience, and engagement — directly applicable to PT student and clinician wellbeing as well.

Stress & Chronic Pain — The Connection

Chronic stress and chronic pain share overlapping neural pathways — HPA axis dysregulation drives both. Elevated cortisol → increased inflammatory cytokines → increased peripheral and central sensitization → pain amplification. The brain areas activated by physical pain and by social/psychological pain are largely the same (anterior cingulate cortex). Catastrophizing (a cognitive stress response) is the strongest psychological predictor of chronic pain outcomes — stronger than the original structural finding. Addressing stress in your chronic pain patients is addressing their pain, not ignoring it.

Substance Use, Tobacco & the PT Perspective
Tobacco, alcohol, opioids, and illicit drugs directly affect PT patients' rehabilitation outcomes, pain management, functional capacity, and safety. PTs are not addiction counselors, but they are frontline healthcare providers who interact with patients affected by substance use more than almost any other clinician. Know the clinical relevance, know your role, and know when to refer.
Tobacco — Biggest Preventable Cause of Death
Statistics

19% of US adults currently smoke cigarettes. 88% of adult daily smokers started before age 18. Nearly 4,000 people under 18 smoke their first cigarette every day in the US. ~400,000 young people become daily smokers each year. 4.3% of middle school students smoke. Factors in youth tobacco use: social norms in media, family and peer examples, anxiety/stress/depression, socioeconomic status.

PT Clinical Impact of Tobacco Use
  • Carbon monoxide displaces O2 from hemoglobin → ↓tissue oxygenation → impaired healing
  • Nicotine causes vasoconstriction → ↓circulation to healing tissues and peripheral extremities
  • ↑CO2, ↓FEV1, reduced exercise tolerance
  • Increased fracture non-union risk (critical in post-surgical and fracture rehab)
  • Worsens COPD, asthma, cardiovascular disease — conditions driving PT referrals
  • Wound healing delays (especially skin grafts, flap surgeries)
PT Role in Smoking Cessation

PT has the professional competency (per Dean 2009) to perform basic smoking assessment and initiate cessation counseling. This does not mean being a cessation specialist — it means asking about tobacco use at every encounter, informing patients of the clinical impact on their specific condition, and referring to cessation resources (nicotine replacement therapy, bupropion/Wellbutrin, varenicline/Chantix, state quitlines). Exercise itself reduces nicotine cravings and withdrawal symptoms — your exercise program IS a cessation tool.

Alcohol & Opioids — PT Awareness
Alcohol — Prevalence & PT Impact

54.9% of full-time college students ages 18–22 drank in the past month. 7.1 million youth (12–20) reported drinking in the past month. Alcohol effects relevant to PT: impaired balance and coordination (fall risk), impaired judgment (safety), peripheral neuropathy (chronic heavy use), liver disease (drug clearance impairment), worsened sleep quality, depression exacerbation, medication interactions (especially with opioids, benzodiazepines, blood thinners). Dietary Guidelines: ≤1 drink/day women, ≤2 drinks/day men.

Opioid Crisis — PT at the Epicenter

91.8 million US adults (37.8%) used prescription opioids in 2015. 11.5 million (4.7%) misused them. 1.9 million had an opioid use disorder. Among those misusing: 59.9% obtained opioids without a prescription; 40.8% obtained from friends or relatives. PT has been positioned as a primary alternative to opioids for musculoskeletal pain — a role the profession has embraced. "Physical therapy is evidence-based, effective, and non-addictive for pain management" — this is your clinical argument for referrals and patient education.

Cannabis — The PT Conversation

11.8% of 8th graders reported marijuana use in the past year (2019). Per APTA's position: while no PT should advise use of any medication, PTs should be able to educate patients on reported benefits (chronic pain, spasticity, chemotherapy-induced N&V) as well as potential adverse effects (cognitive/memory impairment, psychomotor impairment, fall risk, drug interactions). Balance PT: should screen for cannabis use in patients at fall risk or operating machinery. Legalization increases clinician responsibility to understand the evidence, not avoid the conversation.

Drug Education & Prevention — PT's Community Role
Prevention Programs Work

Drug education and prevention programs operate at three levels: Community-based (neighborhood programs, faith communities, recreation centers), School-based (curriculum integration, skill-building), Workplace-based (employee assistance programs, wellness initiatives). PTs practicing in these settings have a legitimate role in prevention programming. Diffusion of Innovation Theory explains how prevention messages spread — community champions and early adopters accelerate uptake.

Exercise as Prevention & Treatment

Physical activity is an evidence-based intervention for: reducing substance use cravings (exercise activates the same dopamine reward pathways as substances — but without the addictive hijacking), reducing alcohol use in college populations, reducing cigarette cravings during withdrawal, managing anxiety and depression (the most common co-occurring disorders with substance use). The PT's therapeutic exercise program is a direct substitute for the neurological reward that substances provide. This is not metaphorical — it is neuroscience.

What are the 6 Stages of Change in the Transtheoretical Model, and what intervention is matched to each?
tap
1. Precontemplation (no intention to change in 6 months): provide information, use persuasion, target attitudes. 2. Contemplation (intends to change in 6 months, still ambivalent): set a date, explore costs and benefits, involve family. 3. Preparation (intends to act in 30 days): develop a plan, set STG/LTG, establish rewards, progressive program. 4. Action (making changes <6 months): adjust to new lifestyle, manage reactions, goal setting. 5. Maintenance (>6 months): vary behaviors (cross-train), work through relapse, expand support. 6. Termination: set new goals, no longer tempted. Key: most intervention programs are action-oriented but most at-risk people are in precontemplation or contemplation — match the intervention to the stage.
Which HBM construct is the single strongest predictor of health behavior change across all studies? What does this mean clinically?
tap
Perceived barriers — the single most powerful predictor in the Health Belief Model across ALL studies and ALL behaviors. More important than perceived severity, susceptibility, or benefits. Clinical implication: your first priority when a patient isn't adhering is to identify and address their actual barriers — not to explain why exercise is good (they already know). "I don't have time." "It hurts." "I don't know how." "My family doesn't support it." — these barriers, addressed practically and collaboratively, produce more behavior change than any amount of health education. Perceived severity is the weakest predictor overall but is strongly related to sick-role behavior (once already ill).
What is self-efficacy, and what are the four sources for building it? Which is most powerful?
tap
Self-efficacy = a person's confidence in their ability to perform a specific behavior and overcome barriers to it. Bandura proposed it as the single most important prerequisite for behavior change. Four sources (most to least powerful): 1. Performance accomplishments (most powerful) — repeated successful experiences; design tasks the patient CAN succeed at initially. 2. Vicarious experience — watching similar others succeed; peer groups, patient success stories. 3. Social/verbal persuasion — credible encouragement from PT, family; "Active people like you do very well on this task." 4. Physiological/affective states — correct interpretation of physical sensations (sweating/HR increase = healthy exertion, not danger). Self-efficacy is behavior-specific — must be assessed and built for EACH target behavior.
Define Motivational Interviewing. What is its central goal, and what is the "one thing to remember"?
tap
MI: "A directive, client-centered counseling style for eliciting behavior change by helping clients explore and resolve ambivalence." Developed by Miller and Rollnick (1991). Key principle: motivation to change is ELICITED from the client — not imposed. Examination and resolution of ambivalence is its central purpose. The one thing to remember: "The goal is to have the client say OUT LOUD their own reasons to change." When patients articulate their own reasons, they own them — and are far more likely to act. You saying "you should exercise" means nothing. Them saying "I need to exercise because I want to be at my daughter's wedding" means everything. ~50% of Americans don't adhere to prescribed plans because the paternalistic approach doesn't work. MI addresses this directly.
What are the 5 principles of Motivational Interviewing?
tap
1. Express Empathy: reflective listening, no judgment — ambivalence is normal. 2. Develop Discrepancy: help the patient see the gap between current behavior and stated values/goals — patient, not clinician, voices the discrepancy. 3. Roll with Resistance: never argue; resistance signals you need to change approach. "You may be right — what do you think might work for you?" 4. Support Self-Efficacy: reinforce belief they are capable; recognize past successes; build the ladder incrementally. 5. Avoid Argument; Respect Autonomy: patients have the right to choose their own path. Respecting autonomy paradoxically INCREASES likelihood of behavior change — people do what they feel free to choose, not what they're told. Elements of MI: collaboration toward client goals, respect for autonomy, curiosity about client ideas.

What BMI cutoffs define overweight, obese, and morbidly obese? How does obesity affect PT practice specifically?

What is the PAR-Q, what are its 7 screening questions about, and what do you do with the results?
tap
PAR-Q (Physical Activity Readiness Questionnaire): Standard pre-exercise health screening tool for ages 15–69. Valid for 12 months. 7 questions screen for: (1) heart condition requiring physician-supervised activity, (2) chest pain with physical activity, (3) chest pain at rest in past month, (4) balance loss due to dizziness or loss of consciousness, (5) bone or joint problem that could be worsened by exercise, (6) currently on BP or heart medications, (7) any other reason not to be active. If BP >144/94 at screening → physician consultation before starting. Use this before prescribing any new exercise program in community/wellness settings.

What is the relaxation response, and how does it differ physiologically from the stress response?
tap
Relaxation response (Benson): "A physical state of deep rest that changes the physical and emotional responses to stress — the opposite of the fight-or-flight response." Technique: concentrate and focus the mind on a single word while blocking competing thoughts. Achievable in 10–20 minutes. Physiological effects: decreases fatigue, pain, BP, and cortisol levels; improves sleep, motivation, productivity, decision-making. Contrast with stress response: stress → ↑adrenaline, ↑cortisol, ↑HR, ↑BP, ↑glucose, impaired immunity, muscle tension. Relaxation response → exactly the opposite: ↓HR, ↓BP, ↓cortisol, ↓oxygen consumption (meditation reduces O2 consumption — "wakeful hypo-metabolic state"). Clinical use: teach in 5 minutes, assign as home program, evidence-based for chronic pain, HTN, anxiety, and sleep disorders. No side effects, no cost.
What is the Theory of Reasoned Action, and what does research show is the strongest predictor of exercise behavior?
tap
TRA (Fishbein, 1967): Behavioral intention is the immediate determinant of behavior. Intention is determined by: (1) Attitude toward the behavior (personal evaluation — "do I think exercising will benefit me?") + (2) Subjective norm (social influence — "do significant others want me to exercise?"). TPB adds perceived behavioral control (how easy/difficult to perform). Research findings (Downs & Hausenblas, 2005): Intention is the strongest determinant of exercise behavior. Attitude most strongly influences intention — stronger than subjective norms or perceived behavioral control. Attitude and PBC both uniquely predict exercise intention, with attitude the strongest. Clinical implication: Before writing the exercise prescription, address the patient's attitude toward exercise — do they believe it will actually help THEIR specific condition? If not, no amount of prescription will produce adherence. Change the attitude first.
How does the Ecological Model explain why individual counseling alone is often insufficient for behavior change?
tap
The Ecological Model recognizes that health behavior exists in nested layers of influence: Personal factors (psychology, biology) → Social factors (family, friends, coworkers) → Institutional factors (workplaces, schools, clinics) → Community factors (resource availability, built environment) → Public policy (laws, taxes, insurance). Some environments promote healthy behaviors; others restrict them. A patient who genuinely wants to exercise but lives in a neighborhood without sidewalks, works 60 hours/week, and can't afford a gym will fail with individual counseling alone — the environment defeats the intention. Effective interventions must work on multiple levels simultaneously. PTs can influence the patient (individual counseling), the family (education), the community (advocacy), and policy (professional voice). Individual behavior change advice alone produces the lowest long-term success rates of any intervention level.
What is the PT's specific role regarding tobacco use and smoking cessation?
tap
Per Dean (2009), PTs have a professional competency to perform smoking assessment and initiate cessation counseling — not as a cessation specialist but as a frontline healthcare provider. Clinical steps: (1) Ask about tobacco use at every encounter (make it routine, like vitals). (2) Inform patients of the specific clinical impact on their condition — e.g., smoking impairs fracture healing (vasoconstriction reduces circulation to healing bone), delays wound healing, worsens COPD and exercise tolerance. (3) Refer to cessation resources: state quitlines (1-800-QUIT-NOW), nicotine replacement therapy (patch, gum, lozenge), bupropion (Wellbutrin), varenicline (Chantix). (4) Prescribe exercise — aerobic exercise reduces nicotine cravings and withdrawal symptoms (this is documented in the literature). 88% of adult daily smokers started before age 18 — youth are the prevention target. Your exercise program IS a cessation tool.
What is nutrigenomics and why is it relevant to clinical PT practice?
tap
Nutrigenomics: the study of interactions between an individual's genome and their nutritional environment. Core insight: genes are not destiny — nutrition modifies the extent to which genes are expressed, and thereby modulates whether individuals attain the potential established by their genetic background. Key point: the presence of a genetic variant merely connotes a PREDISPOSITION; whether that predisposition manifests as disease depends on the diet, lifestyle, and environment. Classic example: MTHFR C677T gene variant → reduced capacity to convert folate to usable form → elevated homocysteine → increased cardiovascular risk and neural tube defect risk — but this risk can be significantly modified by adequate folate and riboflavin intake. PT relevance: informs why the same dietary advice produces different outcomes in different patients. Precision nutrition (personalized dietary recommendations based on genetic profile) is the future. For now: encourage dietary variety, evidence-based eating patterns, and refer to registered dietitians for complex nutrition cases.
What are the Importance and Confidence questions used in MI? How do you interpret low vs. high ratings?
tap
Importance question: "On a scale of 1–10, how important is it that you change your [behavior]?" Confidence question: "On a scale of 1–10, how confident are you that you could change your [behavior]?" Interpretation and response: Low importance (1–4): work on discrepancy — explore their values and how the behavior conflicts with what matters to them. Provide information. Don't push action yet. Moderate importance (5–7): explore ambivalence — what would need to change to make it more important? Decisional balance exercise. High importance but low confidence: work on self-efficacy — break tasks into achievable steps, identify past successes, address specific barriers. High importance AND high confidence: time to plan and act. Key technique: After they rate themselves, ask "Why not a lower number?" — this elicits their own articulated reasons for change, which are more motivating than any reason you could give them.
What is the "three-legged stool" model of overall health, and where does PT fit in it?
tap
Herbert Benson's three-legged stool: Optimal health requires balance among three legs: 1. Surgery and procedures (invasive medical care — what gets most healthcare resources). 2. Pharmaceuticals (medications — second largest share of healthcare spending). 3. Self-care (lifestyle behaviors: exercise, nutrition, stress management, sleep, social connection — dramatically underfunded and underemphasized). The stool is unstable if any leg is too long or too short. The US healthcare system is stool with two very long legs and one short one — over-invested in invasive care and medications, dramatically under-invested in self-care. PT's role: the self-care leg. PT exists to optimize movement, restore function, and empower patients to manage their own health through active lifestyle behaviors. As PT practices shift toward primary prevention and wellness programming, the profession is explicitly filling this gap in the healthcare ecosystem.
What are the six dimensions of wellness? Give one clinical example of how PT touches each dimension.
tap
1. Physical: Exercise prescription, pain management, manual therapy — the most obvious PT domain. 2. Emotional: Providing encouragement during setbacks, teaching pain coping strategies, relaxation techniques — PT improves mood via exercise and recovery progress. 3. Social: Group exercise sessions, community reintegration planning, family education — PT addresses isolation and helps patients return to social participation. 4. Intellectual: Patient education about their condition, home exercise program instruction, helping patients understand anatomy and healing — PT engages the patient's intellect in their own care. 5. Spiritual: Goal-setting around what matters most to the patient ("I want to walk my daughter down the aisle"), values clarification in MI — PT aligns interventions with patient meaning and purpose. 6. Environmental: Home safety assessment, recommending assistive devices, ergonomic recommendations, community accessibility advocacy — PT modifies the physical environment to support health and function. A skilled PT touches all six every visit.
How does chronic stress impair PT outcomes, and what techniques can PT prescribe to address it?
tap
Chronic stress impairs PT outcomes through: ↑Cortisol → ↑inflammation → ↑pain sensitivity (chronic stress and chronic pain share overlapping neural pathways). Impaired immune function → delayed wound healing and increased infection risk. Sleep disruption → ↓tissue repair, ↓cognitive function, ↓motivation for therapy. Muscle tension and trigger points → painful movement, guarding, compensation patterns. Catastrophizing (a stress-driven cognitive pattern) is the strongest psychological predictor of chronic pain outcomes — stronger than structural findings on MRI. PT techniques to address stress: (1) Diaphragmatic breathing — teach in 5 minutes, activates parasympathetic NS. (2) Relaxation response — focused attention reduces cortisol. (3) Progressive muscle relaxation — tension/release cycle. (4) Aerobic exercise — most powerful stress reducer; ↓cortisol, ↑endorphins, ↑BDNF, ↑serotonin. (5) Sleep hygiene education. Addressing stress in your chronic pain patients IS addressing their pain.
What is the decisional balance technique in MI? How should you weight benefits vs. costs?
tap
Decisional balance: A structured exploration of the pros and cons of changing behavior. Steps: (1) "What are some of the GOOD THINGS about [current problem behavior]?" — let them answer without judgment. (2) "What are some of the LESS GOOD THINGS about [current behavior]?" — after each: "How does this affect you?" or "Does this concern you?" This elicits the patient's own articulation of concerns, which is more powerful than you naming them. Evidence-based weighting (from 12 studies): To progress from precontemplation to action, pros must increase ~1.0 SD AND cons must decrease ~0.5 SD. This means: emphasize INCREASING THE BENEFITS twice as much as reducing the costs. Don't just address barriers — amplify the reasons TO change more than you minimize the reasons NOT to change. The math matters: benefits must grow faster than barriers shrink.
