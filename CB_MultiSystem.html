Surgical Concepts & Organ Transplant
Cardiovascular and pulmonary complications are the major cause of perioperative morbidity and mortality. PT has a role in pre-operative assessment and training, post-operative mobilization, and preventing complications. Risk factors: age, smoking, nutrition, weight, exercise, sleep, stress, and cardiac/lung/MSK/neuromuscular/endocrine dysfunction.

Perioperative Care — Pre-Op Through Post-Op

▼
Pre-Operative PT Role

Assessment: Full functional assessment if seeing patient for first time. Respiratory function (breathing pattern, chest wall deformities, accessory muscle use). Circulatory status (venous/arterial insufficiency, neuropathy). Review surgeon's notes and comorbidities that could lead to post-op issues. Pre-op training: Diaphragmatic breathing, lateral costal/segmental breathing, coughing (huffing technique), positioning, UE/LE exercises. Educate patient on what will be expected from PT after surgery — answer questions ONLY if you can.

Factors Contributing to Cardiopulmonary Risk

Anesthesia & supplemental O₂: Depresses diaphragm and intercostal muscles → ↓ TLC, ↓ FRC (further reduced in supine), ↓ residual volume, ↓ lung compliance, ↑ work of breathing.
Pharmacology: Patient response variability to analgesics. Widespread systemic effects: reduced arousal, CVP depression, GI depression, muscle relaxation — all compromise O₂ transport. Exaggerated response in patients with respiratory depression and hypotension.
Other factors: Type/duration of surgery, static body positioning, incisions, dressings/binders, splints/fixation, lines/leads, monitoring devices, chest tubes, catheters, anxiety, pain control, fluid balance, blood/plasma transfusions.

Post-Op PT Goals

Prevention of complications: atelectasis (dressings, binders, inability to hyperventilate), DVT, deconditioning. Positioning: Changed frequently — can the patient assist? Transfers and ambulation encouraged shortly after surgery. Monitor for reduced arousal (trauma, anesthesia, sedation, fluid loss, increased energy for healing). Manage surgical pain. Address any restriction in lung capacity.

Allergic Reactions

Mild/Moderate: Itchy skin, redness, rash, swelling.
Severe (Anaphylaxis): Swelling of face/mouth, difficulty swallowing/speaking, difficulty breathing, abdominal pain, N/V.
Most serious hypersensitivity reaction. More likely with IV route. Most often within 5–30 min of injection. Allergens: chemicals (cleansers), medications, latex, food.

Hypoxemia Signs

Low oxygen in blood. S/S: Confusion, SOB, HA, bluish color (cyanosis), wheezing, ↑ HR.

Heat Exhaustion vs Heat Stroke

Exhaustion: Sweating, N/V, HA, shallow rapid breathing, weak rapid pulse, slightly elevated temp, normal pupils.
Care: Cool compress to forehead/neck, cool sheets, drink cool water.
Heat Stroke:DRY skin (key difference), flushed, labored breathing, strong rapid pulse, very high temp, dilated pupils.
Care: EMERGENCY. Semi-reclining in shade. Remove outer clothing. Cool with large amounts of water. Ice packs to groin, axilla, neck.

Hyper vs Hypoglycemia

Hyperglycemia (gradual onset): Flushed dry skin, fruity breath, deep labored breathing, vomiting, not hungry but thirsty.
Treatment: Take insulin, no sugar. Medical emergency.
Hypoglycemia (sudden onset): Pale moist skin, agitated, hungry but not thirsty.
Treatment: Give sugar immediately. If unconscious → glucose IV.
Key distinction: Hyper = gradual + dry. Hypo = sudden + moist.

Discharge Planning
Discharge planning begins when the patient is first admitted and ends when the patient leaves the final level of care. Goal: enhance QOL, discharge to an area matching medical and functional needs, improve efficiency of care, decrease costs, reduce readmission. Participants: patients, family caregivers, physicians, nurse navigators, PA/NP, PT, OT, SLP, case managers, social workers, and insurance companies.

DC Disposition, Compliance & APTA Guidelines

▼
Ideal Discharge Traits

Timely: Multidisciplinary support critical — discharge planning (therapists, physicians, case managers, patients), communication (everyone), authorization (case management).
Patient adherence: Continuity of care, location convenience, prompt treatment. Supervised environment has been shown to be of equal or greater importance than HEP alone.

Compliance vs Adherence

"Noncompliant" is loaded with implications and stereotypes. Ask: WHY is the patient having difficulty adhering to the plan of care? Approach with empathy. What can be changed or modified to allow the patient to better achieve their goals? Sometimes it's transportation, cost, pain, fear, depression, or lack of understanding — not willful disregard.

APTA Documentation Guidelines for DC

Referrals for additional services. Recommendations for follow-up PT. Family and caregiver training. Equipment provided. Home program. PT scope includes: Direct patient care, education to patients/caregivers, clinical decision making and collaboration with other providers to determine appropriate level of post-acute care. Excellence in communicating, collaborating, and advocating with numerous and diverse specialists and disciplines.

Quick ABG Interpretation

Step 1: Check pH (acid or alkaline?). Step 2: Check PaCO₂ (respiratory component). Step 3: Check HCO₃ (metabolic component). Step 4: Which one matches the pH shift? That's your primary disorder. Step 5: Is the other compensating? Example from Case 1: CO₂ of 24 = low — ask about pH levels to determine if respiratory alkalosis or metabolic compensation.

Review Cards — Multi-System
Tap any card to flip. High-yield acute care pearls.
What electrolyte abnormality is the #1 reason to NOT treat a patient, and at what level in this scenario?
tap
Potassium (K+) >6 mEq/L = DO NOT TREAT. Hyperkalemia >5 puts the patient at risk for cardiac issues. >6 is a contraindication — risk of V-fib and cardiac arrest. Mnemonic "MURDER": Muscle cramps, Urine abnormalities, Respiratory distress, Decreased cardiac contractility, EKG changes, Reflexes. Key principle: Use a systems-based approach. One abnormal lab in isolation ≠ automatic contraindication (e.g., low Hgb/Hct post-surgery is expected from anesthesia/blood loss and is OK to treat with symptom monitoring). But K+ >6 = ALWAYS hold.
Identify the contraindications for mobilizing a mechanically ventilated patient?
tap
MAP <65 mmHg. HR <40 or >130 bpm. RR <5 or >40. SpO₂ <88%. Elevated ICP. Active GI bleeding. Active MI. Patient agitation. Insecure airways. Also assess if patient is on vasopressors/inotropes (norepinephrine, epinephrine, vasopressin, dopamine, phenylephrine, dobutamine) = possible hemodynamic instability. BUT: Early mobilization within 24 hours of intubation is feasible and leads to earlier DC, ↑ respiratory function, ↓ muscle wasting, and ↓ mortality. 4–5% strength loss per week of bed rest, ↓ orthostatic tolerance after just 24 hours.
What is the method to distinguish C. Diff precautions from standard contact precautions?
tap
C. Diff requires enteric precautions — a combination of contact AND airborne. The critical difference: alcohol-based hand rubs are INEFFECTIVE against C. Diff spores. Must wash hands with soap and water for 2 full minutes. C. Diff is anaerobic and spore-forming, found on many surfaces in hospitals where antibiotic use is common. Symptoms: diarrhea and colonic inflammation. Compare: standard contact (MRSA, lice/scabies) = gown + gloves + alcohol rub OK. Airborne (TB, measles) = N-95 + negative pressure room. Droplet (flu, pertussis) = surgical mask. Neutropenic (ANC <1000) = protect the PATIENT from infection.
What is "Keep Your Move in the Tube" and why does it replace traditional sternal precautions in this scenario?
tap
Traditional sternal precautions (no lifting >5–20 lbs for 12 weeks, no overhead reaching, no pushing) lead to muscle atrophy, fear avoidance, and deconditioning. CABG sternotomy wound complications carry 15–40% mortality. "Keep Your Move in the Tube" is evidence-based: shorten the lever arm by keeping arms close to the body. This enables previously contraindicated movements while minimizing lateral pull on the sternum and decreasing leverage during load-bearing actions (rolling a wheelchair, opening doors, lifting). The critical point is lever arm reduction, not movement elimination.
Describe how to distinguish heat exhaustion from heat stroke, and what makes heat stroke an emergency?
tap
Key distinction: SKIN. Heat exhaustion = SWEATING (body still compensating). Heat stroke = DRY SKIN (thermoregulation has failed). Exhaustion: Sweating, N/V, HA, shallow rapid breathing, weak rapid pulse, slightly elevated temp, normal pupils. Care: cool compress, cool sheets, drink cool water. Heat stroke (EMERGENCY): Dry flushed skin, labored breathing, strong rapid pulse, VERY high temp, dilated pupils. Care: get emergency help, semi-reclining in shade, remove clothing, cool with large amounts of water, ice packs to groin/axilla/neck. Heat stroke = organ damage and death if not treated immediately.
List the 5 levels of ambulation and why does this matter for PLOF documentation?
tap
Physiologic: Endurance/strength/assistance makes ambulation non-functional. Walks for exercise only. Limited Household: Walks in home but limited. Rarely walks at home, never in community. Independent Household: Continuous home distances. May need help with stairs. WC outdoors. Limited Community: Walks outside, manages doors/curbs/ramps. WC for long distances. Independent Community: 400m at ≥50% normal speed, manages all terrain. Why it matters: PLOF determines realistic goals and expected outcomes. If a patient was limited household ambulator before admission, independent community ambulation is not a realistic acute care goal. Document specifically — not just "ambulates with FWW."
What fluoroquinolone side effect is most PT-relevant, and what other antimicrobial side effects affect therapy in this scenario?
tap
Fluoroquinolones (Cipro, Levaquin) can cause TENDON ISSUES — tendinopathy and rupture. Avoid aggressive tendon loading. Contraindicated in children <18. Aminoglycosides (Gentamicin, Tobramycin): Accumulate in kidneys and ears → nephrotoxicity + ototoxicity (tinnitus, vertigo, hearing loss) = balance impairment. Vancomycin: Red Man Syndrome if given too quickly (hypotension, flushing). Tetracyclines: Contraindicated in children <8 (teeth/bone growth). Photosensitivity. General: Drowsiness (fall risk), bone marrow suppression (↓ platelets = bleeding risk), diarrhea/colitis.
List the 4 types of shock and their key distinguishing features?
tap
Hypovolemic: Loss of fluid (hemorrhage, dehydration, burns). Low BP + tachycardia. Cardiogenic: Pump failure (MI). ↑ jugular venous pressure → distended neck veins. Inadequate tissue perfusion despite adequate blood volume. Distributive: Vasodilation from infection (septic), anaphylaxis, or endocrine causes. ↓ BP despite adequate blood volume. Most common in ICU. Neurogenic: Loss of sympathetic tone (SCI, spinal anesthesia). Unique: bradycardia + hypotension (NO compensatory tachycardia, unlike other types). All types: end result is inadequate tissue perfusion → organ failure if untreated.

Excessive fatigue, mental confusion, dizziness. Severe leg claudication ≥8/10 VAS. Pallor, cold sweat, ataxia. New heart murmur or ventricular gallop. Increasing rales with ↑ SOB. ECG abnormality. Terminate if: Patient requests to stop. Failure of monitoring equipment. Signs of poor perfusion (lightheadedness, confusion, ataxia, pallor, cyanosis, nausea, cold/clammy skin). SOB ≥2–3/4.

Orthostatic Hypotension Management

Definition: ≥20 mmHg systolic drop OR ≥10 mmHg diastolic drop on standing.
S/S: Dizziness, possible syncope when rising. Common in bed-bound patients — orthostatic tolerance decreases after just 24 hours of bed rest.
Management: (1) Have patient move slowly. (2) If hypotensive → place supine. (3) Wrap LEs prior to standing (compression). (4) LE AROM before standing (muscle pump). (5) Gradual position changes (HOB elevation progression).
Be cautious with patients on antihypertensives.

ICU & Early Mobility
William Dock, MD (1944): "Other vertebrates share man's bipedal erect posture, but so far as I know no other animal lies on his back when ill." Healthy subjects lose 4–5% muscle strength PER WEEK of bed rest. Orthostatic tolerance decreases after just 24 hours. Early mobilization leads to earlier DC, improved respiratory function, reduced muscle wasting, and reduced readmission and mortality.

Sequelae of Bed Rest — Why Early Mobility Matters

▼
What Bed Rest Does to the Body

Musculoskeletal: 4–5% loss of muscle strength per week. Muscle atrophy. Shift in fiber type toward fast-twitch glycolytic (Type II). Contractures and soft tissue limitations common.
Cardiovascular: Tachycardia, ↓ stroke volume, ↓ cardiac output, ↓ peak O₂ uptake. ↓ orthostatic tolerance after just 24 hours of bed rest.
Metabolic: Insulin resistance. ↓ HDL cholesterol. Hypertriglyceridemia. Ectopic fat storage. Reduced capacity to use fat as substrate.
Neurological: ↑ risk of ICU-associated delirium (common in mechanically ventilated patients).
Neuromuscular: Risk of CIPNM (Critical Illness Polyneuropathy and Myopathy) — difficulty weaning from mechanical ventilation, muscle wasting, tetraparesis. ICU-acquired weakness.

Benefits of Early Mobilization

Earlier hospital discharge. ↑ respiratory function. Reduced muscle wasting. Reduction in re-admission and mortality. Feasibility study: 49 subjects received immediate therapy within 24 hours of intubation — daily sedative interruption + AAROM/AROM + bed mobility + transfer training. Nothing good comes from bedrest.

Sternal Precautions — "Keep Your Move in the Tube"

▼
Traditional Sternal Precautions (Being Replaced)

Avoid lifting 5–20 lbs up to 12 weeks. No pushing when rushing from sitting to standing. Only pain-free bilateral arm movement (horizontal, backward, overhead). Prohibit common shoulder joint movements: flexion/extension, abduction/adduction, scapular retraction/protraction, elevation/depression. Problem: These restrictions lead to muscle atrophy, fear-avoidance, and functional decline.

"Keep Your Move in the Tube" (Modern Approach)

Concept: Shorten the length of the outstretched arm (lever arm reduction). Keep arms close to body — patients can modify load-bearing movements and avoid excessive stress to the sternum.
Benefit: Enables patients to perform previously contraindicated movements safely. Limiting humerus movement minimizes lateral pull on the sternum and decreases leverage during load-bearing (rolling WC, opening heavy door, lifting).
Patients often find it painful to sit up from supine or stand from a chair — the tube concept allows these movements with reduced sternal stress. Healing time affected by age, underlying conditions, nutritional status, medications, and tobacco use.

Pain Management & Pharmacology
Pain is frequently encountered in acute care. Adaptive pain (protective — WB on recent fracture) serves a purpose. Maladaptive pain (chronic — leads to prolonged bed rest) interferes with normal physiology. Under-treatment of acute pain may facilitate development of chronic pain by changing how the nervous system perceives painful impulses — benign stimuli become perceived as painful.

Pain Types, Breakthrough Pain & Assessment

▼
Pain Classification

Acute pain: Follows injury. Generally dissipates when injury heals. Serves as a warning.
Chronic pain: Serves no useful function. Persists beyond normal healing time. Under-treatment of acute pain may facilitate its development — changes the nervous system's perception.
Breakthrough pain: Transient increase of moderate-to-severe intensity occurring against a background of controlled mild-to-moderate persistent pain (Portenoy & Hagen, 1990).
Pain is the primary limiting factor in acute care therapy. Schedule therapy around pain medication timing.

Surgical & Organ Transplant Rehabilitation
Cardiovascular and pulmonary complications are the major cause of surgical morbidity and mortality. Risk factors include age, smoking, nutrition, weight, exercise, sleep, stress, and pre-existing dysfunction. PT plays critical roles in pre-operative training, post-operative mobilization, and prevention of complications.

Perioperative Care — 3 Phases & PT Role

▼
Pre-Operative Assessment & Training

Assessment: Respiratory function (breathing pattern, chest wall deformities, chest wall movement, accessory muscle use), circulatory (venous/arterial insufficiency, neuropathy), full functional assessment if first time. Review surgeon's notes, assess comorbidities that could cause post-op issues.
Training: Educate patient on what PT will expect post-surgery. Breathing exercises (diaphragmatic, lateral costal, coughing/huffing techniques). Positioning. UE/LE exercises.
This is your opportunity to answer questions — only if you can answer them.

Factors Contributing to Cardiopulmonary Risk

Type and duration of surgery. Anesthesia → depression of diaphragm and intercostal muscles → ↓ TLC, FRC, residual volume. Lung compliance decreases, work of breathing increases (further reductions in supine). Pharmacology: variable patient response to analgesics, widespread systemic effects (reduced arousal, CVP depression, GI depression, muscle relaxation) — all compromise O₂ transport. Lines/leads, monitoring devices, chest tubes, catheters. Incisions, dressings, splints. Anxiety, discomfort, pain.

Post-Operative Goals (O₂ Transport Focus)

Maximize arousal. Control anxiety and pain. Maximize ventilation and lung compliance. Optimize mucociliary transport and mucus clearance. Minimize aspiration risk. Optimize relaxation and sleep. Maximize chest wall mobility in multiple planes. Optimize posture (sitting, standing, walking, recumbent). Optimize circulatory status and perfusion. Optimize muscle pump action. Maximize endurance. Maintain or increase strength. Maintain skin integrity. Maintain cognitive function. Prevent: atelectasis, pneumonia, DVT, deconditioning, prolonged ICU stay.

THA/TKA Precautions & Common Post-Surgical Protocols

▼
Anterior THA Precautions

Do NOT step backwards with surgical leg (no hip extension). Do NOT allow external rotation. Do NOT cross legs — pillow between legs when rolling. Sleep on surgical side when side-lying.

Posterior THA Precautions

Avoid midline crossing (no adduction past midline). Avoid hip flexion >90°. Avoid internal rotation. Pillow between legs for sleeping/rolling.

Transfer Types by Condition

Slideboard: SCI, bilateral NWB. Stand Pivot: THA, TKA, TTWB, FWB. Squat Pivot: NWB on one leg. Always match the transfer type to the patient's WB status, strength, balance, and cognitive ability.

Low oxygen in blood. S/S: Confusion, SOB, headache, bluish color (cyanosis), wheezing, increased HR. Can progress to respiratory failure if not addressed. Monitor SpO₂ and notify medical team.

Discharge Planning & Bariatric Care
Discharge planning begins when the patient is first admitted. The goal: enhance QOL, match the patient to a level of care appropriate for their medical and functional needs, improve efficiency, decrease costs, and reduce readmission rates. Participants include patients, family, all healthcare providers, and insurance companies.

Discharge Planning & Disposition

▼
Ideal Discharge Traits

Timely: Multidisciplinary support critical (therapists, physicians, case managers, patients). Communication with everyone. Authorization from case management/PMR clinic. Patient adherence: Continuity of care, location-appropriate, prompt treatment initiation. Supervised environment has been shown to be of equal or greater importance than home exercise programs alone.

Non-compliance vs Non-adherence

"Noncompliant" is shorthand for patients who don't follow medical recommendations — but it's a term loaded with implications and stereotypes. Better question: Why is the patient having difficulty adhering to the plan of care? Approach with empathy. What can be changed or modified to allow the patient to better achieve their goals? This shifts the focus from blaming the patient to problem-solving with the patient.

APTA Discharge Documentation Requirements

Referrals for additional services. Recommendations for follow-up PT. Family and caregiver training. Equipment provided. Home program. DC disposition options: Return to primary residence? Live with a family member? SNF? LTACH? IRF? Outpatient? The determination of DC destination allows for appropriate goal setting and therapeutic intervention. DC destination should match what the patient can handle — functional level, medical needs, and available social support.

GI, Kidney & Other Systems in Acute Care

GI Considerations

N/V: Common post-surgery and with medications (Phenergan, Compazine, Zofran for treatment).
Constipation: Common with bed rest, opioid use.
Diarrhea: Fecal management system may be in place. Rule out C. diff (especially if on antibiotics).
NPO status: Patient cannot eat or drink (common pre-surgery, acute pancreatitis). Check NPO status before therapy — affects energy level.

Kidney/Urinary Considerations

UTIs: Patients with indwelling Foley catheters are at risk.
Can we get them to safely mobilize to the bathroom? Proper catheter management is critical. Symptoms: N/V, fever, cloudy/foul-smelling urine, frequency/urgency.
Dialysis patients: Kidneys filter waste — patients feel sick before dialysis because bodies are full of waste. Filter waste, remove fluid, balance electrolytes.
Acute renal failure: Waste buildup, fluid imbalance. Monitor BUN, creatinine, eGFR. Check if patient has fluid restrictions. Nephrostomy tubes = opening between kidney and skin to collect urine — secure both sides, don't kink.

List the contraindications for mobilizing a mechanically ventilated patient?
tap
MAP <65 mmHg. HR <40 or >130 bpm. RR <5 or >40. O₂ sat <88%. Elevated ICP. Active GI bleeding. Active MI. Patient agitation. Insecure airways. If none of these are present, mobilize. Bed rest causes 4–5% strength loss/week, orthostatic intolerance after 24 hours, ↑ ICU delirium, CIPNM risk. Early mobility leads to: earlier DC, ↑ respiratory function, ↓ muscle wasting, ↓ readmission and mortality. If vasopressors/inotropes are running (norepinephrine, dopamine, etc.), patient is hemodynamically unstable — consult team before mobilizing.
Identify the 5 levels of ambulation and how do they guide PLOF documentation?
tap
Physiologic: Ambulation not functional (endurance, strength, or assistance required). May need assist to stand. Walks for exercise only. Limited Household: Walks in home, limited by endurance/strength/safety. Walks rarely at home, never in community. Independent Household: Walks continuously for reasonable home distances. May need help with stairs/curbs. WC outdoors. Limited Community: Walks outside, manages doors/low curbs/ramps. WC for long distances. Independent Community: Walks ~400m at ≥50% normal speed. Manages curbs, stairs, doors safely. Rarely/never uses WC. Why it matters: PLOF determines realistic goals for therapy. A previously independent community ambulator has very different expectations than a limited household ambulator.
What is "Keep Your Move in the Tube" and why is it replacing traditional sternal precautions in this scenario?
tap
Traditional sternal precautions (no lifting 5–20 lbs for 12 weeks, no pushing, restricted arm movements) cause muscle atrophy, fear-avoidance, and functional decline. "Keep Your Move in the Tube" replaces arbitrary load restrictions with lever arm reduction — keep arms close to the body. This shortens the outstretched arm length, enabling patients to perform previously contraindicated movements while avoiding excessive sternal stress. Limiting humerus excursion minimizes lateral pull on the sternum and decreases leverage during WC propulsion, opening doors, or lifting. CABG sternal wound complications: increased morbidity, reduced QOL, prolonged hospitalizations, mortality rate of 15–40% for serious cases. Healing affected by age, underlying conditions, nutrition, medications, and tobacco.
What is the method to differentiate the 4 types of shock?
tap
Hypovolemic: Fluid loss (hemorrhage, dehydration, burns). ↓ BP, ↑ HR (compensatory tachycardia), cold/clammy skin, thirst. Cardiogenic: Cardiac pump failure. ↑ JVP (distended jugular veins), pulmonary edema, ↓ BP. Think MI. Distributive: Vasodilation from infection (septic — warm skin initially, elevated WBC with left shift/bandemia), anaphylaxis (within 5–30 min of exposure, more common with IV route), or endocrine. Neurogenic: Loss of sympathetic tone (SCI, spinal anesthesia). KEY DISTINCTION: Hypotension with BRADYCARDIA (unlike all other shock types which have tachycardia). Sepsis rule: Elevated WBC alone does not rule out sepsis — absence of WBC elevation doesn't rule it out either.
Which antimicrobials cause tendon issues, ototoxicity, and Red Man Syndrome in this scenario?
tap
Fluoroquinolones (Cipro, Levaquin): May cause tendon issues, especially Achilles rupture — higher risk in elderly and steroid users. Also: photosensitivity. Contraindicated in liver disease and children <18. Aminoglycosides (Gentamicin, Tobramycin): Accumulate in kidneys and ears → nephrotoxicity + ototoxicity (tinnitus, vertigo, hearing loss). Monitor balance, hearing, and kidney function during therapy. Vancomycin: Red Man Syndrome if given too quickly — hypotension, flushing, skin rash. Only works against Gram+. Used for MRSA. Other PT-relevant effects: Tetracyclines = photosensitivity + contraindicated in kids <8 (teeth/bone). All antimicrobials can cause bone marrow suppression → thrombocytopenia (↓ platelets → bleeding risk).
