Elbow & Forearm

The elbow links the powerful shoulder to the precise hand — it positions the hand in space and transmits force between them. It is a trochoginglymoid joint (hinge + pivot) that allows flexion/extension for lifting and pronation/supination for turning. Three nerves cross it superficially, making it vulnerable to compression and trauma. The elbow is where repetitive strain meets high demand — understanding its anatomy explains why tennis elbow, golfer’s elbow, and cubital tunnel syndrome are so common. This module covers anatomy, ligaments, nerve pathology, tendinopathy science, and rehabilitation.

Elbow, Forearm & Arm — Comprehensive Anatomy
The elbow is a trochoginglymoid joint — a combined hinge + pivot. It's where the arm's power meets the forearm's precision. Three bones, three joints, two compartments, and five major nerves converge in a small space. Understanding this region means understanding how force gets from the shoulder to the fingertips.
Osteology & Joints
Humerus — Distal Landmarks
  • Trochlea — spool-shaped medial articular surface; articulates with trochlear notch of ulna (humeroulnar joint)
  • Capitulum — rounded lateral articular surface; articulates with radial head (humeroradial joint)
  • Medial epicondyle — common flexor tendon origin; ulnar nerve passes posterior to it
  • Lateral epicondyle — common extensor tendon origin; lateral epicondylalgia = “tennis elbow”
  • Lateral supracondylar ridge — brachioradialis & ECRL attachment
  • Olecranon fossa — posterior; receives olecranon in extension
  • Coronoid fossa — anterior medial; receives coronoid in flexion
  • Radial fossa — anterior lateral; receives radial head in flexion
Radius & Ulna
  • Ulna — stabilizing bone of the forearm. Olecranon (triceps insertion), coronoid process (brachialis insertion), trochlear notch (articulates with trochlea), radial notch (proximal RU joint), ulnar styloid (distal; TFCC attachment)
  • Radius — shorter than ulna, lateral. Radial head (articulates with capitulum + radial notch of ulna), radial neck, radial tuberosity (biceps tendon insertion), ulnar notch (distal RU joint), radial styloid (distal; brachioradialis inserts), dorsal tubercle (Lister's tubercle — EPL pulley)
  • Interosseous membrane — fibrous sheet connecting radius and ulna; transmits force from hand → radius → across membrane → ulna → humerus. Divides forearm into anterior and posterior compartments
Three Joints of the Elbow
  • Humeroulnar — trochlea → trochlear notch. Hinge (uniaxial). Primary flexion/extension. Most stable elbow joint
  • Humeroradial — capitulum → radial head. Allows flex/ext + pronation/supination
  • Proximal radioulnar — radial head → radial notch of ulna. Pivot joint. Annular ligament stabilizes radial head but allows rotation for pronation/supination
  • Distal radioulnar (DRUJ) — ulnar head → ulnar notch of radius. Radius rotates around ulna. TFCC stabilizes
  • Carrying angle — angle between humeral shaft and forearm; ~5° males, ~10–15° females; increased in valgus, decreased in varus
Ligaments
  • Ulnar (medial) collateral ligament (UCL/MCL) — medial epicondyle → coronoid + olecranon. Three bands: anterior (strongest, taut in extension — key in overhead athletes), posterior, oblique (transverse). Resists valgus stress. Most commonly injured in throwing athletes (“Tommy John”)
  • Radial (lateral) collateral ligament (RCL/LCL) — lateral epicondyle → annular ligament. Resists varus stress. Less commonly injured
  • Annular ligament — encircles radial head, attaches to ulna. Stabilizes proximal RU joint. Sacciform recess = synovial pouch between annular lig and radial head
  • TFCC — triangular fibrocartilage complex at distal RU joint. Attaches distal radius to ulnar styloid. Acts as shock absorber, stabilizer, and pivot during pronation/supination

Cubital Fossa & Cubital Tunnel

▼
Cubital Fossa (Anterior Elbow)

Triangular hollow at the anterior elbow — the “antecubital space” where blood draws happen. Think of it as the Grand Central Station of the elbow — everything important passes through.

Boundaries: Superior = line between epicondyles. Medial = pronator teres. Lateral = brachioradialis. Floor = brachialis + supinator. Roof = fascia reinforced by bicipital aponeurosis (protects underlying structures).

Contents (lateral → medial): Biceps tendon, Brachial artery (divides into radial + ulnar arteries here), Median nerve. Mnemonic: TAN — Tendon, Artery, Nerve (lateral to medial).

Also contains: median cubital vein (superficial, used for venipuncture), lateral/medial antebrachial cutaneous nerves.

Cubital Tunnel (Posterior Medial Elbow)

Passageway on the posterior-medial elbow between the olecranon and medial epicondyle, roofed by the tendinous arch of FCU. This is where the ulnar nerve lives — superficial and vulnerable.

Contents: Ulnar nerve, ulnar recurrent artery.

Clinical: Cubital tunnel syndrome = compression of ulnar nerve here — 2nd most common peripheral nerve entrapment after carpal tunnel. Symptoms: numbness/tingling in ring + little finger, grip weakness, hand intrinsic atrophy. The “funny bone” sensation = direct compression of ulnar nerve against medial epicondyle.

Nerve Pathways Through the Forearm — Complete Routes

▼
Median Nerve (C5–T1)

Pathway: Enters cubital fossa with brachial artery (medial to biceps tendon) → passes between two heads of pronator teres (compression site #1) → gives off AIN (pure motor branch) → descends between FDS and FDP → at wrist: between FCR and palmaris longus → passes deep to flexor retinaculum through carpal tunnel (compression site #2) → gives off recurrent branch to thenar muscles → supplies lateral 2 lumbricals + palmar digits 1–3.5.

No branches in the arm. In the forearm, it supplies: pronator teres, FCR, palmaris longus, FDS, and (via AIN) lateral FDP, FPL, pronator quadratus.

Entrapment sites: (1) Between heads of pronator teres (pronator syndrome). (2) Under FDS fibrous arch (sublimis bridge). (3) Carpal tunnel (most common). AIN syndrome: weakness of FPL + lateral FDP + PQ without sensory loss.

Ulnar Nerve (C8–T1)

Pathway: Travels medial to brachial artery in arm → mid-humerus: pierces medial intermuscular septum, descends posteriorly → passes posterior to medial epicondyle (superficial = “funny bone”, compression site #1: cubital tunnel) → enters forearm between two heads of FCU → descends between FCU and FDP → at wrist: passes superficial (anterior) to flexor retinaculum through Guyon's canal (compression site #2) → divides into superficial (sensory to medial 1.5 digits) + deep (motor to all intrinsics except lateral 2 lumbricals, thenar, and 1st dorsal interosseous) branches.

No branches in arm or axilla. In forearm: supplies FCU and medial half of FDP only. In hand: supplies all intrinsics except LOAF (Lateral 2 lumbricals, Opponens pollicis, Abductor pollicis brevis, Flexor pollicis brevis).

Clinical: Ulnar nerve is critical for fine motor / hand dexterity. Complete ulnar palsy = claw hand (hyperextension MCP + flexion IP of digits 4–5), loss of finger abduction/adduction, weakened grip.

Radial Nerve (C5–T1)

Pathway: In arm: wraps posteriorly around humerus in radial (spiral) groove with deep brachial artery (vulnerable to mid-shaft humeral fracture) → pierces lateral intermuscular septum → descends between brachialis and brachioradialis → at cubital fossa: divides into deep branch and superficial branch.

Deep branch: pierces supinator (compression site: radial tunnel / posterior interosseous nerve syndrome) → continues as PIN (posterior interosseous nerve) → supplies ALL posterior forearm muscles (extensors).

Superficial branch: continues distally deep to brachioradialis → becomes cutaneous → supplies dorsolateral hand skin (except distal digits 1–3.5). Radial nerve supplies NO hand muscles.

Clinical: Radial nerve injury = wrist drop (loss of wrist/finger/thumb extension). “Saturday night palsy” = compression in radial groove from arm draped over chair while sleeping/intoxicated.

Blood Supply — Arm & Forearm

▼
Arterial Pathway

Aorta → Subclavian artery (R: via brachiocephalic trunk; L: directly from aorta) → becomes Axillary artery at outer border of 1st rib → becomes Brachial artery at inferior border of teres major → gives off deep artery of arm (travels with radial nerve in spiral groove) → brachial artery divides in cubital fossa into Radial artery (lateral, palpable along brachioradialis) + Ulnar artery (medial, larger).

Ulnar artery gives off common interosseous artery → divides into anterior + posterior interosseous arteries (supply deep forearm). Ulnar artery enters hand superficial to flexor retinaculum → forms superficial palmar arch.

Radial artery descends deep to brachioradialis (pulse palpable along its length). At wrist: passes through anatomical snuffbox dorsally → forms deep palmar arch.

Allen's test: Assesses patency of radial and ulnar arteries at the wrist. Compress both, patient opens/closes fist, release one at a time. Hand should pink up within 5–7 seconds if artery is patent.

Bursae, Carrying Angle & Clinical Correlations

▼
Bursae of the Elbow (12+)

Subcutaneous olecranon bursa — between skin and olecranon. Most clinically relevant — olecranon bursitis (“student's elbow”) from prolonged pressure. Also: intratendinous olecranon bursa, subtendinous olecranon bursa, and multiple around the radial head and ligaments.

Pronation / Supination Summary

Pronation: Pronator teres (strongest) + pronator quadratus. Supination: Supinator (low resistance, slow movements) + biceps brachii (strongest when resistance applied, especially at 90° flexion). In supination, the radius uncrosses from the ulna; in pronation, the radius crosses over the ulna.

Common Fractures

Radial head fracture — most common elbow fracture in adults. FOOSH mechanism. Pain with pronation/supination. Olecranon fracture — direct trauma. Loss of triceps extension. Supracondylar fracture — most common elbow fracture in children. Can compromise brachial artery → Volkmann's ischemic contracture (forearm compartment syndrome). Monteggia fracture = proximal ulna fracture + radial head dislocation. Galeazzi fracture = distal radius fracture + DRUJ dislocation.

Elbow & Forearm — Examination & Special Tests
The elbow is the bridge between shoulder and wrist. Pathology is frequently driven by repetitive overuse (epicondylalgia), overhead sports demands (UCL injury), or compressive neuropathy. Always include a cervical spine screen — radiculopathy mimics nearly every elbow condition.
Clinical Anatomy Reminder — Elbow Palpation Landmarks

Lateral: Lateral epicondyle (common extensor tendon origin), lateral supracondylar ridge, radial head (palpate with pronation/supination). Medial: Medial epicondyle (common flexor-pronator mass origin), medial supracondylar ridge, cubital tunnel (between medial epicondyle and olecranon — ulnar nerve). Posterior: Olecranon / triceps insertion. Anterior: Biceps tendon + aponeurosis, antecubital fossa.

Ligament Integrity Tests

Ulnar Collateral Ligament (UCL) — Medial Stability

▼
Anatomy Note

The UCL has three bundles (anterior, posterior, transverse). Only the anterior bundle provides significant restraint to valgus force from 30–120° of elbow flexion (O'Driscoll, Dugas). UCL injuries are most common in overhead throwing athletes (pitchers, quarterbacks, javelin). The "Tommy John" surgery (UCL reconstruction) is the definitive surgical intervention.

Valgus Stress Test — 0° and 20–30°

  • Stabilize distal humerus with one hand; secure patient's wrist between your hip and elbow
  • Apply a valgus force to the elbow at both 0° and 20–30° flexion
  • (+) = Reproduction of painful symptoms, laxity, or increased gapping of the joint
  • Test at 20–30° unlocks the olecranon from its fossa and isolates ligamentous contribution

Moving Valgus Stress Test

  • Patient seated or standing. Therapist stands behind.
  • Bring affected arm into 90° shoulder abduction + ~120° elbow flexion
  • Apply valgus force (anterior direction) at elbow while extending elbow to full extension
  • (+) = Pain, reproduction of symptoms, or apprehension
  • Most sensitive test for UCL injury — mimics the throwing motion under valgus load

Milking Maneuver

  • Patient supine, shoulder in 90° abduction + slight ER
  • Place patient's elbow in your antecubital fossa; grasp patient's thumb
  • Extend your elbow to provide valgus force while concurrently ER the shoulder via the "milking grip" on thumb
  • (+) = Pain, reproduction of symptoms, or apprehension
Clinical Note — UCL in Overhead Sports

Palpate sublime tubercle (medial ulna) and mid-substance UCL during valgus stress. Tenderness at these sites + positive valgus stress = high clinical suspicion for UCL pathology. Always examine medial elbow in overhead athletes presenting with medial elbow pain, reduced throwing velocity, or pain at late cocking/early acceleration phase.

Lateral Collateral Ligament (LCL/RCL) — Lateral Stability

▼

Varus Stress Test — 0° and 20–30°

  • Stabilize distal humerus with one hand; secure patient's wrist between your hip and elbow
  • Flex elbow to 20–30° to unlock the olecranon
  • Apply a varus force (push lateral condyle medially)
  • (+) = Reproduction of pain, laxity, or increased gapping

Push-Up / Chair Sign (Stand-Up Sign)

  • Patient seated at edge of table, hands on table with forearms supinated (wrist forward, fingers facing backward)
  • Ask patient to attempt to lift themselves off the chair using their arms
  • (+) = Pain or apprehension with this position
  • Supinated forearm position stresses the lateral collateral complex
Clinical Context

LCL injuries are less common than UCL injuries. Often associated with elbow dislocation (lateral is the first structure to fail in valgus extension overload — but LCL fails in varus stress). Posterolateral rotatory instability (PLRI) is the most common pattern of LCL instability — the lateral ulnar collateral ligament (LUCL) is the primary stabilizer. Chair/push-up sign is the functional PLRI test.

Tendon Injury Tests

Lateral Epicondylalgia (Tennis Elbow) — Common Extensor Tendon

▼
Always Include Cervical Screen

Cervical radiculopathy (C6–C7) and thoracic outlet syndrome can mimic lateral epicondylalgia. A positive cervical screen changes management completely. Perform cervical AROM, Spurling's, and ULTT before concluding the diagnosis is purely local lateral epicondylalgia.

Cozen's Sign — Active Stress

  • Place patient's arm in slight elbow flexion, full pronation, full wrist extension
  • Palpate lateral epicondyle
  • Resist patient as you attempt to flex the wrist and extend the elbow
  • Stresses the common extensor tendon actively
  • (+) = Pain at the lateral epicondyle

Mill's Test — Passive Stress

  • Palpate lateral epicondyle
  • Passively extend the elbow and flex the wrist fully, with overpressure
  • Stresses the common extensor tendon passively (stretches it)
  • (+) = Pain at the lateral epicondyle

Maudsley's Test — ECRB Isolation

  • Palpate lateral epicondyle
  • Resist third digit (middle finger) extension
  • Stresses the ECRB (extensor carpi radialis brevis) — the most commonly involved structure in lateral epicondylalgia — which inserts into the lateral epicondyle
  • (+) = Pain at the lateral epicondyle
EBP Treatment Note

Eccentric wrist extension (Tyler Twist / Thera-Band FlexBar protocol) has Level 1 evidence for lateral epicondylalgia. 3×15 daily. Slow eccentric lowering phase is the therapeutic element. Pain during eccentric phase acceptable; pain after exercise is not. Combine with manual therapy and activity modification.

Medial Epicondylalgia (Golfer's Elbow) — Common Flexor-Pronator Tendon

▼

Medial Epicondylitis Test 1 — Active Stress

  • Place patient in slight elbow flexion, forearm supinated, wrist flexed
  • Palpate medial epicondyle
  • Tell patient to resist your force as you attempt to extend the wrist and extend the elbow
  • Stresses the common flexor tendon actively
  • (+) = Pain at the medial epicondyle

Medial Epicondylitis Test 2 — Passive Stress

  • Palpate medial epicondyle
  • Passively extend elbow, supinate forearm, extend wrist fully with overpressure
  • Stresses the common flexor tendon passively (stretches it)
  • (+) = Pain at the medial epicondyle
Medial Elbow DDx — Differentiate UCL from Medial Epicondylalgia

UCL injury: pain with valgus stress at 20–30° elbow flexion; pain at sublime tubercle or mid-substance UCL; associated with overhead throwing; may have joint laxity. Medial epicondylalgia: pain with resisted wrist flexion/pronation; tenderness at medial epicondyle (not sublime tubercle); no joint laxity; often in golfers, climbers, rock climbers. Both present with medial elbow pain — the differentiation determines treatment. Also screen: ulnar nerve (Tinel's at cubital tunnel — ulnar nerve lies directly posterior to medial epicondyle and is often involved with medial epicondylalgia).

Distal Biceps Tendon Rupture Assessment

▼
Not a Special Test — Clinical Assessment Framework

Distal biceps tendon rupture is diagnosed by clinical assessment, not a single special test. Typically occurs in males 40–60s with sudden eccentric load (forced extension during lifting). Assess in this order:

  1. Mechanism of injury — sudden forced extension during active flexion (e.g., lifting a box as it slips)
  2. Observation — ecchymosis in antecubital fossa; pop-eye deformity (biceps muscle belly retracts proximally, visible lump in mid-arm)
  3. Palpation — attempt to palpate the distal biceps tendon in the antecubital fossa. If absent = complete rupture
  4. Strength testing (careful) — resist elbow flexion and/or forearm supination. Supination is the most sensitive function for distal biceps integrity (biceps is the primary supinator)
Biceps Squeeze Test — Key Special Test

Patient seated, forearm resting comfortably in lap. Therapist stands on involved side. Squeeze the biceps muscle belly firmly with one hand while stabilizing the UE with the other. (+) = Forearm does NOT supinate → suspect distal biceps tendon rupture. (Intact distal biceps: squeezing the muscle belly produces forearm supination via intact myotendinous-bone continuity.)

Distal Triceps Tendon Rupture — Same Framework

Apply the same assessment sequence. MOI: forced extension during eccentric load, or direct blow to olecranon. Observation: posterior ecchymosis, palpable defect at olecranon. Strength testing: resist elbow extension — weakness or inability = complete rupture. Often missed clinically; palpate tendon at olecranon carefully.

Nerve Pathology Tests

Tinel's Sign — Cubital Tunnel (Ulnar Nerve)

▼
  • Stabilize patient's forearm in slight elbow flexion
  • Palpate to locate the cubital tunnel — sits between the medial epicondyle and the olecranon process
  • Tap the cubital tunnel firmly to impact the ulnar nerve
  • (+) = Reproduction of patient's symptoms in ulnar nerve distribution (ring finger, little finger, medial forearm)
Ulnar Nerve Distribution

Motor: intrinsic hand muscles (interossei, hypothenar), ring/little finger FDP, FCU. Sensory: little finger, ring finger (ulnar half), medial palm, medial forearm. Clinical presentations: cubital tunnel syndrome (elbow), Guyon's canal syndrome (wrist — hook of hamate). Elbow flexion provocation test: sustained elbow flexion reproduces symptoms in cubital tunnel syndrome.

Clinical Note

Source document note: The positive sign is described as reproduction in "median nerve distribution" — this appears to be a documentation error. Tinel's at the cubital tunnel tests the ULNAR nerve (which runs through the cubital tunnel between medial epicondyle and olecranon). Median nerve Tinel's is performed at the carpal tunnel (wrist flexion crease). The correct positive finding for cubital tunnel Tinel's is symptoms in the ulnar distribution (little finger, ring finger, medial forearm).

Pronator Syndrome Test — Median Nerve at Pronator Teres

▼
  • Stabilize patient's forearm with outside arm; use thumb of stabilizing hand to apply pressure into the pronator teres (~4 cm distal to antecubital fossa)
  • Use other hand in an "arm wrestling" grip with patient's hand
  • Attempt to supinate and extend their elbow; ask patient to resist this
  • Patient's resistance activates pronation and elbow flexion → contracts pronator teres around the median nerve
  • Combined with direct thumb pressure = compressive neuropathy effect on the median nerve
  • (+) = Reproduction of patient's symptoms in median nerve distribution (thumb, index, middle finger, radial palm)
Median Nerve Distribution

Motor: thenar muscles (LOAF: Lumbricals 1&2, Opponens pollicis, Abductor pollicis brevis, Flexor pollicis brevis), forearm pronators, wrist/finger flexors (except FCU and ring/little FDP). Sensory: thumb, index, middle finger, radial half of ring finger, thenar eminence.

Pronator Syndrome vs. Carpal Tunnel

Both involve median nerve. Pronator syndrome: symptoms with resisted pronation + elbow flexion; no nocturnal symptoms; positive at pronator teres level. Carpal tunnel: nocturnal symptoms (classic), positive Phalen's + Tinel's at wrist; provoked by sustained wrist flexion; no provocation with pronation. Treatment differs completely.

EBP Evidence — Lateral Epicondylalgia
Lateral epicondylalgia is NOT inflammatory. The correct term is epicondylalgia or epicondylar tendinopathy — NOT epicondylitis. Pathology involves neurogenic mediators (substance P, CGRP, glutamate), neovascularization, fiber necrosis, and ECRB morphological changes — no inflammatory cells present.
MWM for Lateral Epicondylalgia (Vicenzino / Bisset)
Technique

Laterally directed glide to proximal forearm during patient's painful grip task. Force ~1.9 N/cm (~2/3 max). 6–10 reps per visit. Must include self-MWM at home between visits.

Immediate Effects (RCT evidence)

Single application → immediate increase in pain-free grip force + reduced pressure pain threshold + improved ULTT radial bias range + sympathoexcitatory response. Pain-free grip force = valid sensitive clinical measure correlating with perceived improvement.

Bisset et al RCT — n=198 (BMJ 2006)

MWM+exercise vs corticosteroid injection vs wait-and-see. At 6 weeks: NNT=3 vs wait-and-see. Over 12 months: MWM+exercise superior overall. 90% reduction in recurrence risk vs injection (5/66 vs 47/65). Fewer additional treatments sought in MWM+exercise group.

Cervicothoracic Manipulation for LE (Vicenzino, Cleland)
Cervical Spine Is Frequently Missed

57% of LE patients have cervical impairments identified on exam. Only 37% receive cervical treatment. Concomitant neck pain = strongest predictor of poor 12-month prognosis for LE. Always assess and treat the cervicothoracic spine in LE patients.

Cervical Lateral Glide Technique (Elvey/Vicenzino)

Patient supine, UE in radial nerve bias ULTT. Lateral cervical glide at C5/6 toward contralateral side, ~1.3 Hz. Results: improved ULTT ROM + reduced pressure pain threshold + increased grip force + sympathoexcitatory response.

When to Add Cervicothoracic vs. Local Elbow

Local MWM first if pressure pain threshold normal. Add cervicothoracic if mechanical hyperalgesia present OR concomitant cervical impairments/neck pain. Adding spinal manipulation = significantly fewer PT visits (P<0.01) at similar success rate (retrospective audit, n=112).

Mechanism — Why Manipulation Helps Tendinopathy
  • Non-opioid descending pain inhibition via dorsal periaqueductal gray (dPAG) of midbrain — serotonergic + noradrenergic pathways
  • Strong association between hypoalgesic + sympathoexcitatory effects (r=0.82)
  • Applying MWM to the unaffected elbow of unilateral LE patients → small grip reduction — not simply post-exercise motor facilitation
  • Multi-system physiological response — neuromotor, sympathetic, pain modulation simultaneously
Mill's Manipulation — Lower Evidence

HVLAT at end-range elbow extension + wrist/hand flexed. Head-to-head with eccentric exercise: Mill's + friction massage SUBSTANTIALLY INFERIOR to eccentric exercise program. Widespread use but weak comparative evidence.

Wrist Manipulation (Scaphoid Thrust)

Pilot RCT: less day pain vs multimodal at 6 weeks; no significant difference in success rate (85% vs 67%). Preliminary evidence only.

Tendinopathy Loading Science
From Couppé, Svensson, Silbernagel, Langberg, Magnusson (JOSPT 2015;45:853-863). Evidence applies to all tendinopathies: Achilles, patellar, RC, lateral epicondylalgia.
What We Now Know
NOT Inflammatory — "Tendinitis" Is Outdated

Tendinopathy tissue: collagen degeneration + necrosis + neovascularization + irregular fiber structure + increased ground substance. No elevated inflammatory markers after exercise in chronic tendinopathy. Anti-inflammatories address the wrong mechanism. Use: tendinopathy or tendinosis.

Loading Is the Treatment Mechanism

Tendon fibroblasts require mechanical strain to survive and adapt. Adequate loading = increased collagen synthesis + blood flow → structural adaptation. Depriving tendon of strain leads to degeneration and cell death. The question is how to load optimally, not whether to load.

Eccentric Is NOT Superior to Other Loading

No convincing clinical evidence that isolated eccentric loading produces better outcomes than concentric or heavy slow resistance training at comparable loads. Key variable: load magnitude and duration — not the direction of movement.

Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) — Superior Biochemical Effects
What HSR Adds vs. Eccentric

HSR = high load, low speed, few reps (~70–85% 1RM). Similar pain + function improvements as eccentric protocols, but HSR additionally shows: increased collagen content + reduced glycation + structural changes on imaging. The biochemical and structural adaptations are superior.

Progressive Loading Continuum

Isometric (pain control, early; 5×45 sec at 70% MVC) → Isotonic slow (proliferative) → HSR or eccentric (remodeling) → Plyometric/SSC (power, sport-specific). Each phase adds load as tissue adapts.

Lateral Epicondylalgia Specific

Eccentric wrist extension (Tyler Twist / TherapyBar FlexBar): 3×15 daily; slow eccentric lowering phase is the therapeutic element. Pain during eccentric = acceptable; pain after exercise = reduce load. Level 1 evidence. Combined with MWM = optimal outcome.

UE Plyometric Exercise — Science & Criteria
From Davies, Riemann, Manske (IJSPT 2015;10:760). Plyometrics are the bridge between strength and return to sport — they train power (rate of force development), not just strength.
Phase 1: Eccentric Pre-Stretch

Loads muscle spindle, SEC, and PEC. Three stretch variables: magnitude, rate, duration. Rate is most critical — faster stretch = stronger muscle spindle signal = greater concentric response. SEC stores energy as potential kinetic energy.

Phase 2: Amortization (Time to Rebound)

Time from eccentric cessation to concentric onset. Shorter = more power (stored energy used efficiently). Longer = energy wasted as heat, stretch reflex not activated. Primary training goal: decrease time to rebound.

Phase 3: Concentric Shortening

SEC recoil contributes 70–75% of concentric force increase. RC is 55–60% fast-twitch fibers — FT fibers most affected in RC injuries. Must perform at ≥80–100% MVC to recruit FT fibers needed for power.

Criterion-Based UE Plyometric Initiation
  • Soft tissue healing times respected
  • No pain at injury/surgical site
  • Swelling absent or minimal
  • ROM WNL (bilateral + normative comparison)
  • Proprioceptive tests WNL bilaterally
  • MMT ≥4/5 primary muscles; synergistic 5/5
  • Negative neurological tests; normal special tests
  • Isokinetic ≥80% peak torque bilaterally
  • CKCUEST ≤20% normative — males 21 touches, females 23 touches in 15 seconds
  • Seated shot put within 20% bilaterally
Most Critical Contraindication

Insufficient foundational strength — if criteria not met, motor control is not present for high-demand plyometrics. Recovery: 48–72 hours between sessions. Terminate if form deteriorates.

Elbow Osteoarthritis (OA)

▼
Presentation

Primary (idiopathic) vs. Secondary (history of trauma, instability, or repetitive stress). Diffuse pain and progressive loss of elbow ROM. Patient may report "grating" or "locking" sensation. Diffuse joint swelling possible — in severe cases, secondary nerve entrapment can cause hand numbness/tingling.

Conservative Treatment

Corticosteroid injections (pain flares) · Viscosupplementation (hyaluronic acid injection to joint) · Physical therapy: joint mobilization, ROM restoration, pain-free elbow strengthening, proximal stability, patient education for activity modification.

Surgical Treatment

Arthroscopy: loose body removal, joint resurfacing (debridement of osteophytes). Arthroplasty: Total Elbow Replacement (TEA) — indicated for end-stage OA with significant functional limitation; primary for elderly or lower-demand patients. Radiographic signs: decreased joint space + osteophyte formation.

Clinical Flag

Elbow OA is less common than shoulder, hip, or knee OA. Always consider secondary causes (prior dislocation, fracture, instability). Grating or locking = loose bodies until proven otherwise — assess for mechanical symptoms requiring arthroscopic removal.

Radiocapitellar Chondromalacia

▼
What Is It

Cartilage irritation and/or damage at the radiocapitellar joint. Progression of symptoms leads to Osteochondritis Dissecans (OCD) of the capitellum. Typically seen in adolescent athletes: throwers, gymnasts, swimmers. MOI: repetitive valgus stress → compressive forces at the lateral elbow (radiocapitellar joint).

Presentation

Very similar to OCD of capitellum. Lateral elbow pain with active elbow movements. Clicking, catching, popping, or locking may be present. Swelling and localized tenderness. Numbness/tingling may occur with aggravated symptoms.

Treatment

Conservative (same approach as OCD): Discontinue aggravating activities. PT focus: thoracic spine mobility · scapular stabilization · GH joint mobility and shoulder girdle flexibility · periscapular and RC strengthening · non-painful elbow mobility/strengthening · joint mobilizations (pain-free). Assess throwing mechanics and kinetic chain energy transfer. Surgery not indicated unless progresses to OCD or loose bodies cause mechanical symptoms.

Osteochondritis Dissecans (OCD) of the Capitellum

▼
Panner's Disease vs. OCD — Key Distinction

Do NOT confuse. Panner's = self-limiting osteochondrosis in younger children (avg 8yo), affects entire capitellum, heals spontaneously. OCD = adolescents (avg 12–16yo), focal lesion, does NOT reliably heal without intervention, risk of loose body formation and secondary OA.

Pathomechanics & Presentation

Etiology: focal artery injury → bone necrosis from lateral radiohumeral compressive forces (valgus stress during throwing/gymnastics). May include prior PMH of radiocapitellar chondromalacia. Insidious onset lateral elbow pain; decreased ROM; pain with active/resisted pronation + supination; locking/catching/clicking. Imaging: XR + MRI.

Treatment by Stability

Stable lesions (conservative): Discontinue aggravating activities 3–4 months. PT: thoracic spine mobility · scapular stabilization · GH mobility · periscapular + RC strengthening · non-painful elbow mobility + strengthening · joint mobs (pain-free). Unstable lesions: Debridement, loose body removal, marrow stimulation. OATS (Osteochondral Autologous Transfer System) may be best for unstable lesions.

OATS Evidence

Superior results vs. microfracture (Gudas et al. AJSM 2012; Arthroscopy 2013). 87.5% survival rate at 8 years (Robb et al. AOB 2012). 90% healing rates, excellent function, high return to sports (Iwasaki et al.; Takahara et al.; Yakamoto et al.). Donor site: non-weight bearing lateral femoral condyle. OATS outcome data at elbow mirrors knee superiority over microfracture.

Posterior Elbow Impingement

▼
Pathology & Presentation

Repetitive posterior abutment of bone and soft tissue in the posterior compartment → soft tissue inflammation + osteophyte formation → worsening mechanical symptoms. MOI: repetitive excessive/forceful terminal elbow extension. Athletes at risk: throwers (baseball, softball, javelin, shot put), martial arts, weightlifters. Pain in posterior elbow, increases with passive overpressure into hyperextension. Progressed cases: inability to fully extend due to stiffness, pain, or osteophyte bony abutment. UCL deficiency may coexist — medial instability → increased posterior compressive forces.

Treatment

Conservative: Good results if aggravating activities discontinued + patient is compliant. PT: inflammation reduction → ROM + joint mechanics restoration (joint mobs/STM) → dynamic control of motions → proximal stability (shoulder/scapula). Surgical: Arthroscopy for osteophyte removal if conservative fails. Diagnostic imaging: radiograph + MRI.

Clinical Note

Posterior elbow impingement and UCL deficiency are often concurrent in overhead throwing athletes. Always assess medial stability (valgus stress tests) when posterior impingement is suspected — UCL laxity drives increased compressive forces posteriorly.

Bursa & Tendon Pathology

Olecranon Bursitis

▼
Pathology & Presentation

Inflammation of the olecranon bursa (posterior elbow, tip of olecranon). MOI: direct impact to elbow tip, or repetitive/prolonged pressure through the olecranon. Also called "Student's Elbow" (prolonged elbow-on-desk pressure). Septic vs. Non-septic: septic bursitis = bacterial infection requiring aspiration + antibiotics; non-septic = mechanical inflammation. Acute: reports blow/impact; Chronic: gradual pain + swelling. Decreased elbow ROM, swelling, tenderness. May present as large fluctuant mass over olecranon.

PRICEMEM Framework

Protect area · Rest · Ice · Compression · Elevation · Manual Therapy · Early Motion · Medication (NSAIDs; antibiotics if septic). Aspiration may be performed if large or infected. Surgical (bursectomy): indicated for chronic cases that limit ROM or for infected bursitis that fails conservative management.

Septic Bursitis Screen

Signs of septic bursitis: fever, erythema (redness/warmth), rapidly enlarging bursa, systemically unwell. Aspiration fluid: turbid, elevated WBC. Refer for medical management if septic — PT does not treat infected bursitis acutely.

Distal Bicipital Tendinosis & Rupture

▼
Tendinosis — Presentation

Associated with heavy or repetitive elbow flexion/supination (manual labor, gymnastics, weightlifting, bodybuilding, rock climbing, throwing). MOI: repetitive supination (screwdriver) or elbow hyperextension. Pain: anterior aspect of distal arm; TTP of distal biceps belly, musculotendinous junction, or bicipital insertion at radial tuberosity. Reproduced with resisted isometrics of flexion and/or supination; also with passive shoulder + elbow extension.

Tendinosis — Treatment

Regain strength, endurance, and flexibility of the flexor/supinator mechanism. Strengthen shoulder stabilizers. E-stim, MHP, STM, IASTM, dry needling, KT taping, joint mobilizations. Eccentric training (progressive loading protocol). Surgery rarely indicated.

Rupture — Clinical Signs & Consequences

Partial or complete avulsion at musculotendinous junction or radial tuberosity. Almost exclusively in middle-aged males. MOI: sudden contraction against significant load with elbow at 90° flexion. Presentation: sharp tearing pain, anterior elbow pain (antecubital fossa), Popeye deformity, loss of flexion + supination strength. If not repaired: 30% loss of elbow flexion strength + 40% loss of supination strength.

Post-Op Protocol — Distal Biceps Repair

Phase I (0–7 days): Possible complete immobilization in 90° splint or articulating brace locked at 90°. Phase II (1–6 wks): Wks 1–3: passive elbow flexion full range; passive → AAROM → AROM extension gradually; hand/wrist AROM; shoulder AAROM working toward pain-free AROM; initiated passive supination. Wks 3–6: scapular stabilization + early RC strengthening; gradual full PROM + AROM flexion/extension and pronation/supination. Phase III (wks 6–8): Gradual biceps loading; PROM full; progress resisted biceps strengthening; wrist + forearm all planes. Phase IV (wks 8–12): Continue end range stretching; advance intensity forearm/hand strengthening (wrist extension included); begin global UE gym program at wk 12. Phase V (wks 12–16): Plyometric drills; return to sport per physician approval.

Triceps Tendinosis & Rupture

▼
Tendinosis — Presentation & Treatment

Chronic or repetitive strain of triceps tendon. Associated with competitive weightlifting, boxing, gymnastics, throwing sports, racquet sports. MOI: repetitive forceful elbow extension. Localized tenderness at triceps insertion (posterior elbow); aggravated with resisted elbow extension. Conservative: PRICEMEM + therapeutic exercise focusing on elbow extensor mechanism strength + closed kinetic chain elbow strengthening.

Rupture — Presentation

Tear of distal triceps tendon from olecranon insertion. MOI: deceleration force during elbow extension, or uncoordinated contraction of triceps against a flexing elbow. Partial tear: pain with resisted extension + passive flexion. Complete tear: loss of elbow extension strength, muscular deformity. Full strength will not return without surgery for complete tears. Partial incomplete tears may be treated conservatively with bracing + immobilization → progressive motion/strengthening.

Post-Op Protocol — Triceps Tendon Repair

Phase I — Max Protection (0–6 weeks): Elbow immobilizer. Restrictions: full passive elbow extension allowed; limit elbow flexion to 90°; no weight-bearing on operative arm. Focus: regain terminal elbow extension and flexion to 90°. Phase II — Progressive Stretching + Passive Motion (6–12 weeks): No immobilizer. Restrictions: do NOT advance elbow flexion >10° per week; avoid forceful stretch (tolerable discomfort only, not pain); no WB until 8 weeks, then WBAT. No resistance strengthening until 8 weeks. Phase III — Strengthening (weeks 12–26): No ROM restrictions. Gradually increase ROM exercises and strengthening. Avoid forceful eccentric activities, impact activities. Exercise advancement in slow increments — if pain develops, drop to earlier phase until pain-free.

Medial Epicondylalgia ("Golfer's Elbow")

▼
Pathology & Mechanism

Tendinopathy of the common flexor tendon at medial epicondyle: primary structures FCR, Pronator Teres (humeral head), and flexor-pronator tendon. 1/3 as common as lateral epicondylalgia. Overuse injury. Mechanism: repeated stress to flexor-pronator mass; UCL insufficiency fails to stabilize against valgus forces → flexor-pronator overloads (Glousman, Jobe et al. AJSM 1992). UCL and ME pathology frequently coexist in overhead throwing athletes — always assess both.

Presentation

Pain at medial epicondyle or mid-substance common flexor tendon. Pain during throwing (pitchers) or repetitive tasks. Ulnar nerve distribution NTB (distinguish from cubital tunnel — screen both). Painful/decreased grip strength. Decreased elbow ROM.

Conservative Treatment — 90% Success Rate

Rest, activity modification, local modalities (acute). Restore ROM + strengthen flexor-pronator mass. Gradual return to sport/activity. STM + IASTM to common flexor tendon. Progressive tendon loading. Cervicothoracic assessment (cervical spine involvement mimics ME). Proximal strength deficits (shoulder + scapular) often contributing.

Tests

ME Test 1 (active): slight elbow flex + supination + wrist flex; resist wrist/elbow extension → stresses common flexor tendon actively. ME Test 2 (passive): passively extend elbow + supinate + extend wrist with overpressure → passive stress. Also: palpation to medial epicondyle + common flexor mass; resisted wrist flexion + pronation; passive wrist extension + elbow extension.

Nerve Entrapment

Cubital Tunnel Syndrome — Three Zones

▼
Three Zones (Nirschl)

Zone 1: Proximal to the medial epicondyle. Zone 2: At the level of the medial epicondyle (retrocondylar groove) — most common compression site. Zone 3: Distal to the medial epicondyle (cubital tunnel proper). Localization of symptoms and Tinel's sign helps identify the zone.

MOI & Presentation

MOI: repetitive overuse of tight FCU → cubital tunnel entrapment; localized inflammation; cubitus valgus deformity. Presentation: medial elbow pain + radiating pain; NTB in ulnar distribution (ring + little fingers); worsens at night; + Tinel's at cubital tunnel; ULTT; + Wartenberg's sign (inability to adduct little finger); + Froment's sign (adductor pollicis weakness); progressive inability to separate fingers; atrophy or weakness of ulnar intrinsic hand muscles.

Conservative Treatment — Criteria

Recommended for patients with intermittent symptoms AND without changes in two-point discrimination or muscle atrophy. If atrophy or 2-point discrimination changes present = more severe involvement → consider surgical referral. Activity modification + protection over cubital tunnel (elbow pad) + limitation of repetitive forceful elbow extension + addressing examination impairments. STM, dry needling, KT taping, cupping, nerve gliding. Proximal/distal strength deficits (shoulder + wrist/hand). Neural tension.

Surgical Options

Surgical decompression of the cubital tunnel, or anterior transposition of the ulnar nerve (moves nerve to anterior elbow to relieve tension with flexion). Indicated in extreme/refractory cases or when conservative management fails after adequate trial with atrophy or persistent 2-point discrimination changes.

Pronator Syndrome & Radial Nerve Entrapment

▼
Pronator Syndrome

Compression of median nerve at the pronator teres. Insidious onset or repetitive pronation activity. Pain sites: anterior elbow, radial side of palm, palmar side of 1st–4th digits. Often associated with forearm "heaviness." Pressure 4cm distal to cubital crease + resisted pronation/elbow flexion/wrist flexion reproduces symptoms. Weakness: median nerve including AIN innervation. Paresthesias. Distinguish from AIN syndrome (pure motor) and CTS (wrist-level). Treatment: activity modification + flexibility/STM + flexor/pronator strengthening.

Radial Nerve — 4 Entrapments

Radial nerve is the most commonly injured peripheral nerve due to its spiral course across posterior humerus. Common with humerus fractures. 1. High Radial Nerve Palsy: Motor + sensory involvement. 2. Posterior Interosseous Nerve Syndrome (PINS): Motor involvement only (wrist/finger extensors weak). 3. Radial Tunnel Syndrome (RTS): Pain/tenderness/cramping proximal dorsal forearm; NO motor loss; mimics lateral epicondylalgia — important DDx. 4. Superficial Radial Nerve Palsy: Sensory disturbances dorsal hand only.

What Is Tommy John Surgery?

UCL reconstruction: native UCL (anterior bundle, primary medial stabilizer for valgus stress 30–120°) is replaced with a tendon graft (palmaris longus most common, or gracilis). MWM for lateral epicondylalgia — technique, immediate effects, and Bisset et al key finding.
tap
Technique: laterally directed glide to proximal forearm during painful grip; ~1.9 N/cm force; 6–10 reps; include self-MWM at home. Immediate effects: increased pain-free grip force + reduced pressure pain threshold + improved ULTT radial bias + sympathoexcitation. Bisset et al RCT (n=198): MWM+exercise vs injection vs wait-and-see. At 6 weeks: NNT=3 vs wait-and-see. Over 12 months: 90% reduction in recurrence risk vs injection (5/66 vs 47/65 recurrences). Mechanism: non-opioid descending inhibition via dPAG (serotonergic + noradrenergic).
Tendinopathy — is eccentric loading superior to concentric or HSR in this scenario? What does the evidence say?
tap
No convincing evidence that isolated eccentric loading is superior to concentric or other loading regimes at comparable loads (Couppé et al, JOSPT 2015). Multiple RCTs: eccentric = concentric = combined for pain and function. Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) shows additional biochemical benefits (increased collagen, reduced glycation, structural changes) not seen with eccentric alone. Key principle: load magnitude and duration drive adaptation, not contraction direction. Tendinopathy is NOT inflammatory — "tendinitis" is outdated.

Three phases of plyometrics — what is the amortization phase and why does its duration matter in this scenario?
tap
Three phases: (1) Eccentric pre-stretch — loads muscle spindle + SEC/PEC; stores energy. (2) Amortization ("time to rebound") — time from eccentric cessation to concentric onset. Shorter amortization = more power (stored energy used efficiently). Longer = energy wasted as heat, stretch reflex not activated. Primary training goal: decrease time to rebound. (3) Concentric shortening — SEC recoil contributes 70–75% of force increase. RC is 55–60% fast-twitch fibers — plyometrics selectively recruit FT fibers at ≥80% MVC.

OCD of Capitellum vs. In this case, Panner's Disease — key distinctions and when OATS is used.
tap
Panner's Disease: younger children (avg 8yo), affects entire capitellum, self-limiting, heals spontaneously. OCD: adolescents (avg 12–16yo), focal lesion from lateral radiohumeral compressive forces (valgus/throwing/gymnastics), does NOT reliably heal, risk of loose body formation + secondary OA. Stable lesions: conservative 3–4 months (T-spine mob, scap stab, RC strength). Unstable lesions: debridement or OATS (Osteochondral Autologous Transfer). OATS: 87.5% survival at 8 years (Robb et al.); superior to microfracture (Gudas et al. AJSM 2012). Donor site: non–weight bearing lateral femoral condyle.
Distal biceps tendon rupture — consequences if unrepaired and post-op Phase I.
tap
If NOT repaired: 30% loss of elbow flexion strength + 40% loss of supination strength (Morrey et al.). Rupture occurs almost exclusively in middle-aged males; MOI = sudden contraction at 90° flexion against significant load. Presentation: anterior elbow pain, Popeye deformity, loss of flex + supination. Post-op Phase I (0–7 days): possible complete immobilization in 90° splint or articulating brace locked at 90°. Cubital tunnel syndrome — three zones, conservative criteria, and surgical options.
tap
Three zones (Nirschl): Zone 1 = proximal to medial epicondyle; Zone 2 = retrocondylar groove at medial epicondyle (most common); Zone 3 = distal (cubital tunnel proper). Conservative criteria: intermittent symptoms WITHOUT 2-point discrimination changes or muscle atrophy. Signs: Tinel's at cubital tunnel + Wartenberg's sign (inability to adduct 5th finger) + Froment's sign + nocturnal NTB ring/little. Surgical options: in-situ decompression OR anterior transposition of the ulnar nerve. Atrophy or 2-point discrimination changes = refer for surgical assessment.
Radial nerve — four entrapment types and which one mimics lateral epicondylalgia.
tap
Radial nerve = most commonly injured peripheral nerve (spiral course across posterior humerus). Four entrapments: (1) High Radial Nerve Palsy — motor + sensory. (2) PINS (Posterior Interosseous Nerve Syndrome) — motor only; wrist/finger extension weakness. (3) Radial Tunnel Syndrome (RTS) — pain + tenderness + cramping proximal dorsal forearm; NO motor loss; closely mimics lateral epicondylalgia — important DDx. (4) Superficial Radial Nerve Palsy — sensory only; dorsal hand. RTS: palpation over radial tunnel (3–4cm distal to lateral epicondyle) + resisted supination provokes.
LCL reconstruction post-op — what position must be maintained early and why in this scenario?
tap
Forearm must be kept in full pronation in early phases (0–4 weeks). Rationale: the LCL complex prevents posterolateral rotary instability (PLRI). The most destabilizing position post-op is forearm supination combined with valgus + axial loading — this directly stresses the reconstructed ligament. No aggressive supination stretching early. Supination introduced gradually only at 4 weeks. Phase milestones: brace 30°→full flex wks 2–4; resisted ER/IR/biceps/triceps at wk 12; full gym at wk 16; unrestricted at 1 year. 5° extension limitation acceptable as protection.
Medial epicondylalgia — what structure is the primary driver and what is the conservative success rate in this scenario?
tap
Primary structure: common flexor tendon (FCR, Pronator Teres humeral head, flexor-pronator mass). 1/3 as common as lateral epicondylalgia. Mechanism: UCL insufficiency fails to resist valgus → flexor-pronator overload (Glousman, Jobe et al. AJSM 1992) — UCL + ME pathology often coexist in overhead throwers. 90% conservative success rate. Treatment: rest + activity modification in acute stage → restore ROM → flexor-pronator strengthening → gradual return to sport. Cervicothoracic screen always. Assess UCL stability — medial instability drives ME tendon overload.
Olecranon bursitis — septic vs. In this case, non-septic and PRICEMEM framework.
tap
Septic bursitis: bacterial infection — fever, rapid enlargement, erythema, systemically unwell, turbid aspirate with elevated WBC → requires antibiotics ± aspiration; PT does not treat acute septic bursitis. Non-septic: mechanical inflammation from direct impact or repetitive pressure (Student's Elbow). PRICEMEM: Protect · Rest · Ice · Compression · Elevation · Manual Therapy · Early Motion · Medication. Surgical bursectomy for chronic cases limiting ROM or refractory infected bursitis. Always distinguish septic vs. non-septic before initiating PT.
