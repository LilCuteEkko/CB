Cervical Spine — Comprehensive Anatomy
The cervical spine is the most mobile region of the vertebral column (1:3 disc-to-vertebral height ratio). It houses the vertebral arteries, the spinal cord at its widest, and the brachial plexus. Functionally divided into upper cervical (O–C2) and lower cervical (C3–C7).
Osteology — Cervical Vertebrae
Typical Cervical Vertebrae (C3–C7)
  • Body — small, wider side-to-side than AP. Uncinate processes on superior lateral edges (C3–C7) form uncovertebral joints (of Luschka) — prone to bone spur formation → foraminal stenosis
  • Vertebral foramen — large, triangular (accommodates cervical cord enlargement)
  • Transverse processes — contain transverse foramen for vertebral artery (C1–C6). C7 may have small/absent foramen
  • Articular facets — superior facets face superoposterior; inferior facets face inferoanterior. Orientation → good rotation and lateral flexion
  • Spinous processes — typically bifid (C3–C5). C7 = vertebra prominens (longest SP, easily palpated)
Atypical Vertebrae — Atlas (C1) & Axis (C2)
  • Atlas (C1) — ring-shaped; NO body, NO spinous process. Anterior + posterior arches with tubercles. Wide transverse processes (palpate inferior to mastoid). Superior facets articulate with occipital condyles (flex/ext — nodding “yes”). Inferior facets articulate with C2 (rotation). Anterior arch has facet for dens
  • Axis (C2) — characterized by dens (odontoid process) projecting superiorly from body. Forms pivot for rotation. C1 + occiput rotate as unit on C2 (~50% of cervical rotation). Dens secured by transverse ligament, alar ligaments, and apical ligament
  • Vertebral artery — enters transverse foramen at C6, ascends through C5–C1, makes sharp turns around C1 lateral mass, enters foramen magnum. Vulnerable during cervical rotation/extension → VBI (vertebrobasilar insufficiency) screening essential

Craniovertebral Joints & Ligaments

Atlanto-Occipital Joint (O–C1)

Synovial condyloid joint. Occipital condyles on superior facets of C1. Primary motion: flexion/extension (nodding). ~10–15° flex, ~25° ext. Anterior + posterior atlanto-occipital membranes connect C1 arches to occiput (stability).

Atlantoaxial Joints (C1–C2) — 3 Joints

Median atlantoaxial: dens on anterior arch of C1 — pivot joint. Provides ~50% of all cervical rotation. C1 and occiput rotate as a unit on C2. Lateral (R/L) facet joints: synovial plane joints — orientation allows gliding for rotation. Shape of facets causes C1 to “screw down” 2–3mm during rotation (head depresses slightly).

Upper Cervical Ligaments

Transverse ligament: extends between R/L lateral masses of C1 — holds dens against anterior arch. Part of cruciate (cruciform) ligament complex = transverse lig + superior/inferior longitudinal bands. This is the primary stabilizer of the dens.

Alar ligaments: sides of dens → lateral margins of foramen magnum. Limit excessive rotation. Tested clinically with lateral flexion (should limit contralateral side-bending).

Tectorial membrane: continuation of PLL from C2 to foramen magnum (posterior to cruciate lig).

Apical ligament: apex of dens to foramen magnum.

Clinical: Transverse ligament rupture (RA, trauma, Down syndrome) → atlantoaxial instability → cord compression risk. Sharp-Purser test and alar ligament test assess this. ADI (atlanto-dens interval) >3mm in adults = instability.

Suboccipital Triangle

Boundaries: Medial = rectus capitis posterior major. Lateral = obliquus capitis superior. Inferior = obliquus capitis inferior. Floor = posterior arch of atlas + posterior atlanto-occipital membrane. Roof = semispinalis capitis + longissimus capitis. Contents: vertebral artery (makes sharp turns here — vulnerable), suboccipital nerve (C1), blood vessels. Clinical: manual therapy to this region can affect vertebral artery flow — VBI screening mandatory before upper cervical manipulation.

Cervical Plexus & Triangles of the Neck

Cervical plexus = C1–C4Phrenic N = C3–C5Posterior triangle = brachial plexus exit▼
Cervical Plexus (VPR C1–C4)

Sensory branches: Lesser occipital N (C2) — posterior skull. Greater auricular N (C2–C3) — anterior ear. Transverse cervical N (C2–C3) — anterior triangle skin. Supraclavicular N (C3–C4) — shoulder region.

Motor branches: Phrenic N (C3, C4, C5) — descends on anterior surface of anterior scalene → motor supply to diaphragm. “C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive.” SCI above C3 → ventilator-dependent. Ansa cervicalis (C1–C3) — supplies infrahyoid muscles.

Triangles of the Neck

Posterior triangle: Bordered by posterior SCM, anterior trapezius, superior clavicle. Contains: EJV, brachial plexus trunks, CN XI, subclavian artery, transverse cervical artery. Subdivided by omohyoid into occipital and supraclavicular triangles.

Anterior triangle: Bordered by anterior SCM, midline, mandible. Contains: hyoid muscles, CCA/ECA/ICA, IJV, CN X/XI/XII, thyroid, larynx, pharynx. Four subdivisions: submandibular, carotid, muscular, submental.

Carotid triangle: Most clinically important — contains CCA bifurcation, carotid sinus (baroreceptor) and carotid body (chemoreceptor). Clinical: modality contraindication zone (US, ESTIM near carotid).

Cervical Coupled Motion & Biomechanics

Upper C: rotation causes 2–3mm depressionLower C: LF + rotation to SAME sideOpposite coupling in lumbar▼
Coupled Motion Patterns

Upper cervical (C1–C2): As C1 rotates on C2, the facet shape causes C1 to “screw down” — head depresses 2–3mm during rotation. No true lateral flexion at this level.

Lower cervical (C3–C7): Lateral flexion and rotation are coupled to the SAME side (ipsilateral). When you side-bend right, the vertebral body rotates right (SPs go left/contralateral). Rotation:LF ratio decreases from C3 (2:3) to C7 (1:7).

Contrast with lumbar: In the lumbar spine, lateral flexion and rotation couple to OPPOSITE sides (contralateral). Vertebral body rotates contralateral to the side of LF (SPs go ipsilateral/concave side).

Thoracic transition: Upper thoracic couples like cervical (ipsilateral); lower thoracic transitions to lumbar pattern (contralateral). The exact transition point varies by individual.

Cervical Spine
The cervical spine is inherently fragile and patients remember their neck injuries. 4th leading cause of days lost of life years. 30% US prevalence. Women more affected. People with both LBP and C-spine pain have worse prognosis. High roles conflict (balancing work/life), depressed mood, and perceived muscle tension are risk factors.

WAD, Cervicogenic Headache & Radiculopathy

WAD: 55% have Sx after 10+ yearsCGH: upper cervical dysfunctionC7 = most involved radiculopathy▼
Whiplash Associated Disorder (WAD)

Acceleration/deceleration injury. Soft tissue, ligamentous, bony, neuro injury. 3 recovery pathways: (1) Quick recovery 2–3 months. (2) Moderate with lingering symptoms for years. (3) Poor recovery with significant disability. Things hindering recovery: pain catastrophizing, PTSD, high disability, cold hyperalgesia. 55% still have symptoms after 10+ years. Treatment: education (remain active), mobility exercises, reduce cervical collar, return to activity, tolerable ROM, manual therapy — don't do too much as it may lead to chronic symptoms.

Cervicogenic Headache

Stem from upper cervical dysfunction or suboccipital muscular dysfunction. Trigeminal nerve plays a role. Known trigger mechanism (posture, neck pain). Females 3rd decade+. Unilateral > bilateral. ↓ cervical ROM. Painful upper cervical manual palpation. ↓ deep neck flexor strength. Referral patterns: C0–1 = occiput. C1–2 = "ram's horn." C2–3 = "halo." C3–4 = "rock behind eye." Treatment: C/T manips, soft tissue, deep neck flexor endurance, postural training.

Cervical Radiculopathy by Root Level

C5: "Epaulet" distribution — superior shoulder to lateral mid-arm. Absence of painful shoulder ROM and impingement signs. C6: Neck → lateral biceps → lateral forearm → thumb/index finger (median). C7 (most involved): Posterior shoulder → scapula → triceps → posterior forearm → posterior fingers (radial). C8: Medial arm/forearm → medial hand → 4th–5th digit (ulnar). Difficulty with ADLs using hands.

CPR for Cervical Radiculopathy

(+) Spurling's + (+) Distraction + (+) ULTT median nerve + C/S rotation <60°. 3 positive tests: +LR 6.1. 4 positive tests: +LR 30.3.

Brachial Plexus Injuries

DDx vs. radiculopathy: is it from BP or nerve roots? MOI: traction (yanking on shoulder). Erb's (upper BP, C5–C6): ↑ angle between neck and shoulder → "waiter's tip" posture. Klumpke's (lower BP, C8–T1): Upward pull of limb → "claw hand." Stingers/Burners: Common in football (poor tackle), head sidebends away from injured BP, UE radiating symptoms and NBT.

TOS (Thoracic Outlet Syndrome)

Vascular vs. neurogenic vs. both. Compression at: (1) Scalenes (interscalene triangle — 0.2% have cervical rib at C7). (2) First rib (costoclavicular interval — clavicle depression or first rib elevation from scalene hypertonicity). (3) Pec minor (hyperabduction, hypertonicity, shoulder ABD+ER at 90°). Vascular: changes in hand temp/color, non-dermatomal patterns, arm heaviness.

Key Joint Considerations

O-A Joint: Facilitates flexion–extension (combined 15–20°) but impedes other movements. Axial rotation forced = 8°. Hypermobility if rotation >8°. A-A Joint: Large arch of rotation. Only AA and TL junction provide true spinal rotation. Upper Crossed Syndrome: Tight levator scap, upper traps, SCM, pecs. Weak deep neck flexors, lower scap stabilizers. Forward head, elevated/protracted shoulders, scap winging.

Cervical Fractures

Odontoid = 60% of C2 FxJefferson = axial loading C1▼
Odontoid Fractures

60% of all axis fractures and 10–18% of all C-spine fractures. Most common C-spine fracture in people >70. MOI: high-energy trauma (MVA), or falls/hyperextension in elderly. Type 1: Oblique avulsion from dens tip. Type 2: Through neck of odontoid. Type 3: Extends into body of C2 (15–40% of odontoid fractures).

Jefferson Fracture

Lateral mass fracture of C1 and disruption of C1 ring. Results from axial loading on atlas. Generally minimal neurologic deficit and good prognosis. Type I: bilateral single-arch. Type II: concurrent anterior and posterior arch (classic 4-point break). Type III: lateral mass fracture extending into arch.

Transverse Ligament Injury

Type 1: Disruption of ligament substance without osseous component — needs surgery. Type 2: Fracture/avulsion of tubercle on C1 lateral mass without ligament disruption — heals with orthosis.

Cervical Myelopathy CPR (3/5 = Strong Predictive Value)

(1) Babinski sign. (2) Inverted supinator (normal = elbow flexion/wrist radial dev; abnormal = elbow extension or finger flexion). (3) Hoffmann sign. (4) Ataxic gait pattern. (5) Age >45. Other presentations: non-myotomal weakness especially in LE flexors (hip flexors, DF), "clunkiness" walking, spasms in back/legs, LBP.

What are the 5 D's, 3 N's, and 1 A for VBI screening, and when must you test?
tap
5 D's: Dizzy, Diplopia (blurred vision), Drop attacks (loss of power/consciousness), Dysphagia (swallowing issues), Dysarthria (speech problems). 3 N's: Nystagmus, Nausea/vomiting, Neuro symptoms. 1 A: Ataxia (lack of muscle coordination). Plus severe headache. When to test: Before ANY cervical manipulation, when patient complains of any of these symptoms, and as part of normal cervical exam to assess irritability. Test seated first (10-sec holds in extension, rotation, combined) → progress to supine if negative. At-risk populations: RA, Down syndrome, Marfan syndrome.
What is the CPR for cervical radiculopathy and what are the likelihood ratios?
tap
4 tests: (1) Cervical rotation <60° to affected side. (2) (+) Spurling's (passive SB + axial compression reproduces arm symptoms). (3) (+) Distraction (relief with cervical distraction). (4) (+) ULTT A (median nerve tension). 3 positive = +LR 6.1. 4 positive = +LR 30.3 (extremely high probability of cervical radiculopathy). Most common roots: C7 (most involved) → C6 → C5. Disc herniations are rare <30 yo and uncommon in elderly (stenotic cause more likely).
