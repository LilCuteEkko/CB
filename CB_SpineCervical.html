Cervical Spine

The cervical spine is the most mobile spinal region and the most vulnerable to instability. It houses the spinal cord, the vertebral arteries supplying the brainstem, and 8 nerve roots that control the upper extremities. Cervical dysfunction can produce neck pain, headaches, arm pain, dizziness, and even myelopathy. PTs must be able to differentiate radiculopathy from myelopathy, screen for vertebral artery insufficiency before manipulation, and apply evidence-based clinical prediction rules. This module covers anatomy, upper cervical screening, special tests with diagnostic accuracy, radiculopathy, myelopathy, cervicogenic headache, and treatment classification.

Cervical Spine Anatomy — Clinical Review
7 cervical vertebrae, 5 intervertebral discs (C2–C7), 8 nerve roots (C1–C8). The cervical spine is the most mobile spinal region and the most vulnerable to instability. C-spine nerve roots exit ABOVE their numbered vertebra (C6 root exits above C6 vertebra) — EXCEPT C8, which exits below C7.

Deep Neck Flexors — Cervical Stabilizers

▼
Clinical Importance

Weakness/inhibition of deep neck flexors is the hallmark finding in cervical spine dysfunction. These muscles provide segmental stabilization — like multifidus in the lumbar spine. Longus colli is the primary deep cervical stabilizer. Loss of feedforward activation in neck pain. Craniocervical flexion test (CCFT) assesses deep neck flexor endurance — key outcome measure.

Upper Crossed Syndrome (Janda)

Tight: upper trapezius, levator scapulae, SCM, pectoralis major/minor, suboccipitals. Weak: deep neck flexors, lower trapezius, serratus anterior, rhomboids. Result: forward head posture, protracted/elevated shoulders, increased cervical lordosis + thoracic kyphosis. Contributes to cervicogenic headache, TOS, and impingement.

Before ANY cervical manipulation, screen for upper cervical instability and VBI. These tests identify patients who should NOT receive high-velocity thrust techniques to the cervical spine.

Canadian C-Spine Rules (Stiell, 2001)

Developed for trauma patients to determine if cervical XR is needed.
High-risk factors (need XR): age ≥65, dangerous mechanism (fall >1m, axial load, MVA >100 km/h, bicycle collision), paresthesias in extremities.
Low-risk factors (allow ROM assessment): simple rear-end MVA, sitting in ED, ambulatory at any time, delayed onset neck pain, no midline tenderness. If low-risk: can patient actively rotate 45° each direction? If yes → no XR needed.
SN: 99.4% for clinically significant c-spine injury. Validated in >8,000 patients.

Wainner’s CPR for Cervical Radiculopathy (Wainner et al., 2003)

4 criteria: (1) Positive ULTT-A (median nerve), (2) Involved-side cervical rotation <60°, (3) Positive distraction test, (4) Positive Spurling’s test.
3 of 4 positive: +LR 6.1 (moderately shifts probability toward radiculopathy).
4 of 4 positive: +LR 30.3 (strongly rules in radiculopathy). This cluster is more accurate than any individual test. Reference standard: EMG + nerve conduction studies. Stage: derivation (Wainner 2003), case series validation (Waldrop 2006). Highly specific cluster — best used to rule IN radiculopathy.

Presentation Pattern

Unilateral arm pain > neck pain (arm pain dominant). Follows dermatomal distribution. Worse with cervical extension + ipsilateral sidebending (closes IVF). Better with distraction, contralateral sidebending, shoulder abduction (Bakody’s). May have numbness, tingling, weakness in specific myotome/dermatome. Natural history: most resolve conservatively within 6–12 weeks. Surgical referral if: progressive neurological deficit, failure of 6–12 weeks conservative care, or myelopathy signs.

CPR for Cervical Traction (Raney et al., 2009)

5 criteria predicting success with cervical traction + exercise: (1) Peripheralization with lower c-spine mobility testing, (2) Positive shoulder abduction test (Bakody’s), (3) Age ≥55, (4) Positive ULTT-A, (5) Positive neck distraction test. 3 of 5 positive: +LR 4.81 (pretest 44% → posttest 79% success probability). Stage: derivation. Suggests traction is most effective for nerve root compression with positive neural tension signs.

Cervical Myelopathy
Compression of the spinal cord itself (not just a nerve root). The most common cause of spinal cord dysfunction in adults >55. This is a red flag — progressive myelopathy requires surgical referral. Do NOT manipulate a myelopathic cervical spine.
Cervical Myelopathy CPR (Cook et al., 2010) — 3 of 5 = Strong Predictive Value

Cervicogenic Headache
Headache originating from cervical spine dysfunction (C0–C3). Often misdiagnosed as migraine or tension-type headache. One of the most treatable headache types with PT — strong evidence for manual therapy + exercise.
Diagnostic Criteria (Cervicogenic Headache International Study Group)

Unilateral headache (side-locked, does NOT shift sides). Provoked by neck movement or sustained postures. Decreased cervical ROM. Ipsilateral neck/shoulder/arm pain (non-radicular). No shifting to other side. Known trigger: posture, neck pain. Females > males, 3rd decade+. Referral patterns: C0–C1 = occiput. C1–C2 = “ram’s horn” (temporal wrap).
C2–C3 = “halo” (forehead band).
C3–C4 = “rock behind eye.”

Flexion-Rotation Test (FRT) — Best Single Test

Patient fully flexes cervical spine (chin to chest), then PT passively rotates head. Normal: ≥44° rotation each side. Positive: loss of ≥10° compared to other side, or reproduction of headache. Tests C1–C2 rotation (flexion locks out lower segments, isolating C1–C2). SN: 90–91%. SP: 88–90%. Best diagnostic accuracy of any single test for cervicogenic headache (Ogince et al., 2007; Hall et al., 2010).

Evidence-Based Treatment

Strong evidence: cervical and thoracic thrust manipulation + deep neck flexor strengthening (Jull et al., 2002 — RCT, n=200). Combined manual therapy + exercise was superior to either alone.
Cervical SNAGs (Mulligan) for C1–C2 rotation restriction.
Suboccipital release. Postural correction (forward head/upper cross).
CCFT training for deep neck flexor endurance. Treatment typically 6–8 sessions over 6 weeks. 72% of patients report significant improvement with combined approach.

Evidence-Based Management
Key clinical prediction rules and evidence summaries for cervical spine management. Know these for the NPTE and for clinical decision-making.

CPR for Cervical Manipulation (Tseng et al., 2006)

▼
Thoracic Manipulation for Neck Pain

Patients with mechanical neck pain who benefit most from thoracic manipulation: Symptom duration <30 days, no symptoms distal to shoulder, looking up does NOT aggravate, FABQ-PA <12, decreased upper thoracic kyphosis, cervical extension ROM <30°. Thoracic manipulation is safer than cervical manipulation (no vertebral artery risk) and has strong evidence for mechanical neck pain. Manipulation + exercise superior to exercise alone.

McKenzie / MDT for Cervical Spine

▼
Centralization & Classification

Centralization: repeated movements cause symptoms to move from peripheral (arm) toward midline (neck). Strong prognostic indicator — patients who centralize have significantly better outcomes.
Peripheralization: symptoms move from midline to periphery. Poor prognostic sign.
Derangement: MC classification. Symptoms change with repeated movements. Extension often centralizes, flexion often peripheralizes (but assess individually).
Dysfunction: pain at end-range only, no change with repeated movements. Treated with stretching at end-range.
Postural: symptoms with sustained positions only. Corrected with postural education.

Clinical Examples
Applied clinical scenarios demonstrating examination findings, clinical reasoning, and treatment decision-making.
Clinical Example 1 — Wainner’s CPR

48-year-old with right C6 radiculopathy pattern. ULTT-A positive, cervical rotation 50° (<60°), distraction positive, Spurling’s positive. 4/4 = +LR 30.3. No myelopathy signs. Treatment: cervical traction, thoracic manipulation, neural mobilization, deep neck flexor training.

Clinical Example 2 — Myelopathy Red Flags

61-year-old with bilateral hand clumsiness, ataxic gait, Lhermitte’s-type symptoms. Hoffmann’s bilateral, Babinski right, hyperreflexia, wide-based gait. Myelopathy CPR 4/5. DO NOT manipulate. Urgent surgical referral.

C6–C7 disc herniation. Which root is compressed? Name the deficits?
tap
C7 nerve root (exits below C6–C7 disc). Dermatomal: middle finger, posterior forearm. Myotome: triceps weakness, wrist flexors, finger extensors. Reflex: triceps diminished (C7–C8). MC cervical disc level involved.
Describe how to differentiate radiculopathy from myelopathy?
tap
Radiculopathy (root/LMN): unilateral, dermatomal, hyporeflexia at level, specific myotome weakness. Myelopathy (cord/UMN): bilateral, non-dermatomal, hyperreflexia + Babinski + Hoffmann + clonus, ataxic gait, hand clumsiness, LE weakness > UE. Myelopathy = surgical referral, NO manipulation.
Best single test for cervicogenic headache and what’s the diagnostic accuracy in this scenario?
tap
Flexion-Rotation Test (FRT). Full cervical flexion (locks lower segments) then passive rotation. Tests C1–C2 rotation. Positive: ≥10° loss compared to other side or headache reproduction. SN: 90–91%, SP: 88–90%. Best accuracy of any single cervicogenic HA test.
When should you NOT manipulate the cervical spine in this scenario?
tap
Positive VBI screening (5 D’s, 3 N’s, 1 A). Positive Sharp-Purser (transverse ligament instability, ADI >3mm). Positive alar ligament test. Signs of myelopathy (UMN signs). RA with C-spine involvement (transverse ligament erosion). Down syndrome (atlantoaxial instability — screen before sports). Active infection, tumor, fracture.
What is centralization and why is it clinically important in this scenario?
tap
Centralization: repeated movements cause symptoms to move from peripheral (arm/distal) toward midline (neck/central). It is the strongest prognostic indicator for good outcomes in spinal pain. Patients who centralize respond well to directional preference exercises (McKenzie). Peripheralization = poor prognostic sign.
Normal neck flexor endurance time vs abnormal in this scenario?
tap
Normal: ≥39 seconds. Abnormal: ≤24 seconds. Supine chin-retracted head hold ~1” above table. Patients with neck pain average ~24 sec vs ~39 sec in healthy controls. Correlates with deep neck flexor dysfunction, whiplash, and chronic neck pain.
