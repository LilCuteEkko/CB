Wrist & Hand — Anatomy & Clinical Examination
The wrist and hand contain more pain receptors per area than any other region in the body. A systematic examination is critical — not every test is done every visit. Use clinical reasoning to select what is relevant to the presentation.
Key Anatomy — Wrist Complex
  • Radius & Ulna — distal radius is primary load-bearer (80%); ulna articulates via TFCC
  • DRUJ — distal radioulnar joint; TFCC is the primary stabilizer
  • TFCC (Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex) — stabilizes DRUJ; cushions ulnar carpus; attaches ulna to triquetrum; commonly injured with fall on outstretched hand
  • Proximal carpal row — scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform
  • Distal carpal row — trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate
  • Scapholunate ligament — most important intercarpal ligament; disruption = instability; Watson test assesses this
  • 6 Dorsal extensor compartments — numbered lateral to medial; 1st (APL+EPB) = DeQuervain's target
  • Carpal tunnel — floor: carpal bones; roof: transverse carpal ligament; contents: 9 flexor tendons + median nerve
  • Guyon's Canal — ulnar nerve and artery; between pisiform and hamate hook
Wrist Quadrants — DDx by Location
Radial

1st CMC OA · DeQuervain's · Intersection syndrome · Scaphoid fracture · Scapholunate instability · Thumb UCL sprain · Radial styloid fracture

Ulnar

TFCC tear · DRUJ instability · Ulnar impaction syndrome · Hamate fracture · Guyon's canal · ECU tendinopathy · Triangular fibrocartilage injury

Dorsal

Ganglion cyst (most common) · Extensor tendon injuries · Intersection syndrome (proximal) · Carpal boss · DISI/VISI instability

Volar

Carpal tunnel syndrome · Flexor tendon injuries · Ganglion cyst · Volar ganglion · Volar wrist pain with DRUJ instability

Clinical Examination Framework
Systems Review
  • Vascular — Allen's test (radial + ulnar artery patency), capillary refill, skin color
  • Neurological — screen for double-crush syndrome; proximal causes always considered
  • Psychological — pain catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, CRPS risk factors
  • MSK — global UE assessment; cervical screen when nerve symptoms present
Pain Density Note

The hand has more pain receptors per surface area than any other body region. Pain perception and interpretation must account for this — hand pain is not always proportional to structural injury.

Direct Examination
  • Peripheral joint screen — assess each joint systematically
  • Palpation — bony landmarks, tendons, joint lines, pulses
  • Mobility — can they use the ROM they have? Functional ROM assessment
  • ROM with overpressure — wrist flex/ext, RD/UD, forearm pronation/supination, digit MCP/PIP/DIP
  • Provocative testing — select based on hypothesized diagnosis
Clinical Principle

Don't do every test every visit — do what is appropriate to the clinical presentation. Test selection should follow from your working hypothesis, not from habit.

Strength & Neurosensory Testing

Strength

  • Grip — Jamar dynamometer; 5 positions; compare bilaterally
  • Lateral pinch — key pinch; thumb IP adductor pollicis
  • 2-point (pencil-off-ground) — index + thumb; tripod refinement
  • 3-point (index, long, thumb) — tripod pinch

Neurological

  • Monofilament testing — threshold test of nerve sensitivity; used for peripheral nerve injuries and post-surgical nerve recovery
  • 2-point discrimination — tests nerve's ability to distinguish between 2 points; acute measure of nerve function; normal = <6mm moving, <10mm static
Outcome Measures — Wrist & Hand

DASH / QuickDASH

Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder, and Hand. UE function. MCID=8, MDC=13. Full spectrum UE.

PRWE

Patient-Rated Wrist Evaluation. Wrist-specific. Combines pain (0–50) + function (0–50) = 0–100 total.

FOTO / UEFI

Focus On Therapeutic Outcomes / Upper Extremity Functional Index. Functional outcome tracking tools.

Grasp Patterns

Cylindrical, spherical, hook, diagonal, lateral pinch, tip pinch. Functional grasp assessment for return to work/sport.

Carpal Bones, Joints & Ligaments — Detailed Reference

▼
Carpal Bones — 8 Bones, 2 Rows

Mnemonic (proximal → distal, lateral → medial): Some Lovers Try Positions That They Can’t Handle.

Proximal row: Scaphoid (most commonly fractured carpal; blood supply enters distally → proximal pole AVN risk; snuffbox tenderness), Lunate (most commonly dislocated; sits in lunate fossa of radius), Triquetrum (ulnar side), Pisiform (sesamoid within FCU tendon; not a true carpal row member).

Distal row: Trapezium (articulates with 1st MC; saddle-shaped CMC joint of thumb), Trapezoid (smallest; articulates with 2nd MC), Capitate (largest; articulates with 3rd MC; keystone of wrist), Hamate (hook of hamate = Guyon’s canal landmark; ulnar nerve passes over it).

Key Joints

Radiocarpal: distal radius + scaphoid/lunate (80% load through radius). Biaxial condyloid. Flex/ext + radial/ulnar deviation.

Midcarpal: between proximal and distal carpal rows. Contributes ~50% of wrist flexion.

1st CMC (thumb): trapezium + 1st MC. Saddle joint — most mobile CMC. Flex/ext, abd/add, opposition. Most common site of hand OA. Grind test assesses this joint.

MCP joints: Condyloid. Flex/ext, abd/add. Collateral ligaments taut in flexion — always splint MCP in flexion to prevent contracture.

PIP/DIP joints: Hinge. Flex/ext only. Volar plate prevents hyperextension.

Critical Ligaments

Scapholunate ligament: most important intercarpal ligament. Disruption = scapholunate dissociation (Terry Thomas sign on X-ray — gap >3mm). Watson test assesses this. Can lead to DISI pattern.

Lunotriquetral ligament: disruption → VISI pattern.

Palmar radiocarpal ligaments: allow hand to follow radius during supination. Stronger than dorsal.

Collateral ligaments (radial & ulnar): stabilize wrist medially and laterally.

Carpal Tunnel & Guyon’s Canal — Detailed Anatomy

▼
Carpal Tunnel

Borders: Floor = carpal bones (concave arch). Roof = flexor retinaculum (transverse carpal ligament). Radial attachment: scaphoid tubercle + trapezium. Ulnar attachment: pisiform + hook of hamate.

Contents (10 structures):

• 4 FDS tendons (in common flexor synovial sheath)
• 4 FDP tendons (in common flexor synovial sheath)
• 1 FPL tendon (in its own synovial sheath)
• Median nerve (most anterior/superficial structure — first compressed)

NOT in carpal tunnel: Ulnar nerve (passes through Guyon's canal), palmaris longus (inserts into flexor retinaculum roof), FCR (has its own fibro-osseous tunnel).

Carpal tunnel syndrome: Most common peripheral nerve entrapment. Compression of median nerve → numbness/tingling in thumb, index, middle, and radial half of ring finger. Thenar atrophy in severe cases. Tests: Tinel's, Phalen's, Durkan's compression.

Guyon’s Canal (Ulnar Tunnel)

Tunnel between pisiform (medial) and hook of hamate (lateral). Roof = palmar carpal ligament + palmaris brevis. Floor = flexor retinaculum + pisohamate ligament.

Contents: Ulnar nerve + ulnar artery. The ulnar nerve divides here into superficial (sensory to medial 1.5 digits) and deep (motor to intrinsics) branches.

Clinical: Cyclist's palsy / handlebar palsy = compression in Guyon's canal from prolonged pressure. Hamate hook fracture (from golf club/bat impact) can also compress. DDx from cubital tunnel by location of symptoms.

6 Dorsal Extensor Compartments (Lateral → Medial)

1st: APL + EPB — DeQuervain's tenosynovitis target
2nd: ECRL + ECRB — intersection syndrome when 1st crosses over 2nd
3rd: EPL — hooks around Lister's tubercle; rupture risk
4th: ED + EI
5th: EDM
6th: ECU

Central Compartment — Lumbricals & Interossei

Lumbricals are unique: they originate from tendons (FDP) and insert into tendons (extensor expansion). They flex the MCP while extending the IP joints — the “writing position.” Interossei: Mnemonic DAB / PAD — Dorsal ABduct, Palmar ADduct. All interossei are innervated by the ulnar nerve.

Extensor Expansion (Dorsal Hood) — Why It Matters

The extensor expansion is an aponeurotic hood wrapping around each digit (2–5). The extrinsic extensor tendons (ED, EDM, EI) form the central band, while lumbricals and interossei attach to the lateral bands. This is why lumbricals/interossei can extend the IP joints despite being on the palmar side — they pull on the lateral bands of the dorsal expansion. Disruption of the central band → Boutonniere deformity (PIP flexion + DIP hyperextension). Disruption of the terminal tendon → Mallet finger (DIP flexion). Disruption of the volar plate → Swan neck deformity (PIP hyperextension + DIP flexion).

Superficial palmar arch: primarily from ulnar artery (dominant) + superficial branch of radial. Gives off common palmar digital arteries → proper digital arteries to digits. Located at level of extended thumb. Deep palmar arch: primarily from radial artery (enters through 1st interosseous space after traversing anatomical snuffbox) + deep branch of ulnar. Wrist & Hand Special Tests
13 special tests organized by region and pathology. Sensitivity (how good at ruling out), Specificity (how good at ruling in), and +LR/–LR where available. Always pair tests with clinical context — no single test is diagnostic alone.
Carpal Tunnel / Median Nerve Tests

Tinel's Sign at the Wrist Sen 64%Spec 99%

▼
  • Patient seated, wrist resting on a table or hard surface
  • Therapist taps over the carpal tunnel at the wrist/palm junction (transverse carpal ligament)
  • (+) = tingling or paresthesia distal to the point of pressure following the median nerve distribution (thumb, index, middle, radial ring finger)
Clinical Interpretation

Spec 99% = very high; a positive Tinel's at the wrist strongly rules IN carpal tunnel syndrome. Sen 64% = moderate; a negative Tinel's does NOT rule out CTS. Use with Phalen's and Durkan's for a cluster approach. (Szabo reference)

Phalen's Test Sen 77%Spec 40%

▼
  • Patient sits with shoulders relaxed, backs of hands facing each other
  • Patient flexes wrists maximally by pushing wrists together — holds for 1 minute or until symptoms reproduced
  • (+) = tingling in the median nerve distribution of the hand
Clinical Note

High sensitivity (77%) makes Phalen's useful for ruling OUT CTS — a negative test makes CTS less likely. Low specificity (40%) means many false positives. Combine with Tinel's and Durkan's for better diagnostic accuracy. Provocative via wrist flexion increasing carpal tunnel pressure on median nerve.

Durkan's Test (Carpal Compression Test) Sen 90%Spec 92%

▼
  • Therapist places direct pressure over the carpal tunnel (transverse carpal ligament)
  • Hold up to 30 seconds
  • (+) = reproduction of wrist/forearm symptoms and numbness/tingling in median nerve distribution
Best Single CTS Test

With Sen 90% AND Spec 92%, Durkan's is the most diagnostically accurate single test for carpal tunnel syndrome. Directly compresses the median nerve through the carpal tunnel. If you can do only one CTS test, this is it.

Radial Wrist / DeQuervain's Tests

Finkelstein's Test Sen 81%Spec 50%

▼
  • Patient places thumb inside palm and closes fingers over it (fist around thumb)
  • PT stabilizes distal forearm; ulnar deviates patient's hand
  • (+) = pain over APL and EPB tendons (1st dorsal compartment)
  • Must reproduce patient's symptoms to be truly positive — non-specific pain in this position is common in normal subjects
Alexander / WHAT Test Preferred

The WHAT Test (see below) is preferred over Finkelstein's — it has higher sensitivity (99%) and is easier to perform. Finkelstein's produces pain in many normal wrists due to the stretch position, leading to false positives (Spec only 50%).

WHAT Test — Wrist Hyperflexion & Abduction of Thumb Sen 99%Spec 29%

▼
  • Therapist asks patient to make full wrist flexion with the thumb in full extension and abduction
  • Therapist gradually provides resistance to abduction of the thumb
  • (+) = replication of symptoms along radial wrist/forearm, OR patient unable to counter the applied force
Why WHAT Test is Preferred

Sen 99% — near-perfect sensitivity means a negative WHAT test almost completely rules out 1st dorsal compartment involvement. Easier to perform than Finkelstein's with more consistent results. Low Spec (29%) still means many false positives, but the high sensitivity is the primary clinical value. Use to screen; confirm with clinical presentation.

Thumb & CMC Joint Tests

CMC Grind Test

▼
  • Therapist stabilizes the trapezium proximal to the 1st metacarpal with one hand
  • Other hand grasps the 1st metacarpal
  • Apply axial compressive force with combined rotation (circles)
  • (+) = reproduction of patient's pain; also positive if crepitus noted (indicates arthrosis and synovitis)
Clinical Context

Assessment of 1st CMC OA is often visual first — look for "shoulder sign" (subluxation of CMC joint creating visible prominence at base of thumb). The grind test confirms joint involvement. Combine with Lever Test for full assessment. 1 lb of pinch = 12 lbs of force at the CMC joint — explains why the CMC is the dominant OA site in the hand.

Lever Test (CMC Mobility Test) Sen 82%Spec 81%

▼
  • Therapist grabs the 1st metacarpal just distal to the CMC joint
  • Attempts to glide the joint in radial/ulnar and dorsal/volar directions (back and forth)
  • (+) = reproduction of patient's symptoms
Complementary to Grind Test

Both Sen 82% and Spec 81% are balanced — useful for both ruling in and ruling out 1st CMC pathology. Use grind test + lever test together for comprehensive CMC assessment.

Thumb UCL Stress Test (Valgus Stress MCP) Sen 94%Spec 46%

▼
  • Therapist stabilizes patient's hand; positions thumb into extension
  • Apply a valgus stress to the MCP joint while maintaining extension
  • (+) = valgus movement greater than 30–35° (compared to contralateral side)
Stener Lesion

A Stener lesion occurs when the UCL tears and the adductor pollicis aponeurosis interposes between the torn ligament ends — preventing healing. Requires surgical repair. Suspect if valgus laxity >35° or complete instability. High sensitivity (94%) means a negative test largely rules out significant UCL tear.

Froment's Sign

▼
  • Ask patient to grasp a piece of paper between thumb and index finger in a key grip
  • Therapist attempts to pull the paper away
  • (+) = paper slips out, OR patient flexes the thumb IP joint to compensate
  • IP flexion = substitution of FPL for the weak adductor pollicis (which is ulnar nerve innervated)
Ulnar Nerve — Adductor Pollicis

Adductor pollicis (ulnar nerve) is needed for key pinch force. Weakness = inability to hold paper → patient compensates by flexing FPL (median nerve). The IP flexion sign indicates ulnar nerve weakness, NOT median nerve. Seen in cubital tunnel syndrome and Guyon's canal compression.

Carpal Instability & TFCC Tests

Watson Test — Scaphoid Shift Maneuver Sen 69%Spec 68%

▼
  • Patient: elbow on tabletop at 90° flexion; forearm slightly pronated; wrist starts in ulnar deviation
  • Therapist locates the scaphoid tubercle with thumb (volar wrist, radial side)
  • Other hand stabilizes metacarpals; moves wrist into radial deviation and slight flexion
  • Maintain pressure on the scaphoid tubercle throughout the movement
  • (+) = "clunk" (scaphoid subluxes) + pain at the radial side of the wrist
Scapholunate Instability

When the scapholunate ligament is torn, moving the wrist from ulnar to radial deviation causes the scaphoid to sublux dorsally (clunk). The thumb pressure blocks this movement — release of pressure with movement = shift. Balanced Sen/Spec (69/68) — moderate accuracy. Clinical presentation + XR (widened SL space >3mm on AP view = "Terry Thomas sign") confirms diagnosis.

Ulnar Impaction Test (TFCC Axial Load Test)

▼
  • Patient seated, elbow on table at 90°; wrist in slight ulnar deviation; digits in gentle composite fist
  • Therapist loads the ulnar side of wrist with direct pressure through the 4th and 5th metacarpals
  • (+) = replication of pain symptoms at ulnar side of wrist
TFCC Context

Tests the articulation between the ulnar carpus (triquetrum) and the TFCC. Ulnar impaction syndrome = excessive ulnar loading → TFCC degeneration. Common in patients with relative ulnar positive variance (longer ulna than radius). Golfer's "top of backswing" injury pattern = TFCC load mechanism.

TFCC Piano Key Test Sen 59%Spec 96%

▼
  • Therapist stabilizes the hand and radius
  • Press down on the ulnar head like pressing a piano key
  • (+) = replication of ulnar-sided wrist pain
DRUJ / TFCC Static Stability

Tests static stability of DRUJ and TFCC. Very high Spec (96%) — a positive Piano Key strongly rules IN DRUJ/TFCC pathology. Moderate Sen (59%) — negative test does not rule out. Pair with Ulnar Impaction test for comprehensive TFCC evaluation.

TFCC Press Test Sen 100%Spec N/A

▼
  • Patient attempts to push their body weight up from a chair using their hands on the armrests
  • Creates axial and ulnar-related compressive force through the wrist
  • (+) = replication of ulnar-sided wrist pain
Sensitivity 100%

A negative Press Test virtually rules OUT significant TFCC pathology — a patient who can push up without wrist pain is unlikely to have a clinically significant TFCC tear. No specificity data available — positive test does not confirm TFCC (any ulnar-sided structure could be provoked). Use as a screening tool; confirm with Piano Key Test and imaging (MRI arthrogram is gold standard for TFCC).

Diagnoses — Joints & Soft Tissue
Rheumatoid Arthritis, Osteoarthritis, Dupuytren's Contracture, Ganglion Cyst, and Thumb UCL Sprain. Each condition includes pathomechanics, clinical presentation, key deformities, and treatment framework.

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

▼
Pathomechanics

Autoimmune attack on synovial fluid → protein thickening → inflammation + loss of ROM → cartilage loss, joint space narrowing, capsular instability → unstable joints. In the hand: ligament attenuation on the radial side → ulnar drift at MCPs + radial drift at wrist. Osteophytes roughen extensor tendons → Von Jackson rupture (terminal extension loss — surgery only fix).

Boutonnière Deformity

Mechanism: Central slip rupture → lateral bands migrate volarly. Result: PIP flexion + DIP hyperextension. Treatment: Splint PIP in extension (DIP free to move). If untreated → progressive deformity requiring surgery.

Swan Neck Deformity

Less common. Lateral bands migrate dorsally. Result: PIP hyperextension + DIP flexion. Caused by joint space collapse or acute trauma. Causes functional loss in gripping. Surgery or splinting.

Synovitis → Ulnar Drift Progression

Synovitis → attenuation of sagittal band (first indicator of impending drift). If untreated → MCP ulnar drift. Screen for sagittal band integrity early. Conservative: anti-ulnar drift splint (wraps around palm, pulls back to neutral). Night: resting hand splint in neutral position.

Treatment Framework

Edema control + thermal modalities. Mobility: light AROM to maintain joint lubrication — NOT aggressive PROM or grip strength. Water aerobics. Splinting: anti-ulnar drift + nighttime resting hand splint. Joint protection: modify tasks, tools, or stop provoking activities. Medical: medications (DMARDs, biologics); joint replacement (age-dependent); IP joint fusion into functional position.

Functional Implications

Loss of grasp patterns (cylindrical, spherical, hook, diagonal). Pain-limited use. Weakness from both disease + guarding. Task + environment modifications are cornerstones of PT management for RA.

Osteoarthritis (OA) — 1st CMC & Digital Joints

▼
Pathomechanics — Why 1st CMC?

1 lb of pinch force = 12 lbs at the 1st CMC joint (biomechanical amplification). Repetitive overuse + hypermobility predisposition → cartilage wear → joint collapse. "Shoulder sign/subluxation" — as cartilage erodes, the CMC joint shifts and creates a visible prominence at the base of the thumb. STT (scaphotrapezialtrapezoid) arthritis is less common.

Heberden's vs. Bouchard's Nodules

Heberden's: DIP joints — osteophytes + pain → DIP deviation. Bouchard's: PIP joints — similar presentation. Both from repeated microtrauma + inflammation → osteophyte formation → deviation. Treatment: compression splint + DIP splint + heat education.

Assessment

Visual first — look for shoulder sign. CMC Grind Test (axial + rotational compression; + = pain or crepitus). Lever Test (joint gliding; Sen 82%/Spec 81%). Functional losses: decreased pinch and grip strength, dropping objects.

Treatment — CMC

CMC stabilization exercises — reapproximate CMC joint, improve soft tissue stability (Harkins theatre cup technique). Joint protection (same principles as RA). Splinting: CMC short opponens — stabilizes 1st CMC externally. Surgery: LRTI (Ligament Reconstruction Tendon Interposition) or Suspensionplasty (hammock-type fix). Post-op: extensive rehab; light ROM; progressive strengthening.

Dupuytren's Contracture

▼
Pathomechanics

Palmar fasciitis. Active cellular process with nodule development in palmar and digit fascia following longitudinal tension lines. Results in flexion contractures: MCP > PIP > DIP (MCP most commonly affected). Rings and little fingers most often involved. Genetic predisposition; more common in Nordic descent.

Conservative Treatment Is NOT Effective

Unlike most musculoskeletal conditions, conservative treatment (stretching, splinting, PT alone) does NOT effectively treat Dupuytren's — the pathological fascia does not respond to these interventions. Surgery is the primary treatment once contracture is functionally limiting.

Surgical Options (>30° Contracture)

Xiaflex (Collagenase injection) — enzymatic fasciotomy; dissolves cord; outpatient; relatively quick recovery. Needle Aponeurotomy — percutaneous cord disruption with needle; feel the pop of gradual release; less recovery. Open Release — surgical excision of diseased fascia; most complete but most recovery.

Post-Op Treatment

Wound care → full-time splinting → night splinting. Stretching + functional grasp work to reduce scar adhesions. ROM before grip strength — maintain motion first, then build strength. Manual therapy for scar management.

Ganglion Cyst

▼
Pathology

Thin-walled cyst containing mucoid hyaluronic acid. Occur spontaneously. Most common soft tissue tumor in the hand. Scaphoid dorsal or volar is the most common site. Common in waiters, servers, and those with repetitive wrist use.

Conservative Management

Compression + conservative spinning may mobilize fluid. Aspiration (drainage) = effective for short term but the cyst sac remains → high recurrence. 4 weeks of compression post-aspiration. Observation is reasonable if asymptomatic.

Diagnoses — Tendons & Nerve Entrapment
Tendinopathies, tendon ruptures, flexor/extensor tendon zones, and the four common nerve entrapment syndromes. Tendinitis is a misnomer — the correct terms are tendinosis or tenosynovitis depending on the structure involved.
Terminology — Why It Matters

Tendinitis: "there is an absence of acute inflammatory cells in the load-bearing regions of tendons" — the tendon is NOT actually inflamed. Term should be abandoned. Tendinosis: degeneration of tendon collagen from chronic overuse — Type I collagen (mature) → Type III (immature). Tenosynovitis: inflammation of the synovial sheath surrounding the tendon (specific locations, e.g., 1st dorsal compartment, A1 pulley). Treatment differs: tendinosis = load to stimulate collagen remodeling; tenosynovitis = reduce synovial inflammation first.

DeQuervain's Tenosynovitis

▼
Pathology & Presentation

Inflammation of the 1st dorsal extensor compartment (APL + EPB tendons). Pain with: ulnar deviation, thumb flexion stretch, resisted thumb extension + radial deviation. Tenderness over the 1st dorsal compartment (radial wrist, anatomic snuffbox).

DDx: Intersection Syndrome

Intersection syndrome involves ECRB and ECRL crossing the 1st compartment — pain is more proximal (dorsal forearm, ~4–6 cm proximal to radial styloid) vs. DeQ's pain directly at the styloid. Different location = different structure, different treatment target.

WHAT Test Preferred Over Finkelstein's

WHAT Test (Sen 99%) is preferred because it is easier to perform and has higher sensitivity for the 1st dorsal compartment. Finkelstein's Spec is only 50% — many false positives from the wrist stretch position alone.

Trigger Finger (Flexor Tenosynovitis)

▼
Pathology & Presentation

Inflammation of flexor tendon sheath at the A1 pulley, located at the volar MCP joint. The tendon nodule catches on the pulley creating a "trigger." Progression: (1) Point tenderness volar MCP only; (2) Limited ROM + catching; (3) Locking — finger stuck in flexion or extension requiring passive assist; (4) Fixed flexion contracture.

Three-Tier Treatment

Tier 1 (Conservative): Immobilize 1 joint of finger (PIP is easiest) + anti-inflammatory modalities + light mobility to 2–3 finger joints WITHOUT composite flexion. Trial 4 weeks. Tier 2 (Injection): Corticosteroid injection into tendon sheath to reduce inflammation. Start here for thumbs (stubborn). 4 weeks to assess response. Tier 3 (Surgery): Release of A1 pulley. Must release complete pulley — incomplete release = recurrence.

Flexor & Extensor Tendon Zones

▼
Flexor Tendon Zones (5 zones)

Slow healing due to poor blood supply. Protocols vary by surgical technique and patient factors. Common principles across zones: Dorsal blocking orthosis for 6 weeks. PROM into flexion, AROM ONLY into blocked extension. Zone 2 ("no man's land") = most complicated — within the flexor sheath, tendon ends may not heal well without meticulous surgical repair. Require early protected motion protocols.

Jersey Finger (Zone 1 Avulsion)

FDP avulsion off distal phalanx — unable to flex DIP. Treat with flexor tendon protocol + full dorsal blocking orthosis for 6 weeks. Critical: if not splinted, the tendon can retract into the forearm. Surgery required. Common mechanism: grabbing another player's jersey.

Extensor Tendon Zones (7 zones)

7 zones (odd numbers located on joints). Broader, flat tendons with better blood supply than flexors. Extensor mechanism + juncturae tendinae (cross-links between extensor tendons) aid in splinting effect. Protocol varies by zone of injury.

Mallet Finger (Zone 1–2)

Rupture of terminal extensor tendon or bony avulsion off P3. Common "jammed finger" (sports). Visible DIP droop — patient cannot actively extend DIP. Treatment: DIP extension splint at 10–15° of hyperextension. Wear ALL times for 6 weeks — ONE missed night = restart the 6-week clock. KT tape supplement. Surgical pinning if compliance is in question. After 6 weeks: therapy to remobilize DIP.

Nerve Entrapment Syndromes
Always screen the full neurological chain — double-crush syndrome (proximal + distal compression simultaneously) must be ruled out. EMG is useful but not infallible. Provocative testing is key to localization.
Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (Median Nerve)

Wrist-based median nerve compression

Causes

Distal radius fracture · Inflammatory increase (pregnancy is classic) · Repetitive cumulative trauma (wrist-based: typing, assembly work)

Presentation

Volar wrist pain · Thumb strength loss (thenar weakness) · Tingling/numbness: thumb, index, middle, radial ring finger · Nocturnal symptoms (classic — sleeping position) · Muscle weakness → thenar wasting

Best Tests

Durkan's (Sen 90%/Spec 92%) > Phalen's (Sen 77%) > Tinel's at wrist (Spec 99%)

Cubital Tunnel Syndrome (Ulnar Nerve — Elbow)

Elbow-based ulnar nerve compression

Causes

Elbow fracture or acute injury · Inflammatory (pregnancy) · Repetitive elbow compression or sustained flexion — drivers keeping elbow on window ledge

Presentation

Elbow + ulnar forearm/hand pain · Tingling/numbness: ring finger + little finger + medial forearm · Intrinsic muscle weakness → wasting (ulnar nerve innervates interossei, hypothenar)

Key Signs

+ Tinel's at cubital tunnel · + Froment's Sign (adductor pollicis weakness) · Intrinsic wasting (first dorsal webspace, hypothenar)

Guyon's Canal (Ulnar Nerve — Wrist)

Wrist-based ulnar nerve compression

Causes

Hamate fracture (hook of hamate) · Inflammatory · Repetitive compression: computer use, "cyclist's hand" / biker's hand (ulnar base of hand on handlebars)

Presentation — Distinct from Cubital Tunnel

Pain to ulnar side of hand ONLY (no elbow or forearm symptoms — this differentiates from cubital tunnel) · Tingling/numbness: ring + little finger · Intrinsic weakness → wasting

Radial Tunnel / Pronator Syndrome (Radial Nerve)

Radial nerve or PIN compression

Causes

Mid-shaft radius fracture · Repetitive pronation + loaded wrist extension · Superficial radial nerve (sensory) vs. PIN (motor branch)

Presentation

Weakness of grip or wrist extension · May present as wrist drop ("Saturday night palsy" — prolonged compression in sleeping position with arm over chair) · Finger extensors weak

Clinical Note

Radial tunnel syndrome (sensory only: dorsal hand tingling) vs. PIN injury (motor: no wrist/finger extension). Distinguish by whether motor loss is present. Both far less common than median or ulnar entrapment at wrist/elbow.

Nerve Entrapment — Universal Treatment Framework
  • Reduce compression/inflammation — activity modification, ergonomic assessment, positioning education
  • Soft tissue mobilization — adjacent muscles, fascia, neural interfaces
  • Custom splinting — stabilize and offload the compressed nerve (e.g., wrist splint for CTS in neutral; elbow pad + avoid sustained flexion for cubital tunnel)
  • Nerve glides / flossing — neural mobilization: slider technique (moves nerve through surrounding tissue) vs. tensioner (tensions the nerve); start with slider for irritable presentations
  • Sensory nerve re-education — for established sensory deficits: monofilament grading, texture discrimination, two-point discrimination training
  • Healthy motion — short arc, symptom-free ROM to improve tissue mobility and circulation around nerve
Double-Crush Syndrome

Proximal compression (e.g., cervical radiculopathy at C6) + distal compression (e.g., carpal tunnel) simultaneously. The proximal lesion sensitizes the nerve, making it more vulnerable to distal compression. Always perform a full cervical and proximal UE screen when peripheral nerve symptoms are present. Treating only the distal site will have incomplete results if a proximal source is missed.

EMG / NCS Utility

EMG + nerve conduction studies are helpful for confirming the level of nerve involvement and quantifying severity. However, EMG is NOT infallible — clinical presentation can precede EMG changes, and false negatives occur early in nerve compression. Do not rely on EMG alone; integrate with provocative testing and clinical presentation.

Fractures, Dislocations & CRPS
Wrist and hand fractures are among the most functionally impactful injuries. Small bones, dense sensory innervation, and complex biomechanics make proper treatment and rehabilitation critical. CRPS is a potential complication following any of these injuries and requires early recognition.

Distal Radius Fracture (DRF)

▼
Fracture Types

Colles fracture: dorsal displacement (most common). Smith's fracture: volar displacement. Classification types 1–5. May be comminuted, intraarticular, or displaced. Does NOT have direct correlation to flexion vs. extension lag in recovery.

⚠ MOBILIZE FINGERS AT DAY 3–5

This is critical and often neglected. Finger stiffness develops rapidly with immobilization. Full digit flexion/extension must be started at day 3–5 regardless of whether the wrist is casted. One size does NOT fit all with off-the-shelf splints — custom fabrication is preferred.

AROM first → PROM after stable and cleared → joint mobs only when fracture is stable. STM + scar management early to prevent adhesions. PROM before fracture stability risks displacement. Monitor for CRPS (burning pain, trophic changes, asymmetric swelling).

Scaphoid Fracture

▼
Why Scaphoid is High-Risk

Blood supply enters the scaphoid distally (retrograde flow). The waist (narrowest point) is the most common fracture site — and it is most proximal to the blood supply entry. Fracture at the waist interrupts retrograde flow → avascular necrosis (AVN) of the proximal pole. Prone to nonunion. X-ray may be normal initially — if clinical suspicion is high (anatomic snuffbox tenderness), treat as fracture until MRI confirms.

SNAC Wrist

Scaphoid Nonunion Advanced Collapse — if nonunion progresses, surrounding carpal bones collapse into the scaphoid space. Leads to: Proximal Row Carpectomy (remove proximal row), Wrist Arthrodesis (fusion), or 4-corner fusion (significant permanent ROM loss).

Metacarpal & Phalangeal Fractures

▼
Metacarpal Fractures

Acute trauma most common. CMC dislocations may occur simultaneously. Custom splinting: 4 weeks (ORIF) or 6+ weeks (closed). Immobilize MCP; also immobilize wrist if shaft or base fracture. Boxer's fracture: 5th metacarpal neck specifically. Progress: A/AAROM → PROM → joint mobs when healed → progressive UE strengthening.

Phalangeal Fractures — Key Concept

14 of 27 hand bones are phalanges = most common fracture site. You cannot send edema to just one phalanx — the entire digit and neighboring digits are affected. Expect associated stiffness, edema, and pain in the rest of the digit and adjacent digits. Even small fractures can have significant mobility impact.

Tufts Fracture (Distal Phalanx Tip)

Crush injury to fingertip (sports, door). Commonly injures nail bed. Treatment: protect during healing → mobilize after fracture is stable/set. Nail bed repair if significant injury. Monitor for subungual hematoma.

Volar Lip Fracture (PIP/DIP Volar Plate)

Small fracture to volar aspect of PIP or DIP. Affects volar plate. Creates scarring, adhesions, and long-term stiffness. Common to NOT regain full range. Mobilize early (after stable) and often — adhesion prevention is the primary goal. Aggressive early mobilization within stability constraints is the key to outcome.

PIP Dislocation

Do NOT reduce in the field — can cause rupture of other structures if done incorrectly. Volar dislocation → EDC damaged → splint in extension (3°). Dorsal dislocation → volar plate damaged → splint in flexion. Therapy = protected mobility throughout ROM.

CRPS — Complex Regional Pain Syndrome
Two Types

CRPS Type I (RSD — Reflex Sympathetic Dystrophy): No direct nerve injury. Pain is triggered by another noxious painful event (e.g., DRF, surgery, immobilization). Most common. CRPS Type II (Causalgia): Direct partial or full nerve injury to a named nerve or major branch. Pain types: Allodynia (non-noxious stimulus → pain perception); Hyperalgesia (noxious stimulus → excessive pain); Hyperpathia (excessive pain perception that perseverates abnormally long). Suspect CRPS in any patient with disproportionate pain, trophic changes, or burning quality post-injury or post-immobilization.

Stage 1: Acute Inflammatory

10 days — 3 months (most reversible)

  • Flushed, warm, dry skin
  • Diffuse and severe pain
  • Brawny edema
  • Hair growth (early)
  • Late stage: limb becomes cold, sweaty, brawny
Clinical Pearl

Can be reversed relatively easily if caught early. Early recognition and aggressive (but gentle) treatment is critical. Do NOT miss this stage — Stage 2 and 3 are much harder to reverse.

Stage 2: Dystrophic

3 — 6 months

  • Constricted blood vessels
  • Waxy appearance of skin
  • Pain becomes burning and throbbing
  • Grooved nails (longitudinal) — classic finding
  • Muscle wasting progresses
  • Joint stiffness worsens
  • Black hair growth
Stage 3: Atrophic

6 months+ (largely irreversible)

  • Irreversible damage to muscles and joints
  • Bone weakening (osteopenia)
  • Ankylosis of joints
  • Pain lessens paradoxically
  • Skin cools further → may appear cyanotic
  • Limb becomes non-functional
Prognosis

Hard to reverse at Stage 3. Prevention through early diagnosis (Stage 1) and aggressive management is the only effective strategy against Stage 3 outcomes.

CRPS Treatment
Team Approach Required

Therapy + Pain Management (physician, nurse) + Medical (nerve blocks, medications). CRPS is beyond PT scope alone — multidisciplinary care is essential.

Graded Motor Imagery (GMI)

Changes the sympathetic nervous system response via cortical reorganization. Three sequential stages: (1) Laterality recognition — identify right vs. left hand from images; (2) Motor imagery — mentally simulate writing your name, moving the limb; (3) Mirror box therapy — use mirror to reflect unaffected limb; brain perceives movement of the affected limb → can break pain cycle. Must be done in sequence.

  • Weight bearing + proprioceptive input — normalize sensory input to the affected limb; desensitization progression
  • Gentle AROM — symptom-free ROM; never force
⚠ CRPS — What NOT To Do
Do NOT Do Aggressive PROM

Spiking pain with aggressive PROM = downward spiral. Each pain spike further sensitizes the sympathetic nervous system, worsening the CRPS cycle. Pain flares during treatment set back weeks of progress. Always work within symptom-free ROM — even if that is minimal.

Clinical Indicators — Suspect CRPS

Disproportionate pain (out of proportion to injury severity) · Burning quality · Allodynia (pain with light touch) · Skin color or temperature changes · Grooved nails · Hair growth asymmetry · Severe limb guarding or avoidance · Pain not improving as expected post-injury or post-immobilization

CRPS Post-DRF — Common Scenario

After distal radius fracture + cast removal: burning sensation + discoloration + hair growth + grooved nails + severe ROM loss = CRPS Stage 1–2. Do NOT use traditional aggressive post-fracture mobilization protocols. Gentle, desensitization-first approach required.

Distinguish carpal tunnel (median) vs. cubital tunnel (ulnar) vs. Guyon's canal (ulnar) by presentation.
tap
Carpal Tunnel (median, wrist): thumb/index/middle/radial ring NTB; nocturnal symptoms; thenar weakness/wasting; Durkan's, Phalen's, Tinel's at wrist. Cubital Tunnel (ulnar, elbow): ring + little finger + medial forearm NTB; elbow/forearm pain; intrinsic wasting; Tinel's at cubital tunnel; Froment's sign; sustained elbow flexion provokes. Guyon's Canal (ulnar, wrist): ulnar hand pain ONLY (no elbow/forearm — key distinguisher from cubital); ring + little NTB; intrinsic wasting; hamate fracture or cyclist's hand compression. Same nerve, different level.
TFCC tests — which has the highest sensitivity and which has the highest specificity?
tap
Highest sensitivity: TFCC Press Test (Sen 100%) — patient pushes body weight up from chair; negative test virtually rules OUT significant TFCC pathology. Highest specificity: TFCC Piano Key Test (Spec 96%) — depress ulnar head like piano key; positive test strongly rules IN DRUJ/TFCC pathology. Pair both for comprehensive TFCC evaluation: Press Test to screen (if negative, TFCC unlikely), Piano Key to confirm (if positive, TFCC highly likely). MRI arthrogram is gold standard for definitive diagnosis.
Graded Motor Imagery — three stages and when is it used?
tap
Used for CRPS — changes sympathetic nervous system response via cortical reorganization. Must be done in sequence: (1) Laterality recognition — patient identifies right vs. left hand images (normalizes cortical representation of the limb). (2) Motor imagery — mentally simulate writing name, moving affected limb without actually moving it. (3) Mirror box — reflect unaffected limb; brain perceives movement in affected limb → can break the pain cycle. Progressing stages too fast before the prior stage is comfortable will fail. Evidence level: moderate for CRPS Type I.
Distal radius fracture — what must happen at day 3–5 and what is the mobility progression?
tap
Day 3–5: MOBILIZE FINGERS — full digit flexion/extension must begin regardless of cast/splint status. Finger stiffness develops rapidly; this is a critical intervention. Wrist mobility: ORIF → wrist AROM at week 2; Closed management → AROM when callus confirmed at week 6. Progression: AROM → PROM (after stable + cleared) → joint mobs (only when fracture stable) → strengthening at week 6–8. STM + scar management early for adhesion prevention. Monitor for CRPS (burning, trophic changes).
