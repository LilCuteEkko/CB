Pharmacology — What Every PT Must Know

48.6% of US adults take at least one prescription drug. 4 out of 5 patients in your clinic are on medications. As a PT you are not prescribing — but you are the provider spending the most time with patients during treatment. You will see adverse effects before anyone else does. You need to know what these drugs do, what they prevent, what they cause, and how they interact with exercise and manual therapy. This module covers every major drug category you will encounter across all clinical settings.

Basics, Pharmacokinetics & Why PTs Must Know This
48.6% of US adults take at least one prescription drug. 4 out of 5 of your patients are on medications. Drug side effects are the #3 risk factor for impaired mental function. You spend more time with patients than anyone else on the care team — that makes you the first line of defense for catching adverse drug reactions before they cause harm.
Goals of Drug Therapy
What Drugs Are Trying To Do
  • Treat the cause of a problem, condition, or disease (antibiotic = cure)
  • Treat symptoms — the vast majority of medications do this, not cure (analgesic = relieve pain)
  • Improve quality of life and functional capacity
  • Meet the desired risk vs. benefit threshold
PT's Responsibility

Know: purpose, risks, benefits, contraindications, and PT-relevant side effects of every medication your patient is on. Medication failure may result in PT treatment (poorly controlled pain → therapy). Medication adverse effects may present during your session (hypotension, dizziness, falls, hypoglycemia, arrhythmia). You are the safety net.

Pharmacokinetics — What the Body Does to the Drug (ADME)
Absorbed — Route of Administration

How the drug enters the body determines onset and bioavailability. IV = fastest (100% bioavailability). Oral = subject to first-pass hepatic metabolism. Inhaled = rapid for pulmonary disease. Transdermal (patch) = slow, sustained release. PT relevance: heat (ultrasound, hot packs, iontophoresis) over a transdermal patch can dramatically increase absorption rate — potentially toxic. Never apply heat over a fentanyl or nitroglycerin patch.

Distributed — Where the Drug Goes

Depends on solubility (fat vs. water soluble) and disease states. Fat-soluble drugs distribute widely and last longer. Water-soluble drugs stay in plasma. Protein-bound drugs (many heart/pain meds) can compete with each other — drug interactions. Older adults: increased body fat → fat-soluble drugs accumulate. Decreased albumin → more free drug in circulation → stronger effects and more side effects at same dose.

Metabolized — Liver

Most drugs metabolized by hepatic cytochrome P450 enzymes. Liver disease → drugs accumulate → toxicity. Medicine goes to hepatic portal vein → liver for first-pass metabolism → bloodstream. Some medications attach to proteins in blood. PT relevance: elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST) in your patient — drugs may be accumulating; report to team.

Eliminated — Kidney (primarily)

Renal function determines how fast drugs clear. CrCl = (140 - age) x IBW / (Scr x 72) (x 0.85 for females). Older adults: lower CrCl → drugs stay longer → need lower doses. Elevated serum creatinine = impaired kidney clearance. SNF patient on many meds: renal impairment is common — watch for drug accumulation and toxicity.

Pharmacodynamics — What the Drug Does to the Body
Agonists vs. Antagonists

Agonist: activates a receptor (mimics natural signaling molecule). Direct agonist: works like the natural molecule. Indirect agonist: increases production of the signaling molecule. Antagonist: blocks the receptor — competitive (same binding site) or non-competitive (changes receptor shape). Partial agonist: activates receptor but produces weaker signal than the natural molecule. Functional antagonist: acts as agonist at different receptor to counteract the effect.

Beers Criteria — High-Risk Meds in Older Adults

Guidelines for healthcare professionals to improve medication safety in older adults. These drugs are potentially inappropriate and carry elevated risk of falls, cognitive impairment, and adverse events in elderly patients — the exact population you treat most in SNF. Know this list.

  • Anticholinergics — 1st gen antihistamines (Benadryl/diphenhydramine)
  • Benzodiazepines — Ativan, Valium, Xanax — sedation, fall risk, cognitive impairment
  • Non-benzodiazepine hypnotics — Ambien (zolpidem)
  • Meperidine — Demerol — neurotoxic metabolite accumulates in elderly
  • Non-COX-selective NSAIDs — ibuprofen, naproxen — GI bleed, renal, CV risk
  • Skeletal muscle relaxants — cyclobenzaprine, methocarbamol — sedation, falls
  • Antispasmodics — scopolamine
How PT Interventions Affect Drug Absorption
  • Heat modalities (ultrasound, hot packs) over a transdermal patch = increased absorption — can cause overdose. NEVER apply heat over fentanyl, nitroglycerin, estrogen, or any transdermal patch
  • Massage over an injection site = increased local drug absorption
  • Exercise increases cardiac output → faster drug distribution
  • Iontophoresis = intentional transdermal drug delivery using electrical current — requires understanding of the drug being delivered
Autonomic Nervous System & Neurotransmitters
The ANS is the chemical backbone of most drug classes you will encounter. Beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, antidepressants, antipsychotics, Parkinson's meds, muscle relaxants — all work by modifying these systems. Understanding how each neurotransmitter functions gives you the framework to predict what a drug will do and what side effects to watch for.
Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic
Sympathetic — Fight or Flight
  • ↑ Heart rate, BP, respiratory rate
  • Shunts blood AWAY from viscera → to muscles and brain
  • Releases epinephrine + norepinephrine from adrenal gland
  • Releases glucose from liver
  • Increases catabolism of fat
  • Peripheral vasoconstriction → ↑ BP
  • Dilates bronchi (opens airway)
Parasympathetic — Rest and Digest
  • ↓ Heart rate, BP, respiratory rate
  • Visceral responses of rest and relaxation
  • Promotes digestion, glandular secretion, urination
  • Vasodilation
  • Peristalsis ↑
  • Mediates cardiac inhibition
  • Primary NT: Acetylcholine

Acetylcholine (ACh) — Most Prevalent Neurotransmitter

▼
Functions

Released at nerve fiber ends in somatic and parasympathetic NS. Mediates synaptic activity of the nervous system and skeletal muscles. Most prevalent NT in the body. Crucial for: arousal, learning, memory, and motor function. Effects: vasodilation, cardiac inhibition, peristalsis, cognition, mood changes.

Anticholinergic Uses (blocking ACh)

Treating spastic GI disorders, reducing salivary and bronchial secretions, dilating pupils. Also used for: asthma, COPD, diarrhea, N&V, Parkinson's disease, urinary bladder spasms. Atropine: treats bradycardia, reduces secretions before surgery. When your patient is on any anticholinergic, guard them from falls — blurred vision + mental status changes.

Anticholinergic Side Effects — "Can't See, Spit, Pee, or Poop"
  • Dry mouth (decreased salivation)
  • Blurred vision (dilated pupil)
  • Urinary retention — critical in elderly SNF patients
  • Tachycardia and palpitations
  • Constipation
  • Headache
  • Changes in mental status — especially in elderly
  • Reduced sweating → hyperthermia risk during exercise

Dopamine — Motor Control & Reward

▼
Functions

Multiple systems: reward-motivated behavior, motor control, hormone regulation. Body-wide chemical messenger: vasodilator in blood vessels, increases sodium excretion in kidneys, reduces insulin production in pancreas, reduces gastric motility, reduces lymphocyte activity. Crucial for: cognition, motivation, attention, mood, sleep, voluntary movement, learning, working memory.

Clinical Disease Links
  • Low dopamine → Parkinson's disease — substantia nigra degeneration
  • Too much dopamine → Schizophrenia — typical antipsychotics block dopamine receptors
  • Dopamine reward system → addiction mechanism
  • Monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs) — prevent dopamine/serotonin/NE breakdown — used for depression

Serotonin — Mood, Pain, and Serotonin Syndrome

▼
Functions

Involved in pain transmission and perception. Complex modulation of: mood, cognition, reward, learning, memory, vomiting, vasoconstriction. Influences: tiredness, sleep, mood, mental fatigue. Vasoconstrictor. Inhibits gastric secretions. Stimulates smooth muscle. Zofran (ondansetron) targets serotonin to prevent N&V in cancer patients.

Serotonin Syndrome — Stop Therapy, Call Physician

Caused by excess serotonin from drug interactions or overdose. Severe cases = life-threatening.

Causes: SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, triptans, tramadol, linezolid, meperidine (Demerol), dextromethorphan, metoclopramide, ondansetron, cocaine, St. John's Wort

Symptoms: confusion, agitation, dilated pupils, rapid HR, changes in BP/temp, N&V, diarrhea, tremor, muscle twitching, shivering, heavy sweating. Severe: high fever, seizures, irregular heartbeat, loss of consciousness.

GABA, Norepinephrine, Endorphins & Glutamate

▼
GABA — Chief Inhibitory NT

Gamma-aminobutyric acid. Principal role: reducing neuronal excitability. Responsible for regulation of muscle tone. Functions: sedation, anxiolytic (reduce anxiety), anticonvulsant, muscle relaxant. Benzodiazepines (Xanax, Ativan, Valium) enhance GABA — that's why they sedate, reduce anxiety, and cause muscle relaxation but also impair balance and cognition. Gabapentin (Neurontin) and pregabalin (Lyrica) also work on related pathways.

Norepinephrine (NE) — Excitatory

Increases: oxygen to brain, heart rate, glucose release, breathing rate. Shuts down digestive processes. Constricts visceral blood vessels, dilates vessels to muscles/brain. Stimulates sympathetic NS. SNRIs (Cymbalta, Effexor) block NE reuptake — used for pain AND depression. Orthostatic hypotension a risk when NE is manipulated — guard for falls with position changes.

Endorphins — Body's Natural Pain Relief

Interact with opiate receptors in brain to REDUCE pain perception. Provide feeling of euphoria ("runner's high"). Modulate appetite, sex hormone release, immune response. Endorphins increase pain threshold. Opioid drugs work on the same receptors as endorphins — that's why they're effective for pain but also addictive (mimic the brain's reward system). Exercise increases endorphin release — this is part of why PT itself is analgesic.

Glutamate — Major Excitatory NT

Major mediator of excitatory CNS signals. Involved in most normal brain functions: cognition, memory, learning. NMDA receptors (glutamate receptors) play a key role in chronic pain transition (windup). Ketamine (NMDA antagonist) is used as an adjuvant analgesic for severe chronic and cancer pain. Gabapentin works partially via glutamate pathway modulation. Glutamate is NOT the same as endorphins — does not reduce pain.

Cardiovascular Medications
Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in the US. Your patients — especially in SNF, cardiac rehab, and home health — are on multiple cardiac medications simultaneously. These drugs directly affect how patients respond to exercise: HR response, BP response, fluid balance, energy, and electrolytes. Know them cold.

Beta-Blockers (-olol) — Critical PT Drug Class

▼
Mechanism

Block beta-adrenergic receptors. Beta-1 receptors primarily in heart — decrease cardiac output by slowing HR and reducing contractility. Beta-2 receptors in lungs and arterioles. Selective beta-1 blockers (metoprolol, atenolol) = fewer lung effects. Non-selective (propranolol) = avoid in COPD/asthma (can cause bronchospasm) and in DM (can mask hypoglycemia symptoms).

Indications

Hypertension, acute MI, angina, heart failure, AFib rate control, reduction of cardiovascular mortality. Also used for: anxiety/agitation (propranolol), migraine prevention, essential tremor. First-line for AFib rate control (goal: rest HR 60–80, activity HR 80–110 bpm).

⚠ CRITICAL PT SAFETY POINTS
  • Do NOT use heart rate as exercise intensity guide — beta-blockers blunt the HR response. Use RPE (Borg Scale 12–14 for moderate) or METs instead
  • NEVER suddenly discontinue beta-blockers — risk of rebound tachycardia and sudden cardiac death (withdrawal syndrome lasts 2–3 days)
  • Adverse effects: bradycardia, AV block, hypotension, bronchospasm, fatigue, depression, dizziness
  • Negative inotrope — decreased resting AND exercise HR
  • Avoid non-selective in COPD, asthma, DM

ACE Inhibitors (-pril) & ARBs (-sartan)

▼
ACE Inhibitors — Mechanism & Uses

Block angiotensin-converting enzyme → reduce peripheral vascular resistance (afterload) → lower BP. Prevent aldosterone-mediated Na+ and fluid retention. Show to slow progression of heart failure. Protect against atherosclerosis. Protect kidneys in diabetic nephropathy. First-line drug prescribed for chronic CHF. Adverse effects: dry cough (most common, 10–15% — switch to ARB if cough intolerable), hypotension, hyperkalemia, angioedema (rare but serious: face/lip/tongue swelling = STOP and call 911).

ARBs — "ACE Inhibitors Without the Cough"

Block angiotensin II receptors directly. Same BP-lowering, heart-protecting, kidney-protecting effects as ACE inhibitors. No ACh mechanism so no cough. Generally substituted when patient cannot tolerate ACE inhibitor cough. Adverse effects: hypotension, hyperkalemia. Do NOT take ibuprofen (NSAID) with lisinopril (ACE-I) — NSAIDs counteract BP lowering effect and increase renal toxicity risk.

Calcium Channel Blockers — Dihydropyridine vs. Non-Dihydropyridine

▼
Dihydropyridine CCBs (-dipine)

Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Felodipine. Primarily peripheral vasodilation → ↓BP. Positive inotrope tendency — can cause reflex tachycardia and palpitations. ADEs: peripheral edema (ankles/feet — don't confuse with CHF exacerbation), flushing, headache. Amlodipine + exercise: calcium is needed for muscle contraction — very high doses can theoretically impair contraction but clinically rare; monitor for unusual weakness.

Non-Dihydropyridine CCBs

Diltiazem, Verapamil. Work on heart AND vessels. Negative inotrope — slow HR, reduce cardiac contractility. Used for: HTN, AFib rate control, angina. ADEs: bradycardia, AV block, constipation, hypotension. Verapamil is a CONTRAINDICATION in CHF — worsens cardiac function. Used in AFib rate control alongside or instead of beta-blockers. Combine with beta-blocker with caution — additive bradycardia.

Diuretics — "Water Pills"

▼
Types and Uses

Loop diuretics (furosemide/Lasix): most potent, work at loop of Henle. Used for CHF and severe edema. Thiazide diuretics (HCTZ, chlorthalidone): first-line for HTN per JNC8 guidelines. Potassium-sparing (spironolactone, eplerenone): aldosterone antagonists — keep K+, used in CHF. Essential in CHF treatment — only drugs that can control fluid retention rapidly (hours to days).

CHF Goal: Daily Weight Monitoring

Patient should weigh at same time every day (morning). Weight gain of 2–3 lbs in 1 day or 5 lbs in 5 days = report to physician — fluid accumulating faster than diuretics can clear it. Diuretics decrease fluid but do NOT slow CHF progression.

PT Adverse Effect Concerns
  • Hypokalemia (low K+) with loop and thiazide diuretics — can cause muscle cramps, weakness, arrhythmias. Normal K+ = 3.5–5.2. If K+ <3.5, patient may be unsafe for vigorous exercise
  • Dehydration — especially with loop diuretics. Monitor for dry mouth, orthostatic hypotension
  • Hyponatremia with thiazides — sodium <135 = seizure risk; <120 = medical emergency
  • Hypotension — allow slow position changes
  • If on diuretic + cardiac glycoside (digoxin): hypokalemia increases digoxin toxicity risk significantly

Nitrates — Nitroglycerin & Isosorbide

▼
Mechanism & Use

Peripheral vasodilation → blood pools in legs → reduces cardiac preload and oxygen demand. Also vasodilates collateral coronary vessels and may relieve coronary artery spasm. Used prophylactically: give before exercise if patient prone to exercise-induced angina. Sublingual NTG: onset 1–3 minutes. Patches: sustained 24-hour release.

⚠ Nitroglycerin PT Rules
  • Patient must SIT when taking sublingual NTG — rapid BP drop causes syncope if standing
  • If no relief after 3 sublingual doses = CALL 911 — patient may be having an MI
  • ADEs: dizziness, flushing, headache, reflex tachycardia
  • NEVER apply heat modalities over a nitroglycerin patch — increases absorption → severe hypotension
  • Can be used prophylactically before a therapy session known to trigger angina

Statins, Anticoagulants & Antiplatelets

▼
Statins (-statin) — Cholesterol Lowering

Atorvastatin (Lipitor), Simvastatin (Zocor), Rosuvastatin (Crestor). Lower LDL, raise HDL, lower triglycerides. Indications: LDL goal <100 mg/dL; total cholesterol goal <200. ADEs critical for PT: skeletal muscle pain, tenderness, weakness (myopathy) — can progress to rhabdomyolysis (rare but severe). Patient complaining of unexplained muscle pain on a statin = report to physician immediately. Statin myopathy can look exactly like deconditioning.

Warfarin (Coumadin) — Vitamin K Antagonist

Prevents blood clots. Interferes with 4 vitamin K-dependent clotting factors. Used for: AFib (prevent cardioembolic stroke), DVT/PE treatment, mechanical heart valves. Monitored by INR (International Normalized Ratio). INR normal = 0.8–1.2. Therapeutic range for most indications = 2–3. INR >5 = high bleeding risk — contact physician. CHADS2 score determines if anticoagulation is needed in AFib.

Novel Oral Anticoagulants (NOACs)

Apixaban (Eliquis), Rivaroxaban (Xarelto), Edoxaban (Savaysa) — factor Xa inhibitors. Dabigatran — direct thrombin inhibitor. Advantages: no routine INR monitoring, fewer food interactions, predictable dosing. Disadvantages: reversal agents limited (though exist now). Used increasingly over warfarin for AFib stroke prevention. PT: same bleeding precautions as warfarin — avoid aggressive joint manipulation; note bruising, bleeding gums, blood in urine.

Antiplatelets — Aspirin, Plavix, Brillinta

Aspirin (low dose 81 mg): inhibits thromboxane release from platelets. Used to prevent MI and ischemic stroke — NOT just for pain at this dose. High dose aspirin = stomach ulcer/GI bleed risk. Clopidogrel (Plavix), Ticagrelor (Brillinta), Prasugrel (Effient): inhibit platelet activation. Used after MI, stroke, or stent placement. PT: bruising and bleeding easily — document, report significant bruising or spontaneous bleeds.

CHADS2 Score for AFib Anticoagulation Decision

CHF = 1 point; HTN = 1 point; Age >75 = 1 point; Diabetes = 1 point; Stroke or prior TIA = 2 points. Score 0 = ASA alone; Score 1 = warfarin or ASA; Score 2+ = warfarin (or NOAC). Goal of anticoagulation in AFib: prevent thrombus formation in quivering atria from becoming a cardioembolic stroke.

CHF Drug Targets: Cardiac Glycosides, Phosphodiesterase Inhibitors & Cardiac Overview

▼
Cardiac Glycosides (Digoxin)

Positive inotropic: increases Ca2+ intracellularly → greater contractility → improved cardiac output. Reduces sympathetic activity. Low therapeutic index — therapeutic and toxic doses are close. ADEs: N/V/diarrhea, headache, dizziness, visual disturbances (yellow-green halos), hallucinations. Hypokalemia from diuretics + digoxin = significantly increased digoxin toxicity risk. Monitor electrolytes. Exercise may increase arrhythmias by increasing circulating catecholamines — monitor for irregular rhythms during therapy.

Key Cardiac Lab Values for PT
  • Ejection fraction >60%: healthy heart
  • EF <40%: heart failure — exercise tolerance significantly reduced
  • INR therapeutic: 2–3 (most AFib/DVT patients)
  • INR >5: high bleeding risk — contact physician
  • K+ 3.5–5.2 mEq/L: normal
  • Na+ 135–147: normal; <120 = seizure/emergency risk
  • Cholesterol <200; LDL <100; HDL >40 (M) / >50 (F)

Pain Management Pharmacology
Pain is the most common reason patients seek PT. Understanding pain pharmacology means understanding why certain patients respond differently to treatment, why chronic pain is NOT just ongoing acute pain, and how opioids, NSAIDs, and adjuvants interact with your rehab plan. The PT is a non-pharmacologic pain management specialist — but you must understand the pharmacologic side too.
Acute vs. Chronic Pain — Different Beasts
Acute Pain

Brief duration (hours to weeks). Etiology often known (surgery, trauma, disease). Pain proportionate to cause. Often objective signs (guarding, grimacing). Elevated HR and BP. Usually resolves as injury heals. Physiologically useful — signals danger. Anxiety, anger, fear common. Responds well to standard analgesics and PT intervention.

Chronic Pain — A Different Pathophysiology

Longer in duration (months to years). May not be associated with known injury or illness. Pain disproportionate to etiology — this is NOT faking. Often no objective signs. Depression common. Pathophysiological — the nervous system has been rewired. Repeated pain signals → physiochemical changes to neural pathways → hypersensitive nociception → resistant to antinociceptive input. Standard analgesics often ineffective. Chronic pain is not a continuation of acute pain — it is a separate disease process.

Types of Pain — Drives Drug Selection
Nociceptive Somatic

Located in soft tissue, bone, joints with nociceptors. Usually well-localized. Continuous, aching, dull, gnawing. Examples: fracture, arthritis, bone metastasis, post-surgical. Responds best to: NSAIDs, acetaminophen, opioids.

Nociceptive Visceral

Pain referred to distant sites from viscera. Poorly localized. Sharp, deep, gnawing, vague, pressure-like. Worsens with movement or deep inspiration. Examples: liver disease, pancreatic cancer, pneumonia. Often more opioid-responsive than somatic pain.

Neuropathic Pain — Different Drug Class Needed

Peripheral: polyneuropathy (diabetic, alcoholic, chemo-induced), mononeuropathy (nerve root compression, carpal tunnel). Central: phantom limb (peripheral deafferentation), central sensitization, complex regional pain syndrome. Descriptors: shooting, burning, lancinating, electric, tingling, stinging. Does NOT respond well to NSAIDs or opioids alone. First-line: anticonvulsants (gabapentin, pregabalin), SNRIs (duloxetine), TCAs.

Acetaminophen (Tylenol) & NSAIDs

▼
Acetaminophen (Tylenol)

Acts centrally in CNS — increases pain threshold by inhibiting cyclooxygenase. Antipyretic (reduces fever). Used for: dental pain, dysmenorrhea, fever, headache, mild pain, musculoskeletal pain, myalgia, osteoarthritis. Does NOT reduce inflammation. Maximum dose: 4g/day (3g in elderly or liver disease). Acetaminophen-related liver injury was the leading cause of acute hepatic failure — very common when combined with alcohol or taken at high doses chronically. Many OTC combination drugs contain it — warn patients about inadvertent double-dosing.

NSAIDs — Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Meloxicam, Ketorolac

Competitively inhibit COX-1 and COX-2 → reduce prostaglandin synthesis → analgesic, antipyretic, anti-inflammatory. Most effective when inflammation drives hyperalgesia. ADEs: GI (most frequent — ulcer, bleed, especially with alcohol), renal impairment, hypertension (especially elderly), platelet inhibition. NSAIDs interfere with wound healing — suppress inflammation needed for tissue repair. Do NOT combine ibuprofen with lisinopril (ACE-I) — NSAIDs counteract BP-lowering effect and increase nephrotoxicity. On the Beers List for elderly.

Opioids — Mechanism, Side Effects, Tolerance & Addiction

▼
Mechanism & Uses

Bind mu-opioid receptors (same receptors as endorphins) in CNS and peripheral tissues. Agonist at opioid receptors → analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, decreased GI motility. Used for: moderate-severe acute pain, cancer pain, post-operative pain, palliative care. Equianalgesic dosing: morphine 30mg oral = 10mg IV. Oxycodone 20mg oral ≈ morphine 30mg oral. Fentanyl 0.1mg IV = morphine 10mg IV (patch = 12.5 mcg/hr).

Tolerance vs. Dependence vs. Addiction

Tolerance: physiologic state where increased dose needed to produce same effect. Does NOT equate with addiction. Physical dependence: state of adaptation — abrupt cessation produces withdrawal symptoms (not the same as addiction). Addiction: primary, chronic, neurobiological disease characterized by impaired control over drug use, craving, compulsive use despite harm. Cancer patients taking opioids for pain management are NOT addicts even if physically dependent.

Opioid Side Effects — PT Must Know
  • Respiratory depression — <12 breaths/min = concern; <8 = call physician. If patient on PCA and unresponsive with RR 6: suspect opioid overdose
  • Constipation — universal, does not develop tolerance; prescribe bowel regimen with every opioid
  • Orthostatic hypotension — fall risk; allow slow position changes
  • Sedation, drowsiness — impairs cognition, balance, safety
  • Nausea/vomiting (early, usually resolves)
  • Urinary retention
  • Miosis (pinpoint pupils)
  • Opioid-induced hyperalgesia — paradoxical increase in pain with chronic high-dose use
  • Meperidine (Demerol): neurotoxic metabolite accumulates in elderly/renal impairment — on Beers list
Tramadol — Weak Opioid + SNRI

Mu-opioid receptor agonist + weak NE and serotonin reuptake inhibitor. Less potential for abuse or respiratory depression than full opioids. WHO Step II agent. Equivalent analgesic relief to codeine. Serotonin syndrome risk when combined with SSRIs/SNRIs. Seizure risk at high doses.

Adjuvant Analgesics — Non-Opioid Pain Meds

▼
Anticonvulsants for Neuropathic Pain

Gabapentin (Neurontin): rarely used for seizures now; primarily for nerve pain. Pregabalin (Lyrica): more potent. Used for diabetic neuropathy, postherpetic neuralgia, fibromyalgia, neuropathic pain. Mechanism: reduces calcium channel activity → reduces neuronal firing. ADEs: fatigue/lethargy, dizziness/drowsiness, peripheral edema. Major fall risk in elderly — not on Beers list but be vigilant. Don't confuse peripheral edema from pregabalin with CHF.

Antidepressants for Pain

TCAs (amitriptyline/Elavil, nortriptyline/Pamelor): oldest adjuvant; effective for neuropathic pain at sub-antidepressant doses. ADEs: anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation), sedation, cardiac arrhythmias — avoid in elderly (Beers list). SNRIs (duloxetine/Cymbalta, venlafaxine/Effexor): first-line for diabetic neuropathy and fibromyalgia. Fewer cardiac ADEs than TCAs. ADEs: nausea, dizziness, sweating, sexual dysfunction, insomnia.

Cannabinoids — CB1 and CB2

CB1 receptors: mainly CNS (basal ganglia, hippocampus, cerebellum, cortex). THC is a CB1 agonist — analgesia, nausea relief, appetite stimulation, ↓GI motility. ADEs: cognitive/memory alterations, dysphoria/euphoria, sedation. CB2 receptors: peripheral tissues, immune system, spinal cord. CBD is primarily CB2 — anti-inflammatory, anti-convulsant, anti-psychotic, neuroprotective. Does NOT produce intoxication. CBD may mitigate some THC side effects. Sativex: combination THC+CBD oral spray used for MS spasticity in some countries.

NMDA Antagonists

Ketamine: powerful NMDA antagonist; used in low doses for severe refractory chronic pain and cancer pain. Prevents windup and central sensitization. Methadone: both mu-opioid agonist AND NMDA antagonist — uniquely effective for neuropathic pain. Complex pharmacokinetics — long half-life, drug interactions. Dextromethorphan: common in cough syrup; at higher doses has NMDA antagonism. Capsaicin (topical): depletes substance P from nerve endings — initial burning then desensitization.

Diabetes Pharmacology & Exercise Safety
Diabetes is one of the most common comorbidities in your patient population. Every PT treating diabetic patients needs to know blood glucose target values, which medications cause hypoglycemia, how exercise affects BG, and exactly what to do when BG goes dangerously low or high. This is a patient safety issue, not just background knowledge.
Blood Glucose Reference Values — Know These
Normal BG Levels
  • Fasting: 70–99 mg/dL
  • 2 hours post-meal (postprandial): 70–145 mg/dL
  • Random: 70–125 mg/dL
  • HbA1c normal: <5.7%; pre-DM: 5.7–6.4%; DM: ≥6.5%
  • HbA1c = average BG over past 3 months — "blood sugar report card"
  • Fasting BG <100 mg/dL is goal for stroke prevention
  • HbA1c <7 is the DM management goal
PT Scheduling — When to See Diabetic Patients

Optimal: 1–2 hours after eating — BG in range, patient not hypoglycemic from fasting. Check BG before exercise: ideally above 126 mg/dL. If patient is on insulin or sulfonylurea, have glucose source available. Document BG before and after exercise when possible.

Diabetes Types & Mechanism
Type 1 DM

Immune disorder — body attacks and destroys insulin-producing beta cells in pancreas. Must take insulin. Exercise can dramatically lower BG — check BG every 30–60 min during activity. Avoid exercise if fasting BG >250 with elevated ketones.

Type 2 DM

Insufficient insulin production OR insulin resistance (cells don't respond). Progressive — often starts with oral meds, advances to insulin. Lifestyle (weight loss, exercise, diet) is first-line — exercise lowers BG and improves insulin sensitivity. Hyperglycemia → increased blood viscosity → impaired wound healing → bacteria thrive.

DM Complications

Diabetic retinopathy (→ no heavy lifting/Valsalva), diabetic nephropathy (→ monitor renal function/drug clearance), diabetic neuropathy (→ fall risk, wound care), stroke risk (→ secondary prevention), heart attack risk. Diabetic foot ulcer: no weight bearing without proper offloading. Inspect feet daily.

Hypoglycemia — Recognition, Treatment & Exercise Precautions

▼
Hypoglycemia Symptoms — Recognize Immediately
  • Shakiness, tremor
  • Sweating, clamminess, pallor
  • Rapid heartbeat, palpitations
  • Anxiety, nervousness, irritability
  • Confusion, delirium — may look like stroke
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness
  • Blurred or impaired vision
  • Tingling/numbness in lips or tongue
  • Weakness, fatigue
  • Severe: seizures, unconsciousness
Treatment Protocol (Non-Unconscious)

1. STOP exercise immediately.
2. Consume 15–20g of glucose or simple carbohydrates (glucose tablets, 1/2 cup juice, 1/2 cup regular soda, 5–6 hard candies).
3. Recheck BG after 15 minutes. If still low, repeat.
4. Once BG returns to normal — eat a small snack if next meal >1–2 hrs away.
5. Do NOT resume exercise until BG ≥70 mg/dL.

Glucagon IM injection: hormone that stimulates liver to release stored glucose. Works in 5–15 min. Patient may experience N&V on awakening. D50% (50% dextrose): emergency IV treatment — administered by EMS or nursing staff. As a PT: call 911, protect airway, do NOT give oral glucose to unconscious patient.

Pulmonary Medications — COPD & Asthma
Pulmonary disease dramatically affects exercise tolerance. Beta-agonists, anticholinergics, and corticosteroids — often delivered via inhaler — all interact with PT. Knowing the difference between rescue and maintenance inhalers, the ADE profile of each class, and how to pace therapy around pulmonary limitations is essential for any PT treating COPD or asthma patients.
COPD Overview
Disease Process

Chronic bronchitis: long-term cough with mucus. Emphysema: destruction of lung tissue over time. Most COPD patients have a combination. Primary cause: smoking. Others: occupational fume/gas exposure, secondhand smoke, frequent cooking fire exposure. Symptoms: productive cough, fatigue, dyspnea that worsens with mild activity, wheezing, repeated respiratory infections.

Pulmonary Rehab Goals

Teach patients to breathe differently while staying active. Exercise to maintain muscle strength. Walk to build strength — gradually increase distance. Do NOT talk during walking to avoid SOB. Pursed lip breathing during exhalation — slows breathing, improves gas exchange, reduces air trapping. Inhaler education is a PT responsibility.

Asthma Severity Classification
Severity Levels

Mild: regular medication not necessary. Mild-moderate persistent: rescue + controller medication. Severe persistent: requires continuous drug treatment. Rescue medications (short-acting bronchodilators) = for acute symptoms. Frequent use of rescue inhaler = poorly controlled asthma — report to physician.

Exercise-Induced Asthma — PT Guidance

Pre-treat with short-acting beta-agonist (albuterol) 15–20 min before exercise. Warm-up and cool-down longer than usual. Monitor for symptoms: cough, wheeze, chest tightness, SOB. Cold, dry air = more likely to trigger. Avoid exercise outdoors in high pollen or pollution. Encourage smoking cessation — most important lifestyle factor.

Beta-Agonists — Bronchodilators

▼
Short-Acting Beta-Agonists (SABA) — Rescue

Albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin, Proventil): bronchodilator via beta-2 agonism. Onset: 5–15 minutes. Duration: 4–6 hours. Used for: acute bronchospasm, exercise-induced asthma, COPD exacerbations. ADEs: tremor (<1–20%), tachycardia/palpitations, headache, angina (uncommon). Albuterol acts on beta-2 receptors (lungs) but can spill over to beta-1 (heart). At standard doses, cardiovascular effects are uncommon.

Long-Acting Beta-Agonists (LABA) — Maintenance

Salmeterol, Formoterol, Vilanterol. Duration 12–24 hours. Used for chronic COPD and asthma control. NOT for acute rescue use — too slow to help during bronchospasm. Often combined with inhaled corticosteroid (ICS): Advair (fluticasone+salmeterol), Symbicort (budesonide+formoterol), Breo Ellipta (fluticasone furoate+vilanterol). Same ADE profile as SABA but less pronounced.

Beta-Agonist ADEs — PT Awareness
  • Tachycardia — sinus tachycardia 1–10%; palpitations <10%; angina <3%
  • Tremor — <1 to 20% (dose-dependent; mainly hands)
  • Headache 3–7%
  • Mild-moderate hypertension uncommon at recommended doses
  • ADEs less likely with inhaled vs. systemic formulations
  • Can cause clinically significant ↑HR and BP at high doses — relevant if patient has just used rescue inhaler before PT

Anticholinergics — Ipratropium, Tiotropium

▼
Mechanism

Block muscarinic receptors in airways → bronchodilation (local effect). Reduce mucus secretion. Used for COPD and as adjunct in asthma. Short-acting: ipratropium (Atrovent) — often combined with albuterol (Combivent). Long-acting: tiotropium (Spiriva), umeclidinium — once daily. Combination LABA/LAMA: Anoro Ellipta (vilanterol + umeclidinium). Combination LABA/LAMA/ICS: Trelegy Ellipta.

Anticholinergic ADEs in Pulmonary Drugs
  • Mild xerostomia (dry mouth): 4–16%
  • Dysphonia (voice changes): 1–3%
  • Paresthesias: 1–3%
  • Depression: 1–4%
  • Insomnia: 4.4%
  • Headache: 5.7%
  • Cardiovascular: chest pain 5–7%, AFib and SVT <1% (more common in higher risk patients)
  • Systemic anticholinergic effects less likely with inhaled route

Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS) — Fluticasone, Budesonide, Mometasone

▼
Inhaled Corticosteroids

Anti-inflammatory — reduce airway inflammation, mucus secretion, and hyperresponsiveness. Controller medications — NOT rescue. Examples: fluticasone (Flovent), budesonide (Pulmicort), mometasone (Asmanex). Relatively low risk at recommended doses. ADEs: oropharyngeal candidiasis (thrush) 2–31% — ALWAYS rinse mouth with water after use. Other ADEs: musculoskeletal pain, arthralgia (17%), headache (1–34%), fatigue (16–28%), asthma exacerbation paradox (3–7%), dysphonia (hoarseness).

Oral/Systemic Corticosteroids — Prednisone

Used for acute exacerbations (COPD, asthma, MS). Powerful anti-inflammatory but significant systemic ADEs with long-term use. ADEs of chronic systemic corticosteroids critical for PT: osteoporosis (fracture risk → fall precautions), muscle weakness (steroid myopathy), fluid retention/weight gain, hyperglycemia (raises BG — monitor in diabetics), avascular necrosis of hip (severe chronic use), skin fragility, impaired wound healing, immunosuppression (infection risk), Cushing's syndrome. Patient who stopped prednisone abruptly = adrenal insufficiency risk.

Psychiatric & CNS Medications
Anxiety, depression, agitation, and sleep disorders are pervasive in your patient population — especially in SNF, inpatient, and chronic pain settings. These medications carry significant ADEs that affect PT directly: fall risk from sedation, cognitive impairment, cardiac arrhythmias, serotonin syndrome, and extrapyramidal effects. Know what to watch for.

Antidepressants — SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, and Atypicals

▼
SSRIs — Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors

First-line antidepressants. Block reuptake of serotonin → more serotonin in synapse. Examples: fluoxetine (Prozac), sertraline (Zoloft), paroxetine (Paxil), citalopram (Celexa), escitalopram (Lexapro). Also used for: anxiety, OCD, PTSD, panic disorder. ADEs: QT prolongation (abnormal heart rhythms) — report palpitations or irregular rhythm. Serotonin syndrome risk (especially if combined with tramadol, triptans, MAOIs). Sexual dysfunction, weight changes, insomnia, N&V. Suicidal thoughts risk in under-25 age group — monitor.

SNRIs — Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors

Duloxetine (Cymbalta), venlafaxine (Effexor), desvenlafaxine (Pristiq), milnacipran (Savella). Block reuptake of BOTH serotonin AND norepinephrine. Used for: depression, generalized anxiety, fibromyalgia, neuropathic pain (diabetic peripheral neuropathy), chronic musculoskeletal pain. Cymbalta is commonly prescribed specifically for pain in PT patients. ADEs: nausea, dizziness, sweating, sexual dysfunction, constipation, insomnia, headache, loss of appetite, elevated BP (NE effect).

TCAs — Tricyclic Antidepressants — High ADE Burden

Amitriptyline (Elavil), nortriptyline (Pamelor), desipramine (Norpramin), imipramine (Tofranil), doxepin (Sinequan). Older class; effective but higher ADE burden. Used for: depression, neuropathic pain, migraine prevention, insomnia. ADEs: strong anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, blurred vision, confusion), cardiac arrhythmias (QT prolongation, AV block), orthostatic hypotension, sedation. On Beers list for elderly. High fall risk. Avoid in patients with cardiac disease. Can be fatal in overdose.

Atypicals — Various Mechanisms

Bupropion (Wellbutrin): NE and dopamine reuptake inhibitor. Less sexual dysfunction. No serotonin effects. Used for depression and smoking cessation. Lowers seizure threshold — avoid in eating disorder patients. Mirtazapine (Remeron): increases NE and serotonin release. Significant sedation and weight gain — sometimes used for insomnia and anorexia. Trazodone (Desyrel): used mainly for insomnia at low doses. Priapism risk (rare). Vortioxetine (Trintellix): newer multimodal; cognitive benefit.

Benzodiazepines — Anxiety & Sleep (BEERS LIST)

▼
Mechanism & Indications

Enhance GABA (chief inhibitory NT) → sedation, anxiolytic, anticonvulsant, muscle relaxant effects. Examples: alprazolam (Xanax), lorazepam (Ativan), diazepam (Valium), clonazepam (Klonopin), temazepam (Restoril for sleep). Used for: GAD, panic disorder, acute agitation, alcohol withdrawal, seizures, procedural sedation, insomnia (short-term). Tolerance develops rapidly — not for long-term use. Physical dependence common — abrupt withdrawal can cause seizures.

Benzodiazepine ADEs — CRITICAL for PT Safety
  • Sedation, drowsiness, fatigue, lethargy
  • Impaired balance — major fall risk; on Beers list for elderly
  • Amnesia, memory impairment, cognitive impairment
  • Confusion, disorientation
  • Dizziness, ataxia, dysarthria (slurred speech)
  • Paradoxical agitation — especially in elderly and children
  • Respiratory depression (especially with opioids — dangerous combination)
  • Impaired judgment and coordination — no driving or machines
  • If patient is on benzo + opioid = HIGHEST fall and respiratory depression risk — proceed with extreme caution in PT
Non-Pharm Alternatives PT Can Offer for Sleep/Anxiety

Deep breathing exercises (diaphragmatic breathing). Progressive muscle relaxation. Yoga, tai chi. Regular exercise (especially endurance — ACSM 150 min/week moderate intensity) during the day. Cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I). Sleep hygiene education. Exercise itself reduces anxiety and depression significantly — this is YOUR intervention.

Antipsychotics — Typical & Atypical

▼
Uses & Types

Used for: schizophrenia, bipolar disorder (manic phase), psychotic depression, dementia-related agitation, TBI agitation, delirium. Typical (1st gen): Haloperidol (Haldol). Block dopamine D2 receptors. High risk of extrapyramidal side effects (EPS). Atypical (2nd gen): Risperidone (Risperdal), Olanzapine (Zyprexa), Quetiapine (Seroquel), Aripiprazole (Abilify). Block dopamine + serotonin. Lower EPS risk but more metabolic effects.

Antipsychotic ADEs — PT Awareness
  • Weight gain — especially atypicals (olanzapine worst)
  • Sedation, somnolence — impairs participation in PT
  • Cardiac abnormalities — QT prolongation; monitor for arrhythmias
  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS): akathisia (restlessness), dystonia (muscle spasms), Parkinsonism (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia) — can look like a movement disorder
  • Tardive dyskinesia: involuntary repetitive movements (tongue, face, trunk) — late-onset, may be irreversible
  • Orthostatic hypotension — fall risk
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (rare but life-threatening): hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, altered consciousness

Mood Stabilizers & Generalized Anxiety Disorder Medications

▼
Mood Stabilizers

Lithium: effective for bipolar disorder. Narrow therapeutic index — toxic and therapeutic doses are close. Requires blood level monitoring. Toxicity signs: tremor, ataxia, confusion, nausea — if patient on lithium appears uncoordinated or confused, report immediately. Dehydration and NSAIDs increase lithium toxicity. Divalproex (Depakote) / Valproic Acid: used for bipolar, seizures, migraine prevention. ADEs: weight gain, sedation, liver toxicity (monitor LFTs), hair loss.

GAD — Buspirone vs. Benzodiazepines

Buspirone (Buspar): non-benzo anxiolytic. Works on serotonin receptors. NOT addictive. Does NOT cause sedation or impair cognition. Delayed onset (2–4 weeks for full effect). Better long-term option than benzodiazepines for GAD. Also used in combination with SSRIs. Serotonin syndrome risk when combined with other serotonergic drugs. No tolerance or withdrawal issues.

Neurological Condition Medications
Parkinson's disease, MS, GBS, and stroke are the primary neurological diagnoses you will treat. Each has a unique pharmacological profile that directly shapes how you schedule sessions, what to expect from the patient, and which PT interventions complement or conflict with the medications. This is where pharmacology meets your core clinical practice.

Parkinson's Disease Medications

▼
">
Pathophysiology & Drug Goal

PD = degeneration of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra. Low dopamine → tremor (resting), rigidity, bradykinesia, postural instability, shuffling gait. Drug goal: replace or mimic dopamine, or block competing pathways. The substantia nigra and basal ganglia are damaged with reduced dopamine → movement initiation impaired.

Levodopa/Carbidopa (Sinemet) — Gold Standard

Levodopa = dopamine precursor (crosses blood-brain barrier where dopamine cannot). Carbidopa = prevents peripheral breakdown of levodopa → more reaches brain with fewer peripheral side effects. Most effective drug for PD motor symptoms. ADEs: N/V (especially early), orthostatic hypotension, dyskinesias (involuntary movements — sign of too much dopamine), on-off fluctuations (wearing off between doses → sudden loss of motor function). "On" period = medication working = best time for PT.

Other PD Medications

Dopamine agonists (bromocriptine, pramipexole, ropinirole): directly stimulate dopamine receptors. Used alone early or with levodopa. ADEs: nausea, orthostatic hypotension, hallucinations, impulse control disorders. MAO-B inhibitors (selegiline, rasagiline): prevent dopamine breakdown — extend levodopa effect. Anticholinergics (benztropine): reduce tremor by blocking ACh. ADEs: full anticholinergic profile — dry mouth, confusion, urinary retention. Used mainly for tremor-predominant PD. Amantadine: reduces dyskinesias; also antiviral.

⚠ PD Medication — PT Scheduling Rules
  • Schedule PT 1 hour after patient takes PD medication — peak drug effect = best motor function, greatest compliance with therapy
  • On-off fluctuations: patient may function well one hour, be almost frozen the next — build therapy schedule around medication timing
  • Ensure bowel and bladder care BEFORE therapy — increased abdominal pressure during exercise + anticholinergic-reduced GI motility = risk
  • If patient is having a "bad day" or appears more rigid than usual — check if dose was taken, if medication regimen has changed
  • Orthostatic hypotension is common — allow slow position changes, especially sit-to-stand
  • Anticholinergics: blurred vision + confusion — guard closely during gait and balance training
PT Focus for PD Patients

Big amplitude movements (LSVT BIG). Gait training with cues (visual/auditory). Balance training including perturbations to improve reaction time. Posture correction (kyphosis, forward head). High-intensity exercise for neuroplasticity and BDNF upregulation. Treadmill training. Dual-task practice. Exercises: focus on big movements — PD patients have difficulty with amplitude scaling.

Multiple Sclerosis Medications

▼
Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs)

Slow the rate of symptom worsening and reduce relapse frequency. Beta interferons (Avonex, Betaseron, Rebif): injectable; slow MS progression. Flu-like ADEs post-injection. Glatiramer (Copaxone): injectable; blocks immune attack on myelin. Natalizumab (Tysabri): interferes with immune cell movement to CNS. Fingolimod (Gilenya): oral; traps immune cells in lymph nodes. Mitoxantrone (Novantrone): immunosuppressant; cardiac toxicity limits use. Common theme: all immunomodulate — infection risk is elevated.

Spasticity Management

Baclofen (Lioresal): oral skeletal muscle relaxant via GABA-B agonism. Reduces spasm frequency, pain, clonus, rigidity. Improves ROM. ADEs: drowsiness (10–63%), dizziness, fatigue, headache, N/V, constipation, confusion, weakness. Abrupt withdrawal = dangerous (seizures, hyperthermia) — NEVER suddenly stop. Tizanidine (Zanaflex): reduces spasticity by blocking nerve impulses. Does not decrease muscle strength as much as baclofen. ADEs: weakness/fatigue (41–78%), hypotension (16–33%), bradycardia (2–10%). Both: monitor for excessive sedation and hypotension before PT sessions.

PT Relevance in MS

MS is CNS demyelination (oligodendroglia). GBS is PNS demyelination (Schwann cells). PT goals for MS: reduce spasticity, increase muscle strength and mass, improve balance, improve standing time, endurance, and stamina. Help reconnect neural pathways through targeted exercise. Heat sensitivity (Uhthoff's phenomenon) = exercise in cool environments; avoid hot showers. Fatigue management = pacing, energy conservation techniques.

Fatigue — Universal MS Symptom

Not just physical tiredness — central MS fatigue is disproportionate to activity. Multiple factors: MS disease process, depression, sleep disruption, medications (baclofen, interferons, tizanidine all cause sedation/fatigue). PT approach: cooling strategies, pacing, energy conservation, exercise timing around fatigue pattern (typically worse in afternoon). Amantadine sometimes prescribed for MS-related fatigue.

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) — Pharmacology & PT

▼
GBS Treatment

Hospitalization and monitor: respiratory function (PFTs), cardiac status (EKG, HR, BP), progression of muscle weakness. IVIG (intravenous immunoglobulin) OR plasmapheresis — equally effective for patients too weak to walk, rapid progression, or poor prognosis. Analgesics: acetaminophen, NSAIDs (ibuprofen, ketorolac), opioids, anti-epileptics (gabapentin) — GBS pain can be significant. Anticoagulants: immobility → high DVT risk → anticoagulation mandatory (heparin, enoxaparin). Antibiotics: treat infections as they arise. Mild sedative-hypnotics: alleviate anxiety — CAUTION: increased sensitivity from autonomic dysfunction + respiratory depression risk.

GBS PT Safety Essentials
  • Take vitals BEFORE and THROUGHOUT therapy — labile BP is common (autonomic dysfunction)
  • Monitor breathing: GBS can rapidly progress to respiratory failure — watch RR, SpO2
  • Monitor for bluish tint to fingers (cyanosis) — needs oxygen immediately
  • Look for pressure sores — immobile patient, decreased sensation
  • Safety belt mandatory for gait training — weakness can be profound
  • Goal: improve strength for posture, balance and coordination, reaction speed
  • Progress slowly — physical activity is very taxing for GBS patients
  • May appear similar to PD (weakness, ataxia) but is PNS — different prognosis and recovery pattern

Stroke Secondary Prevention Medications

▼
Ischemic Stroke Secondary Prevention

Goal: prevent recurrent stroke. Antiplatelets (aspirin, clopidogrel/Plavix, ticagrelor/Brillinta): for non-cardioembolic ischemic stroke. Anticoagulants (warfarin, NOACs): for cardioembolic stroke from AFib. Statins: for all ischemic stroke regardless of initial cholesterol — evidence shows plaque stabilization benefit beyond cholesterol lowering. Antihypertensives: BP goal <140/90 (or <130/80 per newer guidelines). ACE inhibitors and diuretics preferred post-stroke.

Modifiable Risk Factors — PT Targets

HTN (#1 modifiable risk — goal BP <140/80). High cholesterol — LDL <100. Diabetes — fasting BG <100, HbA1c <7. Smoking cessation — refer to cessation program. Sedentary lifestyle — ACSM 150 min/week moderate intensity. Obesity — BMI target 18.5–24.9. AFib — anticoagulation. Women: OCPs, migraine with aura, preeclampsia history all increase risk. Exercise prescription is a direct medication for secondary stroke prevention.

  • Normal resting: 60–100 bpm
  • Bradycardia <60: concern if symptoms (dizziness, syncope, fatigue). Can be from beta-blockers — do not start PT if symptomatic brady
  • Tachycardia >100 at rest: investigate cause before exercise. Can be from albuterol, fever, hypovolemia, pain, anxiety, hyperthyroidism
  • On beta-blockers: do NOT use target HR for exercise intensity — use RPE 12–14 or MET level
  • AFib rate control goal: rest 60–80, activity 80–110 bpm
Respiratory & Oxygen Parameters
SpO2 & Respiratory Rate
  • Normal SpO2: 95–100%
  • SpO2 <90% = stop exercise, apply supplemental O2 if available, call team
  • COPD patients may baseline at 88–92% — know the patient's baseline
  • Normal RR: 12–20 breaths/min
  • RR >28–30 at rest (as in asthma/CHF exacerbation cases) = do not start exercise
  • RR <10 on opioids = respiratory depression — call team immediately
When to STOP Exercise & Escalate
  • Chest pain, pressure, or radiation to arm/jaw
  • Severe SOB disproportionate to activity
  • Syncope or near-syncope
  • Severe headache (rule out hypertensive emergency)
  • New or worsening neurological symptoms
  • BG <70 mg/dL
  • SpO2 <90%
  • BP >250/115 during exercise
  • RR <10 (possible opioid overdose)
  • Signs of serotonin syndrome (fever, agitation, tremor, diaphoresis)

Your patient is on metoprolol. How do you guide exercise intensity?
tap
Do NOT use heart rate. Metoprolol is a beta-1 selective beta-blocker that blunts the HR response to exercise. The patient's HR will not rise normally with exertion, so a target HR is meaningless. Use RPE (Borg Scale: 12–14 = moderate) or MET-based prescription instead. Also: never abruptly discontinue beta-blockers — rebound tachycardia and sudden cardiac death risk (withdrawal syndrome 2–3 days). Check that patient is still taking it if vitals suddenly look "normal" HR at rest.
What are the anticholinergic side effects? What mnemonic helps you remember?
tap
"Can't See, Spit, Pee, or Poop" — plus Confused and Tachy
Blurred vision (dilated pupil). Dry mouth (decreased salivation). Urinary retention. Constipation. Tachycardia and palpitations. Headache. Changes in mental status/confusion. Reduced sweating → hyperthermia risk during exercise. PT action: guard patient from falls (blurred vision), monitor mental status, do not push exercise in heat. Drugs with anticholinergic effects: scopolamine, first-gen antihistamines (Benadryl), TCAs, atropine, benztropine (PD), bladder drugs. Benadryl in elderly = Beers list = avoid.
What is serotonin syndrome? What causes it and what are the signs?
tap
Serotonin syndrome = excess serotonin, often from drug combinations. Stop therapy and call physician if suspected. Causes: SSRIs + MAOIs, SSRIs + tramadol, SSRIs + triptans, SNRIs, St. John's Wort, meperidine (Demerol), dextromethorphan, cocaine. Signs: confusion, agitation, dilated pupils, rapid heart rate, changes in BP/temperature, tremor, muscle twitching, shivering, heavy sweating, diarrhea, N&V. Severe (life-threatening): high fever, seizures, irregular heartbeat, loss of consciousness. This can present in your clinic — recognize it.
A patient's BG is 62 mg/dL mid-session. What do you do?
tap
STOP exercise immediately. Recognize hypoglycemia: shakiness, sweating, palpitations, anxiety, confusion, dizziness. Give 15–20g of glucose or simple carbohydrates — glucose tablets, 1/2 cup fruit juice, 1/2 cup regular soda, 5–6 hard candies. Recheck BG after 15 minutes. If still low — repeat. Do NOT resume exercise until BG ≥70 mg/dL. If unconscious: do NOT give oral glucose. Call 911. Position safely. If glucagon is available and you are trained — administer IM. Document everything.
Why should you NEVER apply heat over a transdermal patch?
tap
Heat modalities (hot packs, ultrasound, diathermy) over a transdermal patch dramatically increase the rate of drug absorption — the heat increases skin vasodilation and diffusion, delivering much more drug than intended. Fentanyl patch + heat = opioid overdose. Nitroglycerin patch + heat = severe hypotension and syncope. Other patches to watch: estrogen, nicotine, scopolamine, clonidine. Always ask patients if they have any patches before applying any heat modality — check the whole body, not just the treatment area. This is a never event in PT.
What are statin ADEs relevant to PT? What should you report?
tap
Statins (atorvastatin, simvastatin, rosuvastatin) lower LDL cholesterol. PT-critical ADEs: skeletal muscle pain, tenderness, and weakness (statin myopathy). Can range from mild myalgia to severe rhabdomyolysis (muscle breakdown, myoglobin in urine, renal failure — rare). Statin myopathy looks exactly like deconditioning — the PT is often first to identify it. Risk factors: high dose, older age, female sex, renal impairment, hypothyroidism, concurrent medications (gemfibrozil increases risk significantly). If patient complains of unexplained muscle pain/weakness on a statin: report to physician before progressing exercise. CK (creatinine kinase) levels may be ordered — elevated CK confirms muscle damage.
INR is 3.5 in your patient. Normal therapeutic range? When is it a PT concern?
tap
Normal INR: 0.8–1.2 (healthy person not on anticoagulant). Therapeutic range for most indications (AFib, DVT/PE): 2–3. INR 3.5 = slightly above therapeutic range but may be acceptable depending on indication. INR >5 = high bleeding risk — contact physician before vigorous exercise. PT precautions at any therapeutic anticoagulation level: avoid aggressive joint manipulation, document bruising, report spontaneous bleeds, watch for fall risk. Warfarin is used to prevent cardioembolic stroke in AFib patients. Warfarin in case study: INR 3.5 with hemoglobin of 10 = possible internal bleeding — flag this combination.
Why schedule PT for a Parkinson's patient 1 hour after their medication dose?
tap
Parkinson's disease meds (primarily levodopa/carbidopa) have a clear peak effect window: the medication takes about 30–60 minutes to reach peak brain concentration after ingestion. The "on" period = medication working = best motor function, least rigidity, most response to PT cues and exercises. During the "off" period (medication wearing off between doses), the patient may be significantly more rigid, slower, and tremoring — therapy is less effective and more frustrating for the patient. Additionally: ensure bowel and bladder care BEFORE the session (increased abdominal pressure during exercise + anticholinergic-reduced GI motility = accidents). Watch for orthostatic hypotension — slow position changes.
What is the difference between drug tolerance, physical dependence, and addiction?
tap
Tolerance: physiologic state where an increased dose is needed to produce the same effect. Normal with chronic opioid use. Does NOT equal addiction. Physical dependence: physiologic adaptation where abrupt cessation produces withdrawal symptoms. Also normal with chronic opioid use. Does NOT equal addiction. Addiction: primary, chronic neurobiological disease characterized by impaired control over drug use, compulsive use, craving, and continued use despite harm. Genetic, psychosocial, and environmental factors contribute. Clinical importance: a cancer patient on chronic morphine who needs escalating doses and experiences withdrawal if it is stopped is NOT an addict — they are tolerant and physically dependent. These are normal physiological responses to opioid therapy.
What are the Beers Criteria drugs most relevant to PT practice?
tap
The Beers List = medications potentially inappropriate for older adults. PT-critical Beers drugs: Benzodiazepines (Ativan, Xanax, Valium) — sedation, fall risk, cognitive impairment. Non-benzo hypnotics (Ambien/zolpidem) — somnambulism, falls. First-gen antihistamines (Benadryl/diphenhydramine) — anticholinergic, sedation, confusion. TCAs (amitriptyline) — anticholinergic, arrhythmia risk, falls. Skeletal muscle relaxants (cyclobenzaprine, methocarbamol) — sedation, anticholinergic, falls. NSAIDs — GI bleed, renal impairment, CV risk. Meperidine (Demerol) — neurotoxic metabolite accumulates. All = heightened fall risk in your elderly SNF patients.
What are the ADEs of benzodiazepines critical for PT, and what non-pharm alternatives can you offer?
tap
Benzo ADEs (Beers list): sedation, drowsiness, fatigue. Impaired balance and coordination — MAJOR fall risk. Amnesia and memory impairment. Confusion, disorientation. Dizziness, ataxia, dysarthria. Paradoxical agitation (especially elderly/children). Respiratory depression (especially with opioids — most dangerous combination). Impaired judgment. Non-pharm alternatives PT can provide: diaphragmatic breathing exercises. Progressive muscle relaxation. Yoga, tai chi. Regular endurance exercise (ACSM 150 min/week moderate intensity during day — reduces anxiety significantly). Sleep hygiene education. Cognitive-behavioral strategies. Exercise itself is a proven anxiolytic and antidepressant — this is your evidence-based intervention.
What are the exercise BG cutoffs for diabetic patients? What do you do at each level?
tap
<70 mg/dL: STOP exercise immediately. Treat hypoglycemia (15–20g carbs, recheck in 15 min, repeat until ≥70). 70–100 mg/dL (on hypoglycemic meds): consume carbohydrates BEFORE exercising. 100–250 mg/dL: safe to exercise — ideal range. T1DM with BG >250 — check urine ketones: no ketones = exercise with caution; ketones present = DO NOT exercise. BG >300: risk of dehydration and ketoacidosis — postpone exercise. T2DM: may exercise if feels well and hydrated (clinical judgment). Special: retinopathy = no heavy lifting. Diabetic foot ulcer = no weight bearing. Peripheral neuropathy = inspect feet daily, protective footwear.
What are the ADEs of systemic corticosteroids (prednisone) most relevant to PT?
tap
Systemic corticosteroids (prednisone, methylprednisolone) have massive ADE burden with long-term use: Musculoskeletal: proximal muscle weakness (steroid myopathy — insidious, can mimic deconditioning), osteoporosis (↓bone density → fracture risk — fall precautions are critical), avascular necrosis of femoral head with chronic use. Metabolic: hyperglycemia (raises BG significantly — monitor diabetic patients on corticosteroids more frequently), weight gain, fluid retention. Immune: immunosuppression → infection risk → poor wound healing. Skin: fragility, easy bruising. CNS: mood changes (euphoria or depression), insomnia. NEVER stop abruptly — adrenal insufficiency. If patient stopped prednisone on their own (as in asthma case) — expect disease exacerbation AND adrenal crisis risk.
What do you do if a patient takes sublingual nitroglycerin during your session and has no relief after 3 doses?
tap
CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY. No relief after 3 sublingual NTG doses = patient may be having a myocardial infarction (MI). NTG protocol: (1) Have patient SIT or lie down — NTG causes rapid BP drop and will cause syncope if standing. (2) Administer sublingual NTG. (3) Wait 5 minutes. (4) If no relief, administer 2nd dose. (5) Wait 5 minutes. (6) If no relief, administer 3rd dose. (7) Still no relief = call 911. While waiting for EMS: monitor vitals, keep patient calm and still, do NOT allow them to walk. NTG ADEs: dizziness, flushing, headache, reflex tachycardia. Never apply heat over NTG patch.
MS vs. GBS — how do medications and PT approach differ?
tap
MS = CNS (brain and spinal cord) demyelination — oligodendroglia. Medications: disease-modifying therapies (beta-interferons, glatiramer, natalizumab, fingolimod), baclofen/tizanidine for spasticity. PT: reduce spasticity, exercise in cool environment (Uhthoff's phenomenon — heat worsens symptoms), energy conservation, strength and balance training. GBS = PNS demyelination — Schwann cells. Acute: IVIG or plasmapheresis + supportive care. Medications: analgesics (gabapentin, opioids), anticoagulants (DVT prevention from immobility), antibiotics. PT: monitor respiratory function (can progress to respiratory failure), monitor vitals (labile BP from autonomic dysfunction), slow progression of exercise, fall precautions. MS is chronic/relapsing; GBS is typically acute and most patients recover significantly.
What insulin types carry the highest hypoglycemia risk during PT, and when are peaks?
tap
Rapid-acting insulins (Humalog/lispro, Novolog/aspart, Apidra/glulisine): onset 5–15 min, PEAK ~1 hour, duration 2–4 hrs. Highest hypoglycemia risk occurs ~1 hour after injection. Schedule PT session to avoid this window if possible (wait 2–3 hrs after injection). Regular/short-acting (Humulin R): onset 30 min, peak 2–3 hrs, duration 3–6 hrs. Intermediate NPH: onset 2–4 hrs, peak 4–12 hrs, duration 12–18 hrs — late-day exercise may coincide with peak. Long-acting (Lantus/glargine, Levemir/detemir): flat profile, minimal peak — lowest hypoglycemia risk. Sulfonylureas also cause hypoglycemia independent of meal timing — always have glucose source available for these patients.
What ADEs from baclofen and tizanidine affect PT participation?
tap
Baclofen (oral GABA-B agonist for spasticity): drowsiness is most common (10–63% of patients — very frequent). Also: asthenia/weakness, confusion, dizziness, fatigue, headache, N/V, constipation. NEVER abruptly stop baclofen — can cause hallucinations, psychosis, seizures, hyperthermia (withdrawal is serious). Tizanidine (central alpha-2 agonist for spasticity): weakness and fatigue 41–78%. Hypotension 16–33% (dose-dependent). Bradycardia 2–10%. PT impact: check BP before session (significant hypotension risk), allow slow position changes, reduce exercise intensity if patient is notably weaker or drowsier than baseline. Both drugs: if patient seems excessively sedated or has weakness beyond expected MS level, consider drug as a contributing factor.
What is the CHADS2 score and how does it determine anticoagulation in AFib?
tap
CHADS2 score determines stroke risk in patients with atrial fibrillation to guide anticoagulation decision: CHF = 1 pt. HTN = 1 pt. Age >75 = 1 pt. Diabetes = 1 pt. Stroke or prior TIA = 2 pts. Score 0: ASA alone (low risk). Score 1: warfarin or ASA. Score 2+: warfarin (or NOAC). Mechanism: AFib causes atria to quiver rather than contract — blood pools → thrombus forms — embolizes → cardioembolic stroke. Anticoagulation prevents the thrombus. PT: anticoagulated AFib patients have bleeding risk — document bruising, monitor INR, fall precautions are critical.
Patient on opioids has RR of 6 breaths/min. What is happening and what do you do?
tap
Opioid-induced respiratory depression. Normal RR = 12–20 breaths/min. RR of 6 = severe respiratory depression — medical emergency. This can occur from: opioid overdose, PCA malfunction (patient receiving excessive doses without adequate monitoring), drug-drug interaction (opioid + benzodiazepine is most dangerous combination). Actions: (1) Stop any PT activity immediately. (2) Call for help — call nursing staff/physician or 911 if no medical staff available. (3) Position patient safely, do not leave alone. (4) Apply oxygen if available and trained. (5) Reversal agent: naloxone (Narcan) — antagonizes opioid receptors. Reverses respiratory depression but also reverses analgesia (patient may awaken in pain). Document the RR, time, patient status, and your actions.
Why should NSAIDs NOT be combined with lisinopril? And how do NSAIDs affect wound healing?
tap
NSAID + ACE inhibitor (lisinopril): two problems. First, NSAIDs promote sodium and water retention via prostaglandin inhibition in the kidney — this directly counteracts the BP-lowering effect of the ACE inhibitor. Second, NSAIDs can worsen renal function, and ACE inhibitors also affect renal blood flow — combined, they can precipitate acute kidney injury (AKI). This is a well-documented dangerous drug interaction. NSAIDs and wound healing: prostaglandins are essential for the inflammatory phase of wound healing (they recruit macrophages, initiate angiogenesis, stimulate fibroblast proliferation). NSAIDs block COX enzymes → suppress prostaglandins → impair inflammatory response → delayed wound healing. Do NOT use NSAIDs routinely in patients with active wounds — clinically relevant in your wound care patients.
