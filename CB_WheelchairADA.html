Wheelchair & ADA Accessibility

Wheelchair types, components, measurement, fitting, propulsion, and functional mobility skills. Plus ADA accessibility standards for built environments — the regulatory framework every clinician needs to know for discharge planning and community integration.

WC Types & Components Fitting & Measurement Propulsion Techniques Functional Mobility ADA Standards Pressure Relief
Wheelchair Types
Wheelchair selection is driven by patient goals, functional abilities, environment, and prognosis. Every W/C is a trade-off between stability, maneuverability, weight, cost, and independence. Know the indications for each type.
Selection Factors — Before Choosing Any W/C
Patient-Centered Factors
  • Patient's goals and desires — what they want to do
  • Anticipated use — indoors only, community, sports
  • Abilities vs. limitations — UE strength, cognition, vision, tone
  • Age, height, and weight — drives size and weight capacity
  • Projected changes over time / prognosis — progressive vs. stable condition
Contextual Factors
  • Caregiver needs — ability to push, lift, manage equipment
  • Financial resources — insurance coverage, funding sources
  • Community and social resources — accessible environment
  • Home environment — doorways, ramps, flooring surface
Key Principle

Wheelchair options present many trade-offs for the user. No single W/C is optimal for all situations. Good W/C fit increases functional independence; poor fit limits function and can cause injury.

Standard Wheelchair Structure
All wheelchairs consist of two main systems: a postural support system and a mobility base.
Postural Support System
  • Seat — primary weight-bearing surface; depth and width critical
  • Back — height determines trunk support level
  • Armrests — height affects posture and propulsion
  • Front rigging — legrests and footplates; support LEs
Mobility Base
  • Frame — folding vs. rigid; weight and durability trade-offs
  • Axle — position affects stability and propulsion efficiency
  • Drive wheels, tires, and locks — rear large wheels; propulsion surface
  • Casters — front small wheels; maneuvering
Wheelchair Types

Standard Adult Wheelchair

Most commonAll-purpose manual▼

Most commonly prescribed manual wheelchair. Foldable for transport. Suited for patients with adequate UE strength for self-propulsion, or for dependent propulsion by a caregiver. Comes in a range of seat widths and depths. The baseline against which other W/C types are compared.

Transport Wheelchair

Smaller rear wheelsDependent only▼

Smaller, lighter, with smaller rear wheels — patient cannot self-propel (no large handrims accessible). Designed strictly for caregiver-assisted transport. Useful for patients who will never self-propel. Lighter and easier to fold/stow.

One-Arm Drive Wheelchair

Hemiplegia / CVADouble rim or lever system▼

Designed for patients with one functional UE (e.g., hemiplegia post-CVA). Uses a double-pushrim system on the functional side — two pushrims on one wheel control both wheels. Inner rim controls ipsilateral wheel; outer rim controls contralateral. Alternatively: lever-drive system. Allows independent propulsion with one arm. Can also be propelled with one UE and one LE.

Amputee Wheelchair

Axle shifted posteriorlyCompensates for posterior CoG▼

Rear axle is positioned further posterior than in a standard W/C. This compensates for the posterior shift in center of gravity (CoG) caused by the absence of one or both lower extremities. Without this modification, an amputee in a standard W/C would be at high risk of tipping backward. As the patient is fitted for a prosthesis and begins bearing weight through the LE, the axle may be repositioned forward.

Reclining Wheelchair

Adjustable back anglePressure management▼

Back reclines to various angles, allowing partial or near-full supine positioning. Useful for patients who cannot tolerate prolonged upright sitting (orthostatic hypotension, spasticity, tone management), or who need catheter care while in W/C. Heavier and less maneuverable than standard W/C.

Tilt-in-Space Wheelchair

Pressure reliefMaintains seat angle▼

The entire seat-to-back angle stays the same as the chair tilts posteriorly. This distributes weight off the ischial tuberosities onto the posterior back and head — effective pressure relief without changing the hip-to-trunk angle. Used for patients with high spasticity (changing seat angle would increase spasm), poor trunk control, or severe pressure injury risk. Very different from a reclining W/C which changes the back-to-seat angle.

Tilt vs. Recline

Tilt-in-space: Whole unit tilts back; seat-to-back angle stays fixed. Better for spasticity. Reclining: Only the back reclines; seat-to-back angle changes. May increase extensor tone.

Power Wheelchair & Scooter

Insufficient UE strengthJoystick or specialty control▼

Power Wheelchair

For patients who cannot manually propel (insufficient UE strength, endurance, or coordination). Controlled via joystick, sip-and-puff, head control, or eye-gaze systems depending on level of function. Requires adequate cognition and visual-perceptual skills for safe operation.

Scooter — Competency Checklist

  • Speed control
  • Effective braking
  • Maneuvering through doorways, bedrooms, bathrooms
  • Smooth and safe parking at tables

Other Specialized Types

Sports / Pediatric / Standing▼
  • Sports wheelchairs — lightweight, angled wheels for stability and speed; sport-specific designs (basketball, racing, tennis)
  • Pediatric W/C — sized for children; adjustable to accommodate growth
  • Integrated Standing W/C — allows controlled transition to partial or full standing while in chair; promotes bone density, circulation, ROM
  • Custom/specialty configurations — e.g., chair designed for patient with hemiplegia + transtibial amputation (modified footrest on one side, adapted armrest)

Seating & Positioning Goals
Alignment

Improve postural alignment — head, trunk, pelvis in neutral as possible

Deformity Prevention

Decrease risk of developing secondary deformities from prolonged abnormal positioning

Tissue Integrity

Prevent tissue damage — distribute pressure over maximum surface area

Function

Enhance UE function, communication, feeding, and interaction with environment

Seating — Comfort Factors
Clinical Seating Goals
  • Support for pelvis — foundation of all sitting posture
  • Stability — enough postural support to feel secure
  • Sitting balance — positioned to allow maximal available balance
  • Pressure distribution — avoid focal pressure on bony prominences
  • Comfort — sustainable posture over hours
Sacral Sitting — Watch For This

Pelvis posterior-tilted, patient "sitting on tailbone." Reverse lumbar curve. Causes IT pressure → pressure injury, and limits UE function. Quick check: look for posterior pelvic tilt before confirming fit.

Cushion Types

Standard Foam

Basic comfort and distribution. Compresses over time. Least expensive.

Gel Cushion

Better pressure distribution; heavier. Good for moderate pressure risk patients.

Air Cushion (Roho)

Highest pressure relief. Interconnected air cells distribute load. Requires correct inflation. For high pressure risk (SCI, non-sensate patients).

Solid Seat Insert

Prevents sling seat hammocking. Provides firm base for positioning. Often used under foam/gel cushion.

Wheelchair Fitting & Measurement
Measurements are converted to W/C dimensions. Good fit = independence and skin protection. Poor fit = injury, poor posture, limited function. Measure with patient in optimal position (~90°–90°–90°: hips/knees/ankles) and accommodate typical clothing, footwear, and cushion.
Measurement Principles

Measure patient in optimal position (~90°–90°–90°) · Do NOT contour tape measure to follow body contours · Accommodate typical clothing, footwear, and cushion thickness · Positioning the pelvis well will help position the rest of the body well · Beware of sacral sitting during measurement

The 5 Key Measurements

Seat Depth

Measure − 2 inchesSacral sitting risk▼

How to Measure

  1. Measure from back of hips to popliteal fold
  2. Subtract 2 inches to determine seat depth
Too Much Seat Depth

Popliteal pressure → restricts circulation, nerve compression behind knee. Patient slides forward into sacral sitting to relieve pressure.

Too Little Seat Depth

Inadequate ischial tuberosity support → increased focal IT pressure + lack of trunk support.

Seat Width

Measure + 1–2 inches▼

How to Measure

  1. Measure distance between outside edges of hips
  2. Add 1 to 2 inches to determine seat width
Too Wide

Difficulty propelling — arms abduct too much to reach pushrims efficiently. Harder to maneuver through doorways.

Too Narrow

Excessive lateral pressure on hips and thighs → pressure injury risk. Difficulty with pressure relief shifts.

Seat-to-Floor Height (Front Rigging Length)

Sole to popliteal − cushion + 2 inches▼

How to Measure

  1. Measure from sole of the shoe to the popliteal fold
  2. Subtract the thickness of the compressed cushion (if used)
  3. Add ~2 inches for footrest clearance to determine front rigging length
Too High

Footrests too low → thighs hang; increased IT pressure; lack of trunk support.

Too Low

Footrest strikes the ground → difficulty with transfers; potential tipping hazard on uneven terrain.

Seat Back Height

Seat surface to inferior scapular angle − 4 inches▼

How to Measure

  1. Height varies depending on level of support needed
  2. For standard W/C: measure from seating surface to inferior angle of scapula (or to axilla)
  3. Subtract ~4 inches
  4. Add compressed cushion thickness if used
Too High

Restricts shoulder movement and arm function — difficulty using arms for propulsion or table activities.

Too Low

Insufficient trunk support → fatigue, poor posture, risk of falling backward.

Armrest Height

Olecranon to seat + cushion + 1 inch▼

How to Measure

  1. Position upper arm close to trunk; flex elbow to 90°
  2. Measure from olecranon process vertically to seating surface
  3. Add compressed cushion thickness if used
  4. Add 1 inch to determine armrest height
Too High

Shoulder shrug → poor functional position; increased upper trapezius tension; difficult to reach items.

Too Low

Poor posture — patient leans to one side or forward to reach armrest; spinal asymmetry over time.

Patient & Family Education
Pressure Management — Non-Negotiables
  1. Skin inspection at bony prominences daily (ITs, sacrum, trochanters, heels)
  2. Pressure relief schedule — every 15–30 minutes in W/C
  3. W/C cushion does NOT eliminate the need for pressure relief
  4. Check for changes in circulation (color, temperature, swelling)

Wheelchair Propulsion
Propulsion technique affects efficiency, upper extremity health, and patient dignity. For the clinician assisting with dependent propulsion, body mechanics are critical. Good W/C fit is the prerequisite for efficient independent propulsion.
Independent Propulsion — Level Surfaces
Both Hands (Standard)
  • Start position: 10 o'clock on the pushrim
  • Release at 2 or 3 o'clock — smooth follow-through
  • Long, smooth strokes are more efficient and less damaging to shoulders than short rapid strokes
  • Turning: move one pushrim forward while holding the other still or pulling it back
Other Propulsion Methods
  • One or both feet: requires good shoe grip and low enough seat height; effective for hemiplegia
  • Combined UE + LE: one hand on pushrim, opposite foot on floor; efficient for hemiplegia
  • One-arm drive W/C: double pushrim system allows bilateral wheel control from one side
Dependent Propulsion
Level Surfaces
  • Good body mechanics — use LEs for pushing; bend knees slightly
  • Smooth, even pace — protect patient's dignity
  • Apply wheel locks when stationary — always, even briefly
  • Keep patient's feet on footrests; watch for foot-to-floor contact on tight turns
Uneven Surfaces
  • Carpet, grass, gravel, and sand increase resistance significantly
  • May need to tip W/C back to lift casters off surface to prevent casters from catching or digging in
  • Use tipping lever with foot; maintain wide stance for stability
  • Maintain patient communication — tipping can be alarming
Wheelies
Independent Advanced Skill

Balancing the W/C on the drive wheels only (casters lifted off the ground). Necessary for independent community mobility: curbs, rough terrain, soft surfaces. Requires strong UE, excellent balance, and practice. Must be practiced in an appropriate supervised setting with a spotter until proficiency is demonstrated.

Functional Wheelchair Mobility
Teach patients and caregivers to safely manage the environments they will actually encounter. Doorways, ramps, curbs, and steps are daily challenges. Technique matters — for patient safety and clinician back health.
Doorways
Dependent — Door Opens Away from Patient
  1. Clinician moves backward through door while pushing W/C
  2. Once through, turn W/C to face direction of travel
Dependent — Door Opens Toward Patient
  1. Clinician steers W/C beyond the door to one side
  2. Opens the door fully
  3. Moves W/C through the doorway while preventing door from closing on patient
Independent — Door Opens Away
  1. Push door open wide
  2. Propel quickly through doorway
  3. Use front rigging or hand to block door from closing
Independent — Door Opens Toward
  1. Pull door open wide
  2. Propel W/C through doorway
  3. Use front rigging or hand to block door from closing
Ramps
Ramp Standards (ADA)

Maximum slope: 1:12 (1 inch rise per 12 inches of length). Maximum overall rise: 30 inches between landings.

Ascending — Dependent
  • Up forward with all four wheels on the ground
  • Can zigzag if slope is steep
  • Patient facilitates by leaning forward and pushing on handrims
Descending — Dependent
  • Option 1 (preferred): Backward, all four wheels on ground; attendant focuses on fixed object ahead to maintain straight path, glances back periodically; patient maintains erect trunk, applies friction to pushrims with palms of gloved hands
  • Option 2: Forward in tipped position; attendant wide BOS, deep stance
Curbs — Dependent
Four methods depending on direction of approach and descent. Ascending forward is easiest; descending backward is also generally easier. Know which direction is more challenging for each.

Ascending Forward (Easiest)

Preferred ascent▼
  1. Tip wheelchair back — lift casters onto curb
  2. Place casters up on curb
  3. Roll drive wheels up against and over the curb
  4. Use LEs to power the roll of drive wheels up onto curb

Descending Backward (Easiest)

Preferred descent▼
  1. Back drive wheels close to edge of curb
  2. Control roll of drive wheels down over curb — use forward LE against wheelchair back as brake
  3. Tip wheelchair back to lift casters off curb
  4. Set casters down gently once they clear curb

Ascending Backward (More Difficult)

Harder ascent▼
  1. Roll drive wheels back against curb
  2. Tip chair back
  3. Use LEs to roll drive wheels up onto curb
  4. Set casters down gently once safely up on curb

Descending Forward (More Difficult)

Harder descent▼
  1. Tip wheelchair back
  2. Roll drive wheels forward to edge of curb
  3. Control descent of drive wheels down over curb with LEs in deep stance
  4. Set casters down gently once chair is fully down
Steps — Dependent
Steps Require 3 People

Managing steps with a wheelchair requires three attendants. Tallest/strongest behind the chair (leading). Two others hold at sides of chair using non-removable parts only. This can be frightening for patients — communicate throughout. Use good body mechanics throughout.

Up Steps (3 People)
  1. Back the chair up against the first step
  2. Tip the chair well back
  3. On "three": lead attendant pulls drive wheels up over step; side helpers assist
  4. Pause at every step
Down Steps (3 People)
  1. Roll chair close to top step
  2. Tip the chair well back
  3. On "three": lead attendant controls roll of drive wheels down over step; side helpers assist
  4. Pause at every step
ADA Accessibility Standards
Title III of the Americans with Disabilities Act requires public accommodations to remove architectural barriers when "readily achievable." As PTs, ADA knowledge drives home assessment, community reintegration planning, and advocacy for patients who use assistive devices.
Four ADA Priorities for Barrier Removal

Priority 1: Accessible approach and entrance · Priority 2: Access to goods and services · Priority 3: Access to restrooms · Priority 4: Any other measures necessary. Address in this order when resources are limited.

PT Clinical Application — Why ADA Matters in Practice

ADA knowledge is essential for home assessment (identifying barriers before discharge), community mobility training (teaching patients to navigate accessible and inaccessible spaces), and documentation (supporting adaptive equipment justification and home modification recommendations). When patients use wheelchairs or assistive devices, environmental barriers become clinical barriers to independence.

Why does an amputee wheelchair have the rear axle shifted posteriorly?
tap
The absence of one or both lower extremities shifts the patient's center of gravity posteriorly. Shifting the rear axle back compensates for this shift, preventing the chair from tipping backward. As the patient begins WB through a prosthesis, the axle may be moved forward.
Tilt-in-space vs. reclining wheelchair — what's the key difference?
tap
Tilt-in-space: the entire unit tilts back; the seat-to-back angle stays FIXED. Better for patients with high spasticity (changing seat angle would increase spasm). Reclining: only the back reclines; seat-to-back angle changes. May increase extensor tone. Tilt provides pressure relief without postural disruption.
How do you measure for seat depth and what's the formula?
tap
Measure from back of hips to popliteal fold. Then subtract 2 inches to get seat depth. The 2-inch subtraction prevents popliteal pressure while keeping adequate IT support. Confirmation: 2 fingers horizontally between popliteal fold and front edge of seat.
What are the 5 two-finger confirmation checks for W/C fit?
tap
(1) Seat width: 2 fingers between hip and side of chair. (2) Seat depth: 2 fingers between popliteal fold and front edge of seat. (3) Seat height: 2 fingers between front of chair and distal thigh. (4) Legrest length: 2 fingers between footplate and floor. (5) Back height: 2 fingers between top of back and patient's axilla.
What is the most critical patient/family education point about W/C cushions?
tap
A wheelchair cushion does NOT eliminate the need for pressure relief. Patients must still perform pressure relief every 15–30 minutes regardless of cushion type. Even the most advanced air cushion (Roho) does not replace active pressure relief. Also: daily skin inspection at bony prominences (ITs, sacrum, trochanters, heels).
Independent propulsion on level surfaces — what is the correct stroke technique?
tap
Start hands at 10 o'clock on the pushrim, release at 2 or 3 o'clock. Long, smooth strokes are more efficient and less damaging to shoulder structures than short rapid strokes. Turning: move one pushrim forward while holding the other still or pulling it back.
Descending a ramp dependently — which direction and what does the patient do?
tap
Preferred method: backward, all four wheels on the ground. Attendant focuses on a fixed object ahead to maintain straight path, glancing back periodically. Patient maintains erect trunk and applies friction to pushrims with palms of gloved hands to help control speed. Alternative: forward in tipped position with attendant in wide BOS, deep stance.
Curb management — which combination is easiest? Which is hardest?
tap
Easiest ascending: Forward (tip back → casters up → roll drive wheels up). Easiest descending: Backward (back to edge → control drive wheel roll down → tip to clear casters → set down gently). Harder: ascending backward, descending forward. Always set casters down gently after clearing the curb.
How many people are needed to take a wheelchair up steps, and who leads?
tap
Three attendants required. The tallest/strongest leads from behind the chair. Two others hold at the sides using non-removable parts only of the frame. On "three," lead pulls drive wheels up over step. Pause at every step. This can be frightening for patients — communicate throughout. Use good body mechanics.
ADA: What is the minimum door clear opening and what must the pull side have?
tap
Minimum 32 inches clear door opening. Pull side must have 18 inches of clear wall space next to the handle — a person using a wheelchair needs this space to approach close enough to pull the door open. Handle must be operable with a closed fist (lever or loop — not round knob) at maximum 48 inches height. Interior door force: maximum 5 lbf.
ADA: What are the ramp slope and rise requirements?
tap
Maximum slope: 1:12 (1 inch rise per 12 inches of length). Maximum rise between landings: 30 inches. Ramps longer than 6 feet need railings on both sides at 34–38 inches height. 5-foot level landing required at every 30 feet of horizontal length, at top and bottom, and at switchbacks. Surface must be non-slip.
ADA: What are the 4 priorities for barrier removal, in order?
tap
Priority 1: Accessible approach and entrance (route, ramps, parking, doors). Priority 2: Access to goods and services (interior circulation, counters, controls, signage). Priority 3: Access to restrooms (stall, grab bars, lavatory, toilet height). Priority 4: Any other measures (fountains, phones, elevators). Address in this order when resources are limited.
ADA: Accessible restroom stall dimensions and toilet seat height?
tap
Accessible stall: 5 feet × 5 feet clear of door swing. Grab bars behind and on side wall nearest toilet. Toilet seat height: 17–19 inches (standard is 15" — ADA requires raised height). Lavatory rim: max 34". Clear space in front of lavatory: 30" × 48". Faucet: closed-fist operable. Mirror bottom edge: max 40".
Seat back height — how to measure and what does the formula account for?
tap
Measure from seating surface to inferior angle of scapula (or axilla), then subtract ~4 inches. Add compressed cushion thickness if used. The 4-inch subtraction allows free arm movement for propulsion and upper extremity function. Too high = restricts arm use; too low = insufficient trunk support.
