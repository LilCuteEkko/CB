Spinal Manipulation — Screening, Technique & Safety
The most reported adverse event from cervical manipulation is cerebrovascular accident from vertebrobasilar insufficiency (VBI). Red flag presentations mimic neuromusculoskeletal cervicocranial syndromes — invariably, the two conditions coexist. Manual therapists MUST include cervical arterial dysfunction screening in daily clinical decision-making.

Cervical Arterial Dysfunction — Screening & Recommendations

▼
8 Key Recommendations for Cervical Manipulation Safety

(1) Develop a high index of suspicion for cervical vascular pathology, particularly after cervical trauma. Chronic pain and psychological issues are big factors. (2) Neck pain and headache may be precursors to posterior circulation ischemia. (3) Expand theory to encompass the WHOLE cervical vascular system, including carotid arteries (not just vertebral). (4) Include hemodynamic principles and their relationship to movement anatomy and biomechanics. (5) Awareness that current objective tests have limitations — reliance on testing alone is NOT enough. (6) Enhance subjective/objective exam by including vascular risk factors (HTN) and procedures (cranial nerve exam, simple eye exam). (7) For acute onset headache “unlike any other,” use conservative techniques in early stages. (8) Where frank arterial injury is suspected, immediate triage to emergency center with report of any treatment methods undertaken.

HVLAT Manipulation — Mechanisms

Biomechanical: Addresses mechanical spine pain through joint mobilization/cavitation. Hypoalgesic: Central mediated responses — manipulation activates descending pain inhibitory pathways. Neurophysiologic: Muscle relaxation of paravertebral muscles, increased pain and pressure thresholds, increased muscle activation post-manipulation. Comprehensive PMH/subjective questioning is required to determine any contraindications or need for imaging BEFORE manipulation.

Clinical Reasoning — Expert vs Novice Patterns
Clinical decision-making is the process of PT interaction with the patient that helps structure meaning, goals, and health management strategies based on clinical data, patient choice, professional judgment, and knowledge. Three premises: (1) thought leading to action requires deliberation within a specific context, (2) the nature of thought is tied to prior experiences, (3) prior experiences provide the framework for developing action plans.

Expert vs Novice Clinician Traits

Expert PT demonstrates: Efficient forward-reasoning (hypothesis → test → confirm/refute). Balance of analytical problem-solving WITH narrative reasoning focused on the patient as individual. Ongoing collaborative reasoning WITH the patient. Emphasis on empowering, engaging, and educating the patient rather than relying on own skills/techniques. Greater use of reflection-in-action and self-assessment during interactions. Novice clinician: Relies more on techniques than patient empowerment. Less frequent reflection-on-action. Limited reflection-in-action. More likely to miss the patient-as-a-person in favor of the diagnosis. Key finding: Participants who gave greater attention to patient education and empowerment also demonstrated greater use of reflection-in-action.

Dunning-Kruger Effect & Treatment Paradigm

Dunning-Kruger: Cognitive bias where people with limited knowledge greatly overestimate their own knowledge relative to objective criteria. Be aware of this in yourself and in students. Treatment paradigm: (1) Eval/re-eval. (2) Manual therapy. (3) Test/retest. (4) Motor control/re-education. (5) Functional movement/activation. (6) Main impairment focus. Every treatment should follow this cycle: intervene → measure the effect → adjust. Don’t just “do techniques” — reason through the WHY at every step.

Overhead Athlete — 5 Pillars, GIRD & Plyometrics
Overhead athletes require specialized evaluation and treatment. The exam includes ROM (total arc, GIRD, GERG, cross-body, lat flexibility, horizontal abduction), strength (RIMS, dynamometer, prone T endurance), motor control (scapular assessment), video analysis of mechanics, and structured return-to-sport through 5 progressive pillars.

Plyometric Exercise — 3 Phases (Davies 2015)

SAID principle (Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands) → periodization in terminal rehab phases. Phase 1 — Eccentric Pre-stretch: Pre-loads/pre-stretches the muscle spindle and non-contractile tissue. Stimulates neurophysiological-biomechanical response → enhanced resultant concentric contraction. More energy stored during eccentric = more powerful concentric. Phase 2 — Amortization (Time to Rebound): Time from cessation of eccentric to onset of concentric. Shorter = better (energy stored and used more efficiently). Primary goal of plyometric training = decrease amortization time. Phase 3 — Concentric Shortening: Power production and performance phase. The payoff.

GIRD Classification Disagreement

GIRD, TRROM (total range of rotational motion), and humeral torsion-based classifications of shoulder risk in throwing athletes are NOT in agreement and should NOT be used interchangeably. Only 1.9% of athletes were classified as “at-risk” by all 3 approaches. GIRD vs TRROM: poor agreement (Kappa = –0.142). Use multiple measures rather than relying on any single classification system.

Dry Needling — Indications, Contraindications & Technique
Dry needling targets myofascial trigger points (MTrPs) where nociceptive receptors are affected. Creates micro-trauma to stimulate natural tissue healing. Low to moderate evidence for long-term effects. Limited research overall. Should always be used in combination with other treatments — patient education with exercises should be the priority.

Indications, Contraindications, Precautions & Technique

▼
Contraindications

Fear of needles (belonephobia). Bleeding disorders. Cognitive disorder / language barrier / inability to consent. Lymphedema. Diabetes. Neuropathy. Immunocompromised patients.

Precautions (Proceed with Caution)

Cancer. History of fainting or syncope. Skin rash or dermatologic conditions. Children. Pregnant patients (especially first trimester). Anxiety. Anticoagulant use.

Running Gait Analysis — The 4 S’s Framework
Always watch a runner actually run. 25.9% of runners reported at least one run-related injury in 8 weeks (Buist 2008). Males more prone. No previous running experience = most important risk factor. Younger age in males associated with injury risk. The 4 S’s provide a simple, systematic framework for conducting a running analysis.

The 4 S’s — Sound, Strike, Step Rate & Speed

▼
SWhat to AssessClinical Implications
1. SoundDoes runner land quietly or sound like they’re putting a hole through the treadmill? Asymmetry between feet? Does sound change shod vs unshod? Different shoe types?Loud noise = overstriking. Cue: “Quiet your feet.” Helpful if goal is to reduce vGRF. This may increase ankle and knee joint excursions. If landing sound increases: vGRF increases, decreased ankle excursion, increased hip excursion
2. StrikeHeel strike, midfoot (“flat-footed”), or forefoot? Symmetrical? Pattern changes over course of run?Forefoot strike helps with: Tibial stress syndromes, anterior compartment syndrome, anterior knee pain. Caution with FF strike if: Hx of calf complex or metatarsal injury. Heel/rearfoot strike helps with: Achilles tendinopathy, hx of calf strain. FFS results in greater plantarflexion and less negative knee power (absorption)
3. Step Rate (Cadence)Increasing steps reduces magnitude of each loading cycle despite increasing total cycles. Benefits only occur WITH reduction in stride length↑ 5% cadence at constant velocity: Reduces shock absorption at knee by up to 20%. ↑ 10% cadence: Reduces peak hip adduction angle, hip adduction and IR moments. Increasing cadence regardless of strike: Decreases Achilles stress. Decreasing stride length via step rate: Wider step width, decreased contralateral pelvic drop, decreased peak hip adduction, decreased peak ankle eversion, decreased peak ITB strain and strain rate
4. Speed (Velocity)Classification: Jogging = 4.5 mph. Slow running = 7.8 mph. Medium = 11 mph. Fast = 15 mph. Sprinting = 17.9 mphTo run faster: push ground harder OR push more frequently. At speeds <7 m/s: ankle plantarflexors reign supreme. At faster speeds: energy source is more proximal (hip extensors/flexors). This explains why sprinters need hip/glute power while distance runners need calf endurance
Clinical Application

Load management: Increase 10% per week of volume. Try utilizing 2 pairs of shoes (foam decreases after certain mileage threshold, becomes more moldable). Wolf’s Law applies: Bone adapts to loading demands. Skeletal immaturity in younger males contributes to higher RRI risk. Women have wider pelvis → higher Q-angle → different loading patterns. More flexible shoe is generally better for most runners unless specific stability needs exist.

Pregnancy & Postpartum — Exercise, Physiology & Pelvic Floor
Pregnancy creates significant physiological changes that directly affect PT assessment and treatment. Blood volume increases 50% (mostly in first trimester). Joint laxity changes from relaxin and progesterone. Ventilation needs increase. Community support decreases risk of postpartum depression.

Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale & Labor Stages

PPD risk factors: Women with anxiety and depression have higher chance. C-section also higher risk for PPD. Community support is important to decrease risk. Edinburgh scale = screening tool for postnatal depression. Gravidity = how many times pregnant. Parity = how many deliveries. Delivery types: Vaginal, C-section, forceps (still vaginal), vacuum-assisted. Pitocin = medication to help if no progression. Episiotomy = surgical cut at vaginal opening during childbirth. 4 stages of labor: Stage 1 = contractions. Stage 2 = active pushing (increased tear risk if prolonged). Stage 3 = delivery of placenta. Stage 4 = mom and baby recovery.

17-year-old pitcher with medial elbow pain during arm acceleration and cocking. In this case, DDx and exam approach?
tap
High DDx: UCL sprain (medial pain during acceleration/cocking in a pitcher is classic). Moderate: Flexor-pronator mass strain (similar location, different mechanism). Low: Cubital tunnel syndrome, thoracic outlet. Eliminate: OA (too young), little league elbow (medial epicondylar apophysitis — he had this before, different presentation now). Exam: RIMS and ROM of elbow AND shoulder AND wrist (elbow problem can be a shoulder or wrist problem). Moving valgus stress test for UCL. Assess total arc of motion and GIRD at shoulder. Check thoracic mobility. Key history: He plays on 1 travel team year-round, wants college recruitment, hasn’t worked out since pain started (fear avoidance). Fastball hurts most (highest valgus stress). Important: Up-the-chain and down-the-chain assessment is essential — the elbow is often the victim, not the culprit.
Name the 3 phases of plyometric exercise and why is the amortization phase the most important to train?
tap
Phase 1 — Eccentric Pre-stretch: Pre-loads the muscle spindle and non-contractile tissue. Stimulates neurophysiological-biomechanical response that enhances the concentric contraction. More energy stored during eccentric = more powerful output. Phase 2 — Amortization (Time to Rebound): Time from end of eccentric to start of concentric. This is the most important phase to train because shorter amortization = more efficient use of stored elastic energy. If amortization is too long, stored energy dissipates as heat and the plyometric benefit is lost. Phase 3 — Concentric Shortening: Power production phase. The payoff. SAID principle: Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands — plyometric training must be sport-specific. Terminal phase of rehab should include periodization programs that incorporate plyometrics to bridge the gap between clinical rehab and sport performance.
