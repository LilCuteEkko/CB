Pediatrics — Management of the Pediatric & Adolescent Patient

The complete pediatric PT reference — from prenatal development and primitive reflexes through NICU care, cerebral palsy (GMFCS classification), spina bifida, pediatric TBI, childhood cancer, early intervention (IDEA Part C), school-based practice (504/IEP), assistive technology, and transition to adulthood. Organized as a learning pathway: master normal development first, then learn to recognize deviations.

Child Development — Prenatal Through Adolescence
5 domains of development: Motor (gross and fine), Cognitive, Language, Social-Emotional, and Self-Help. Development follows predictable patterns: cephalo-caudal, reflex before cortical, proximal before distal, medial before lateral, gross before refined, extensor antigravity before flexor antigravity. Dynamic Systems Theory: development emerges from interaction of neuromuscular, musculoskeletal, cognitive, perception, and environment/opportunity.

Primitive Reflexes, Reactions & Developmental Milestones
Primitive reflexes are reflex actions originating in the CNS exhibited by normal infants but not neurologically intact adults. Majority are seen in the first 6 months and integrated by 6 months. Pathological reflexes = reappearance of primitive reflexes following brain injury. Central pattern generator theory helps explain why these reflexes exist.

Pediatric Examination — Unique Considerations
Key questions for peds: Any challenges through pregnancy? How many weeks? Weight? Feeding (breast or bottle, trouble latching)? For older kids: social, vision, hearing, discomfort with movement, school performance. FLACC pain scale for non-verbal children. Wong-Baker Faces for verbal children. MMT not valid until 5 years old.

Key Examination Considerations

Prone exam: Physiologic flexion, suck-swallow reflex, calming, lack of irritation (GERD typically when supine), quality of head/neck movement, UE position, WB on upper trunk/face.
Pull-to-sit: Check head lag quality (requires co-contraction of trunk flexors/extensors).
Prone suspension: Head and hip position to horizontal ("superman"). Insight on extension-based postural muscles.
Functional strength tests: Touch back of head, touch low back, supination, opposition, supine-to-stance transition, sit-to-stand, step up 6 inches.
Assessment approach: Top-down = traditional but ignores child's needs.
Bottom-up = identifies needs in collaboration with family (person-driven, people-centered, encouraging self-management).

Standardized Tests in Peds

Norm-referenced: Compared to a group of children. Diagnostic (how child differs from group). Evaluates at one point in time.
Criterion-referenced: Tests mastery of skill in one child. Can be correlated to intervention.
Common tests: AIMS (0–18mo), INFANIB (1–18mo), Denver Screen (1wk–6yr), TIMP (34wk gestation–4mo post-term), WeeFIM, SFA (School Function Assessment), GAS (Goal Attainment Scaling).
Rotational profile: Foot progression angle, hip rotation (femoral torsion), thigh-foot angle (TFA — positive = out-toeing, negative = in-toeing). In-toeing is common during second year. Shoe modifications are INEFFECTIVE for in-toeing correction.

NICU — Levels, Complications & PT Role
NICU provides education on handling and positioning, promotes development and midline orientation. Level I = standard nursery. Level II = intermediate (some PT). Level III = highly specialized (teaching hospitals). Level IV = ECMO-capable. Premature infants lack physiologic flexion, have decreased muscle fibers, low tone, increased soft tissue laxity, and are sensitive to sounds and light.

Congenital Cardiac Anomalies

Acyanotic (pink, oxygenated blood mixes): ASD (most common CHD — hole between atria), VSD (hole between ventricles → pulmonary HTN), Coarctation of aorta.
Cyanotic (blue, blood skips lungs): Tetralogy of Fallot (most common cyanotic — VSD + pulmonary stenosis + overriding aorta + RV hypertrophy), Transposition of Great Arteries (fatal without surgery in first days). PDA = communication between aorta and pulmonary artery doesn't close (RUBELLA).
If infant has feeding problems → think heart issue.

PT in the NICU

Goals: Maintain life. Promote maturation/development (hold infant, skin-to-skin). Promote healing environment (calming, reduce noxious stimuli, protect sleep). Streamline interventions for sufficient rest.
Assessment: APGAR (0–10, predictor of support/mortality), observation of posturing/position, behavioral states (deep sleep → light sleep → drowsy → quiet alert → active alert → crying), sensory systems (tactile, vestibular, gustation, hearing, vision).
Signs of stress: Physiologic (HR, RR, O₂ sat changes, apnea) and behavioral (trunk/leg extension, finger splaying, gaze aversion).
Tone tests: Heel-to-ear (closer = lower tone = more premature), popliteal angle (>90° = low tone), scarf sign (past shoulder/midline = more hypotonic).

Cerebral Palsy — Classification, GMFCS & Management
CP is the most common movement disorder in children. Medical diagnosis with significant history: prematurity, genetic predisposition (prepartum), HIE (peripartum), hypoxia (neonatal), IVH, PVL. It is a non-progressive brain injury that results in progressive musculoskeletal impairment. Muscle weakness in CP results from loss of muscle mass, reduced contractile material, actin/myosin dysfunction, and overstretched sarcomeres that cannot produce force.

Comorbidities & Movement Analysis

Comorbidities: Sensation/sensory processing, visual-perception, communication/oral motor, behavior/emotions, intellectual impairment, hip subluxation/dislocation risk.
Movement analysis questions: What moves first? Is it typical/expected? Is it controlled? Is there rapid movement? What is the prime mover? What stabilizes proximally? What joint(s) moving and how? What is impairing the movement? Is there laxity? Patella alta? Spasticity management: Orthotics, electrical stim, stretching/strengthening agonist + antagonist, biofeedback, prolonged stretch, tendon pressure.
Medical: Botox (inhibits ACh release), Baclofen (oral or intrathecal pump), nerve blocks, selective dorsal rhizotomy, diazepam.

Spina Bifida — Types, Levels & Functional Outcomes
Second most common birth defect. Caused by lack of maternal folic acid leading to failure of neural tube to close (normally closes at 26 weeks gestation). 85% survive to adulthood. Greater than 90% survival with early treatment (back closure within 72 hours, shunt for hydrocephalus). If ambulation not present by 9 years, it is unlikely to occur.

Types, Hydrocephalus & Arnold-Chiari Malformation

▼
Myelomeningocele (Aperta)

Most severe. Dorsal opening with meninges AND spinal cord involved. Severe neurologic deficits correlating with level. Cervical = death before birth. Thoracic = poorest neurologic presentation. Associated with hydrocephalus, scoliosis, osteoporosis, sensory deficits, cognitive/language dysfunction, latex allergy. High maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein → diagnosis in utero.

Spina Bifida Occulta

Hidden lesion. Incomplete fusion of posterior vertebral arch. Usually no neurologic deficits. Often discovered incidentally on imaging. Much milder than myelomeningocele.

Hydrocephalus & Arnold-Chiari Malformation

90% of spina bifida patients also have hydrocephalus. Arnold-Chiari: prolapse of medulla and cerebellum through foramen magnum → obstructed 4th ventricle.
VP (ventriculoperitoneal) shunt is preferred treatment. VA (ventriculoatrial) shunt = CSF to right atrium.
Shunt malfunction signs: Irritability, projectile vomiting, blurred/double vision.
Tethered cord: Rapidly progressive scoliosis, hypertonus in LEs, gait pattern changes, urologic function changes, discomfort in back. Requires surgery.
2/3 of shunted children have normal intelligence; 1/3 have mild cognitive impairment.

Functional Outcomes & Orthotics by Level

▼
Orthotics by Level

Thoracic/High Lumbar: A-frame, swivel standers, HKAFOs, RGOs (lateral weight shift causes unweighted leg to swing forward), rolling walkers, forearm/axillary crutches (swing-through gait). Wheelchair sports toward adolescence.
Low Lumbar: AFOs, KAFOs, forearm crutches, walkers.
Sacral: AFOs may be sufficient. Most adults use wheelchair as primary mobility.
Key facts: Child's ability to walk outdoors and use wheelchair by 7 = good prognostic indicator. If ambulation NOT present by 9 years, unlikely to occur. All children show decreased ambulation around 12 years. Below L3 + IQ ≥80 + no LE contractures + normal quad strength = strongly associated with independence in self-care.

Young Adult Issues

Obesity, incontinence, recurrent UTIs (most common morbidity), chronic decubiti, joint pain, HTN, neurologic deterioration, depression. Social integration, vocational issues, sexual counseling. Transition planning must start early (~14yo).

Pediatric TBI, Childhood Cancer, Burns & Other Conditions
TBI is the leading cause of death and permanent disability in children. Higher in children <4 years and 15–19 years. Children's brains are more plastic → greater recovery potential. Childhood cancer survival rates now exceed 80%.

Pediatric TBI — Causes, GCS & Rancho

▼
Causes by Age

Infant: Abuse (Non-Accidental Trauma/NAT), falls.
Preschool: Falls, MVAs.
School-age: MVAs, sports/recreation.
Adolescent: Sports/recreation, GSW, MVA.
Consequences: <5yo = more language issues. 5–21yo = more emotional issues.
Most recovery in first 2 months, can continue for first 2 years. ICP: in infancy → bulging fontanels. >5yo → contents forced through foramen magnum.
Heterotopic ossification: Mature lamellar bone in soft tissue. Risk: age >11, longer coma. At elbow, shoulder, hip, knee. Signs: decreased ROM, pain, swelling, erythema, warmth.

Coma Stimulation

Structure is important when children emerge from coma. Agitation common → confusion follows. Sensory input through vestibular, visual, tactile, auditory, olfactory. Use items of importance to child. Schedule: 15 minutes at a time, 8–10 times per day.

Early Intervention & School-Based Practice
IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act): Part C = infants/toddlers (0–3 years, IFSP). Part B = school-age (3–21 years, IEP). Section 504 = accommodations without special education. Family-centered care empowers the family to participate fully. Culture must be respected. "Longer treatment is not better — BETTER treatment is better."

IDEA Part C (Early Intervention) & IFSP

▼
IDEA Part C — Early Intervention

Federal grant program for infants/toddlers 0–35 months.
Eligibility: Diagnosis of significant developmental delay, established condition (e.g., Down syndrome), or significant risk of delay if services not provided.
Goals: Enhance development, maximize independence potential, decrease likelihood of delay, decrease future educational costs, help families acquire tools.
Service environment: Primarily the HOME. Also typical community settings (daycare, playgroups, parks).
Activity-based instruction: Observe child → identify current interests → work on functional skills within natural routines.

IFSP (Individualized Family Service Plan)

Written in family-friendly language. Addresses family's concerns, priorities, and resources. Multidisciplinary. Outcomes address child's development AND family's ability to promote development — must be meaningful to the family AND measurable. Must include transition plan (from EI to daycare, preschool, Part B, or community). Transition should begin before preschool. EI can be extended if allowed by state. Evaluated at least annually.

School-Based Practice — IEP, 504 & Service Delivery

▼
IEP (Individualized Education Program)

Under IDEA Part B. For students eligible for special education. PT evaluates to determine eligibility or gathers info on already-eligible student. Should NOT establish discipline-specific goals — should describe student's ability to function in school environment and participate in educational program. Requires formalized testing.

Section 504 Plan

Under Rehabilitation Act. Disability = mental or physical impairment limiting a major life activity (walking, self-care, working, etc.). No formalized testing mandated. Does NOT reduce academic expectations — provides necessary modifications for equal access. No federal mandate on specific content. 504 Coordinator assists school compliance.

Service Delivery Models

3 I's: Impairment, Intervention, Individual (participant + supporters).
Direct service: Skilled PT provides treatment.
Indirect service: PT checks in periodically, others deliver service.
Integrated approach: Teachers, support staff, home programs carry out strategies.
Transition planning: At 14: start discussing post-graduation plan. At 16: plan completed through age 21. Options: postsecondary education, vocational training, employment/assisted employment. PT involved in pre-job training, workplace accessibility, community mobility, energy conservation for ADLs.

Assistive Technology, Orthotics & Transition to Adulthood
Mobility impairment is the #1 reason for AT use. Equipment should not substitute for treatment but should facilitate function. Common factor with children = growth — equipment must be reassessed frequently. The goal of AT is to promote development, independence, social competence, and safety.

Precautions with AT

Static positioning restricts learning of active postural transitions. Poor planning → equipment that fails to meet needs. Caregivers must be taught correct donning, doffing, and use (color coding, numbering straps, teach multiple caregivers). Balance between support and function. Inspect equipment frequently. Equipment should facilitate function, not substitute for treatment. Establish realistic goals including capability, cost, accessibility, and transportation.

Transition to Adulthood & Federal Laws

▼
ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act 1990)

Title I: Equal opportunity employment (employers >15 employees, reasonable accommodations).
Title II: State/local government accessibility (architectural standards, paratransit).
Title III: Public accommodations (restaurants, stores, theaters, doctor's offices — nondiscrimination, architectural standards, reasonable modifications).
Title IV: Communications (TRS 24/7, closed captions).

Living & Work Options

Living: Home with parents (usually first choice), independent living (philosophy of self-determination, may include in-home assistance), assisted living (services but not SNF level, maximizes choice/dignity/autonomy), residential/ICF (group home — 24hr supervision, health-related care).
Work: Competitive (full/part-time, competitive wages, only needs help finding job), supportive (Rehab Act 1986 — competitive work with ongoing support), sheltered (self-contained site, subcontracted tasks).
Vocational Rehabilitation: Services for people with disabilities to acquire/maintain gainful employment.

Infant born at 28 weeks, now 4 months old. In this case, What is the corrected age, and what complications should you screen for?
tap
Corrected age = 4 months minus 3 months premature = approximately 1 month corrected. Born at 28 weeks (12 weeks early = 3 months). Use corrected age for developmental expectations until age 2. Screen for: PVL (periventricular leukomalacia — associated with CP, spastic diplegia, common <32wk), IVH (most common preemie lesion, graded I-IV), RDS (lack surfactant), BPD (from prolonged O₂/ventilation), NEC (bowel necrosis from enteral feeds), ROP (retinal ischemia). Assessment: Heel-to-ear (closer = lower tone), popliteal angle (>90° = low tone), scarf sign. Less physiologic flexion expected. Observe behavioral states and signs of stress. Use AIMS or TIMP for standardized testing.
Child with CP, GMFCS Level III. In this case, What does this mean functionally, and what orthotics/equipment are appropriate?
tap
GMFCS III = walks with assistive device. Walker inside, wheelchair for outside/community. Equipment: Walker (posterior promotes more erect posture), AFOs (solid or articulating depending on spasticity and need for DF), wheelchair for community distances. Stander prescription: 1-2x/day, 30-60min, 5 days/week. Spasticity management: Botox (inhibits ACh), oral or intrathecal baclofen, orthotics, stretching agonist + antagonist, prolonged stretch. Comorbidities to screen: Vision/perception, communication/oral motor, behavior/emotions, intellectual impairment, hip surveillance (subluxation/dislocation risk). Movement analysis: What moves first, is it typical, what stabilizes proximally, what is impairing the movement. Muscle weakness mechanism: Overstretched sarcomeres can't produce force — not just weakness but structural contractile dysfunction.
Child with spina bifida at L3 level. In this case, What is the functional prognosis, and what are the key associated conditions?
tap
L3 level: Quad function present (key for ambulation). Below L3 + IQ ≥80 + no LE contractures + normal quad strength = strongly associated with independent self-care. Ambulation: AFOs with forearm crutches likely. Walking outdoors by 7 = good prognostic indicator. If not ambulating by 9, unlikely to occur. All children show decreased ambulation around 12. Most adults use wheelchair primarily. Associated conditions: Hydrocephalus (90% — VP shunt preferred, watch for malfunction: irritability, projectile vomiting, blurred vision), Arnold-Chiari malformation, tethered cord (progressive scoliosis, hypertonus, gait changes, urologic changes → requires surgery), latex allergy, bowel/bladder dysfunction (catheterization independence expected by 5yo). Young adult issues: Obesity, recurrent UTIs (most common morbidity), chronic decubiti, depression. Transition planning starts at 14.
A child can roll segmentally, sit independently in ring sitting, and has anterior protective reactions but no lateral protective reactions. In this case, Approximately how old?
tap
Approximately 6–7 months. Segmental rolling begins at 5–6 months (body righting reaction, not neck righting). Independent ring sitting = 5–9 months (ring provides larger BOS). Anterior protective reactions develop at 6–7 months. Lateral protective reactions develop at 7–9 months (this child doesn't have them yet). What's the difference between an IFSP and an IEP, and when does each apply in this scenario?
tap
IFSP (Individualized Family Service Plan): IDEA Part C. Ages 0–3 years (can extend in some states). Written in family-friendly language. Addresses family's concerns, priorities, and resources. Service primarily in the HOME and natural environments. Must include transition plan. IEP (Individualized Education Program): IDEA Part B. Ages 3–21 years. For students eligible for special education. PT should NOT establish discipline-specific goals — should describe student's ability to function in school and participate in educational program. Requires formalized testing. Section 504 Plan: NOT special education — provides accommodations. No formalized testing mandated. Does NOT reduce academic expectations. No federal mandate on specific content. Service delivery (3 I's): Direct (PT provides skilled service), indirect (PT checks in, others deliver), integrated (teachers/staff carry out strategies). Child with leukemia — labs show Hct 27%, Hgb 9, platelets 45,000. In this case, Can you exercise them?
tap
LIGHT EXERCISE ONLY. Guidelines: No exercise: Hct <25%, Hgb <8, platelets <20,000. Light exercise: Hct 25–30%, Hgb 8–10, platelets 20,000–60,000 (THIS PATIENT). Can use resistance: Hct >30%, Hgb >10, platelets >60,000. Additional rules: No exercise within 2 hours of treatment. Check blood counts BEFORE PT. Vincristine side effects include foot-drop and weakness (from peripheral neuropathy). Depression is common. Interventions: therapeutic exercise to maintain strength, ROM, pain management, endurance. Leukemia types: ALL = proliferates quickly, 90% survival. AML = slower, 70% survival, needs bone marrow transplant. Most prevalent cancer in children. Survival rates now exceed 80% overall for childhood cancers.
