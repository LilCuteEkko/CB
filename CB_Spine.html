Red Flags & Spine Screening
The spine is the most common site for serious pathology masquerading as MSK pain. Red flag screening must be completed before any classification or treatment approach. Every PT must know when to refer out immediately versus when to treat.

CPR for Compression Fracture

Female + Age >70 + Trauma (fall/MVA) + Corticosteroid use. ¾ criteria = 100% specificity (+LR 218). If these fit, refer for imaging. Key populations: adolescents (backpack) and elderly females (osteoporosis). Fractures due to osteoporosis most common at T9–T11.

Inflammatory Back Pain Screening (Under 45 + Sx >3 months)

All patients under 45 with symptoms >3 months must be asked: (1) Back stiffness >30 min? (2) Night pain awakening? (3) Alternating buttock pain? (4) Pain relieved by rest? 2/4 = 70% SN, 81% SP. 3/4 = 33% SN, 100% SP for inflammatory back pain (ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic SpA, reactive SpA).

Key Rule

If patient can't point to pain — think visceral. Visceral and vascular pain is poorly localized and achy. If pain is unaffected by position changes and doesn't respond to movement — it's likely not MSK.

SINSS Framework & Classification Approach

SINSS + LOCIDAATBC 4 categories▼
SINSS

Severity (0–10 VAS, rate min/mod/severe). Irritability (how long to provoke? how long to relieve? reflects tissue stress tolerance). Nature (bone, muscle, ligament, joint, nerve). Stage (acute/subacute/chronic). Stability (improving, stable, worsening?).

LOCIDAA

Location. Onset. Characteristics. Intensity. Duration. Aggravating factors. Alleviating factors. Plus: 24-hour behavior.

Treatment-Based Classification (TBC) — 4 Categories

De-emphasizes identifying specific anatomical lesions after red flag screening. 1. Mobilization/Manipulation. 2. Specific Exercise. 3. Traction. 4. Stabilization. Match intervention to classification, not to pathoanatomy.

Symptom Modulation Staging

High SINSS / High pain: Manual therapy (careful), directional preference exercise, modalities (heat), Grade 1–2 mobs, manipulations if acute (<30 days, no symptoms past knee). Mod SINSS: Sensorimotor exercises, stabilization, flexion exercises, mobility. Low SINSS: Strength/conditioning, sport/work-specific tasks, aerobic exercise, general fitness. Injury prevention phase.

Lumbar Spine — Examination & Patterns
LBP is the leading cause of activity limitation and work absence. 60–70% lifetime prevalence. Peaks at 5th–6th decade. Chronic LBP (>3 months): if >6 months, less than half return to work. 25–33% will still have LBP after 1 year. $100 million work days lost per year.

Lumbar History — Pain Patterns & Clinical Clues

Leg > back pain = nerve rootDejerine's triad = disc▼
Pain Location Clues

Central back pain: Usually not unilateral (not SIJ or zyga joint). Bilateral pain: Rare to have central origin — exception is central IVD protrusion. Buttock pain as referral: Can be zyga joint inflammation. Groin pain: Common with high lumbar IVD herniation — dull ache, deep, hard to localize, some NBT. Leg pain: May indicate radiculopathy or pseudo-radiculopathy.

Dejerine's Triad

Abdominal activation, coughing, and sneezing (Valsalva maneuver) make symptoms worse. Three-part test for nerve compression. Increases CSF pressure. Seen in IVD pathology and sacroiliitis. If (+) → suspect disc involvement.

Palliative & Provocative Factors

Supine with knees flexed / side lying: ↓ spinal column pressure, ↓ nerve tension. Pts with neurocompressive disc herniation will NOT like this. Standing/extension: Less provoking for disc pathologies. Sitting/flexion: Less provoking for spinal stenosis — opens vertebrae. "Shopping cart sign" = leans forward to open canal.

Key Aggravation Patterns

Flexion relief + walking aggravation: Zyga joint, spondylolisthesis, spinal stenosis. Sitting/stooping/lifting aggravation + recumbency relief + standing/walking OK: IVD lesion (protrusion or annular tear). Constant pain: Inflammatory process. Pain with movement: Mechanical cause. Morning stiffness: Inflammatory. Worse during evening: Mechanical symptoms.

Centralization & Peripheralization

Centralization: Progressive retreat of most distal symptoms back toward LB spine — GOOD sign, indicates reducible lesion. Peripheralization: Opposite — symptoms moving distally — BAD sign, lesion is increasing in size. Once a motion/position that reduces symptoms is identified, focus should be to encourage this motion or posture.

Age-Related DDx

10–20 yo: Spondylolisthesis. 15–40 yo: IVD and inflammatory conditions. 45+: OA, spondylosis. 65+: Cancer, stenosis, AAA, compression fracture. Back pain in a young child is NOT normal — think spondylolysis.

Observation & Postural Assessment

Lateral pelvic shift = disc + sciaticaExcessive lordosis = pelvic crossed▼
Lateral Pelvic Shift

Common in pts with disc-related LBP and sciatica. Herniation LATERAL to nerve root: Deviation AWAY from side of irritated nerve (draws nerve root away from disc fragment). Herniation MEDIAL to nerve root: Deviation TOWARDS side of lesion (decompresses nerve root). Listing is protective — can also be from zyga joint irritation, spinal nerve irritation, spasm of QL or iliacus.

Excessive Lordosis Indicates

Pelvic crossed syndrome (erector spinae + iliopsoas shortened; abs + glute max weak). Shortened PLL, lengthened ALL. Spondylolisthesis. Anterior pelvic tilt (weak abs, shortened TFL/iliopsoas, lengthened hamstrings/glutes).

Flat Back Indicates

Spinal stenosis or lateral recess stenosis. Lateral shifting. Posterior pelvic tilt. Short hamstrings. Disc protrusion. Weak hip flexors. Reversed lordosis (sway back) = thoracic kyphosis + posterior pelvic tilt.

Lumbar Pathologies
Lumbar pathologies range from soft tissue dysfunction to spondyloarthropathies. Understanding the spectrum from ligament sprains to spondylolisthesis to stenosis is critical for accurate classification and treatment.

Ankylosing Spondylitis & Spondyloarthropathies

"Bamboo Spine"90% HLA-B27 positiveSacroiliitis first▼
Ankylosing Spondylitis

Inflammatory arthritis of spine — "Bamboo Spine." Affects facets, tendons, ligaments. Enthesitis (inflammation at disc-vertebrae attachment) → bone erosion → new bone (calcification) at facets, costotransverse, costovertebral joints. AF can calcify, fusing segments. Sacroiliitis usually first to appear → SIJ fuses → progressive immobility → stress fractures → excessive kyphosis → may crowd internal organs. 90% are HLA-B27 positive. Men 3:1. Age of onset 15–40. Can affect eyes, knees, hips, ankles, shoulders. Chest expansion <2.5 cm is pathologic (normal = 5 cm at nipple line).

Other Spondyloarthropathies

Psoriatic Arthritis: Small joints (hands/feet), "sausage digit," psoriasis skin rash. Reactive Arthritis: Joint inflammation from infection (intestinal, UTI). IBD-associated: Crohn's, UC — ask about B&B, stress-related. Juvenile SpA: Childhood onset, enthesitis dominant.

Degenerative Spinal Stenosis — Central vs Lateral

65% = neurogenic claudicationFlexion opens canal▼
Central Stenosis

Narrowing of spinal canal around thecal sac (contains cauda equina). Can present symptoms similar to cauda equina. Causes: facet arthrosis, ligamentum flavum thickening/bulging, spondylolisthesis.

Lateral Stenosis

Encroachment of spinal nerve in lateral recess. Causes: facet joint hypertrophy, loss of IVD height, IVD bulging, spondylolisthesis.

Presentation

Long history of LBP. Unilateral or bilateral leg pain. 65% present with neurogenic claudication (compression affecting LE innervation, affects gait). ↓ LB lordosis. Aggravation with extension and side bend. Extension closes canal, flexion opens canal. Shortened hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris), lengthened hip extensors (glute max, hamstrings) → early recruitment of LB extensors → excessive LB extension → more stenotic compression.

Intervention

William flexion exercises. Manual therapy (thrust and non-thrust LB mobs/manips). Hip flexor, paraspinal, rectus femoris stretching. Glute and core strengthening/stabilization. Posterior pelvic tilt positioning.

Zyga Joint Dysfunction, Instability & Soft Tissue

Zyga = unilateral LBPQL dysfunction = broad stiffnessPsoas = big LBP contributor▼
Zygapophyseal Joint Dysfunction

Result of lesion to joint and pain-sensitive structures. Can result from hypomobility OR hypermobility/instability. Presentation: Unilateral LBP, aggravated with certain movements (flexion OR extension, especially end range). Extra-articular: ↓ PPIVM, normal PPAIVM. Periarticular: Restriction in both, hard capsular end-feel. Pathomechanical: Restriction in both, abrupt slightly springy end-feel. Treatment: specific joint mobs, postural education, correct muscle imbalances, core stabilization.

Clinical Instability

Recurrent, constant, catching, or locking pain. Feeling of giving way. Aggravated by sustained sitting, prolonged standing, semi-flexed postures, forward bending, sudden movements, lifting, sneezing. Returning to upright from forward bend is key complaint. Posterior creases, fulcrum point, excessive AROM. Treatment: remove abnormal joint stress, ↑ global musculature strength (core and trunk).

QL Dysfunction

Broad stiffness, very tight. ↓ lateral side bend away from dysfunction. Referred pain to glutes. Treatment: put QL on stretch (sidelying, affected side up, glutes on stretch), dry needling, trigger point release.

Psoas Syndrome

Big contributor to LBP that lasts. Anterior pelvic tilt, ↑ lordosis, muscle spasms, forward flexed posture. Treatment: stretch (thunderdome, mod Thomas — not 2–3×/day, too much stretch is bad), STM (likes firm pressure), MFR, KT tape.

Thoracolumbar Fascia & LBP

↑ thickness of TLF with LBP >1 year. ↓ shear plane motion by 20–50%. Fibrosis (fibroblasts don't turn off, continue laying down fibrin). Adhesion of LB muscles → poor movement patterns. Treatment: MFR on stretch, dynamic fascial stretching (TLF runs obliquely — L lats to R glute to R hammy and vice versa), yoga, postural/ergonomic education.

Lower Crossed Syndrome

Erector spinae and iliopsoas are tight. Abs and glutes are weak. Results in anterior pelvic tilt + ↑ LB lordosis. Mirror image of upper crossed syndrome.

Disc Pathology & Radiculopathy
Disc degeneration is a normal aging process beginning around the 3rd decade. The nucleus pulposus starts to be replaced by fibrocartilage and dehydrates. Understanding the 3-stage degeneration cascade and compression by root level is essential for every PT.

Disc Degeneration Cascade & Herniation Types

Stage 1 = annular tearsStage 2 = instabilityStage 3 = osteophytes▼
3 Stages of Disc Degeneration

Stage 1 — Early Dysfunction: Outer annular tears, endplate separation, some cartilage destruction, facet inflammation. Stage 2 — Intermediate Instability: Loss of disc space and height, laxity of posterior joint capsule and AF. "Giving way and catching" through AROM. Abnormal movement patterns. Stage 3 — Final Stabilization: Osteophyte formation, stenosis, laying down extra tissue for stabilization, loss of disc material, radial tears of AF, fibrosis of posterior joint capsules.

DegenerationSlight herniation of NP into AF. NP becoming fibrocartilaginous. Poor shock absorptionGradual onset. Stiffness

Cord Compression Concerns

Extrusion and sequestration can put pressure on spinal cord → myelopathy (RED FLAG) with UMN symptoms and hyperreflexia (3–4+). Pressure on nerve roots distal to conus medullaris → cauda equina syndrome. Conus medullaris = tapered lower end of spinal cord at L1–L2.

DDx for LE Symptoms (Not Disc)

Hip or SIJ pathology. Claudication (iliac/iliofemoral arteries). Spondylolisthesis. Muscle strain, fracture, or CA. Peripheral nerve injury. Diabetic amyotrophy (lumbosacral neuropathy — asymmetric painful muscle wasting of quads/adductors/hip flexors, poor glycemic control). Meralgia paresthetica: Entrapment of lateral femoral cutaneous nerve under inguinal ligament — lateral/anterior paresthesias, sensation only (NO motor or reflex), ↑ symptoms with hip extension, relief with sitting.

Clinical Pearls

Pain worse in LE than LB = nerve root irritation. Pain worse in LB than LE = probably referred from spinal structure. 20–30% of asymptomatic people have abnormal imaging findings. Do not perform full spinal motions shortly after rising from bed (disc is most hydrated). Repetitive contact of spinous processes during max extension → periostitis ("kissing spine" / Baastrup's disease).

Lumbar Post-Op — Fusion Approaches

ALIF = anterior approachPLIF = posteriorTLIF = unilateral▼
Lumbar Fusion — Goal: Restore Anatomical Alignment

ALIF (Anterior Lumbar Interbody Fusion): Through abdomen to remove disc and insert hardware. Avoids thoracolumbar musculature. L4–L5 is hardest (aortic/vena cava bifurcation). Faster approach, avoids thecal sac. Wire cage or bone graft placed. PLIF (Posterior): Traditional posterior approach through paraspinals. TLIF (Transforaminal): Unilateral approach — less tissue disruption than PLIF.

Cervical Spine
The cervical spine is inherently fragile and patients remember their neck injuries. 4th leading cause of days lost of life years. 30% US prevalence. Women more affected. People with both LBP and C-spine pain have worse prognosis. High roles conflict (balancing work/life), depressed mood, and perceived muscle tension are risk factors.

WAD, Cervicogenic Headache & Radiculopathy

WAD: 55% have Sx after 10+ yearsCGH: upper cervical dysfunctionC7 = most involved radiculopathy▼
Whiplash Associated Disorder (WAD)

Acceleration/deceleration injury. Soft tissue, ligamentous, bony, neuro injury. 3 recovery pathways: (1) Quick recovery 2–3 months. (2) Moderate with lingering symptoms for years. (3) Poor recovery with significant disability. Things hindering recovery: pain catastrophizing, PTSD, high disability, cold hyperalgesia. 55% still have symptoms after 10+ years. Treatment: education (remain active), mobility exercises, reduce cervical collar, return to activity, tolerable ROM, manual therapy — don't do too much as it may lead to chronic symptoms.

Cervicogenic Headache

Stem from upper cervical dysfunction or suboccipital muscular dysfunction. Trigeminal nerve plays a role. Known trigger mechanism (posture, neck pain). Females 3rd decade+. Unilateral > bilateral. ↓ cervical ROM. Painful upper cervical manual palpation. ↓ deep neck flexor strength. Referral patterns: C0–1 = occiput. C1–2 = "ram's horn." C2–3 = "halo." C3–4 = "rock behind eye." Treatment: C/T manips, soft tissue, deep neck flexor endurance, postural training.

Cervical Radiculopathy by Root Level

C5: "Epaulet" distribution — superior shoulder to lateral mid-arm. Absence of painful shoulder ROM and impingement signs. C6: Neck → lateral biceps → lateral forearm → thumb/index finger (median). C7 (most involved): Posterior shoulder → scapula → triceps → posterior forearm → posterior fingers (radial). C8: Medial arm/forearm → medial hand → 4th–5th digit (ulnar). Difficulty with ADLs using hands.

CPR for Cervical Radiculopathy

(+) Spurling's + (+) Distraction + (+) ULTT median nerve + C/S rotation <60°. 3 positive tests: +LR 6.1. 4 positive tests: +LR 30.3.

Brachial Plexus Injuries

DDx vs. radiculopathy: is it from BP or nerve roots? MOI: traction (yanking on shoulder). Erb's (upper BP, C5–C6): ↑ angle between neck and shoulder → "waiter's tip" posture. Klumpke's (lower BP, C8–T1): Upward pull of limb → "claw hand." Stingers/Burners: Common in football (poor tackle), head sidebends away from injured BP, UE radiating symptoms and NBT.

TOS (Thoracic Outlet Syndrome)

Vascular vs. neurogenic vs. both. Compression at: (1) Scalenes (interscalene triangle — 0.2% have cervical rib at C7). (2) First rib (costoclavicular interval — clavicle depression or first rib elevation from scalene hypertonicity). (3) Pec minor (hyperabduction, hypertonicity, shoulder ABD+ER at 90°). Vascular: changes in hand temp/color, non-dermatomal patterns, arm heaviness.

O-A Joint: Facilitates flexion–extension (combined 15–20°) but impedes other movements. Axial rotation forced = 8°. Hypermobility if rotation >8°. A-A Joint: Large arch of rotation. Only AA and TL junction provide true spinal rotation. Upper Crossed Syndrome: Tight levator scap, upper traps, SCM, pecs. Weak deep neck flexors, lower scap stabilizers. Forward head, elevated/protracted shoulders, scap winging.

Cervical Fractures

Odontoid = 60% of C2 FxJefferson = axial loading C1▼
Odontoid Fractures

60% of all axis fractures and 10–18% of all C-spine fractures. Most common C-spine fracture in people >70. MOI: high-energy trauma (MVA), or falls/hyperextension in elderly. Type 1: Oblique avulsion from dens tip. Type 2: Through neck of odontoid. Type 3: Extends into body of C2 (15–40% of odontoid fractures).

Jefferson Fracture

Lateral mass fracture of C1 and disruption of C1 ring. Results from axial loading on atlas. Generally minimal neurologic deficit and good prognosis. Type I: bilateral single-arch. Type II: concurrent anterior and posterior arch (classic 4-point break). Type III: lateral mass fracture extending into arch.

Transverse Ligament Injury

Type 1: Disruption of ligament substance without osseous component — needs surgery. Type 2: Fracture/avulsion of tubercle on C1 lateral mass without ligament disruption — heals with orthosis.

Thoracic Spine & Rib Dysfunction
Most rigid region of spine. Important for load transfer between upper and lower body. Little research compared to C/S and L/S (15% of spine pain). Upper T-spine treated similar to cervical, lower T-spine similar to lumbar. High evidence for joint mobs and manips for mid-thoracic pain.

Thoracic Postural Dysfunction & Kyphotic Deformities

Postural Dysfunction

Very common. Hard to reproduce on exam — exacerbated by prolonged static postures. Worse with stress/fatigue. Middle-aged, F > M, office workers, anyone who sits a lot. No true MOI — repetitive stress disorder. Complaints: interscapular, periscapular, upper traps, lev scap, midline T-spine, TL and CT junction. Stiffness. Worse with sustained positions and carrying heavy weights. ↓ with rest. Objective: weak scapular muscles, poor scapulohumeral rhythm, poor T-spine mobility (extension and rotation).

Thoracic Disc, Zyga Joint & Rib Dysfunction

T-disc = 0.25-0.75% of all herniationsRib 1 = most concerning▼
Thoracic Disc Herniation

Constitutes only 0.25–0.75% of all disc herniations. Much more common in lower T-spine. Major cause = degenerative change. Rarely requires surgery. Disc height:body ratio = 1:5 (vs. cervical 2:5, lumbar 1:3). Herniations by level: T1–T2 = rare (rib protection), numbness/weakness hand, pain arm/medial forearm. T1 associated with Horner's disease. T2–T3 = rarest, refers to clavicle/spine of scapula. T3–T8 = symptoms at side/front of trunk. T6 compression can cause pain from base of neck to waist. T9–T11 = pain radiating to buttock (rule out lumbopelvic origin).

Rib Dysfunction

Costovertebral joint may be involved in inflammatory or degenerative joint disease (ankylosing spondylitis). Inflammation causes localized pain 3–4 cm from midline. Pump handle: Ribs 2–7, sagittal plane motion. Bucket handle: Lower ribs 8–10, frontal plane motion. Floating ribs: 11–12. Rib dysfunctions: structural (true subluxation from trauma), torsional (twisting → held in IR/ER), respiratory (poor posture → restricted inspiration/expiration).

Rib Fracture Considerations

10% of patients after blunt chest trauma have ≥1 rib fracture. Fractured ribs can lacerate pleura, lung, or abdominal organs → hemothorax, pneumothorax. Upper rib/clavicle fractures (3–15%) can cause brachial plexus or vascular injury. 1st rib fracture: injury to brachial plexus and subclavian A/V. Flail chest: 3+ rib segments fractured → paradoxical breathing (inspiration: ribs draw inward; expiration: ribs push outward).

Scheuermann's Disease, T4 Syndrome & Scoliosis

Scheuermann's = self-limiting at pubertyT4 syndrome = stocking-glove paresthesia▼
Scheuermann's Disease

Defect in ring apophysis → anterior wedging from flexion overload. End plate can crack → disc material may bulge into vertebral body (Schmorl's node). Self-limiting past puberty. "Thoracic Kyphosis." Affects 10% of all people, M = F equally. Pubescent athletes. 20–30% also have scoliosis. Pain with T-spine extension and rotation. Treatment: stretching pecs, strengthening T-spine extensors and scapular adductors.

T4 Syndrome (Can Be T2–T7)

No known cause — thought to be sympathetic reaction to hypomobility (resolves with manual therapy). Sympathetic trunks lie on or just lateral to costovertebral joint. Abnormal deformation with poor posture (forward head, rounded shoulders). Women > men (3:1). Night symptoms common. Bilateral stocking-glove paresthesia — confused with carpal tunnel and cervical radiculopathy. Headaches, neck pain, UE pain. (+) Slump, (+) ULTT, hypomobile segments.

Scoliosis

Progressive 3D deformity of spinal segments. Dextroscoliosis: Right-sided curve. Levoscoliosis: Left-sided curve — more severe, may damage internal organs and pulmonary function. May indicate neurological condition. Tietze Syndrome: inflammation of costosternal cartilage (2nd–3rd junction), gradual onset, worse with deep inspiration/coughing/sneezing, localized swelling.

Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE) Key Takeaways

There is NOT always a direct link between tissue damage and pain level. Pain is produced by the brain after concluding body is in danger. Nociceptive fibers are danger signal fibers, NOT "pain fibers." CNS interprets signal magnitude. Changes can occur along the entire nervous system (ion channel upregulation, immune response, demyelination, dorsal horn interneuron death). Pain is a matrix — no single brain center. Repeated signaling creates "pain memory." Types: Nociceptive (proportionate, clear aggravating/alleviating), Peripheral Neurogenic (dermatomal, (+) limb tension), Central Sensitization (disproportionate, diffuse, high SINSS, psychosocial components). Motion is lotion. 6 miles of running = endorphins equivalent to morphine. Exercise-induced analgesic effect starts at 10 min aerobic.

Sacroiliac Joint & Pelvic Girdle Pain
Pelvic girdle pain (PGP) arises from pregnancy, trauma, arthritis, or OA. Diagnosis of PGP can only be reached after exclusion of lumbar causes. 50% of pregnant females complain of PGP. Must have 3 positive pelvic girdle tests for diagnosis.

Base at top (anterior), apex at bottom (more posterior). Transmits forces between spine and pelvis. Concave sacral base on convex L5. Hard to isolate SIJ pathologies — isolation occurs after ruling out hip and lower lumbar. Intercornual ligament: Connects sacral cornu to coccyx — small ligament but big source of pain (coccydynia).

SIJ Assessment & Pelvic Girdle Conditions

Laslett: 2/4 stress tests = SIJClear lumbar & hip first▼
Pain Provocation Tests (Laslett's Cluster)

Good sensitivity if 2 positives: Distraction, Thigh thrust, Compression, Sacral thrust (spring). If 2 tests recreate pain, no further tests needed. Diagnosis of PGP requires: Posterior pelvic pain provocation test (P4), FABER, palpation of long dorsal ligament, Gaenslen's test. Palpation of pubic symphysis + modified Trendelenburg. ASLR = functional pelvic test. Must have 3 positive pelvic girdle tests.

Fryette's Laws of Spinal Motion

Law 1: In neutral position (facets not engaged), rotation is coupled to the OPPOSITE side of side-bending. Law 2: In extension or near full flexion (facets engaged), rotation and side-bending occur in the SAME direction. Law 3: If movement is introduced in any plane, ROM in the other two directions will be reduced.

Pelvic Girdle Conditions

Athletic Pubalgia (Sports Hernia): Pain along lower abdomen and proximal adductors. Shearing at pubic symphysis. Pain with resisted adduction, cough/sneeze. 3rd–4th decade, mostly men. 4 stages from unilateral to bilateral to ADL-limiting. Symphysis Pubis Dysfunction: Ligaments stretched (pregnancy hormones — progesterone, relaxin). Clicking, limited hip motion, antalgic gait. Coccydynia: Coccyx stuck in flexion. Trauma, pregnancy, obesity. Flexion >25° or extension >20° = instability.

Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ)
People use the TMJ ~600 times per day. TMD is a MSK condition resulting in craniofacial pain, activity limitation, and disability. Symptoms include decreased jaw mobility, pain, clicking, headaches, ear pain, neck pain, and tinnitus. Always screen TMJ in cervical evaluations.

TMJ Anatomy & Biomechanics

3rd class lever2 knuckles = functional, 3 = normal▼
Articular Disc

Biconcave, viscoelastic (collagen + GAGs + elastic). Three bands: anterior (pes meniscus), intermediate (pars gracilis — most WB, avascular, aneural), posterior (pars posterior). Functions: joint congruence, stability/mobility, ↓ friction, ↓ biomechanical stress, separates superior and inferior joints. Bilaminar retrodiscal pad: Superior lamina (elastic — allows anterior translation during depression, helps reposition disc), inferior lamina (inelastic — tethers disc).

TMJ Kinematics

Depression: 40–50mm. 2 knuckles = functional AROM, 3 knuckles = normal AROM. Two phases: rolling at inferior joint → then anterior glide (lateral pterygoid is the main muscle for pulling and eccentrics during closing). Protrusion/Retrusion: Upper TM joint only, no rotation. Lower teeth should surpass upper, 6–9mm. Lateral Excursion: 8–11mm. Ipsilateral condyle spins posteriorly. Contralateral condyle translates anterior + medial + slightly inferior.

Dysfunction Patterns

Deviation (S-curve): During depression, jaw moves away from midline then returns to midline by end of movement. Deflection (C-curve): Jaw moves away from midline and stays there. Causes: hypomobility, asymmetry. Ipsilateral pain = muscular. Contralateral pain = joint.

TMD — Disc Displacement & Internal Derangement

DDWR = clickDDWOR = blockOA/RA causes▼
TMD Causes & Symptoms

OA/RA (pain, stiffness, warmth, edema), trauma, loss of posterior teeth (OA), disc dislocation, involuntary guarding of muscles of mastication. Symptoms: ↓ jaw mobility, pain, joint clicking, headaches, ear pain, neck pain, tinnitus, pain of intraoral structures.

Disc Displacement WITH Reduction (DDWR)

Reciprocal click — disc is anterior, clicks back into place during opening. Posterior click when closing (disc displaces anterior again). Click = reduction.

Disc Displacement WITHOUT Reduction (DDWOR)

Disc stays anterior the entire time. More limited motion. Block = no reduction. More significant functional limitation.

Conservative Treatment

Tissue mobilization. Postural education. Therapeutic exercise. Joint manipulation (TMJ, cervical, thoracic). Intraoral application. Cortisone injection. Capsulitis → capsular fibrosis if untreated.

What is the difference between centralization and peripheralization, and why does it matter clinically?
tap
Centralization: Progressive retreat of the most distal symptoms back toward the LB spine — this is a GOOD prognostic sign and indicates a reducible lesion. Peripheralization: Symptoms moving distally — BAD sign, lesion is increasing in size. Once a motion/position that centralizes is identified, encourage that motion/posture. This is the foundation of the McKenzie approach: find the directional preference that centralizes symptoms and use it therapeutically. Derangement syndrome responds to this approach.
How do you differentiate a lateral pelvic shift from a disc herniation lateral vs. medial to the nerve root?
tap
Herniation LATERAL to nerve root: Patient deviates AWAY from the side of the irritated nerve root. This draws the nerve root away from the disc fragment. Herniation MEDIAL to nerve root: Patient deviates TOWARDS the side of the lesion. This decompresses the nerve root. The listing is a protective position. Can also be caused by zyga joint irritation, spinal nerve irritation, or QL/iliacus spasm. This is why observation of lateral shift direction combined with neural tension testing is critical for classification.
What distinguishes spondylosis, spondylolysis, and spondylolisthesis?
tap
Spondylosis (OA/DJD): Wear and tear of spine. ↓ disc hydration → bony contact → inflammation → osteophytes → ↓ foraminal space. "Shopping cart sign." Extension/rotation painful, flexion relieves. Spondylolysis (pars Fx): Defect/fracture at pars articularis ("Scotty dog"). 5–7 yo. Back pain in young child is NOT normal. Hyperextension + rotation + loading. Spondylolisthesis (slippage): Pars fracture + anterior slippage. Graded 1–5 (≥3 = surgery). Step-off deformity. 10–15 yo. ↑ lordosis, waddle gait, ask about B&B.
What are the 5 D's, 3 N's, and 1 A for VBI screening, and when must you test?
tap
5 D's: Dizzy, Diplopia (blurred vision), Drop attacks (loss of power/consciousness), Dysphagia (swallowing issues), Dysarthria (speech problems). 3 N's: Nystagmus, Nausea/vomiting, Neuro symptoms. 1 A: Ataxia (lack of muscle coordination). Plus severe headache. When to test: Before ANY cervical manipulation, when patient complains of any of these symptoms, and as part of normal cervical exam to assess irritability. Test seated first (10-sec holds in extension, rotation, combined) → progress to supine if negative. At-risk populations: RA, Down syndrome, Marfan syndrome.
What is the CPR for cervical radiculopathy and what are the likelihood ratios?
tap
4 tests: (1) Cervical rotation <60° to affected side. (2) (+) Spurling's (passive SB + axial compression reproduces arm symptoms). (3) (+) Distraction (relief with cervical distraction). (4) (+) ULTT A (median nerve tension). 3 positive = +LR 6.1. 4 positive = +LR 30.3 (extremely high probability of cervical radiculopathy). Most common roots: C7 (most involved) → C6 → C5. Disc herniations are rare <30 yo and uncommon in elderly (stenotic cause more likely).
What is the SLR test interpretation at different angles?
tap
Pain <70°: Neurological irritation or disc herniation (usually lateral disc protrusion). Pain >70°: L-spine or SI joint pain. Pain that does NOT increase with neck flexion: Hamstring tightness or SIJ. If >70°, repeat bilaterally: Pain <70° bilateral = SIJ. Pain >70° bilateral = lumbar. Sensitize by adding: ankle PIP (peroneal nerve), TED (tibial nerve), SID (sural nerve). Crossed SLR: Positive when raising the asymptomatic leg reproduces pain in the symptomatic leg — indicates disc herniation MEDIAL to the nerve root, often with extruded fragment.
What is the T4 syndrome and why is it confused with carpal tunnel?
tap
T4 syndrome (can be T2–T7) is thought to be a sympathetic reaction to a hypomobile thoracic segment — it resolves with manual therapy. Sympathetic trunks lie on/just lateral to costovertebral joints and can undergo abnormal deformation with poor posture (forward head, rounded shoulders). Women 3:1. Presents with: night symptoms, headaches, neck pain, UE pain, and crucially — bilateral stocking-glove paresthesia. This bilateral hand numbness is why it's confused with carpal tunnel or cervical radiculopathy. Key differentiators: (+) Slump, (+) ULTT, hypomobile T-spine segments, responds to thoracic mobilization.
What is ankylosing spondylitis, and how does it present in the thoracic spine?
tap
"Bamboo Spine." Inflammatory arthritis affecting facets, tendons, ligaments. Enthesitis (inflammation at disc-vertebrae attachment) → bone erosion → calcification at facets, costotransverse, costovertebral joints → AF can calcify → segments fuse. Sacroiliitis usually first. 90% HLA-B27+. Men 3:1. Age 15–40. Key thoracic finding: chest expansion <2.5 cm is pathologic (normal = 5 cm at nipple line). Stooped position. Morning stiffness >30 min, improves with exercise, night pain, alternating buttock pain. Stress fractures where new bone forms → excessive kyphosis → may crowd organs.
What distinguishes DDWR from DDWOR in the TMJ?
tap
Disc Displacement WITH Reduction (DDWR): The disc is anteriorly displaced at rest, but during opening it "reduces" (clicks back into place). This produces a reciprocal click — click on opening (reduction) and click on closing (re-displacement). The patient has relatively normal ROM. Disc Displacement WITHOUT Reduction (DDWOR): The disc stays anterior the entire time — it never reduces. This produces a block — more limited motion, no click. More significant functional limitation. Key clinical distinction: click = with reduction. Block/limited ROM = without reduction.

What is the CPR for lumbar manipulation and what makes a patient a good candidate?
tap
A patient is likely to benefit from lumbar manipulation if they meet these criteria: (1) LBP <16 days (some sources say 30 days). (2) No symptoms distal to the knee. (3) FABQ score <19 (low fear avoidance). (4) At least 1 hypomobile segment in the lumbar spine. (5) At least 1 hip with IR >35°. The more criteria met, the higher the likelihood of improvement. This CPR helps classify patients into the manipulation category of the Treatment-Based Classification system, which de-emphasizes specific pathoanatomy in favor of matching treatment approach to patient presentation.
