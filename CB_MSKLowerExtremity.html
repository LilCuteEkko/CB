Anatomy & Kinesiology
Hip Anatomy & Kinesiology
The hip is a ball-and-socket joint built for both stability and mobility — more stable than the shoulder due to deep acetabular coverage, thick capsule, and strong ligaments. The coxofemoral joint transmits 1.8–3.8× body weight during normal gait; any force >3× BW raises risk of early degeneration. Hip dysfunction has downstream effects on gait, posture, and the entire kinetic chain.

Hip Muscles — Action Groups

Glute Med = deltoid of hipGlute Max = most powerful extensorNo primary IR muscle existsIliopsoas = most powerful flexor▼
Flexors

Prime: Iliopsoas (most powerful), Sartorius, TFL, Rectus femoris, Pectineus, Adductor longus. Assist: Adductor brevis, Gracilis, Gluteus minimus (anterior fibers). Key: TFL anteromedial fibers active during running/sprinting — overactivation leads to compressive bursa/tendinopathies at greater trochanter and proximal lateral tibia.

Extensors

Prime: Gluteus maximus (most powerful, most active in hip flexion position), Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus, Biceps femoris (long head), Adductor magnus (posterior head). Assist: Gluteus medius (posterior fibers). Glute max is active with trunk extension, lateral sway control, and bipedal locomotion — greatest activity during sprinting.

Abductors

Prime: Gluteus medius (deltoid of the hip — primary dynamic stabilizer, active ~60% of gait cycle, decelerates hip adduction), Gluteus minimus, TFL. Assist: Sartorius, Rectus femoris, Piriformis.

Adductors

Prime: Adductor magnus (both heads), Adductor longus, Adductor brevis, Gracilis, Pectineus. Assist: Biceps femoris (long head), Gluteus maximus (posterior fibers). Adductor longus and magnus are the most common muscles involved in groin-region pain. Test adductor strength at 0° (gracilis), 45° (longus/brevis), 90° (pectineus).

External Rotators

Prime: Gluteus maximus, Gemelli inferior & superior, Obturator internus, Quadratus femoris, Piriformis (at <60° hip flexion). Assist: Gluteus medius posterior fibers (ER in ext, IR in flexion), Biceps femoris, Sartorius, Obturator externus. Short ER muscles function like the rotator cuff — compressing the hip joint for stability.

Internal Rotators — No Primary Muscle

NO muscle has IR as its primary function. Secondary IR: anterior gluteus medius, gluteus minimus, TFL, adductor longus/brevis, pectineus, posterior head of adductor magnus. Semimembranosus, semitendinosus, gracilis, piriformis (at 90° flexion) assist.

Hip Ligaments & Bursae

Iliofemoral (Y lig) = strongest in bodyIschiofemoral = most commonly injuredLigamentum teres → deep posterior hip pain▼
Iliofemoral Ligament (Y Ligament)

Strongest ligament in the body. Reinforces anterior hip capsule. AIIS to intertrochanteric line. Function: limits hip extension and ER; prevents anterior translation; maintains upright posture, reducing need for hip extensor contraction in balanced stance. Hip adduction tightens its superior portion. Taut in extension.

Pubofemoral Ligament

Obturator crest to intertrochanteric line. Function: tightens in extension AND ER; reinforces medial capsule; prevents over-abduction. Works with iliofemoral to limit extension.

Ischiofemoral Ligament — Most Commonly Injured

Posterior capsule thickening, winds posteriorly. Function: tightens IR in flexion AND extension; tightens with adduction of flexed hip. Most commonly injured hip ligament. Posterior surgical approach to THA can disrupt this — careful repair reduces posterior dislocation risk.

Ligamentum Teres (Capitis Femoris)

Acetabular notch to fovea capitis. Prevents subluxation when hip is ER in flexion or IR in extension. Tightens with adduction, flexion, and ER. Injury sign: deep posterior hip pain with hip flexion activities. Blood supply (internal iliac artery) passes through — injury disrupts vascularity → risk of AVN. Tears lead to microinstability → labral and cartilage damage.

Bursae

Iliopsoas bursa: Largest (up to 7×4 cm), present in 98% of adults. Deep to iliopsoas tendon between inguinal ligament and hip capsule. Inflamed with overuse, OA, RA, infection.
Trochanteric bursae: Subgluteus medius (superoposterior GT) and subgluteus maximus (between GT and gluteus maximus/ITB).
Ischiogluteal bursa: Between ischium and gluteus maximus. "Weaver's Bottom" — compressed by sitting on hard surfaces.

Femoral Triangle — VAN (Medial → Lateral)

Borders: Superior = inguinal ligament; Medial = adductor longus; Lateral = sartorius. Contents (medial → lateral): Femoral Vein, Femoral Artery, Femoral Nerve (VAN). Use caution palpating here or applying soft tissue techniques.

Osteology, Load, & Angles of Inclination

Coxa Vara <125° = fracture riskCoxa Valga >125° = dislocation risk>3× BW = early degeneration▼
Angle of Inclination

Normal adult: 125–139°. Coxa Vara (<125°): decreased limb length, increased load on femoral neck → predisposes to fracture. Coxa Valga (>125°): increased limb length, decreased stability → predisposes to dislocation/subluxation. Ward triangle: area of weakness in femoral neck trabecular system — common osteoporotic fracture site.

Hip Joint Loading

Coxofemoral joint peak forces during gait: 1.8–3.8× body weight. Forces >3× BW increase risk of early degeneration — driven by higher body mass and impact loading (running, jumping). Any force >3× BW → focus on mechanical weight acceptance. Femoral anteversion increases load more than retroversion.

Arthrokinematics (OKC)

OKC: convex femoral head moves on concave acetabulum → femoral head glides opposite to distal femur motion (rule of convex on concave). Anatomical flexion = parasagittal plane = Flex + Abd + IR. Extension = Ext + Add + ER. Abduction = Extension + ER of femoral head. Adduction = Flexion + ER.

Key Osteological Avulsion Sites

ASIS: Sartorius — avulsion in adolescent runners. Pubic tubercle: Rectus abdominis, adductor brevis/longus — tendinopathy site, groin pain referral. Ischial tuberosity: Hamstring origin — avulsion in athletes, longer healing; hamstring syndrome = sciatic N compression mimicking hamstring strain.

Hip Assessment Framework — Subjective, Lumbar Screen, SIJ Screen

Location drives DDxLaslett cluster = 3+ for SIJIR + Flexion first to goNight pain = red flag▼
Location-Based DDx
  • Groin pain: Ask MOI (hockey, ballet, gymnastics). OA, labral, lumbar referral, inguinal hernia, adductor strain, iliopsoas, gynecological/vascular
  • Posterior/buttock: Rule out SIJ, gluteal bursitis, hamstring syndrome, lumbar spine, labrum, posterior pubic symphysis
  • Posterolateral: Trochanteric bursitis, gluteal insertional tendinopathy (Trendelenburg gait), THA component loosening
  • Groin pain with sitting: Ant/superior acetabular labral compromise — posterior/inferior glide helps
  • Posterior pain with sitting: Ischial bursitis, hamstring syndrome, LB spine
  • Night-only pain: Red flag — rule out tumor (osteoid osteoma common in long bones)
Age-Based Clinical Considerations
  • Boys 4–10: LCPD (painful limp, painless initially)
  • Males <6: Transient synovitis (most common sudden hip pain)
  • Adolescent 11–14: SCFE (especially overweight boys, ER gait)
  • Young athletic female: Stress fracture (female triad risk — disordered eating, amenorrhea, osteoporosis)
  • 35–50: Labral lesions (wear & tear, catching/clicking)
  • >40 males: Labral cysts, sacral pathologies, stress fractures
  • >45: OA (groin + knee pain; hip pain often presents first as knee pain)
Lumbar Screening (Clear the Spine)

Subjective: Dermatomal/myotomal distribution; achy/shooting/stabbing; must clear piriformis syndrome; sensation changes. Objective: AROM functionally; lumbosacral roots via dural testing (SLR, SLRD — good sensitivity and specificity). Bilateral knee pain → think lumbar referral.

SIJ Screening — Laslett Cluster

Subjective clues: unilateral pain, feeling of being stuck on one side, one-finger sign (PSIS), postpartum instability. Laslett cluster — 3 or more = highly likely SIJ: Dorsolateral provocation, Thigh Thrust, Gaenslen, Ventromedial provocation, Sacral Thrust. ASLR: perform SLR without assistance then with innominate stabilization — if pain changes, high probability of SIJ dysfunction.

Motion Restrictions

First motions to diminish in OA and labral issues: Flexion and IR. Capsular pattern: IR > Flexion > Extension (most limited = IR). Hard end-feels indicate OA or coxarthrosis. Significant motor function loss → consider subluxation, stress fracture, nerve injury — may need imaging. Foot drop + atrophy = refer out.

Pathologies — Capsular, Noncapsular & Intra-articular
Hip Pathologies — Capsular, Noncapsular & Intra-articular
Hip pathologies are organized by pattern of limitation. Capsular pattern (arthritis, synovitis): IR > Flexion most limited. Noncapsular pattern (SCFE, AVN, loose body): any pattern other than capsular. Intra-articular (labral tear, FAI): common report is groin pain with mechanical symptoms.

Capsular Pattern — Osteoarthritis & Synovitis

IR > Flex most limitedOA CPR: 3 of 5Knee pain 10 yrs before hip pain>45 y/o, overweight▼
Hip OA Background

28 million Americans estimated to have OA (2011). Generally >50 years, overweight. Post-traumatic OA presents 10 years later. Pain at hip often presents first as knee pain. X-ray: joint space narrowing + marginal spurring. Conservative: cane, NSAIDs, exercise + weight loss (aquatics), acupuncture, PRP. Movement is critical — avoid immobility.

Traumatic Arthritis

Age >20. Repetitive hyperextension + rotation from work or sports (kickers, soccer, horse riders). Pain: groin or anterior thigh with sitting, walking, ascending stairs. Objective: PROM limited in capsular pattern.

Coxarthrosis — "Frozen Shoulder of the Hip"

Disorder of articular cartilage/bone balance — slow progressive course. Primary: genetic, older, female more common and more severe. Secondary: underlying pathology (instability, dysplasia, intra-articular fractures). Progression: pain with WB → constant pain at rest → pain subsides but movement limited (capsular pattern). Treatment: manual therapy, joint mobilization, ther ex for function (not pain), neuromuscular re-education + HEP.

OA Clinical Prediction Rule — 3 of 5
  1. Squatting is an aggravating factor
  2. Active hip flexion causes lateral hip pain
  3. Scour test with adduction causes lateral hip pain or groin pain (catching may occur)
  4. Active hip extension causes pain
  5. Passive IR <25°
OA PT Treatment Protocol

Mobilization/manipulation: ventral + lateral + caudal directed force at femur. Traction (long-axis): applied perpendicular; contraindicated with joint instability, degenerative bone disorders, anticoagulant therapy. Soft tissue: adductors typically hypertonic (wanting stability), abductors weaker. NMR: functional mobility focus — use UE reaching in decompressed mode to activate hip muscles during WB activities.

Transient Synovitis

Males under age 6 — most common cause of sudden hip pain and limp. Viral, autoimmune, or microtrauma origin; 40% have preceding illness. Limited IR (capsular pattern). Antalgic gait. Imaging may be normal. Lasts 2–3 weeks; typically full recovery. Differentiate from sciatica. In some cases progresses to juvenile arthritis.

Noncapsular Pattern — AVN, SCFE, LCPD

SCFE: boys 11–14, ER gait, internal fixation ASAPLCPD: boys 4–10, painless limpAVN: CT/MRI, core decompression▼
Avascular Necrosis (AVN) of Femoral Head

Disruption of blood supply to proximal femur (circumflex arteries). Adult causes: fracture, dislocation, surgery, alcohol, steroids, radiation. Presentation: gradual groin pain, possible hip movement limits, crepitation and gluteal pain with increased severity. Dx: CT scan or MRI (X-ray late to show changes). Treatment: Early = nonsurgical (stem cells, magnetics). Surgical = core decompression or osteotomy (PWB 3–6 months, AROM to tolerance). End-stage = THA (cemented shows most improvement in pain and function).

SCFE — Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis

Background: femoral head slips posteriorly off femoral neck at growth plate. Adolescents 11–14; more common in boys; overweight (80–100th percentile); diabetes; cancer treatments. Presentation: hip/medial thigh/knee pain; antalgic gait; tends to walk with ER foot; positive log roll sign and SL raise against resistance; pain with IR relieved with ER. Radiograph: femoral head displaced posteriorly and inferiorly. Internal fixation ASAP — goal is to prevent AVN.

Legg-Calvé-Perthes Disease (LCPD)

Femoral head blood supply disrupted → AVN → collapse and flattening. Blood supply returns over months; bone replacement takes 2–3 years. Boys 4–10, unilateral. Presentation: painless limp (comes and goes), hip stiffness (flex/IR/abd), constant thigh/groin pain, limb length discrepancy, upper thigh atrophy. X-ray/MRI: small femoral epiphysis; sclerosis and flattening if chronic. Conservative: short bed rest, limited WB, possible casting, PT for strength and soft tissue. Surgery only if conservative fails. Prognosis: good if <6 years; high deformity/arthritis risk if >6 years.

Trauma & Fractures — Red Flags
  • Fall → think fracture first: inability to bear weight = femoral hip fracture until proven otherwise
  • Stress fractures: pelvic rami and femoral neck most common; X-ray only 10% sensitive early — bone scan better
  • High risk: female triad, amenorrhea, low aerobic fitness, smoking, steroid use
  • Myositis ossificans: bone forms inside muscle from blunt trauma — ask about mechanism and time since injury
  • Hip pointer: contusion to iliac crest — lasts a long time; treat with soft tissue + ROM

Intra-articular — Labral Tears, FAI, Loose Bodies

Common report: groin painFAI: CAM + Pincer (mixed most common)Labral MRA: 90% SN, 100% SP+FADIR + FABER▼
Labral Tears

Background: degenerative or repetitive (torsion/twisting, LCPD hx, dysplasia). Ages 35–50. Anterior labrum: greatest force in hip flexion + adduction. Presentation: anterolateral/anterosuperior groin pain; catching/locking/clicking in 50%; dull pain with intermittent sharp pain in IR/ER; prolonged sitting; antalgic or Trendelenburg gait. Exam: +FADIR, +FABER; loss/apprehension of IR; catching (mechanical symptoms). Imaging: MRA 90% SN, 100% SP. Conservative PT: glute max stabilization, quadriceps/hamstring co-contraction, myofascial work (adductor insertion, glute med insertion, piriformis, ITB), MWM for capsular restriction, weak hip abductors in CKC. Goal: full ROM with rotation in full WB at velocity.

Loose Bodies

Ossified or non-ossified fragments (osteochondral, chondral, fibrous, foreign). Usually secondary to other processes or trauma. Presentation: hx of dislocation or low-energy trauma; popping/catching/clicking/giving way; sudden leg pain "twinges" when WB; possible stiffness; springy end feel during passive extension. Imaging: X-ray, MRI with contrast, MRA. Surgery: arthroscopic removal — gold standard.

Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI)

Types (Ganz): CAM — bony abnormality of femoral head (blocky, non-spherical); young male athletes; impingement in flex + ADD + IR. Pincer — increased acetabular rim coverage; middle-aged women in athletics. Mixed: most common. Associated with labral and chondral damage; significant cause of hip OA. Cardinal sign: sharp anterior hip pain with deep flexion, deep squat, IR, or abduction. "C sign." Objective: limited IR <20° at 90° flexion; +FADIR, +FABER. Common in hockey, dancers, soccer, football. Conservative PT: discontinue sport temporarily; joint mobs (create joint space); dry needling; hip abductor strengthening; CARS exercises; banded squats for valgus collapse control. Surgical: arthroscopic osteoplasty; 93% return rate (Philippon 2008).

Hip Microinstability

Shallow acetabulum, excessive femoral anteversion, Ehlers-Danlos/Marfan/Down syndrome. Repetitive hip rotation + axial loading in dancers, ballet, martial arts, tennis, baseball. Presentation: "loosey-goosey" feeling, apprehension at end range; no motion loss but excessive motion (especially IR + ER); insidious anterior/groin pain. Treatment: core + global hip strengthening, biomechanical analysis; avoid repeated subluxations. Surgical: arthroscopic capsulorrhaphy if conservative fails.

Extra-articular Pathologies
Extra-articular Hip Pathologies
Extra-articular pathologies involve tendons, bursae, muscles, and nerves surrounding the hip joint — not the joint surface itself. They are often overlooked when a patient presents with "hip pain." Snapping hip, bursitis, adductor/hamstring strains, piriformis syndrome, and stress fractures all require distinct examination and management approaches.

Coxa Saltans — Snapping Hip Syndrome

Internal: iliopsoas over iliopectineal prominenceExternal: ITB over greater trochanterBallet dancers, runners▼
Mechanisms of Snapping
  • Internal (anterior): Iliopsoas tendon snapping over iliopectineal prominence or lesser tubercle — most common cause of groin pain in runners
  • External (lateral): IT band snapping over greater trochanter — trochanteric bursitis
  • Iliofemoral ligament over femoral head
  • Long head biceps femoris over ischial tuberosity
Internal Snapping Hip (Iliopsoas Tendinitis)

Pain or discomfort with internal snapping = indication of iliopsoas tendinitis. More common in ballet dancers (often not painful). Ask if activities were ramped up when pain started — overuse pattern. Chronic degeneration → tendinopathy (chronic pain + mental pain component). Presentation: anterior groin pain moving from hip extension to flexion; popping/clicking/catching; can be associated with LBP (hip flexors tied to pelvic and lumbar position).

ITB/Greater Trochanteric Bursitis & External Snapping

Thickened ITB or glute max tendon snapping over greater trochanter = irritation and friction. Primary cause: increased hip adduction + knee IR. Presentation: pain over greater trochanter radiating down lateral thigh; painful with sleeping and direct pressure; stair climbing often painful; +Trendelenburg; associated hip abductor weakness; snapping with sidelying hip moved from flex to ext and IR to ER. More common in middle-aged women; growing prevalence in young runners. Diabetes, OA, and thyroid issues = increased risk. Treatment: STM + hold/relax stretching (ITB, rectus femoris, iliopsoas); manual + self-MFR to ITB; hip abductor strengthening in SLS; gait training; dry needling + KT tape (star pattern); address pronated foot or poor mechanical control.

Gluteus Medius/Minimus Injury & Piriformis Syndrome

Gmed/Gmin = rotator cuff of the hipPhase 1→2→3 progressionPiriformis: sciatic N compression▼
Gluteus Medius/Minimus Injury

Gmed and Gmin = "rotator cuff of the hip." More common in women; recently active. Triggered by falls, prolonged single-leg WB, overuse, sports. Presentation: dull lateral hip/groin/buttock pain; focal tenderness at insertion; leg length discrepancy; weak hip abduction; pain with PROM/MMT of ER at 90° hip flexion; +pain with SLS >30 seconds. Tears → constant pain that feels better in therapy but doesn't improve overall. Intervention — 3 Phases: Phase 1: protection, low-load exercises, minimize dynamic influence, NO hip abd strength training yet. Phase 2: standing sagittal plane focus, static → dynamic, add planes one at a time. Phase 3: dynamic multiplanar with reaction, stress length-tension (posterolateral lunge).

Piriformis Syndrome

Background: compression of sciatic nerve as it exits pelvis under piriformis muscle. Pain in sciatic distribution. Three contributing conditions: (A) myofascial referred pain from trigger points in piriformis (most common); (B) adjacent muscle/nerve/vascular entrapment at greater sciatic foramen (12%); (C) SIJ dysfunction altering piriformis position. Presentation: chronic buttock pain, numbness, paresthesia; pain with sitting, squatting, standing, bowel movements, and dyspareunia in women; tenderness over sciatic notch; exacerbated by active hip ER; pain may lessen with lying down or bending knee. Tests: +Pace, +Freiberg, +Lasegue, +log roll, +FAIR test. DDx from radiculopathy: sciatic N entrapment pain increases with IR during SLR; lumbosacral radiculopathy doesn't change with hip rotation; sciatic N entrapment spares gluteal muscles (radiculopathy causes palpable atrophy).

Adductor Strain, Hamstring Strain & Stress Fractures

Adductor longus/magnus most affectedGrade 1–2–3 gradingStress Fx: bone scan > X-ray earlyFemale triad risk▼
Adductor Strain

MOI: forced push-off or kick in frontal plane, repetitive kicking, overuse during fatigue. Hockey, skating, soccer, rugby. Adductor longus and magnus most frequent. Testing by position: 0° = gracilis; 45° = adductor longus/brevis; 90° + adduction = pectineus. Grades: 1 = pain, minimal strength loss; 2 = compromised strength, no complete loss; 3 = complete disruption + complete function loss. Predisposing factors: adductor weakness, adductor-abductor imbalance, restricted hip ROM. Treatment: non-surgical; collagen deposition management; return to sport with full strength/ROM; address adductor-abductor-abdominal imbalance.

Hamstring Strains

Biceps femoris most commonly involved (short sprint). MOI: short sprint, jump/kick, fall or collision. Increased anterior pelvic tilt (tight hip flexors or restricted anterior capsule) → increased hamstring tension → higher strain risk. Hamstring syndrome: mimics hamstring strain but is actually sciatic N compression at ischial tuberosity. Biceps femoris may be contributing.

Stress Fractures

Two types: Fatigue fracture (normal bone + abnormal stress — runners) and Insufficiency fracture (normal stress + abnormal bone — osteoporosis). Most common sites: pelvic rami, femoral neck. Risk factors: female triad, amenorrhea, low aerobic fitness, smoking, steroid use, nutritional compromise. Exam: diffuse radiating groin/hip pain; +fulcrum test; X-ray only 10% sensitive early — bone scan is better. Night pain, inability to absorb shock during gait. Treatment: Tension-sided = surgical pinning to prevent AVN. Compression-sided = 6–8 weeks limited WB, PT for weakness/myofascial/neuromotor imbalances, cross-training (aquatics).

Iliopsoas Strain

MOI: forced extension while hip is actively flexed. Presentation: pain with acceleration and high-stepping; RIMS — increased pain with resisted hip flexion, adduction, and ER. Recovery can be lengthy; reaggravation common.

The 5 Laslett Tests

SI Distraction • SI Compression • Thigh Thrust • Gaenslen's • Sacral Thrust (Spring). FABER is often added as a 6th. Thigh Thrust: supine; flex hip 90°, adduct slightly; apply force through long axis of femur creating A-P shear on SIJ. Distraction: supine; cross-armed pressure to ASIS gapping the SIJ. Sacral Spring: prone; vigorous downward pressure on sacrum at S3 (up to 6 times).

Post-Operative
Hip Post-Operative Rehabilitation
Post-op hip management varies by procedure. Know the surgical approach, what structures were cut, and what the precautions are before touching the patient. Goals: restore nearly-normal anatomy and biomechanics, decrease long-term pain, improve long-term function, promote long-term joint health.

Knee Anatomy & Clinical Evaluation
The knee is inherently unstable — support comes from ligaments, muscles, and menisci. Multiple articulating surfaces (tibiofemoral + patellofemoral) each have distinct pathologies. VMO is the first quad to atrophy and the last to rehab. Bilateral knee pain → think lumbar spine referral first.

Meniscus Anatomy & Function

Blood Supply Zones — Critical for Treatment Decisions

Red zone (outer 10–30%): vascularized — can heal with suturing. White zone (inner 65–75%): avascular — nourished by synovial fluid via diffusion and mechanical pumping — poor healing potential. Tears in white zone = partial meniscectomy. Tears in red zone = repair preferred (especially in younger patients). Medial meniscus more common for degenerative pathologies.

Functions of Meniscus
  • Load transmission (distributes forces across tibial plateau)
  • Shock absorption
  • Joint lubrication and nutrition
  • Secondary mechanical stability
  • Limit hyperextension
  • Proprioceptive input (similar to labrum at hip)
Medial vs. Lateral Meniscus

Medial: larger, thicker, less mobile; wider posteriorly; ACL and PCL blend into its horns; more commonly torn in degenerative populations (wear and tear). Lateral: more mobile; both horns attached to tibia; attaches via ligaments of Humphrey (anterior MFL) and Wrisberg (posterior MFL); tears less common but from twisting motions. Bucket handle tear = surgical emergency (locked knee).

MOI → Structure Injured

  • Valgus + rotation: ACL, posteromedial capsule
  • Varus force (+rotation): LCL, PL capsule, PCL
  • Hyperextension: ACL + medial meniscal tears; buckling/giving way
  • Flexion + posterior force: PCL (dashboard injury)
  • Twisting (foot planted): ACL if tibia moves forward; meniscus with compression + rotation

Ligament Anatomy — ACL, PCL, MCL, LCL

ACL — Anterior Cruciate Ligament

Primary restraint to anterior tibial translation and IR tibia. Secondary restraint to varus/valgus stress. Two bundles: Anteromedial (AM) — max tension at 30–60° flexion, limits anterior translation throughout ROM; Posterolateral (PL) — max tension in full extension, lax at 60–90° (allows tibial rotation). Tensile strength stronger than MCL/LCL but weaker than PCL. Poor blood supply — does not heal well.

PCL — Posterior Cruciate Ligament

Primary restraint to posterior tibial translation and IR tibia. Secondary restraint to varus/valgus. Thicker than ACL — handles more tensile strength. More taut in flexion overall. Two bundles: Anterolateral bundle = taut in flexion; Posteromedial bundle = taut in extension. Attaches posteriorly to posterior horns of both menisci. Innervated by posterior articular nerve (proprioceptive mechanoreceptors).

MCL — Medial Collateral Ligament

Primary restraint to valgus stress and ER tibia. Secondary restraint to anterior/posterior translation. Good blood supply — heals well (sometimes intentionally left untreated). Two bands: Superficial (thick, fan-like; first to be injured from valgus force; covers medial genicular artery and nerve; anterior fibers taut in flexion, posterior fibers taut in extension) + Deep (blends with medial meniscus — explains medial meniscus involvement with MCL injuries). More common than LCL. Associated with medial meniscus and ACL injuries in higher grade sprains.

LCL — Lateral Collateral Ligament

Primary restraint to varus stress and ER tibia. Secondary restraint to anterior/posterior translation. Runs from lateral femoral condyle to head of fibula. Forms part of arcuate ligament complex. Less commonly injured than MCL. Part of posterolateral corner (PLC) complex — rarely isolated; major factor in failed ACL surgeries when not addressed.

PFPS — Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome

Catch-all diagnosis of exclusion. Also called: chondromalacia patella, patellar arthralgia, runners knee. Women 2:1 vs men. Risk factors: decreased quad flexibility, decreased knee extension strength, diminished quad explosive strength, hypermobile patella. Best diagnostic combo: pain with squatting + palpation of medial/lateral patellar borders + isometric quad resistance at 90°. Treatment (individualized): hip abductors/glutes; hip mobility; quad strength (№1); foot posture/orthotics; taping (short-term); motor control.

Chondromalacia Patella

Irritation/softening of posterior patellar cartilage. Teenagers/young adults/females. Signs: grinding/noisy knee; pain descending stairs/squatting; often asymptomatic (not a direct PT diagnosis to write). Look for muscle imbalances increasing PFJ compression. Pain with compression along the joint.

Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee)

Actual tendon issue — not PFPS. Males 15–30, jumping sports. Signs: pain at inferior pole of patella on patellar tendon; pain with resisted extension; "movie theater sign" (pain with prolonged sitting); worse with energy storage exercises (depth jump). Treatment: blend eccentric + isometric + isotonic loading; address jumping/landing biomechanics; identify training change that initiated symptoms.

Apophysitis — Youth

Osgood Schlatter's: Males 10–15, jumping/cutting/squatting sports, growth spurts. Tibial tuberosity pain + bony protuberance + quad weakness. Self-limiting — symptom modulation, modify activities. Sinding-Larsen-Johansson: Males adolescent, similar demographics. Inferior pole pain (like patellar tendinopathy location). Quad weakness/tightness + recent growth spurt.

Plica Syndrome

Remnant of embryonic tissue — up to 50% of people have it asymptomatically. Medial plica most commonly symptomatic (1st–3rd decade). Signs: anterior/anteromedial knee pain; clicking/popping/locking when pinched; palpable painful string along medial parapatellar area; giving way. Diagnose by ruling out other pathologies + arthroscopic finding. Treatment: ITB tightness, quad weakness stretching (quads, hamstrings, gastroc).

MCL & LCL Sprains

MCL: valgus ± rotation; more common; often with medial meniscus and ACL. MCL has good blood supply — can intentionally be left torn during surgery. Treat: avoid valgus stress and tibial rotational stress; ROM + swelling + strength + neuromotor re-education. LCL: varus stress; less concomitant damage. Same impairment-based treatment principles. Avoid varus stress and tibial rotational stress.

PCL Sprain

30% of ligamentous injuries. Dashboard injury or posterior-directed force. Signs: audible pop; minimal swelling; minimal pain with extension; loss of flexion ROM (10–20°, pain >90°); posterior knee pain (aggravated by kneeling); possible anterior pain. MRI = gold standard. Grade 1–2: favorable outcomes conservative — quad strengthening (reduces posterior tibial translation), ROM, return to sport 2–6 weeks (90%). Grade 3: avoid isolated hamstring exercises ×6–8 weeks (increases PCL stress); CKC environment; co-contraction; return to sport 3 months even without surgery.

ACL Sprain

Non-contact deceleration most common. Tibial IR on ER femur; valgus + hyperextension with contact. Female risk factors: landing patterns, quad dominance/hamstring imbalance, hormonal factors, hip anatomy. Signs: audible pop; significant hemarthrosis (1–2 hrs); feelings of instability; decreased extension ROM. Associated medial meniscus tears are common. Acute treatment: ROM ASAP; CKC strengthening (quads, gastroc-soleus, hamstrings); avoid OKC knee ext >30° and deep knee flexion >90° early. Copers criteria (return without surgery): isolated ACL, full pain-free ROM, no effusion, >70% MVIC quads bilaterally, >80% on SL hop battery, ≤1 giving-way episode.

PLC Sprain (Posterolateral Corner)

Rarely isolated — varus on flexed knee, posterolateral force, hyperextension. Major factor in failed ACL reconstructions (must address before or with ACL surgery). Signs: instability especially in full extension; difficulty squatting/stepping; flexion resisted exercises limited (deep flexion and posterior glide avoided). Treatment: symptom modulation at area + global hip and knee stability focus.

Meniscal Pathology

Most common cause of mechanical knee symptoms. MOI: twisting/changing direction WB; contact with planted LE; degeneration (older, medial). Signs: popping, clicking, locking; joint line tenderness (due to capsular strain from tear); swelling; decreased ROM (especially end-range flexion pain); Meniscus Composite Score: JLT + extreme flex/ext + popping/clicking/catching. Types: Longitudinal (bucket handle → surgery), horizontal, radial, flap (parrot beak). Surgery only for bucket-handle tear with locking.

Knee OA

Most common disability in US. Risk factors: physically demanding job, female, obesity, previous knee trauma/meniscus injury, quad weakness. Tibiofemoral presentation: swelling (Baker's cyst, bursal, fat pad), warmth, pain with WB, decreased knee extension, muscle weakness, joint deformity. Patellofemoral presentation: stiffness, crepitus, same signs. Mild/moderate treatment: education (over 45, changes are common), strengthening, motor control/proprioception, assisted devices, weight management, manual therapy. Severe OA: lubrication injections, unloading braces, TKA. Pre-op strength linked to post-op function.

Chondral Pathology

Younger pt: acute trauma. Older pt: degenerative. Risk: ligamentous instability, overweight. Signs: dull ache/deep joint pain; pain with WB (descending stairs, squatting); changes in gait/screw home mechanism. Treatment: poor conservative evidence; protection of joint surfaces through strengthening and graded loading exposure. Surgical options: debridement, ACI, OATS, microfracture surgery.

Patellofemoral Instability

Dislocation/subluxation of patella relative to femoral trochlea. Lateral most common. Prior episode = 7× more likely to recur. Risk factors: young females (increased laxity, genu valgum, increased Q angle); small/high-riding patella (patella alta); shallow trochlear groove; VMO/VL imbalance; generalized laxity. Exam: TTP at medial retinaculum and distal patellar pole; instability identified through history of repeated events.

Post-Operative
Knee Post-Operative Rehabilitation
Arthrogenic Muscle Inhibition (AMI) is the enemy. Even 20–30 mL of joint fluid produces quadriceps activation failure. Gaining full knee extension in the first 2 weeks is essential — extension loss of 3–5° vs. contralateral increases OA risk. Extension always before flexion post-op.

General Considerations — AMI, Extension Priority, Anterior Interval

Arthrogenic Muscle Inhibition (AMI)

Ongoing inhibition of musculature surrounding a joint — a protective reflex mechanism. Even experimental knee effusions with just 20–30 mL saline produce quadriceps activation failure. Cycle: joint injury → AMI → muscle weakness → muscle atrophy → immobilization → perpetuates AMI. Treatment: NMES to quads (Russian protocol: 2×/day, biphasic 50 pps, 250μs pulse duration, 10s on:10s off); cryotherapy (disinhibitory); compression + elevation; patient education.

Extension Priority Rule

Normal knee extension = approximately +5° hyperextension. A patient who "lacks 5° from neutral" actually has 10° of terminal extension loss. Loss of 3–5° extension vs. contralateral = increased OA risk. >5° extension loss = more dramatic OA development. Post-op position of comfort is 10–20° flexion — if maintained, patient stiffens in this position. Hamstrings and posterior capsule become tight. Anterior interval becomes fibrotic (Hoffa's syndrome).

Anterior Interval (Patellofemoral Complex)

Infrapatellar area comprised of patellofemoral complex, bursa, and infrapatellar fat pad. Often becomes fibrotic following arthroscopy due to location of medial and lateral portals. Patellofemoral joint mobilizations are crucial to maintain proper gliding, decrease pain, and improve function. Assess patellar mobility (superior/inferior and medial/lateral) at every post-op knee session.

OKC vs. CKC Exercise Selection — ACL Stress
  • Greatest ACL stress: OKC 10–50° flexion (peaks 10–30°)
  • Avoid OKC knee extension >30° early in ACL rehab
  • CKC: deep knee flexion >90° also increases ACL load — avoid early
  • Load changes with exercise technique (quad dominant squat = more ACL stress)
  • No differences in final laxity, pain, and function between OKC and CKC at end of rehab

Partial Meniscectomy

For non-reparable tears (usually white zone). Wide range of patient population. Rehab: WBAT; no loaded deep knee flexion; ROM guidelines; address AMI; normalize gait early; gradually progress strength. Timeline: 4–8 weeks rehab course. Complication: 3× increased risk of knee OA at 14-year follow up, especially with meniscal involvement.

Meniscus Repair

Suturing torn pieces together arthroscopically. Red zone tears only (blood supply needed for healing). Younger, active population. Rehab (longer — 4–6 months): Poor blood supply = increased healing time. Hinged knee brace. WB: NWB 4–6 weeks (surgeon-dependent); WB with brace locked in extension initially. No flexion >90° for 4–6 weeks. Priority: full terminal knee extension, quad neuro re-education, then flexion within precautions. Failure of repair = mechanical symptoms, inability to regain ROM, persistent pain.

Ankle & Foot Anatomy
The foot and ankle complex is the foundation of the kinetic chain. Triplanar motion (supination and pronation from tibial IR/ER) is translated into sagittal plane motion during ambulation. Ankle DF restriction has cascading effects all the way to the lumbar spine. Ligament strength ranking lateral ankle (weakest to strongest): ATFL < PTFL < CFL < Deltoid complex.

Lateral Ligaments — ATFL, CFL, PTFL

ATFL — Anterior Talofibular Ligament

Primary function: resist anterior displacement of talus relative to ankle mortise; talocrural IR; plantarflexion. Most commonly injured in inversion sprain — weakest of the lateral ligaments (lowest maximal load to failure) but highest strain to failure. First ligament torn in an inversion injury in PF position. Accessory functions: resists anterior talar displacement from mortise + IR of talus within mortise.

CFL — Calcaneofibular Ligament

Extra-articular structure covered by peroneal tendons. Spans both talocrural AND subtalar joints (different axes of rotation). Larger and stronger than ATFL. Resists ankle inversion and calcaneal adduction throughout full ROM. Taut in DF; most taut resisting varus when in plantigrade position. If ankle is in neutral, CFL may be injured with ATFL or alone. CFL injury = more profound functional effect than isolated ATFL.

PTFL — Posterior Talofibular Ligament

Intracapsular but extrasynovial. Runs from posterior medial fibula to lateral tubercle of talus. Resists posterior movement of talus. Rarely injured in typical ankle sprain before CFL and ATFL. Movements that injure PTFL: extreme DF + foot ER + limb IR. More commonly involved in syndesmotic (high ankle) sprains. In DF: PTFL maximally stressed, CFL taut, ATFL loose. In PF: ATFL taut, CFL and PTFL become loose.

Medial Ligament — Deltoid Complex

Strongest ligament in foot/ankle. 6 bands total forming a triangular shape (deltoid). Typically injured with eversion ankle sprains. Four main fibers: Tibionavicular (resists lateral translation and ER of talus), Posterior tibiotalar (resists DF, lateral translation, ER), Tibiocalcaneal (resists abduction; tibiocalcaneal ligament is usually first ruptured with eversion injury), Anterior tibiotalar (strong deep fiber; resists abduction in PF and eversion — injury often associated with avulsion fracture).

Distal Tib-Fib (Syndesmosis) — High Ankle Sprain

AITFL + PITFL + interosseous membrane. Separation of distal tib-fib joint from rotation of talus in ankle mortise. MOI: excessive DF + tibial ER + planted foot (closed chain). Signs: deep global ankle pain; pain with ER of foot (talus widening the joint); pain exceeds amount of swelling; TTP over anterior distal tib-fib; +ER stress test and squeeze test; typically less swelling than lateral ankle sprain. Always rule out fractures and lateral ankle sprain. ORIF if diastasis present.

Foot Posture — Pes Planus vs. Pes Cavus

Pes Planus (pronated/"flat feet"): talus PF; places stress on medial/plantar structures; can lead to flexible foot during push-off; causes: gastroc-soleus tightness, post. tibialis dysfunction (decreased eccentric foot control). Pes Cavus (supinated/"high arch"): much less common; rigid/stiff foot; prevents normal pronation during stance — decreased shock absorption. Ottawa Ankle Rules: if bony tenderness at distal 6 cm fibula/lat. malleolus, medial malleolus, base of 5th metatarsal, or navicular — X-ray needed. Also: inability to bear weight immediately after injury AND for 4 steps at initial evaluation.

Foot Arches — Three Types

Medial longitudinal arch: highest; calcaneus-talus-navicular-3 cuneiforms-1st 3 metatarsals; creates space for plantar soft tissues with elastic properties. Support: tibialis anterior/posterior, fibularis longus, flexor digitorum longus, FHL, intrinsics, plantar ligaments, plantar aponeurosis. Lateral longitudinal arch: flatter, makes more ground contact; calcaneus-cuboid-4th/5th metatarsals. Transverse arch: coronal plane; metatarsal bases-cuboid-3 cuneiforms; fibularis longus and tibialis posterior maintain it.

Windlass Mechanism

During terminal stance and push-off, toe extension tightens the plantar fascia (like a windlass tightening a rope) — raising the medial arch and supinating the foot for rigid propulsion. The plantar fascia should be rigid during push-off. When the fascia is degenerated or inflamed, this mechanism is impaired — relevant in plantar fasciitis treatment (1st MTP extension stretches plantar fascia). Assess with windlass test at the medial calcaneal tuberosity.

Plantar Fasciitis / Fasciosis — Plantar Heel Pain

Background & Risk Factors

Fasciosis = degenerated, non-inflamed phase; Fasciitis = inflammatory phase. Degenerative irritation at insertion (medial calcaneal tuberosity). Structures: plantar fascia inflammation + increased thickness + altered heel fat pad + microtears. Risk factors: pes planus/cavus, limited ankle DF, high BMI (non-athletes), tight hamstrings/posterior chain, running, prolonged WB at work, underuse-to-overuse pattern. Demographics: 20–34 y/o and 75+; F > M runners; soccer, running, gymnastics, dance; jobs with prolonged standing.

Presentation (Classic)
  • Extreme pain with first steps in morning (cold, contracted stiff fascia; improves with warming up)
  • Plantar medial heel pain (medial calcaneal tuberosity)
  • More pain while barefoot; pain with prolonged sitting
  • Recent increase in WB activity (new job, training change)
  • TTP at proximal insertion; fascia feels like a hard string
  • Limited AROM/PROM talocrural DF (often bilateral)
  • Posterior chain tightness (Achilles + soleus)
Treatment Protocol

Conservative PT is #1 treatment. Manual therapy: improve DF ROM; STM/MFR to plantar fascia and posterior chain; joint mobs (improve DF and 1st MTP extension); deep friction massage (tool-assisted). Exercise: posterior chain flexibility (gastroc/soleus — downward dogs!); foot intrinsic strengthening (towel curls, marble pickups, toe taps); hip and core NM. Modalities: MHP (moist heat) not ice; KT application. Orthotics + night splints. Other conservative: corticosteroid or PRP injections; immobilization. Surgery (fasciotomy): release of proximal medial insertion — complication: flattening of longitudinal arch (global instability).

Lateral Ankle Sprain — Grades, 4-Phase Rehab & Functional Instability

Grading
  • Grade 1: Minimal to no swelling; tenderness to ATFL; return to sport <2 weeks
  • Grade 2: Local swelling; diffuse lateral ankle tenderness; return to sport 2–6 weeks
  • Grade 3: Diffuse pain, swelling, bruising; return to sport >6 weeks. Grade III with mortise widening = ORIF
4-Phase Rehab
  1. Active inflammatory: Pain + edema control (STM, lymphatic drainage, KT); PROM/AROM; external support for WB; monitor gait — equal stance phase = external support no longer needed
  2. Fibroblastic repair: PWB → FWB; supported balance + proprioceptive training; joint mobs and MWM (improve DF, proprioception, WB tolerance)
  3. Remodeling: Unilateral balance training; UE and LE reaches; dynamic proprioceptive training
  4. Maturation: Jogging → running; sport-specific training; train MOI direction (proprioception in multiple planes)
Functional Ankle Instability (FAI)

Feeling of ankle instability due to proprioceptive and neuromuscular deficits from multiple ankle sprains. Damages afferent structures (capsules, ligaments, mechanoreceptors). Signs: giving way; decreased peroneal strength; difficulty on uneven surfaces; decreased balance (SEBT); inverted heel at heel strike. Conservative treatment: proprioceptive training main focus (4-week balance program reduces self-reported instability and improves joint position sense); NM re-education; stable → unstable surfaces and static → dynamic; wobble board, BAPS, Airex, Dynadisc, BOSU; progress to cognitive tasks with balance.

Peroneal Tendinopathy

Overuse + repeated inversion sprains. Pes cavus posture more common. Young ice skaters, dancers, runners. Signs: pain behind and distal to lateral malleolus; pain with resisted eversion; instability with toe walking. Treatment: identify training errors; proprioceptive training; MFR (tool-assisted); KT; cupping; orthotics for foot posture; joint mobs (address subtalar hypomobility, improve pronation/calcaneal eversion); normalize ankle ROM and strength. Surgery: peroneal tendoscopy; repair if longitudinal tear; retinaculum repair if tendon subluxation.

Achilles Tendinopathy — Noninsertional & Insertional; Achilles Rupture

Noninsertional Achilles Tendinopathy (Most Common)

4–6 cm proximal to Achilles insertion — relatively avascular zone. Tendinosis (intratendinous degeneration without inflammation from repetitive microtrauma or aging) or peritendinitis (inflammation of peritendon from overuse). Demographics: 30–50 years old. Risk factors: decreased DF ROM; decreased PF strength; hyperpronated foot; pes cavus/planus; comorbidities (diabetes, HTN, obesity); long-distance runners; training errors. Presentation: intermittent stiffness + pain with exercise; post ankle stiffness decreasing as exercise continues; TTP of Achilles; thickening; decreased DF ROM (lack of 20° DF in knee ext = shortened gastroc; lack of 30° in knee flex = shortened soleus). Treatment: eccentric PF strengthening (№1 for both types — 3×15, 180 reps/day for 12 weeks per Dutton); MFR anterior chain (not posterior); lower level laser; DF mobility training; foot orthosis; KT/IDN/cupping.

Insertional Achilles Tendinopathy

Pain more distal (bone-tendon junction). Associated with Haglund's deformity and retrocalcaneal bursitis. Older recreational athletes; obese; poorer prognosis than noninsertional. Presentation: morning ankle stiffness; posterior heel pain; swelling that increases with activity (pain continues with activity — unlike noninsertional); TTP at bone-tendon interface; limited DF ROM.

Achilles Tendon Rupture

MOI: pushing off forefoot while extending knee; sudden aggressive DF with full WB; landing on PF foot. Findings: sudden calf pain; audible snap; swelling of calf; palpable defect ("hatchet strike"); positive Thompson squeeze test (squeeze calf in prone — no PF = positive = complete rupture). Males:females = 3:1. Surgery (open repair) = most common.

Haglund's Deformity & Retrocalcaneal Bursitis

Haglund's ("pump bump"): bony prominence of posterior superior calcaneal tuberosity (2–3 cm). Risk: poorly fitted shoes, cavus foot, genetic predisposition, decreased gastroc length. Conservative: soft heel counter, increase shoe size, Achilles stretch and strengthening, joint mobs for DF. Retrocalcaneal bursitis: inflamed bursa anterior to Achilles tendon + superior to insertion. Treat: open-backed shoes, heel lifts, MFR to posterior chain (gastroc/soleus, plantar fascia, hamstrings, ITB), improve DF ROM.

Additional Ankle/Foot Pathologies — Impingement, Tibialis Posterior, Sever's

Anterior Ankle Impingement

Mechanical obstruction from osteophytes — secondary to talocrural hypomobility (decreased posterior talar glide). Gymnasts, soccer players, long-distance runners, ballet dancers; jobs with repeated squatting/lunging. Presentation: pinching in anterior talocrural joint line with squatting, lunging, descending stairs; pain aggravated by forced DF; ankle stiffness/blocking with DF; limited DF; +anterior impingement test (lunge keeping heel on floor = pain). Conservative: posterior talocrural joint mobs; MWM into DF; ankle distractions; MFR posterior compartment; lots of downward dogs!; strengthen dorsiflexors. Surgery: partial synovectomy; excision of osteophytes.

Tibialis Posterior Tendinopathy — 4 Stages

Progressive loss of medial longitudinal arch height (acquired flatfoot). Stage 1: medial ankle/foot ache worse with WB; tibialis posterior weakness; mild swelling; pain with resisted inversion. Stage 2: medial + lateral hindfoot pain; calcaneal valgus and forefoot abduction (toe-out, more in WB); tibialis posterior elongation; reduced inversion. Stage 3: uncorrectable rigidity of rearfoot + forefoot; OA in subtalar joint and transverse tarsal joints. Stage 4: complete rupture; navicular subluxed inferiorly; unable to produce toe-off in terminal stance. Observe WB foot posture — flat foot or pronated is common. Cause: poor eccentric control of posterior tibialis.

Sever's Disease — Calcaneal Apophysitis

Traction apophysitis at Achilles insertion on calcaneus. Self-limiting disease. As bone grows, gastroc has trouble keeping up → increased traction on apophysis. Boys 8–13, often bilateral. Growth spurt athletes. MOI: overuse with repetitive gastroc loading + growth spurt complications. Presentation: intermittent or continuous heel pain; pain with WB; increased pain with first steps in morning that does NOT get better after warming up (distinguishes from plantar fasciitis). Treatment: gastroc-soleus stretching; heel cups/wedges (decrease Achilles tension); avoid barefoot walking short-term; wear cushioned shoes; NSAID.

Post-op ankle rehab depends heavily on what was repaired. Achilles repair: avoid passive DF beyond 0° for first 4 weeks (can overstretch the repair — difficult to regain PF strength). Lateral ligament reconstruction: avoid passive inversion stress to the repair. ORIF: immobilization until bony healing confirmed, then aggressive ROM and strengthening.

Achilles Tendon Repair

Surgical Procedure

Open repair most common. Longitudinal incision proximal to heel. Evacuate wound, re-approximate tendon, suture together. Males:females 3:1 for Achilles rupture.

WB & ROM Progression
  • 0–6 weeks: NWB (splint/cast/CAM boot)
  • Week 4–6: WBAT in CAM boot + heel wedges (remove one wedge per week)
  • Week 8+: WBAT in CAM boot without wedges → WBAT in tennis shoe
  • ROM first 4–8 wks: AVOID passive DF beyond 0°
  • Resisted AROM into PF = allowed early
Key Complication: PF Strength Deficit

Can be hard to regain. Over-stretching the repair → inability to achieve full strength or power. This is why passive DF beyond neutral is avoided in early phases — healing tissue must be protected from excessive tensile loads. Stay on top of wound inspection. RSD/CRPS or chronic pain are possible complications. Return to work/sport: Sedentary work 2–6 weeks; Manual labor/sport 16+ weeks.

Lateral Ligament Reconstruction (Broström / Broström-Gould)

Procedure Types

Broström: direct repair of ATFL and CFL with sutures. Broström-Gould: repair ATFL + CFL + reinforce CFL with extensor retinaculum. Graft: hamstring graft most common for ligamentous replacement when direct repair not possible. Population: younger females; indoor/court sports; Grade III ankle sprain with radiographic instability; failed conservative treatment; symptomatic functional instability.

Rehabilitation

Typically seen 4–6 weeks post-op. WB: Initially NWB within first 6 weeks (surgeon-dependent); splint → cast → CAM boot; progress to WBAT in CAM boot, NWB outside of boot. ROM: AVOID PASSIVE INVERSION STRESS to the repair. Late rehab goals: train proprioception in multiple planes; remember the patient's MOI — need to prepare ankle for the forces that caused injury. Return to sedentary work: 2–6 weeks. Return to manual labor/sport: 3–6 months.

Ankle ORIF — Danis Weber Classification & Rehab

Danis Weber Classification

Type A: fracture below syndesmosis — most stable. Type B: fracture at the level of the syndesmosis — partial or total syndesmotic rupture — most common. Type C: fracture above the syndesmosis — typically with full syndesmotic disruption — least stable. Surgical procedure: fixate bone with hardware (screws, plates, nails); potential ligament reconstruction. Appropriate for anyone — no age restriction.

Rehab Framework

Immobilization via splint, cast, or CAM boot. WB status depends on fracture type, concomitant injuries, and comorbidities. With isolated ORIF, typically no ROM limitations — but ankle is typically stiff and swollen. Progress: PROM → AAROM → AROM → closed chain strengthening. Gait training. MFR to improve gastroc/soleus extensibility (critical following immobilization). Posterior talocrural glides to increase DF. MWM into DF. Restore subtalar joint mobility (calcaneal inversion/eversion; supination/pronation). Complications: DVT, infection, hardware failure, RSD/CRPS, chronic pain.

What is the hip capsular pattern, and what is the OA Clinical Prediction Rule?
tap
Capsular pattern: IR > Flexion > Extension (IR is most limited). Most limited = IR. OA CPR — 3 of 5: (1) squatting aggravates; (2) active hip flexion causes lateral hip pain; (3) scour test with adduction causes lateral hip/groin pain (±catching); (4) active hip extension causes pain; (5) passive IR <25°. These three things drive OA treatment: mobilization with ventral+lateral+caudal force at femur, long-axis traction, and soft tissue (hypertonic adductors, weak abductors).
What are the THA posterior approach precautions, and why?
tap
Posterior approach cuts: capsule and short external rotators (released to gain access). Protect sciatic nerve and superior gluteal nerve/artery. Precautions ×3 months: No hip flexion >90°; no hip adduction past midline; no IR of operated hip. Reason: these combined motions recreate the position that would dislocate the hip posteriorly — through the cut posterior capsule/ER tendons before healing is complete. Careful repair of posterior capsule and ER tendons significantly reduces dislocation risk. Anterior approach spares short ER muscles — different precautions (no posterior dislocation risk).
What are the hip arthroscopy post-op ROM and WB restrictions by procedure?
tap
All procedures: Flexion restricted to 0–90°. Labral repair: TTWB ×3–4 weeks; ER <30°; ABD <30°. Microfracture: NWB ×0–6 weeks; ER <30°; ABD <30°. Osteoplasty: PWB ×0–4 weeks; ER <30°; ABD <30°. Capsular repair (most restrictive): TTWB ×3–4 weeks; ER = 0°; Extension = 0°. Critical: Avoid long lever arm exercises early (SLR, prone planks) — can spike inflammation via hip flexor overactivation. Hip flexor tendinopathy is the most common post-arthroscopy complication.
SCFE vs. LCPD — demographics, presentation, and clinical distinguishing features?
tap
SCFE (Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis): Adolescents 11–14, more common in boys, overweight (80–100th percentile), diabetes, cancer treatment. Femoral head slips posteriorly off femoral neck. Presentation: hip/medial thigh/knee pain; tends to walk with ER foot; pain with IR relieved with ER; positive log roll and SL raise against resistance; antalgic gait. Treatment: internal fixation ASAP to prevent AVN. LCPD (Legg-Calvé-Perthes): Boys 4–10, unilateral. Blood supply disrupted → AVN → femoral head flattens. Presentation: painless limp (comes and goes); hip stiffness (flex/IR/abd); constant thigh/groin pain; limb length discrepancy; upper thigh atrophy. Prognosis: good if <6 yo; high deformity/arthritis risk if >6 yo.
What structures are involved in the "unhappy triad" and what MOI causes it?
tap
The unhappy triad (O'Donoghue's triad) = ACL + MCL + medial meniscus. MOI: valgus force with rotation at the knee — the same mechanism that damages all three structures simultaneously. This is why higher-grade MCL sprains have common concomitant damage to ACL and medial meniscus. The MCL has stronger attachment to the joint capsule (which commonly gets involved) and a deep band that blends with the medial meniscus — explaining the meniscal involvement. Important: the MCL has good blood supply and can sometimes be left untreated during surgery because it heals well on its own.
Lachman's vs. Anterior Drawer for ACL — which is more accurate and why?
tap
Lachman's (30° knee flexion): SN:.85 / SP:.94. More accurate. At 30° flexion, the posterolateral ACL bundle is taut — maximum tension for testing anterior translation. Hamstrings are minimally activated at 30°, so they don't splint the joint and hide the laxity. Anterior Drawer (90° flexion): Acute: SN:.49 SP:.58; Chronic: SN:.92 SP:.91. Acute injuries: hamstrings and posterior capsule splint the tibia at 90° — masking laxity. The PL bundle is lax at 90°. Also: must exclude PCL injury first — posterior tibial sag at 90° gives a false-positive anterior drawer. Lachman's is THE test to perform for suspected ACL injury.
What is AMI and why is gaining full knee extension within 2 weeks so critical post-op?
tap
AMI (Arthrogenic Muscle Inhibition): ongoing inhibition of musculature surrounding a joint as a protective mechanism. Even 20–30 mL of saline (about 1 oz) in the knee produces quadriceps activation failure. Cycle: injury → AMI → weakness → atrophy → immobilization → perpetuates AMI. Why early extension matters: Post-op position of comfort is 10–20° knee flexion. If maintained, the patient stiffens there: hamstrings + posterior capsule tighten; anterior interval becomes fibrotic (Hoffa's syndrome); decreased quad strength → decreased terminal knee extension → increased fall risk. Extension loss of 3–5° vs. contralateral = increased OA risk. >5° loss = more dramatic OA. Normal knee extension = +5° hyperextension — lacking 5° from neutral = 10° total loss.
What differentiates noninsertional from insertional Achilles tendinopathy clinically?
tap
Noninsertional (most common): 4–6 cm proximal to insertion — relatively avascular zone. Demographics: 30–50 y/o; active long-distance runners; better prognosis. Stiffness and pain at start of activity that IMPROVES as exercise continues (tendinosis pattern). TTP at mid-substance Achilles. Pain eases after warm-up. Decreased DF (20° with knee extended = gastroc; 30° with knee flexed = soleus). Insertional: Pain at bone-tendon junction (distal). Associated with Haglund's deformity and retrocalcaneal bursitis. Older, less active recreational athletes; obese; poorer prognosis. Swelling increases with activity AND pain continues with activity (doesn't ease with warm-up). Treatment for both: eccentric PF strengthening = #1 (3×15, bodyweight first, 180 reps/day for 12 weeks per Dutton).
What is the Ottawa Ankle Rule and what are the X-ray indications?
tap
Ottawa Ankle Rules: Clinical decision tool to determine if X-ray is needed following ankle trauma. Only ONE criterion needs to be present. X-ray indicated with bony tenderness at: distal 6 cm of posterior edge of fibula or tip of lateral malleolus; distal 6 cm of posterior edge of tibia or tip of medial malleolus; base of 5th metatarsal; navicular. Most common trigger: inability to bear weight immediately after injury AND for 4 steps at initial evaluation. Sensitivity of Ottawa Ankle Rules: 77%. Compare to Bernese Ankle Rules (sensitivity 55%) — OAR is more sensitive (better rule-out). Use OAR in inversion/ankle trauma; use clinical judgment for fracture suspicion even with negative rules if concerned.
What are the ACL graft weakness timeline and the criteria for return to sport?
tap
Ligamentization: After surgery, graft undergoes revascularization + maturation. GRAFT IS WEAKEST AT 12 WEEKS. Tensile properties increase with time — this is why time-based progressions are replaced by criteria-based progressions. Functional RTS criteria (Phase 4): Plyometric power in multiple planes; dynamic balance; multiplanar agility; speed; CV endurance. SL hop tests: 80–85% of contralateral LE = normal. Tests: single hop for distance, triple hop for distance, timed 6m hop, crossover triple hop. Re-injury stats: 15% re-tear if RTP within 7 months; athletes <20 = 6× re-injury risk; cutting/pivoting sports = 3.9× re-injury; 64% returned to prior level at 5 years.
What is the FADIR test, what does it indicate, and what's its diagnostic accuracy?
tap
FADIR (Flexion, ADduction, Internal Rotation): Patient supine; PT passively flexes hip to 90°, adducts, and internally rotates. Positive: pain reproduced in the groin. Indicates: labral tear or femoroacetabular impingement (FAI). Diagnostic accuracy: SN:.99 / SP:.25. Interpretation: very high sensitivity (excellent rule-OUT test — if negative, labral/FAI very unlikely), but very low specificity (positive result alone is not diagnostic — many people without labral tears will be positive). Must combine with other tests (FABER, scour, Fitzgerald anterior/posterior) and clinical history. In FAI: also expect limited IR <20° at 90° flexion and possible C-sign (patient cups hand over lateral hip/waist area to describe pain location).
What are the McMurray test technique differences for medial vs. lateral meniscus?
tap
McMurray's Test: Patient supine with knee fully flexed. PT extends knee while simultaneously rotating tibia. Positive = click accompanied by pain. Medial meniscus testing: ER tibia (external rotation of foot) while extending knee — loads and stresses the medial meniscus. Lateral meniscus testing: IR tibia (internal rotation) while extending knee — loads the lateral meniscus. Mnemonic: turn tibia lateral = test medial; turn tibia medial = test lateral. Accuracy: Medial: SN:.50 SP:.77; Lateral: SN:.21 SP:.94. Lateral meniscus testing has very high specificity — if positive, likely lateral meniscal tear. If patient has extreme flexion pain AND end-range extension pain AND popping/clicking/catching — add Bounce Home Test and Apley's to the meniscal cluster.
What is the TKA post-op stiffness definition, and what is the escalating treatment?
tap
Post-op TKA stiffness defined as: Flexion <75° AND 15° lack of extension (note: both must be present). Treatment escalation: First line = intensive PT (in clinic 5 days/week for first 2 weeks post-op; ROM is the early emphasis). If PT fails = Manipulation Under Anesthesia (MUA) — surgeon manually breaks the scar tissue under anesthesia, then immediate aggressive ROM PT follows. Prevention through monitoring: 2-week goal = approaching 90°; 6-week goal = approaching 120°. Address barriers routinely: pain, swelling, and fear avoidance. Pre-op ROM linked to post-op ROM — prehabilitation strengthening and ROM work before surgery directly predicts functional outcomes. Pre-op strength is also linked to post-op function — another reason prehab matters.
What is the Laslett Cluster for SIJ, and at what threshold do you treat?
tap
Laslett Cluster (5 tests): SI Distraction, SI Compression, Thigh Thrust, Gaenslen's, Sacral Spring Test. FABER often added as 6th. Clinical threshold: 3 or more positive tests = highly likely SIJ — treat accordingly. Sensitivity/specificity at each threshold: 1+ = SN:1.00 (rules out perfectly if all negative); 3+ = SN:.94 SP:.78 (best clinical balance); 5+ = SN:.27 SP:.88 (very specific, too insensitive to require all 5). Subjective clues for SIJ: unilateral pain; feeling of being stuck on one side; one-finger pointing to PSIS; postpartum instability. ASLR: if pain changes when you provide innominate stability, high probability of SIJ dysfunction.
What distinguishes plantar fasciitis morning pain from Sever's disease morning pain?
tap
Plantar fasciitis: Extreme pain with FIRST STEPS in the morning that IMPROVES with warming up (cold, contracted fascia loosens as blood flow increases). Pain returns after long periods of sitting. Age: 20–34 runners AND 75+ sedentary. TTP at medial calcaneal tuberosity (plantar surface). Limited ankle DF. Posterior chain tightness. Sever's Disease (calcaneal apophysitis): Boys 8–13, growth spurts. Heel pain with WB. Increased pain first steps in morning that does NOT improve with warming up — activity makes it worse. TTP at posterior heel (calcaneal tuberosity — Achilles insertion). Often bilateral. Gastroc unable to keep up with bone growth — increased traction on apophysis. Self-limiting. Treatment: gastroc-soleus stretching, heel cups, cushioned shoes, avoid barefoot walking.
