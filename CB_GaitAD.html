Gait Training & Assistive Devices

Weight-bearing status, assistive device selection and fitting, gait patterns, safety principles, and clinical decision-making. The foundation of functional mobility in PT practice — from the acute care floor to community reintegration.

Weight-Bearing Status
WB status is determined by the physician or surgeon and must be strictly followed. It drives every decision about assistive device selection and gait pattern. Know the exact definitions — "WBAT" and "PWB" are not the same thing.
NWB — Non-Weight Bearing

0% weight through involved extremity.

Foot must not contact the ground at all. Patient must be capable of full weight-bearing through upper extremities. Requires most stable devices — crutches or walker.

Required UE Strength

NWB in LE = FWB in both upper extremities (BUE). Patient must be screened for UE strength before issuing crutches. If UE strength insufficient → walker (wider base, distributes load).

TTWB / TDWB — Toe Touch / Touch Down WB

Toes rest on ground for balance only — no weight transmitted through LE.

Foot may rest on floor but the limb is not bearing weight. Serves as proprioceptive grounding. Functionally treated like NWB for device selection. Some sources use TTWB and TDWB interchangeably.

PWB — Partial Weight Bearing

Specific percentage of body weight (typically 20–50%) established by physician.

Unlike WBAT, the amount is determined by the physician, not patient tolerance. Requires patient education on how to gauge partial loading (scale practice, verbal cues). Bathroom scale training is helpful.

WBAT — Weight Bearing As Tolerated

Amount determined by patient tolerance — typically 50–100%.

Patient self-regulates based on pain and comfort. More permissive than PWB. Most patients bear as much as comfortable — device used primarily for support and safety rather than strict WB limitation.

FWB — Full Weight Bearing

No weight-bearing restriction — 100%. Device is used for balance only, not for off-loading. Patient has unrestricted loading through the limb. Focus shifts entirely to safety, balance, and mobility retraining.

Assistance Levels — Documenting What You Provide

I (Independent) · MI (Modified Independent) · S (Supervision) · CGA (Contact Guard Assist) · Min A (Minimal Assist, <25% effort) · Mod A (Moderate, 25–50%) · Max A (Maximal, 50–75%) · ×2 (Two-person assist required). Always document the WB status alongside the assist level.

Assistive Device Types
Devices exist on a spectrum from maximum stability (parallel bars) to minimum support (single cane). Choice depends on WB status, UE strength, balance, cognition, environment, and patient goals. Least restrictive device that does not compromise safety.

Walkers

Standard (Pick-Up) Walker

▼

Advantages: Most stable walker. Maximum BOS. Appropriate for NWB/TTWB when UE strength is insufficient for crutches. No rolling — cannot slip.

Disadvantages: Must be picked up with each step → interrupts reciprocal gait. Requires adequate UE strength to lift. Not suitable for stairs. Slow. Cognitively demanding to learn step-to pattern.

Gait pattern: Step-to only (walker → affected LE → unaffected LE). No step-through with standard walker.

WB status: NWB, TTWB, PWB, WBAT, FWB

Front-Wheeled Walker (2-Wheeled)

▼

Advantages: Maintains forward momentum better than standard walker. Better for patients who cannot lift the walker fully (e.g., Parkinson's festinating gait where external cue of wheels rolling helps). Less cognitively demanding. Disadvantages: Less stable than standard walker — can roll away on inclines. Not appropriate for NWB.

Rollator (4-Wheeled Walker)

▼

Advantages: Allows near-normal reciprocal gait. Folds, has seat for rest and basket for carrying items. Good for endurance-limited patients (e.g., COPD, MS, cardiac). Disadvantages: Least stable walker — rolls freely. Patient must have good cognition and coordination to manage brakes. NOT appropriate for NWB, TTWB, or PWB. Risk of rollator sliding away.

Safety Note

Always teach brakes FIRST. Locking brakes for sit-to-stand, unlocking for ambulation. Patient must be cognitively able to manage brakes safely.

Platform Walker

▼

Forearms rest on padded platform instead of handgrips. Used when patient cannot bear weight through the wrist/hand (e.g., RA with wrist involvement, recent UE fracture, carpal tunnel). Load transfers through the forearm rather than the hand. Requires more UE involvement to stabilize.

Crutches

Axillary Crutches

▼

Advantages: Most versatile crutch. Capable of all WB statuses including NWB. Can be used on stairs. Allows multiple gait patterns. Good for temporary injuries in young, UE-strong patients.

Disadvantages: Requires significant UE strength. Risk of crutch palsy (radial nerve compression) if patient leans on axillary pad — must be educated that weight goes through HANDS, not axilla. Not suitable if UE weakness, poor coordination, or poor cognition.

Critical Teaching Point

Weight goes through the HANDS and wrists — NOT the axilla. Resting weight on the axillary pad compresses the radial nerve → crutch palsy (wrist drop). 2–3 finger widths must always be maintained between the axillary pad and the axilla.

WB Status compatible: NWB, TTWB, PWB, WBAT, FWB — all patterns available.

Lofstrand / Forearm / Canadian Crutches

▼

Advantages: Preferred for long-term crutch use (e.g., paraplegia, post-polio). Forearm cuff allows patient to release hand without dropping crutch. More energy-efficient gait. Allows more reciprocal, normal-appearing gait. Can use hands momentarily while cuff maintains grip.

Disadvantages: Less stable than axillary crutches. Requires high UE strength and coordination. Longer learning curve. Requires good trunk control.

WB Status compatible: NWB, TTWB, PWB, WBAT, FWB

Platform Crutch

▼

Forearm rests on padded platform trough. Used when patient cannot bear weight through wrist/hand (e.g., RA, wrist fracture, UE orthopedic conditions). Load through forearm. Less commonly used than axillary or forearm crutches.

Canes

Standard (Straight) Cane

▼

Advantages: Lightweight, least restrictive, easy to use on stairs and in community. Appropriate for WBAT and FWB patients who need modest balance support. Contralateral hand to affected limb.

WB status: WBAT and FWB only. Not appropriate for NWB, TTWB, or PWB.

Which Hand?

Cane in the contralateral hand to the affected/weaker leg. This is the biomechanically correct position — allows normal trunk mechanics and reduces hip abductor demand on the affected side.

Quad Cane (LBQC / SBQC)

▼

LBQC = Large Base Quad Cane (wider base = more stable).
SBQC = Small Base Quad Cane (smaller base = more mobile).
Advantages: Can stand alone. Greater stability than straight cane. Good for patients who cannot maintain grip or need more lateral stability (e.g., hemiplegia/hemiparesis).
Disadvantages: Heavier, cumbersome on stairs, slower gait. All four feet must contact floor simultaneously for stability — fails on uneven terrain.
WB status: WBAT and FWB.

Hemi-Walker

▼

A walker used with one hand — sized to go on one side of the patient. Used for patients with hemiplegia/hemiparesis who cannot use bilateral devices (e.g., non-functional UE after CVA). More stable than a quad cane. Provides broader base of support than quad cane but less than bilateral walker. Disadvantages: Cannot be used on stairs. Slower. WB status: WBAT, FWB primarily.

Device Selection — Decision Framework
Indications for Assistive Devices

Musculoskeletal

WB restriction (injury/surgery) Disease or deformity Pain Weakness or paralysis Decreased sensation Open wounds on WB surfaces

Neuromuscular / Balance

Poor motor control or coordination Abnormal tone or reflexes Primary balance deficit Secondary balance deficit Fear of falling

Guidelines for Choosing — PSSA

Protection — post-injury/post-surgical WB restrictions drive the choice Support — NWB in LE requires FWB in BUE; assess UE strength first Stability — more stable device for less stable patient Accessibility / Adaptability — least restrictive without compromising safety; consider environment (stairs, terrain, home layout)

Other Factors to Consider

Cognition · Coordination · Endurance/fatigue · UE strength and integrity · Home environment (stairs, doorways, surface types) · Community access · Patient goals and compliance · Anticipated duration of use

Fitting & Measurement
Proper fit is non-negotiable. A poorly fitted device is dangerous — too tall creates shoulder shrug/lateral trunk lean; too short causes forward trunk lean and increased WB through device. Always check fit before initiating gait training.
Universal Reference Points
Greater Trochanter

The primary bony landmark for handpiece height for canes, walkers, and crutch handpieces. With patient standing erect in shoes, handle should be at the level of the greater trochanter.

Ulnar Styloid Process

Alternate landmark — with arms relaxed at sides, handle aligns with ulnar styloid process. Both GT and ulnar styloid result in approximately 20–30° elbow flexion at the handpiece — the target position.

Device-Specific Fitting

Axillary Crutches — Detailed Fitting

▼

Standing Measurement (Primary Method)

Axillary rest: 2–3 finger widths (2") below the floor of the axilla — patient standing upright Handpiece: Level of greater trochanter or ulnar styloid Elbow flexion: 20–30° Crutch tip position: 2"(–4") lateral and (4"–)6" anterior to small toe

Formulas (when patient cannot stand)

Formula 1 — Height Method

Crutch length = 77% of patient's height
Example: 66" × 0.77 = 50.82" → 51" crutches
Or: Height − 16" (66" − 16" = 50")

Formula 2 — ATNR Sitting Method

Arms abducted to shoulder height, one elbow flexed 90°, other fully extended (like an "/" position). Crutch top = olecranon of flexed arm. Crutch tip = tip of middle finger of opposite hand.

Lofstrand / Forearm Crutches

▼

Handpiece: Same as cane — level of GT or ulnar styloid; 20–30° elbow flexion Forearm cuff: 1–1.5" distal to the olecranon process (so cuff sits on proximal forearm, not the elbow itself)

Cane & Walker

▼

Handle height: Greater trochanter OR ulnar styloid process Resulting elbow flexion: 20–25° (slightly less than crutches)
Check: No shoulder elevation; no forward trunk lean; comfortable grip; rubber tip in good condition

Parallel Bars

▼

Width: 2" clearance on each side of the greater trochanters (patient should not be squeezed or leaning out) Height: 20–25° elbow flexion; same GT/ulnar styloid reference Therapist stands inside the bars to guard patient during initial training

General Maintenance Check (Before Every Session)

Inspect rubber tips for wear (replace if smooth). Check all bolts/wing nuts are tightened. Confirm height settings are locked. Look for cracks, bends, or damage to frame. For rollators: test brake function. Never use a device that is damaged or in disrepair.

Pattern Details

3-Point Gait — NWB (Non-Weight Bearing)

▼

Sequence

(1) Both assistive devices advance simultaneously WITH the NWB leg (2) Weight-bearing leg advances — step-to or step-through

Step-To vs. Step-Through

Step-to: WB foot advances to line of devices. Safer, more controlled.
Step-through: WB foot advances beyond line of devices. Faster, longer stride, requires more balance.

Devices & WB Status

Axillary crutches, Lofstrand crutches, or Walker NWB and TTWB only — if ANY weight placed on affected LE, it becomes 3-point PWB pattern NOT for PWB, WBAT, or FWB

3-Point Gait — PWB (Partial Weight Bearing)

▼

Sequence

(1) Assistive device(s) advance simultaneously with the affected LE(2) FWB leg advances — placing the affected leg on the ground as it steps through

Key distinction from NWB 3-point: the affected leg touches down with each step — weight limited to prescribed %.

Crutches, bilateral canes, or walker PWB and TTWB — NOT NWB Step-to vs. step-through applies here too

4-Point Gait

▼

Sequence (alternating)

(1) One AD advances (2) Opposite LE advances (3) Other AD advances (4) Remaining LE advances

Crutches or bilateral canes 3 points of contact on ground at all times → most stable crutch patternNOT for NWB — both LEs are bearing weight TTWB, PWB, WBAT compatible Slowest pattern — high energy, low speed Uses normal trunk rotation (reciprocal)

2-Point Gait (Reciprocal Gait)

▼

Sequence

(1) One AD + contralateral LE advance simultaneously (2) Other AD + other LE advance simultaneously

Mimics normal reciprocal walking with trunk rotation. Most energy-efficient crutch pattern after swing-through.

Crutches or bilateral canes NOT for NWB or TTWB PWB and WBAT only Uses normal trunk rotation — most natural pattern Requires coordination to move contralateral limbs simultaneously

Swing-To & Swing-Through

▼

Swing-To

(1) Both crutches advance simultaneously (2) Both LEs swing forward to the line of the crutches

Safer than swing-through. Requires less trunk control and balance. Less efficient.

Swing-Through

(1) Both crutches advance simultaneously (2) Both LEs swing beyond the crutch line

Fastest gait pattern available. Requires excellent UE strength, trunk control, and balance. Used by patients with SCI/complete paraplegia who are strong ambulators with KAFOs.

Stairs & Curbs
Ascending Stairs — "Good Leads Up"

"The good go to heaven." Unaffected (good) leg leads going UP.

(1) Hold rail with one hand (if available), crutch(es) in other (2)Unaffected LE steps up first(3) Crutches + affected LE follow up to same step

Descending Stairs — "Bad Leads Down"

"Bad guys go to hell." Affected (bad) leg leads going DOWN.

(1) Hold rail with one hand (if available), crutch(es) in other (2)Crutches + affected LE step down first(3) Unaffected LE follows down

Therapist Position on Stairs

Ascending: Therapist is BEHIND and slightly to the side (below the patient) — to catch a fall backward. Descending: Therapist is IN FRONT and slightly to the side (below the patient) — to catch a forward fall. Always maintain grip on gait belt.

Mnemonic


Ascending: Unaffected (good) leg leads up first. Crutches + affected follow.  |  Descending: Crutches + affected (bad) leg lead down first. Unaffected follows.

Safety, Guarding & Clinical Technique

Guarding Technique
Level Surface Guarding

Position: behind and slightly to one side of patient Preferred side: affected/weaker side (where fall is most likely) Maintain safe grip on gait belt at all times — underhand grip preferred Never grasp the patient's arm or clothing — this can cause injury to either party and cannot support a fall safely Anticipate that patient could lose balance at ANY time

If Patient Begins to Fall

Human instinct is to reach/grab — resist this. You will injure yourself or the patient. Use gait belt to control descent Two types of fall: collapsing fall (straight down) or angular fall (to one side) Options: re-orient pt to upright by lifting belt; lower pt to your thigh; lower pt to floor (controlled) Know your options BEFORE starting — be prepared

Pre-Gait Activities — Parallel Bars
Always begin in parallel bars before progressing to assistive devices. Bars allow maximum safety while the patient learns the basics of upright activity.
Foundational Activities

Sit ↔ Stand transitions Static balance (double then single limb) Weight shifting side-to-side and forward-backward Turning in place

Preparatory Activities

Marching in place "Push-up" on bars (unweighting for relief/strengthening) Maintaining appropriate WB status throughout Practice gait pattern in bars BEFORE attempting with device

Guarding in Parallel Bars

Therapist stands inside the bars Maintain grip on gait belt Stay close but allow patient to bear own weight Do not hold patient up — let them learn to bear their own weight

When to Change or Discontinue a Device
Progress to Less Restrictive Device When...

WB status advances (physician order) Patient demonstrates safe, consistent technique Strength and balance have improved Patient can manage community-level challenges (uneven terrain, curbs, crowds) Patient requests less restrictive device and is safe to use it

Change or Terminate When...

Patient condition changes (new injury, acute illness) WB status becomes more restrictive Patient cannot safely manage current device Device is damaged, worn, or no longer fits properly Vital signs become unstable during activity Patient fatigues beyond safe capacity

Clinical Case Scenarios
10 clinical cases requiring assistive device selection, gait pattern choice, and clinical reasoning. Each case walks through the decision framework: WB status → UE integrity → balance/cognition → environment → device → gait pattern → teaching cues.
Clinical Reasoning Framework

For every case: (1) WB status, (2) UE strength and integrity, (3) Balance and coordination, (4) Cognition and compliance, (5) Home environment (stairs, surfaces, layout), (6) Social support, (7) Duration of need, (8) Specific pathology precautions.

Case 1 — Post-ACL Reconstruction, 28 y/o Male

▼
Scenario

28-year-old male, 2 days post-ACLR (BTB autograft). WBAT per surgeon. Brace locked in full extension. Lives alone in a 2nd-floor apartment (1 flight of stairs, no elevator). Works as a software engineer (sedentary). Strong UEs, no comorbidities.

Device Selection & Rationale

Bilateral axillary crutches. WBAT allows progressive loading, but early post-op requires offloading. Brace locked in extension = stiff-knee gait requiring swing-through clearance — crutches accommodate this better than a walker. Lives alone on 2nd floor = crutches are the ONLY option for stair access (walker cannot be used on stairs). Young, strong UEs, no balance deficits = appropriate candidate for crutches. Pre-op crutch training session recommended since he lives alone.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

3-point modified pattern initially (both crutches + involved leg advance together, then uninvolved steps through). Progress to 2-point reciprocal as WB tolerance increases. Stairs: “up with the good, down with the bad” — uninvolved leg leads ascending, crutches + involved leg lead descending. Key cues: “Swing the leg like a pendulum — don’t try to bend the knee.” Train sit-to-stand, toilet transfer, car transfer. Emergency plan: phone accessible from floor level if he falls. Ice/elevation protocol for home.

Case 2 — Bilateral TKA, 70 y/o Female with Hand Arthritis

▼
Scenario

70-year-old female, POD 1 after bilateral TKA. WBAT per surgeon. Significant RA in both hands and wrists (grip strength 8 lbs bilaterally). Chronic low back pain. Discharging to inpatient rehab in 2 days. Husband at home, single-story ranch house.

Device Selection & Rationale

Platform walker (forearm platforms). Standard walker requires wrist extension and grip — painful/impossible with RA hands. Platform walker shifts load to forearms, bypassing the wrist. Bilateral TKA = both legs involved, bilateral device essential. Crutches contraindicated: bilateral involvement + hand arthritis + age + balance risk. At rehab, progress to 4-wheeled rollator when more stable (allows near-normal gait, seat for rest breaks relevant to LBP, upright posture vs forward lean of standard walker).

Gait Pattern & Teaching

Step-to pattern with platform walker initially. Progress to rollator with reciprocal gait as strength and confidence improve. Address LBP by ensuring upright posture — standard walker encourages forward lean which worsens lumbar symptoms. Bilateral symmetry of loading important. Teach energy conservation, knee swelling management (ice, elevation, compression), and seating modifications at rehab.

Case 3 — Traumatic AKA + Casted UE, 52 y/o Male

▼
Scenario

52-year-old male, 10 days post-traumatic right above-knee amputation (industrial accident) + right radius/ulna ORIF with long arm cast. NWB right LE (no prosthetic yet). Right UE non-functional for WB. Married, wife can assist. Mobile home with 3 entry steps.

Device Selection & Rationale

This is a high-complexity case. Right AKA = NWB right LE. Right UE casted = cannot grip or bear weight through right hand. This eliminates bilateral axillary crutches (require bilateral UE function). Options: (1)
Platform attachment on right side of walker (forearm bears weight through cast), (2)
Wheelchair as primary mobility with limited guarded ambulation using platform crutch/walker for short distances. Wheelchair is the safest primary device in the acute phase. Pre-prosthetic training is the real priority: residual limb care, wrapping/shaping, hip strengthening, transfers.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

Wheelchair for primary mobility now. Limited ambulation only under direct PT supervision with platform walker adaptation. Hopping on left leg for very short transfers (bed→chair) with maximum assist. Teach: wheelchair management including 3-step entry to home (wife assist + ramp evaluation), residual limb positioning (avoid hip flexion contracture — prone lying), wrapping technique. Emotional support critical — traumatic amputation at 52 is devastating. Discuss prosthetic timeline with surgeon.

Case 4 — Femoral Metastasis, 43 y/o Female, 30% PWB

▼
Scenario

43-year-old female with left femoral metastasis (breast cancer). Surgeon prescribes max 30% WB left LE. Tumor removal scheduled in 3 days. Married, 2-story colonial home, bedroom on 2nd floor. Adequate UE strength. Anxious about the diagnosis.

Device Selection & Rationale

Bilateral axillary crutches for strict 30% PWB. Crutches over walker because: 2-story home requires stair access, crutches allow stairs. Walker cannot be used on stairs.
Scale training is ESSENTIAL — she must accurately gauge 30% body weight. Example: if she weighs 140 lbs, 30% = 42 lbs. Practice on bathroom scale until she can consistently reproduce this force.
Pathological fracture risk is the overriding concern — excessive loading could fracture the femur through the tumor. Non-compliance = catastrophic.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

3-point PWB pattern. 30% is very close to TTWB functionally. Cue: “Set the foot down gently, like stepping on thin ice — let the crutches carry you.” Scale practice before every session until she can reproduce 30% reliably. Discuss: temporary 1st-floor sleeping arrangement until post-op (avoid stairs with pathological fracture risk if possible). Fall prevention is absolute priority — falling = almost certain fracture. Husband educated on guarding technique. Address anxiety — pain neuroscience education, reinforce that careful WB is safe.

Case 5 — Pediatric Legg-Calvé-Perthes, 11 y/o Male

▼
Scenario

11-year-old male diagnosed with right Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease. Surgeon prescribes NWB × 4 weeks, then WBAT × 4 weeks. Lives with both parents and older sister. Multi-level home, bedroom on 2nd floor. Attends middle school.

Device Selection & Rationale

Pediatric axillary crutches (Phase 1: NWB). Children learn quickly at this age. Phase 2 (WBAT): progress to bilateral forearm crutches or single cane, then wean. School mobility is a key concern — crowded hallways, cafeteria, recess. Crutches are more maneuverable than a walker in school corridors. Request school letter for elevator access and modified PE.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

Phase 1 (NWB): 3-point NWB with crutches. Step-through for speed in school hallways. Cue: “Your right foot is invisible — it can’t touch the ground.” Phase 2 (WBAT): Transition to 3-point PWB → 2-point reciprocal → cane → no device. Train on stairs (bedroom on 2nd floor). Critical: educate parents that NWB means NO weight — an 11-year-old will try to keep up with peers. Compliance education for the child using age-appropriate language.

Case 6 — Spastic Diplegia CP, 4 y/o Female

▼
Scenario

4-year-old female with spastic diplegia CP (GMFCS Level III — walks with AD, limited community mobility). Bilateral LE spasticity (hamstrings, hip adductors, gastroc-soleus). Bilateral AFOs. Crouched gait pattern. Starting preschool. Parents want her walking at school.

Device Selection & Rationale

Posterior walker (reverse walker — behind the child). NOT a standard (anterior) walker. A standard walker encourages forward trunk lean and increased hip flexion — which worsens the already-flexed, crouched posture of spastic diplegia. A posterior walker forces the child to stand more upright, facilitates hip extension, promotes a more normalized gait pattern. Wheels with directional locks allow forward movement only. Adjust height so child stands tall, not leaning.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

Reciprocal gait pattern with posterior walker. Focus on: upright trunk, hip extension during terminal stance, heel contact (AFOs assist). Parents: practice on level surfaces first, then grass/playground. Preschool: ensure walker fits through classroom doorways, accessible bathroom. Wheelchair or adaptive stroller for community distances (mall, zoo). Discuss GMFCS level with family — realistic expectations. Long-term: may progress to forearm crutches as trunk control improves.

Case 7 — CVA with Right Hemiparesis, 76 y/o Male

▼
Scenario

76-year-old male, 5 days post left MCA stroke with right hemiparesis (UE flaccid, LE emerging movement — can activate hip flexors and quads against gravity). FIM mobility score: 3 (moderate assist). Right ankle dorsiflexor weakness (foot drop). Lives with wife in a single-story home with 2 entry steps. Retired teacher.

Device Selection & Rationale

Hemi-walker or quad cane (large base) in LEFT hand (unaffected side). Right UE is flaccid = cannot grip a device. Must use unaffected left hand. Hemi-walker provides wider BOS than quad cane but still allows one-handed operation. Standard walker requires bilateral grip. Crutches require bilateral UE function. As LE recovery progresses: hemi-walker → quad cane → single-point cane → no device. Consider AFO for right foot drop to clear toes during swing phase.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

Modified 2-point pattern: hemi-walker advances with right (affected) LE → left (unaffected) LE steps through. Cue: “Move the walker and your right leg together, then step through with your left.” Train: sit-to-stand from hemiparetic side (use left hand on armrest, weight shift forward, stand). Stair strategy: 2 entry steps with railing on left + hemi-walker. Wife educated on guarding and emergency assist. AFO consult for foot drop. Monitor for neglect (left MCA stroke can cause right-sided neglect).

Case 8 — Newly Diagnosed MS, 36 y/o Female

▼
Scenario

36-year-old female, newly diagnosed relapsing-remitting MS. Current exacerbation: bilateral LE fatigue and weakness (hip flexors 4/5, ankle DF 3+/5), balance deficits, walks 200 feet before needing to rest. No UE involvement. EDSS 4.0. Works as a high school teacher (on her feet all day). Single, lives alone in a ground-floor apartment.

Device Selection & Rationale

4-wheeled rollator with seat and basket. Rollator allows near-normal reciprocal gait (more energy-efficient than pick-up walker for a patient whose primary limitation is fatigue). The built-in seat is critical — allows rest without searching for a chair (essential for a teacher who walks hallways all day). Basket allows hands-free carrying of books/materials. Standard walker would impose step-to pattern = more energy-costly = faster fatigue = worse. Cane alone provides insufficient support for bilateral LE weakness.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

Reciprocal gait with rollator. Teach: brake management (squeeze to stop, lock to sit), energy conservation strategies (“plan your route, sit before you need to”), pacing (alternate teaching periods with seated desk work). Bilateral AFOs may be needed as DF weakness progresses. Discuss heat sensitivity (Uhthoff’s phenomenon) — cooling vest for school days. Key: MS is progressive. Choose a device with room to grow. The rollator can serve her for years as disability fluctuates. Avoid over-prescribing (no wheelchair yet at EDSS 4.0) but have the conversation early.

Case 9 — Post-Polio with Bilateral KAFOs, 15 y/o Male

▼
Scenario

15-year-old male with post-polio syndrome (childhood polio, bilateral LE flaccid paralysis below L2). Recently fitted with bilateral locked KAFOs. Strong UEs (rock climbing pre-diagnosis). Wants to walk at school graduation. Lives in a single-story accessible home. Wheelchair is primary mobility.

Device Selection & Rationale

Bilateral forearm (Lofstrand) crutches. Bilateral KAFOs lock the knees and ankles = must swing legs through using UE strength and trunk momentum. Axillary crutches would work but forearm crutches allow more natural arm positioning, are lighter, and are more functional for a teenager. Strong UEs = excellent candidate for crutch gait. Wheelchair remains primary mobility for distances; crutch ambulation is supplemental/therapeutic.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

Swing-through gait pattern with bilateral forearm crutches + bilateral KAFOs. Both crutches advance → both legs swing BEYOND the crutch line. Fastest bilateral LE NWB pattern. Case 10 — GBS Recovery, 48 y/o Female

▼
Scenario

48-year-old female, 6 weeks post-onset Guillain-Barré syndrome. Was ventilator-dependent for 2 weeks, now weaning. Currently in inpatient rehab. Bilateral LE weakness (proximal 3/5, distal 2/5 — ascending recovery pattern). Bilateral UE weakness (grip 12 lbs, shoulder flexion 3+/5). Can sit at edge of bed with supervision. Husband and 2 teenage children at home, split-level house with 6 stairs to bedrooms.

Device Selection & Rationale

Progression approach: Currently: tilt table → standing frame for upright tolerance and orthostatic management. Progress to: standard walker (most stable, allows bilateral UE offloading despite weakness) with maximal assist. Then: walker with less assist → rollator → forearm crutches → cane → no device. GBS recovery is distal-to-proximal (ascending) = feet/ankles recover last. Bilateral AFOs likely needed for foot drop during recovery period. Device changes weekly/monthly as strength returns — don’t commit to one device.

Gait Pattern & Teaching

Initial: step-to pattern with standard walker, max assist, bilateral AFOs. As proximal strength improves: step-through → reciprocal. Key: GBS has an excellent prognosis (80% recover to independent ambulation within 6–12 months) but the recovery is SLOW and patients get frustrated. Set weekly goals, not daily. Monitor for autonomic dysfunction (orthostatic hypotension — check vitals with positional changes). Home evaluation before discharge: 6 stairs to bedrooms = temporary ground-floor sleeping arrangement until stair-ready. Fatigue management is critical — GBS recovery requires enormous energy.

Key Mnemonics

What is the difference between TTWB and PWB ?
tap
TTWB (Toe Touch/Touch Down): Toes rest on floor for balance only — no weight transmitted through the LE (~0%). Functionally treated like NWB for device selection. PWB: Specific physician-prescribed percentage of body weight (typically 20–50%). Unlike TTWB, the patient actually loads the limb to that prescribed amount.
Which gait patterns are NOT appropriate for NWB ? Which IS appropriate?
tap
NOT for NWB: 4-point, 2-point (both require LE WB), 3-point PWB. Appropriate for NWB: 3-point NWB (crutches advance with affected LE, then WB leg steps); Swing-to; Swing-through; Step-to (walker). Key: the affected leg must NOT contact the ground bearing weight.
Two formulas for axillary crutch length — what are they ?
tap
Formula 1: Height × 0.77 (77% of height). Example: 66" × 0.77 = ~51" crutches. Formula 2: Height − 16". Example: 66" − 16" = 50" crutches. Both result in approximately the same length. Use these when patient cannot stand for direct measurement.
Axillary crutch fit — 5 key measurements/positions.
tap
(1) Axillary pad: 2–3 finger widths below axilla. (2) Handpiece: level of GT or ulnar styloid. (3) Elbow flexion: 20–30°. (4) Crutch tips: 2" lateral, 6" anterior to small toe. (5) Weight through HANDS — NOT axilla. Pad is for positioning, not weight-bearing.
Stability spectrum — order from most stable to most mobile (all 9).
tap
Parallel bars → Standard Walker → Bilateral Axillary Crutches → Bilateral Forearm Crutches → Bilateral Canes → Hemi-Walker → Quad Cane → Single Cane → No Device. More stable = less mobile. Choose the least restrictive device that does not compromise safety.
4-point gait pattern — sequence and when is it used ?
tap
Alternating: R crutch → L leg → L crutch → R leg (or vice versa). Always 3 points of contact = most stable crutch pattern. Uses normal trunk rotation. NOT for NWB (both legs bear weight). Appropriate for TTWB, PWB, WBAT with bilateral involvement or poor balance. Slowest pattern.
Where do you hold the cane and why ?
tap
Contralateral hand to the affected/weaker leg. Biomechanical rationale: cane on opposite side allows normal trunk rotation, reduces demand on hip abductors of the affected side (reduces Trendelenburg), and allows cane to advance with affected leg (normal reciprocal pattern). Ipsilateral cane placement disrupts trunk mechanics and increases hip load.
Swing-to vs. swing-through — difference and who uses them?
tap
Swing-to: Both crutches advance → both LEs swing TO the line of crutches. Safer, slower, less balance needed. Swing-through: Both crutches advance → both LEs swing BEYOND the crutch line. Fastest gait pattern. Requires excellent UE strength, trunk control, balance. Used by SCI/paraplegic patients with KAFOs.
Stairs mnemonic — which leg leads going up vs. going down?
tap
"Up with the Good, Down with the Bad" (or: Good go to heaven, Bad go to hell). Ascending: Unaffected (good) leg steps up first, then crutches + affected follow. Descending: Crutches + affected (bad) leg step down first, then unaffected follows. Therapist: behind and below when ascending, in front and below when descending.
Why must you NEVER grab a patient's arm or clothing if they fall ?
tap
Grabbing an arm or clothing cannot safely support body weight and will likely injure either the patient (joint dislocation, skin tear, fracture from sudden force) or yourself (back injury, UE injury from sudden load). Proper technique: maintain grip on gait belt. Use belt to control descent — lower to your thigh then to the floor in a controlled manner.
Why would you choose a rollator over a standard walker for an MS patient ?
tap
Rollator (4-wheeled) allows near-normal reciprocal gait without the disrupted step-to pattern of a pick-up walker — better for patients who fatigue easily (MS) since normal gait is more energy-efficient. The built-in seat allows frequent rest without searching for a chair — critical for MS fatigue management. The basket also allows hands-free carrying. However, patient must have adequate cognition and coordination to manage brakes safely.
Why is a posterior walker preferred over an anterior walker for a child with spastic diplegia CP ?
tap
A posterior (behind the child) walker promotes upright trunk posture and facilitates a more normal gait pattern. An anterior (standard) walker encourages forward trunk lean and increased hip flexion, which worsens the already-flexed posture of spastic diplegia. Posterior walker placement forces the child to stand more upright and push the pelvis forward — facilitating better hip extension and a more functional gait pattern.
List the 3 indications categories for assistive devices?
tap
Musculoskeletal: WB restriction, injury/surgery, disease, deformity, pain, weakness/paralysis, decreased sensation, open wounds on WB surfaces. Neuromuscular: Poor motor control/coordination, abnormal tone/reflexes. Balance: Primary balance deficit, secondary balance deficit, fear of falling.
NWB in one LE — what does this require of the upper extremities ?
tap
FWB in both upper extremities (BUE). The entire body weight that would normally go through the NWB leg must be redistributed through the UEs via the assistive device. Screen UE strength, wrist/hand integrity, and shoulder function before issuing crutches for NWB. If UE strength insufficient → walker distributes load differently, may still be appropriate. UE pathology = significant limiting factor for NWB ambulation.
What is the purpose of an assistive device, biomechanically ?
tap
To increase the base of support (BOS) and provide additional means of support for a larger area, allowing the center of gravity (CoG) to move without loss of balance. Additionally: off-load weight from an injured/healing extremity; provide proprioceptive input; reduce energy cost of abnormal gait; increase confidence and reduce fear of falling.
