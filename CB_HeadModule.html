Skull Osteology
The skull consists of 22 bones: 8 neurocranium (calvaria + cranial base) and 14 facial skeleton. Know each bone, its parts, and clinical significance.

Facial Muscles & Sensory Innervation
All muscles of facial expression = CN VII. All muscles of mastication = CN V3. All sensory from face = CN V (3 divisions). This is the foundation for Bell’s palsy vs stroke differentiation.

Divides into 5 branches through parotid gland: Temporal, Zygomatic, Buccal, Mandibular, Cervical. Mnemonic: “To Zanzibar By Motor Car.” Injury/Bell’s palsy → ipsilateral facial paralysis.

Peripheral vs Central CN VII Palsy

Peripheral (Bell’s): forehead INVOLVED — entire ipsilateral face paralyzed (all 5 branches). Central (stroke): forehead SPARED — only lower face droops (bilateral cortical representation of forehead). This distinction localizes the lesion without imaging.

Trigeminal Neuralgia

Severe facial pain along sensory distribution of one CN V branch. MC = maxillary (V2) division. “Tic douloureux” — excruciating, electric-shock-like pain triggered by light touch, chewing, or cold wind.

Facial Vasculature

▼
Blood Supply to Face

Primary supply via ECA (external carotid artery): facial artery, maxillary artery, superficial temporal artery. Region above eyes via ICA (internal carotid): ophthalmic artery → supraorbital + supratrochlear arteries. The ECA/ICA anastomosis provides backup blood supply to brain if ICA occluded.

Eyes & Orbits
The orbit is formed by 7 bones with 3 major foramina. The eyeball has 3 layers (outer fibrous, middle vascular, inner neural). Aqueous humor drainage failure = glaucoma.

Orbit — Bones & Foramina

▼
7 Bones Form the Orbit

Frontal (roof), maxillary (floor), zygomatic (lateral), lacrimal (medial), ethmoid (medial), sphenoid (posterior), palatine (floor). Three key openings: Optic canal (CN II + ophthalmic artery), Superior orbital fissure (CN III, IV, VI, V1), Inferior orbital fissure (V2 — maxillary nerve).

Chambers & Glaucoma

Anterior chamber (cornea to iris) + posterior chamber (iris to lens). Aqueous humor: secreted by ciliary processes → posterior chamber → pupil → anterior chamber → drained via canal of Schlemm. Glaucoma: impaired drainage → increased IOP → retinal damage → blindness. Open-angle (chronic, MC) vs closed-angle (acute emergency). Light pathway: cornea → aqueous humor → lens → vitreous body → retina.

Lacrimal Apparatus

▼
Tear Drainage Pathway

Lacrimal glands (superolateral orbit) → tears circulate across cornea → lacrimal lake (medial canthus) → lacrimal punctum → lacrimal canaliculi → lacrimal sac → nasolacrimal duct → inferior meatus of nose (why crying makes nose run).

Weber & Rinne Tests

Weber: tuning fork on midline of head. Conduction loss = louder in blocked ear. Sensorineural = louder in normal ear. Rinne: fork on mastoid → when sound disappears, move to ear. Normal: air > bone conduction. Conduction loss: bone > air (patient can’t hear fork at ear).

BPPV — PT Relevance

Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo = otoconia (calcium crystals) from utricle displaced into semicircular canals. MC cause of vertigo. Posterior SCC most commonly affected. Dix-Hallpike test identifies affected canal. Epley maneuver = excellent evidence-based PT treatment.

The oral cavity, tongue, salivary glands, nasal cavity, and paranasal sinuses. Know tongue innervation (complex!) and sinus drainage patterns.

General sensation anterior 2/3: V3 (lingual nerve). Taste anterior 2/3: CN VII (chorda tympani). General sensation + taste posterior 1/3: CN IX. Motor (all intrinsic + most extrinsic): CN XII. Genioglossus: largest extrinsic muscle. CN XII damage → tongue deviates to WEAK side.

Arches & Tonsils

Palatoglossal arch (anterior) + palatopharyngeal arch (posterior). Palatine tonsils between arches. Waldeyer’s ring: pharyngeal (adenoids) + palatine + lingual tonsils. “Say Ahh” tests CN IX + X. Uvula deviates to STRONG side (CN IX).

Brain & Meninges
SCALP layers, cranial meninges with hemorrhage types, Circle of Willis, and brain overview. The hemorrhage comparison table is the most clinically tested content.

SCALP Layers — 5 Layers

▼
SCALP Mnemonic

S = Skin (thick, hair-bearing, bleeds profusely). C = Close subcutaneous tissue (vessels held open → profuse bleeding). A = Aponeurosis (galea aponeurotica — connects frontal + occipital bellies). L = Loose connective tissue (“danger zone” — infection spreads easily via emissary veins). P = Pericranium (periosteum, adherent to bone).

Circle of Willis & Cerebrovascular Anatomy

▼
ICA Territory

ICA enters via carotid canal → ophthalmic artery (orbit, anastomosis with ECA) → ACA (medial surfaces, LE motor/sensory) + MCA (lateral hemispheres, face + UE, Broca’s, Wernicke’s). MCA = most common stroke territory.

Vertebrobasilar Territory

Vertebral arteries enter via foramen magnum → unite to form basilar artery → PCA (occipital/visual cortex). VBI: 5 D’s (dizziness, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, drop attacks). Must screen before cervical manipulation.

Circle of Willis

Anterior communicating → R/L ACA → R/L ICA → R/L posterior communicating → R/L PCA (from basilar). Berry aneurysms MC at ACA/anterior communicating junction. Venous drainage: superior sagittal sinus → transverse → sigmoid → IJV via jugular foramen.

Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ)
Modified hinge joint with rotation + translation. Articular disc divides into 2 compartments. Lateral pterygoid attachment to disc is key to understanding TMJ dysfunction.

TMJ Joint Structure & Biomechanics

▼
Joint Components

Head of mandibular condyle + mandibular fossa + articular tubercle of temporal bone. Articular disc divides joint into 2 compartments: upper (translation/gliding) and lower (rotation). Disc attached anteriorly to superior head of lateral pterygoid.

Biomechanics

Opening: First 25mm = rotation (lower compartment). Beyond 25mm = translation (upper compartment, condyle + disc glide down eminence). Full opening = 40–50mm (3 finger-widths). Closing: reverse. Protrusion: bilateral lateral pterygoid. Retrusion: posterior temporalis + digastric.

TMJ Pathology

Disc displacement with reduction: click/pop (disc reduces during translation). Without reduction: locked jaw, limited opening <25mm. Capsular pattern = limitation of opening.

Mandible Landmarks

▼
External

Mental protuberance (chin), mental foramen (mental nerve V3), body, angle, ramus, coronoid process (temporalis), condylar process (TMJ head), mandibular notch.

Internal

Lingula (near mandibular foramen — IAN block target), mandibular foramen (V3 entry), mylohyoid line (mylohyoid origin), submandibular fossa.

Review Cards
Tap to reveal answers.
Patient hit in the temporal region (pterion). Lucid interval then rapid decline. What happened?
tap
Epidural hematoma from middle meningeal artery rupture at pterion (thinnest skull point). Biconvex/lens on CT. Doesn’t cross sutures. Emergency craniotomy.
Bell’s palsy vs stroke — how do you differentiate?
tap
Bell’s (peripheral CN VII): forehead INVOLVED — entire ipsilateral face. Stroke (central): forehead SPARED — only lower face droops (bilateral cortical representation of forehead).
Patient can’t close right eye, has drooling from right corner of mouth. CN VII or V?
tap
CN VII (facial) — motor to muscles of facial expression. Orbicularis oculi (eye closure) and orbicularis oris (lip closure) are both CN VII. CN V is sensory to face + motor to mastication only.
Tongue deviates left on protrusion. Which CN is damaged and on which side?
tap
Left CN XII (hypoglossal) is damaged. Tongue deviates to the WEAK side. The functioning right genioglossus pushes the tongue toward the damaged left side.
“Worst headache of my life” — what is it and where does it bleed?
tap
Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) from ruptured berry (saccular) aneurysm at Circle of Willis junction (MC at anterior communicating artery). Blood in subarachnoid space. Star sign on CT. Neurosurgical emergency.
Which foramen transmits CN V3 and why is it special?
tap
Foramen ovale. V3 (mandibular) is the ONLY division of CN V with motor function — supplies muscles of mastication. Lesion → jaw deviates to WEAK side + lower face numbness.
Patient with vertigo, nystagmus on Dix-Hallpike. Diagnosis and treatment?
tap
BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo). Otoconia displaced into posterior SCC (most common). Treat with Epley maneuver — excellent PT evidence. Repositions crystals back to utricle.
Red Flags — Head Region

“Worst headache ever” = SAH. Fixed dilated pupil = uncal herniation (CN III). Uvula deviation = CN IX. Tongue deviation = CN XII. Battle’s sign + periorbital bruising = basilar skull fracture.
