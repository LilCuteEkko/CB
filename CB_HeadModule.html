Muscles of Facial Expression — CN VII (Facial Nerve)

All muscles of facial expression are innervated by CN VII (facial nerve). The nerve divides into 5 branches as it passes through the parotid gland: Temporal, Zygomatic, Buccal, Mandibular, Cervical. Mnemonic: “To Zanzibar By Motor Car.” Clinical: CN VII injury/Bell’s palsy → paralysis of ipsilateral facial muscles.

Muscles of Mastication — CN V3 (Mandibular Branch of Trigeminal)

All muscles of mastication are innervated by CN V3 (mandibular division of trigeminal nerve). These are the only muscles of the head/face NOT innervated by CN VII. Masseter and temporalis are the primary jaw closers.

Extraocular Muscles — CN III, IV, VI

Six muscles control eye movement. Mnemonic: LR6SO4 — all others 3. Lateral rectus = CN VI (abducens). Superior oblique = CN IV (trochlear). All others = CN III (oculomotor). CN III also controls: levator palpebrae superioris (eyelid opening) + pupil constriction.

Tongue Muscle — CN XII

Genioglossus is the primary tongue muscle, innervated by CN XII (hypoglossal). Unilateral damage → tongue deviates toward the injured side. Test: ask patient to protrude tongue and observe for deviation.

Chambers & Aqueous Humor Circulation

Anterior chamber: between cornea and iris/pupil. Posterior chamber: between iris/pupil and lens/ciliary body. Aqueous humor is secreted by ciliary processes into posterior chamber → flows through pupil into anterior chamber → drained via canal of Schlemm (trabecular meshwork) back into venous blood.

Glaucoma: impaired drainage of aqueous humor → increased intraocular pressure → progressive retinal damage → blindness if untreated. Open-angle (chronic, MC) vs. closed-angle (acute emergency — painful, red eye, fixed mid-dilated pupil). Cataracts: opacity of the lens → progressive blurred vision.

Pathway of light: External light → cornea → aqueous humor → lens → vitreous body → retina.

Lacrimal Apparatus — Tear Drainage Pathway

Lacrimal glands (superolateral orbit) produce tears → circulate across cornea → collect in lacrimal lake (medial canthus) → enter through lacrimal punctum (tiny openings on medial eyelids) → drain through lacrimal canaliculi (small tunnels) → into lacrimal sac (medial orbit) → down nasolacrimal duct → empties into inferior meatus of the nose (explains why crying makes your nose run). Lacrimal caruncle: fleshy mound in lacrimal lake at medial eye.

CN 8 Clinical Pearls
•CN 8 (vestibulocochlear) has two divisions: Cochlear (hearing) and Vestibular (balance)
•Sensorineural hearing loss from CN 8 damage = acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma) is most common
•Conduction loss: external or middle ear problem (wax, effusion, ossicle damage)
•Eustachian tube dysfunction: pressure equalization failure → middle ear effusion (common in young children, ear infections)
Vestibular — PT Relevance
BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo) = calcium carbonate crystals (otoconia) from utricle into semicircular canals. Most common cause of vertigo — treated with Epley maneuver. Dix-Hallpike test identifies affected canal. Posterior SCC most commonly affected. PT has excellent evidence for BPPV treatment.

Nasal Cavity — Boundaries, Regions & Paranasal Sinuses

▼
Nasal Cavity Boundaries

Roof: cribriform plate of ethmoid (CN I rootlets pass through). Floor: palatine process of maxilla + horizontal plate of palatine bone (= hard palate). Medial wall (septum): perpendicular plate of ethmoid + vomer + septal cartilage. Lateral wall: superior & middle conchae (ethmoid) + inferior nasal conchae (separate bone). Conchae create turbulence for air filtration/warming.

Three Regions of Nasal Cavity

Vestibule: region immediately posterior to nostrils. Lined with skin and vibrissae (nasal hairs) — first-line filtration. Respiratory region: lower 2/3 — warms, moistens, and cleans inspired air. Rich blood supply (Kiesselbach’s plexus on anterior septum = most common site of nosebleeds). Olfactory region: upper 1/3 — innervated by CN I olfactory nerve rootlets through cribriform plate. Conveys sense of smell.

Oral Cavity — Tongue, Salivary Glands, Arches & Tonsils

▼
Oral Cavity Structure

Roof: hard palate (anterior 5/6 — palatine process of maxilla + horizontal plate of palatine) + soft palate (posterior — moves posterosuperiorly to close off nasopharynx during swallowing/speech). Uvula: midline process of soft palate. CN IX damage → uvula deviates to strong side. Floor: tongue + mylohyoid/geniohyoid muscles. Test: “Say Ahhhh” = CN IX + CN X.

Tongue

Divided into anterior 2/3 and posterior 1/3. Anterior 2/3: lingual papillae (taste buds). Posterior 1/3: lingual tonsils (lymphatic tissue).

Innervation is complex: General sensation anterior 2/3: CN V3 (lingual nerve). Taste anterior 2/3: CN VII (chorda tympani). General sensation + taste posterior 1/3: CN IX (glossopharyngeal). Motor (all intrinsic + most extrinsic): CN XII (hypoglossal) — except palatoglossus (CN X).

Genioglossus: largest extrinsic tongue muscle. Protrudes and depresses tongue. CN XII. Damage → tongue deviates to weak/injured side on protrusion.

Inferior surface landmarks: lingual frenulum (midline fold), openings of submandibular ducts (Wharton’s ducts) on either side of frenulum.

Arches & Tonsils

Palatoglossal arch (anterior) = palatoglossus muscle (CN X). Palatopharyngeal arch (posterior) = palatopharyngeus muscle (CN X). Palatine tonsils sit between the two arches — the tonsils that get removed in tonsillectomy. Three sets of tonsils form Waldeyer’s ring: pharyngeal (adenoids), palatine, and lingual tonsils.

Hemorrhage Comparison — Quick DDx

Epidural: arterial (middle meningeal A), biconvex/lens on CT, doesn't cross sutures, lucid interval, emergency craniotomy. Subdural: venous (bridging veins), crescent on CT, crosses sutures, acute or chronic, common in elderly. Subarachnoid: arterial (berry aneurysm), “worst headache ever,” star sign on CT, blood in CSF on LP, MC at Circle of Willis.

Cerebrovascular Anatomy — Circle of Willis & Venous Sinuses

▼
Internal Carotid Artery (ICA) Territory

ICA enters cranium via carotid canal → passes through cavernous sinus → gives off ophthalmic artery (supplies orbit; anastomosis with ECA via supraorbital/supratrochlear arteries — backup if ICA occluded) → terminal branches: Anterior cerebral artery (ACA) — supplies medial surfaces of frontal/parietal lobes (lower extremity motor/sensory strip) + Middle cerebral artery (MCA) — supplies lateral hemispheres (face + UE motor/sensory, Broca’s, Wernicke’s). MCA stroke is the most common stroke territory.

Vertebrobasilar Territory

Vertebral arteries enter cranium via foramen magnum → branches supply spinal cord and brainstem → R/L vertebral arteries unite to form basilar artery on anterior pons → basilar gives branches to pons and cerebellum → terminates as posterior cerebral arteries (PCA) — supply occipital lobes (visual cortex) + inferior temporal lobes.

VBI (vertebrobasilar insufficiency): reduced blood flow through vertebral arteries. Symptoms: dizziness, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, drop attacks (“5 D’s”). Provoked by cervical extension + rotation (vertebral artery compressed around C1). Must screen before cervical manipulation — VBI test mandatory.

Circle of Willis (Cerebral Arterial Circle)

Ring of anastomosing arteries at the base of the brain providing collateral circulation if one supply artery is occluded. Components (anterior → posterior): Anterior communicating artery (connects R/L ACA) → R/L ACA → R/L ICA → R/L posterior communicating arteries (connect ICA to PCA) → R/L PCA (from basilar). Berry aneurysms most commonly occur at branch points of the Circle of Willis — MC at ACA/anterior communicating junction. Rupture → subarachnoid hemorrhage.

Venous Drainage

Venous blood from brain drains into dural venous sinuses (between dural layers): Superior sagittal sinus (in falx cerebri, along sagittal suture) → confluence of sinuses (at internal occipital protuberance) → R/L transverse sinuses → R/L sigmoid sinuses → exit skull via jugular foramen as internal jugular veins (IJV). Most blood leaves the skull via the IJVs. Cavernous sinus: lateral to sella turcica — contains CN III, IV, V1, V2, VI + ICA. Cavernous sinus thrombosis = life-threatening (often from facial/sinus infection spreading retrograde).

SCALP Layers & Cranium

▼
SCALP Mnemonic — 5 Layers (Superficial → Deep)

S = Skin (thick, hair-bearing, rich blood supply — scalp lacerations bleed profusely).
C = Close subcutaneous tissue (dense CT with fat, blood vessels, nerves; bleeding vessels held open by this layer → profuse bleeding).
A = Aponeurosis (epicranial/galea aponeurotica) (tendinous sheet connecting frontal and occipital bellies of occipitofrontalis; provides structural integrity).
L = Loose connective tissue (“danger zone” — allows above 3 layers to glide over skull. Infection in this layer can spread easily across entire scalp and into cranial cavity via emissary veins).
P = Pericranium (periosteum) (dense CT layer firmly adherent to skull bones; stops here at suture lines).

TMJ Anatomy — Detailed Joint Structure, Mandible & Biomechanics

▼
TMJ Joint Structure

The TMJ is a synovial modified hinge (ginglymoarthrodial) joint — meaning it performs both rotation AND translation (gliding). Articulating surfaces: head of mandibular condylar process + mandibular fossa + articular tubercle of temporal bone.

Articular disc: fibrocartilaginous disc divides the joint into 2 compartments. Upper compartment (disco-temporal) = primarily translation/gliding. Lower compartment (disco-mandibular) = primarily rotation. The disc is attached to the joint capsule peripherally and to the superior head of lateral pterygoid anteriorly — this is why lateral pterygoid dysfunction directly affects disc mechanics.

Joint capsule: loose, allowing wide range of motion. Reinforced laterally by the lateral (temporomandibular) ligament — the only true ligament of the TMJ. Accessory ligaments: sphenomandibular and stylomandibular (provide minimal support).

Mandible Landmarks

External: mental protuberance (chin), mental foramen (mental nerve — CN V3 branch), body, angle, ramus. Ramus landmarks: coronoid process (temporalis insertion), condylar process (head articulates with temporal bone — TMJ), mandibular notch (between coronoid and condylar). Internal: lingula (near mandibular foramen entrance — inferior alveolar nerve block target), mandibular foramen (V3 entry), mylohyoid line (mylohyoid muscle origin), mylohyoid groove, submandibular fossa.

TMJ Biomechanics

Opening (depression): First 25mm = rotation in lower compartment (condyle rotates on disc). Beyond 25mm = translation in upper compartment (condyle + disc glide anteriorly down articular eminence). Full opening = ~40–50mm (3 finger-widths). Closing (elevation): reverse sequence — translation first, then rotation.

Protrusion: bilateral anterior gliding (lateral pterygoid bilateral contraction). Retrusion: posterior gliding (posterior fibers of temporalis + digastric). Lateral deviation: lateral pterygoid unilateral contraction → mandible deviates to opposite side. Ipsilateral condyle rotates while contralateral condyle translates forward.

Clinical: Disc displacement with reduction = click/pop on opening (disc reduces back onto condyle during translation). Disc displacement without reduction = locked jaw (disc blocks condyle translation → limited opening <25mm). Capsular pattern of TMJ: limitation of mouth opening.

Jaw Opening Force Couple

Opening the jaw against gravity requires minimal force, but jaw opening against resistance uses a coordinated group: lateral pterygoid (pulls condyle + disc forward) + suprahyoid muscles (digastric, mylohyoid, geniohyoid — pull mandible down when hyoid is fixed) + gravity. The infrahyoid muscles fix the hyoid so the suprahyoids can act on the mandible. This is a classic force couple — like the rotator cuff compressing while the deltoid abducts.

Red Flags
"Worst headache of my life" = subarachnoid hemorrhage (ruptured berry aneurysm) · Fixed dilated pupil = uncal herniation (CN3 compression) · Uvula deviation = CN9 lesion · Tongue deviation = CN12 lesion · Battle's sign (mastoid bruising) + periorbital bruising = basilar skull fracture
Peripheral vs Central CN7 Palsy
Peripheral (Bell's Palsy): forehead INVOLVED (ipsilateral eyebrow cannot raise, eye cannot close) — all 5 branches affected. Central (stroke): forehead SPARED (bilateral cortical representation) — only lower face droops. This distinction localizes the lesion clinically without imaging.
Key Clinical Rules
Uvula deviates to STRONG side (CN9 lesion) · Tongue deviates to WEAK side (CN12 lesion) · Pterion = thinnest skull = middle meningeal artery below = epidural hematoma risk · BPPV = PT treats with Epley maneuver · Parotid = CN7 relationship (surgery/tumor → facial palsy risk)
