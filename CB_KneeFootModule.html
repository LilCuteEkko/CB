Knee Complex — Anatomy
The knee is one of the most injured joints in the body. Three articulations: tibiofemoral (medial + lateral), patellofemoral, and proximal tibiofibular. Hip and ankle dysfunction have profound downstream effects on the knee.
Tibiofemoral Joint Type

Double condyloid joint — 3 degrees of angular motion:

  • Flexion/Extension — sagittal plane, coronal axis
  • Medial/Lateral (IR/ER) rotation — transverse plane, longitudinal axis
  • Abduction/Adduction (varus/valgus) — frontal plane, AP axis
Physiological Angulation

Normal: 180–185° (slight valgus). Genu valgum (knock-knee) >185°. Genu varum (bow-leg) <175°.

Femur — Distal Detail
  • Oblique angulation: medially and inferiorly from proximal to distal end
  • Convex femoral condyles
  • Medial femoral condyle — larger overall
  • Lateral femoral condyle — shifted more anteriorly (key for patellar tracking)
  • Intercondylar notch — houses ACL and PCL
  • Patellar groove (trochlea) — guides patella
Tibia — Proximal Detail
  • Relatively flat tibial condyle surfaces — concave (with menisci)
  • Medial plateau — longer in the anteroposterior direction
  • 7–10° posterior slope on tibial plateau — allows for knee flexion; clinically relevant in ACL reconstruction (angle affects graft tension)
  • Tibial tubercle — patellar tendon attachment
  • Gerdy's tubercle — IT band insertion (lateral)
  • Fibular head — LCL + biceps femoris attachment
Patella
  • Largest sesamoid bone in the body
  • Inverted triangular shape embedded in quadriceps tendon
  • One of the most incongruent joints in the body
  • Primary function: anatomical pulley for quadriceps — increases extensor moment arm ~30–50%
  • Insall-Salvati Index = patellar tendon length ÷ patellar length; normal = 1:1
  • Patella Alta — longer tendon (>1.2) → higher subluxation risk
  • Patella Baja — shorter tendon (<0.8) → increased PF compressive stress
Menisci — Detailed
Shape & Arrangement
  • Medial meniscus — larger, "C" shaped; less mobile (attached to MCL deep layer + capsule) → more injury prone
  • Lateral meniscus — smaller, "O" shaped; more mobile; moves more with femur
  • Fibrocartilaginous; wedge shaped — thick peripherally, thin centrally
  • Axial load dispersed in a radial direction by wedge shape
  • Greater load transmitted through medial compartment
  • Can take 50–70% of load imposed on knee
  • Compressive load: 3–5× BW during gait and stair climbing
Movement & Injury Patterns
  • Meniscus moves with the femur to maintain joint congruency during motion
  • Greater compressive forces at end-range motions
  • Posterior horn injury — at end-range flexion (squatting, deep knee flexion)
  • Anterior horn injury — at hyperextension
  • Medial meniscus = secondary restraint to anterior tibial translation; becomes primary restraint after ACL tear
Vascularity Zones — Healing Potential

Red Zone (outer 25–33%): well vascularized → good healing potential. White Zone (inner/central): avascular, relies on synovial fluid diffusion → poor healing. First year of life = fully vascularized; WB onset reduces vascularity. After age 50: only periphery has blood supply.

Joint Capsule
Structure & Functional Role
  • Thick fibrous outer layer + thin synovial membrane inner layer
  • Most congruent in full extension → ligaments taut (CPP)
  • In flexion → periarticular structures become lax → translation and rotation of tibia possible
  • Critical in restricting excessive joint motion
  • Strongly innervated by nociceptors (pain) and mechanoreceptors (position sense)
  • Mechanoreceptors initiate reflex-mediated muscular stabilization of the knee — loss of this = proprioceptive deficit post-injury
Fibrous Layer — Regional Anatomy
  • Anterior: Extensor retinaculum; deep to this = medial + lateral retinacula (patellar support); MPFL (medial patellofemoral ligament) = thickest and most clinically important patellar stabilizer
  • Medial: Deep + superficial MCL; deep MCL connected to medial meniscus
  • Lateral: Reinforced by ITB + thick fascia superficially
  • Posterior: Arcuate ligament (posterolateral) + posterior oblique ligament (posteromedial)

Screw-Home Mechanism
Why It Happens

The medial femoral condyle is larger than the lateral condyle. As the knee reaches terminal extension, the lateral condyle exhausts its articular surface first → the medial side continues rolling → tibial ER occurs automatically as a final "locking" event. This is passive — driven by bony geometry and ligamentous tension.

OKC — Terminal Extension
Tibia externally rotates on femur (~10°). Begins at 30° but not visibly apparent until the final 5° of terminal extension. Locks joint into CPP for standing.
CKC — Terminal Extension
Femur internally rotates on tibia at terminal extension. Same timing — begins ~30°, apparent at 5°.
Unlocking — Popliteus
To initiate flexion, popliteus IR tibia (OKC) or ER femur (CKC) to "unlock" the screwed-home position. The "key that unlocks the knee."
Accessory Motion
With knee in flexion: tibia can IR ~30° and ER ~40° relative to femur. This rotation is lost in full extension (CPP locks it).

Patellofemoral Joint — Detailed
Patella Tracking

Patella contact area changes throughout knee ROM due to incongruency:

  • Full extension — patella sits above the trochlea (no contact)
  • Early flexion — patella enters trochlea; shifts medially
  • Deeper flexion — patella stays medial or shifts slightly laterally
  • Contact area: smallest at low flexion, greatest at ~45–60°
PF Stress Mechanism

Quads pull patella superiorly; patellar tendon pulls it inferiorly → creates a posterior bowstring force on the femoral trochlea. This increases with knee flexion. Highest PF stress ≈ 90° flexion.

Frontal Plane Stability

Near full extension the patella is most at risk for instability:

  • Patella sits on the shallow superior femoral sulcus in extension
  • Physiological valgus of femur + tibia → quadriceps and patellar tendon action lines don't coincide
  • Net lateral pull on patella → medial soft tissue must counteract
  • MPFL = most important medial stabilizer

Q-Angle — Full Definition
Measurement

Two lines intersecting at the patellar midpoint:

  • Line 1: ASIS → midpoint of patella
  • Line 2: Tibial tuberosity → midpoint of patella
  • Normal: ~15–20° (males ~15°; females ~18°)
  • Increased Q-angle → increased PF subluxation risk
Factors that Increase Q-Angle
  • ↑ Femoral anteversion → femoral IR
  • Genu valgum (knock-knee)
  • Lateral tibial torsion
  • Lateralized tibial tuberosity
  • Foot pronation → tibial IR → functionally ↑ Q-angle
  • Wider pelvis (explains higher female prevalence of PFPS)

Ankle & Foot — Anatomy
The foot and ankle complex contains 26 bones, 33 joints, and over 100 muscles, tendons, and ligaments. It functions as a mobile adaptor at initial contact and a rigid lever at push-off.
Three Functional Segments

Rearfoot (posterior): Talus + Calcaneus · Midfoot (middle): Cuboid + Navicular + 3 Cuneiforms · Forefoot (anterior): Metatarsals + Phalanges

Talocrural (Ankle) Joint
  • Type: Synovial hinge joint
  • Mortise: Distal tibia + medial malleolus (tibial) + lateral malleolus (fibular) → concave
  • Talus: convex dome; wedge-shaped (wider anteriorly)
  • DoF: PF/DF only (sagittal plane, coronal axis)
  • Oblique axis: 14° medial-to-lateral in transverse plane; 23° rotated from frontal plane
  • Min 10° DF required for normal gait
Wedge Shape of Talus — Clinical Impact

Wider anterior talus: during DF, talus "wedges" into mortise → separates tibia and fibula slightly → enhances bony stability. DF = most stable position.

Subtalar (Talocalcaneal) Joint
  • Axis of motion: 42° inclination from transverse plane; 16° medially from AP axis → crosses all 3 planes
  • DoF: Inversion/Eversion + Pronation/Supination (triplanar)
  • Posterior facet: Concave talus on convex calcaneus
  • Anterior/Middle: Convex talus on concave calcaneus
  • Closed Pack: Full supination (inversion/PF)
  • Open Pack: Inversion + PF
Tibiofibular Joints
  • Proximal TFJ: Plane synovial joint; convex tibial facet + concave fibular facet (most common pattern)
  • Distal TFJ: Syndesmosis; convex fibular + concave tibial; fibroadipose tissue fills gap; stabilized by anterior/posterior tibiofibular ligaments + interosseous membrane
  • Fibula transmits <10% WB forces but critical for mortise stability
  • During DF: fibula moves superiorly + posteriorly (distal) to accommodate wider talus
Transverse Tarsal Joint (Chopart's Joint)
  • Talonavicular + Calcaneocuboid joints acting together
  • Talus + Calcaneus move together on Navicular + Cuboid
  • Longitudinal axis: 15° from transverse, 9° medially → IV/EV predominate
  • Oblique axis: 52° from transverse, 57° from sagittal → DF/PF + ABD/ADD predominate
  • Closed Pack: Full supination
  • Open Pack: Pronation
Key Clinical Principle

Subtalar + Transverse Tarsal joints are mechanically linked. Supination of subtalar locks the TT joint → rigid lever for push-off. Pronation unlocks TT joint → flexible adapter for load absorption.

Valgus vs. Varus — Definition

Valgus = increased medial angle between two bones = angulation >180° (knock-knee, calcaneal eversion). Varus = decreased medial angle = angulation <180° (bow-leg, calcaneal inversion).

Talus — Articular Facets
The talus has no muscular attachments — it is entirely covered by articular cartilage or ligament. Three superior facets articulate with the mortise.
Trochlear / Superior Facet

Convex. Central groove. Wider anteriorly (wedge shape) — during DF, wedges into mortise → spreads tibia/fibula → enhanced bony stability. DF = CPP.

Lateral / Fibular Facet

Larger of the two malleolar facets. Articulates with lateral malleolus (fibula).

Medial / Tibial Facet

Smaller of the malleolar facets. Articulates with medial malleolus (tibia).

Ankle Ligaments
The ankle capsule is fairly weak — ligamentous support is critical. The medial and lateral collateral ligaments are structurally and functionally asymmetric, which explains why inversion sprains vastly outnumber eversion sprains.
Medial Collateral — Deltoid Ligament
  • Fan-shaped multi-band complex from medial malleolus
  • Extremely strong — rarely torn in isolation
  • Checks eversion and pronation
  • When eversion force is sufficient, medial malleolus avulses before deltoid tears
  • Deep layer: talotibial (taut in all positions)
  • Superficial layer: tibiocalcaneal, tibionavicular, posterior talotibial
Spring Ligament (Plantar Calcaneonavicular)

Sustentaculum tali → inferior navicular. Continuous medially with deltoid. Critical passive stabilizer of the medial longitudinal arch. Bifurcate ligament connects it laterally. Spring lig laxity = arch collapse.

Lateral Collateral Ligament — 3 Bands

Weaker than deltoid. Checks inversion and supination. Most commonly sprained ligament complex in the body.

  • ATFL (Anterior Talofibular) — fibula → anterior talus; taut in PF; most commonly sprained (grade I/II inversion sprains)
  • CFL (Calcaneofibular) — fibula → calcaneus; taut in neutral/DF; spans talocrural + subtalar joints; grade II/III sprains
  • PTFL (Posterior Talofibular) — fibula → posterior talus; taut in DF; strongest of the three; rarely torn except complete dislocation
Superior Peroneal Retinaculum

Lies parallel to the CFL. Reinforces the CFL and holds peroneal tendons. Can avulse in severe inversion sprains → peroneal subluxation.

Subtalar Joint — Ligaments & Sinus Tarsi
Cervical Ligament

Strongest ligament of the subtalar joint. Lies anterior to the sinus tarsi. Joins neck of talus to neck of calcaneus. Primary restraint to subtalar inversion.

Interosseous Talocalcaneal Ligament

Lies medially within the tarsal canal. Runs obliquely from talus to calcaneus. Works with cervical ligament to resist excessive subtalar motion.

Tarsal Canal & Sinus Tarsi

The tarsal canal runs between posterior and anterior/middle subtalar facets, dividing the joint into two separate capsular compartments. The large lateral opening is the sinus tarsi — located anterior to the fibular malleolus. Tenderness here = sinus tarsi syndrome, common after lateral ankle sprains. The posterior facet has its own capsule; the anterior/middle facets share a capsule with the talonavicular joint.

Transverse Tarsal Joint — Ligament Detail
Talonavicular Joint
  • "Ball and socket" — convex head of talus into concavity of navicular + anterior/middle calcaneal facets
  • Spring ligament (plantar calcaneonavicular) forms the floor of the socket
  • Bifurcate ligament — Y-shaped; connects calcaneus to navicular (medial band) and cuboid (lateral band)
  • Shares joint capsule with anterior/middle subtalar facets
Calcaneocuboid Joint
  • Anterior calcaneus + posterior cuboid
  • Reciprocal saddle-shaped joint surface — provides multi-directional mobility
  • Reinforced by: joint capsule, bifurcate ligament, dorsal CCL, short plantar ligament, long plantar ligament
  • Supports transverse tarsal joint function and lateral longitudinal arch
Distal Foot Joints
Tarsometatarsal (TMT) Joints — Lisfranc
  • Plane synovial joints between distal tarsals and metatarsal bases
  • 1st TMT: own independent joint capsule
  • 2nd–3rd: share joint capsule
  • 4th–5th: share joint capsule
  • Deep Transverse Metatarsal Ligament: prevents excessive metatarsal head splaying
  • Ray axes diverge: 1st ray axis medial, 5th ray axis lateral
Lisfranc Injury

Disruption of 2nd TMT base (keystone of the arch) and Lisfranc ligament. Often missed on X-ray. Requires weight-bearing X-ray. High morbidity if untreated.

Metatarsophalangeal (MTP) Joints
  • Condyloid, synovial joints
  • 2 DOF: flexion/extension (DF/PF) + ABD/ADD
  • Convex: metatarsal head · Concave: base of proximal phalanx
  • 1st MTP: 2 sesamoid bones underneath; act as anatomic pulleys for FHL + intrinsics
  • Supported by: plantar plates, joint capsule, collateral ligaments
  • Need ~60° extension at 1st MTP for normal terminal stance push-off (windlass)
Interphalangeal (IP) Joints
  • Synovial hinge joints
  • 1 DOF: flexion/extension only
  • Great toe: 1 IP joint (proximal phalanx + distal phalanx)
  • Toes 2–5: 2 IP joints each (PIP + DIP)
  • Structure essentially identical to hand IP joints
  • Claw toe = MTP extension + IP flexion; Hammer toe = PIP flexion; Mallet toe = DIP flexion
Plantar Arches — Structure & Function
The foot has 3 arches that work together. Arthrology (joints + ligaments) provides the first level of passive stability; muscles and tendons provide dynamic support. The arches must be simultaneously mobile enough to absorb impact and rigid enough to transmit propulsive force.
Medial Longitudinal Arch
  • Largest of the three arches
  • Apex: head of talus
  • Spans calcaneus → talus → navicular → cuneiforms → 1st–3rd MT heads
  • Passive support: spring ligament (primary), interosseous talocalcaneal ligament, deltoid ligament, plantar aponeurosis
  • Dynamic support: tibialis posterior (most consistent), FHL, FDL, plantar intrinsics
Lateral Longitudinal Arch
  • Lower profile than medial arch
  • Spans calcaneus → cuboid → 4th–5th MT heads
  • Passive support: long plantar ligament, short plantar ligament
  • More rigid — helps with ground contact stability
Transverse Arch
  • Runs across the width of the foot at two levels
  • Apex at distal metatarsals: 2nd metatarsal (highest point)
  • Apex at TMT joints: middle cuneiform
  • Maintained by deep transverse metatarsal ligament + peroneus longus (crosses to 1st MT base)
Arch Function — Mobility
  • Dampen impact of weightbearing forces
  • Dampen rotational motions transmitted from the ground
  • Adapt to surface changes — uneven terrain, slopes
Arch Function — Stability
  • Distribute weight through foot for proper WB mechanics
  • Convert to rigid lever in terminal stance for push-off propulsion
  • Transition from mobile adaptor (pronation/loading) to rigid lever (supination/push-off) is essential for efficient gait
Plantar Aponeurosis (Plantar Fascia) — Mechanism

Runs the full length of the foot from calcaneal tuberosity to the bases of the proximal phalanges. Metatarsal heads act as pulleys. During MTP extension in terminal stance, tension on the plantar aponeurosis increases → heel and MTPs are drawn closer together → arch height increases → foot supinates → rigid lever for push-off. This is the windlass mechanism. Clinically: plantar fasciitis = degeneration/inflammation at calcaneal origin; hallux rigidus impairs windlass activation.

Pes Planus vs. Pes Cavus
Pes Planus (Flatfoot)

Low/absent medial longitudinal arch. Calcaneus everted (valgus). Navicular dropped. Talar adduction + PF. Associated with overpronation, tibial IR, knee valgus. Can be flexible (corrects with toe raise) or rigid.

Pes Cavus (High Arch)

Excessively high medial arch. Calcaneus inverted (varus). Rigid foot — poor shock absorption. Associated with plantar fasciitis, stress fractures, lateral ankle instability. Often neurological in origin.

Why We Care — Gait Context

IC → Midstance: foot pronates to absorb shock → tibia IR → knee valgus load. Terminal stance → Push-off: foot supinates → tibia ER → locked TT joint creates rigid lever for propulsion. If this sequence fails (e.g., overpronation), the entire kinetic chain is altered.

Ray Definition

Rays 1–3: metatarsal + associated cuneiform (functional unit). Rays 4–5: metatarsal alone. The crossed oblique axes of rays 1 and 5 allow the forefoot to twist and adapt to uneven terrain.

Supination Twist (during end-range pronation)

Triggered by strong medial tibial rotation → end-range subtalar pronation → calcaneal EV + talar ADD/PF → forces navicular inferiorly → limits TT joint supination.

  • Rays 1–2: pushed into DF + IV by ground reaction force from below
  • Rays 4–5: move into PF + IV to maintain ground contact
  • Result: forefoot twists — medial side rises, lateral side depresses
Pronation Twist (during end-range supination)

Triggered by strong lateral tibial rotation → end-range subtalar supination → calcaneal IV + talar ABD/DF → TT joint pulled into close-packed supination.

  • Rays 1–2: move into PF + EV to attempt ground contact
  • Rays 4–5: pushed into DF + EV by ground reaction force
  • Result: forefoot twists opposite — lateral side rises, medial side depresses
Windlass Mechanism

Great toe extension (MTP extension in terminal stance) tightens the plantar fascia → raises the medial arch → supinates the foot → provides a rigid lever for push-off. Loss of great toe extension = impaired push-off, reduced propulsive force.

Transverse Tarsal Joint — Kinetic Chain Integration Scenarios
The TT joint is not simply locked in supination or free in pronation — its behavior depends on both the subtalar position AND the demands of the task and terrain.
WB Pronation — 3 Scenarios
  • Bilateral stance: medial tibial rotation + subtalar + TT pronation — TT relatively free to move
  • Initial SLS: medial tibial rotation + subtalar pronation + TT supination (relative) to maintain forefoot contact with ground
  • Uneven terrain (medial forefoot loaded): medial tibial rotation + subtalar pronation + increased TT supination to keep forefoot on ground
WB Supination — 3 Scenarios
  • Level surface: lateral tibial rotation + subtalar supination + TT relative pronation
  • Uneven terrain / ↑ lateral rotation: subtalar supination → CPP + TT supination
  • Maximum lateral rotation: maximal subtalar supination → maximal TT supination → full rigid lever
Key Clinical Principle

TT joint can pronate OR supinate depending on environmental need — not a fixed relationship. The subtalar/TT system is an adaptive mechanism that compensates for both rearfoot position AND terrain variation. Failure of this adaptation = compensation above (knee, hip) or below (metatarsal stress).

Metatarsal Break
What It Is

The hinge or "break" point of the foot — located at the metatarsal heads as they collectively move into extension during terminal stance. The heel rises and the body pivots over the metatarsal heads while toes remain weightbearing.

  • Overall range during gait: 36–65°
  • 1st MTP alone: approximately 60–70°
  • Motion occurs around an oblique axis (54–73° from sagittal plane)
  • Convex metatarsal heads move on concave phalangeal bases (CKC)
Clinical Significance

Adequate metatarsal break extension is required for:

  • Windlass mechanism activation (plantar fascia tensioning)
  • Normal gait push-off propulsion
  • Subtalar supination in terminal stance (rigid lever)
When It Fails

Hallux rigidus (1st MTP stiffness) → incomplete metatarsal break → foot pronates through push-off instead of supinating → loss of propulsion, compensatory limb ER, metatarsal stress fractures (transfer loading to 2nd–3rd MTs).

Calcaneal Position in Midstance

In single limb stance (midstance), observe calcaneus from posterior. Normal = slight eversion (~3–5°). Excessive eversion = overpronation. Inversion in midstance = rigid supinated foot. Check symmetry R vs. L — asymmetry suggests structural or neuromuscular etiology.

MTP Extension at Terminal Stance

Assess great toe extension during push-off. Requires ~60° MTP extension for normal gait. Decreased extension = impaired windlass mechanism → reduced propulsive force, shortened stride length, compensatory hip hiking. Etiology: hallux rigidus, plantar fascia tightness, 1st MTP capsule restriction.

The Three Rockers
The foot acts as a series of three rockers during stance phase — each one enabling forward progression over the planted foot. Loss of any rocker disrupts the entire chain.
1. Heel Rocker

IC → Loading Response

Foot rotates around the heel/calcaneus as pivot point → forefoot lowers to the ground. Pretibials (tibialis anterior) eccentrically control the forefoot lowering to prevent foot slap.

If Lost

Tibialis anterior weakness → forefoot slap (audible), uncontrolled foot drop to floor.

2. Ankle Rocker

Midstance

Body progresses forward over a stable planted foot. Tibia advances over the foot (dorsiflexion). Gastrocnemius/soleus eccentrically control tibial advancement. Requires ≥10° DF.

If Lost

Limited DF → early heel rise, compensatory knee hyperextension, forward trunk lean, shortened step length.

3. Forefoot Rocker

Terminal Stance

Body moves ahead of the stance limb; heel rises and weight transfers to forefoot/toes. Calf muscles eccentrically control ankle DF and allow heel rise. Contributes to contralateral step length.

If Lost

Weak gastroc/soleus → no heel rise → reduced push-off → shortened contralateral step length.

Regional Effects on Gait
Every region of the body influences gait. Use a stability vs. mobility bucket framework: mobility deficits reduce available motion; stability deficits alter load transfer and motor control. Observe systematically head-to-toe.
Stability vs. Mobility Bucket Framework

Mobility bucket (ROM deficit): lack of ROM → compensation above/below (e.g., limited DF → knee hyperextension or hip flexion compensation). Stability bucket (motor control/strength deficit): hanging on ligaments, altered load transfer, frontal plane deviations, audible contacts. Both produce compensatory gait patterns — identifying which bucket drives clinical intervention.

Cervical Spine

Minimal gait effect▼

FHP = cervical flexion — doesn't dramatically affect gait pattern itself. Gradual cervical lordosis loss = less mobile head. Cervical spine doesn't have a major mechanical gait role — vestibular and visual input matter more.

Thoracic Spine

Trunk rotation▼

↑ Kyphosis → ↓ trunk rotation → ↓ arm swing → less energy-efficient gait, more frontal plane compensation. ↓ Thoracic curve → less shock absorption → louder/harder heel contact. Scoliosis → trunk lateral lean preference toward concave side, asymmetric step length.

Lumbar Spine

Hip extension link▼

↑ Lordosis → tight hip flexors → reduced hip extension in terminal stance → ↑ anterior shear forces at lumbar spine → compensatory anterior pelvic tilt and forward lean.

↓ Lordosis (flat back) → posterior pelvic tilt → stiffer, less mobile spine → ↓ step length, reduced energy storage in passive structures.

Hip & Pelvis

Frontal plane key▼

Hip hike → compensation for shortened swing limb (LLD, foot drop, knee stiffness). IR vs ER posture → foot becomes toe-in or toe-out. Translations (sway back/chest forward) → altered CoM trajectory. Hip backward position → increased hip extension + IR. Genu valgum from abductor weakness → stability bucket, loading and midstance affected — audible heel contact, frontal plane deviations. Sway back → hanging on ligaments = stability bucket → forceful/uncontrolled weight acceptance.

Knee

Valgus · Recurvatum▼

Valgus — at loading response due to foot pronation. Pretibials may be weak (poor eccentric DF control); hip abductors weak (not keeping knee out); ankle DF restriction. Recurvatum — hanging on posterior capsule/ligaments; lack of stability; compensation for weak quads. Both are stability bucket presentations.

Ankle & Foot

Pronation · Supination▼

↑ Pronation → ↓ effective DF; toes out; ↑ tibial IR → knee valgus; louder footfall. Less hip IR possible. ↑ Supination → better arch height, better heel strike, rigid foot = less balance/adaptation. Less shock absorption.

DF Screening tip: Place towel under heels during squat — if squat improves, DF limitation is driving the compensation. Test ankle DF actively.

Top Gait Deviations by Region
Cause → kinetic chain effect → long-term consequences. Identify which bucket (mobility vs. stability) drives each deviation.
Ankle, Foot & Toes

Foot Drop / Insufficient DF (Swing)

Swing Phase▼

Cause: Tibialis anterior weakness (deep peroneal N.), heel cord tightness. Compensation: Steppage gait (hip/knee hyperflexion), circumduction, trunk lean. Long-term: Knee/hip overload, trip/fall risk, energy inefficiency.

Decreased Push-Off / Absent Heel Rise

Terminal Stance▼

Cause: Gastroc/soleus weakness, Achilles tendon dysfunction, PF restriction, limited great toe extension. Effect: Shortened contralateral step length, reduced gait speed, compensatory hip flexion to advance limb. Bucket: Mobility (limited PF) or stability (weak calf).

Excessive Pronation / Calcaneal Eversion

Loading → Midstance▼

Cause: Pes planus, tibialis posterior weakness, ligamentous laxity, limited subtalar inversion. Chain effect: Tibial IR → knee valgus → hip IR → patellar stress, medial knee stress, PFPS, tibial stress syndrome. Louder footfall = stability deficit.

Decreased Great Toe Extension (Push-Off)

Terminal Stance▼

Cause: Hallux rigidus, plantar fascia tightness, 1st MTP capsule restriction. Effect: Windlass mechanism fails → no arch stiffening → reduced propulsive force → shortened stride, compensatory ER of entire limb to allow push-off through 2nd ray.

Hip, Pelvis & Trunk

Trendelenburg Gait (Gluteus Medius Lurch)

Midstance▼

Cause: Ipsilateral glute med weakness — stability bucket. Deviation: Contralateral pelvis drops OR trunk lurches toward stance limb (reduces abductor demand by shifting CoM). Long-term: Lateral hip overload, lumbar lateral flexion stress, ipsilateral knee valgus.

Hip Hiking (Circumduction)

Swing Phase▼

Cause: LLD, foot drop, insufficient knee flexion, or hip flexor weakness. Effect: QL overuse, lateral trunk lean, ↑ energy cost. Bucket: Mobility (limited knee/ankle) or stability (weak hip flexors, poor limb advancement).

Knee Hyperextension (Genu Recurvatum)

Stance Phase▼

Cause: Quad weakness → extension thrust; PF contracture; anterior trunk lean. Bucket: Stability (hanging on posterior capsule/ligaments) or mobility (limited DF driving compensation). Effect: Posterior capsule stress, ACL stress, ankle PF compensation.

Antalgic Gait

All Phases▼

Shortened stance phase on painful limb. Decreased step length on contralateral side. Decreased cadence. Trunk lean to reduce JRF. Can occur with any joint pathology. Analyze stance vs. swing time asymmetry for localization.

Clinical Assessment Strategies
Six practical strategies for effective gait assessment in the clinic. Use the 30-second postural snapshot first, then move to dynamic gait.
Tip 1 — Vantage Point

Take your vantage point into consideration. In small spaces, start seated to match the patient's CoM height. Change positions as needed. Consider: anthropometrics, personality, Ankle / Knee / Pelvis / Spine / Scapulae / GH, and base of support.

Tip 2 — Postural Snapshot

Use your 30-second postural snapshot first (plumb line assessment, head-to-toe static) to establish baseline. Static deviations predict dynamic compensations — but static ≠ dynamic; both must be assessed.

Tip 3 — Speed Up to Exaggerate

Have the patient speed up to exaggerate the gait cycle. Compensations that are subtle at normal speed become obvious at faster pace. Useful for confirming or ruling out a suspected deviation.

Tip 4 — Eyes Start at Pelvis

Eyes should start at the pelvis and travel cranially or caudally based on excessive motion, lack of motion, or abnormalities. The pelvis is the hub — it reflects what's happening both above and below. Assess the entire chain.

Tip 5 — Orthopedic Hypothesis

Make an orthopedic hypothesis from your observations. Do all your findings support the hypothesis? Are they related to each other? If not, reconsider. Gait deviations should tell a coherent story — bucket (stability/mobility), region, phase.

Tip 6 — Confirm or Negate

Choose a straightforward movement test to confirm or negate your hypothesis. Single leg squat, step down, squat variation, or specific ROM/strength test. Use the test-retest framework — does your intervention immediately change the gait pattern?

Temporal-Spatial Measures in Practice
Cadence

Steps per minute. Normal: ~110–120 steps/min. Count one side × 2. Decreased cadence = slow pathological gait or pain avoidance. Measure over 30ft walkway.

Walking Velocity

Distance ÷ Time (m/s). Normal: ~1.2–1.4 m/s. Community ambulation threshold: ≥0.8 m/s. Powerful predictor of functional outcomes, hospitalization, and mortality in older adults.

Step Length Symmetry

Step length = R→L heel distance. Measure both sides — asymmetry indicates pain, weakness, or limb length discrepancy. Paint-and-paper method: dip feet in paint, walk on paper, measure directly.

Scoliosis Screening

Patient bends forward with feet together, knees straight, arms hanging. Examiner observes from posterior for rib hump or trunk asymmetry. A left rib hump indicates the right thoracic convexity — the vertebrae rotate toward the convex side, carrying the ribs posteriorly on that side.

Structural: Rib hump persists on forward bend. Vertebral rotation is fixed. Does NOT correct with lateral bending toward convexity or unloading. Requires Cobb angle measurement. PT role: manage symptoms, respiratory function, quality of life; surgical consult if Cobb >40°.

Functional: Curve corrects on forward bend or when cause is removed (e.g., leg length discrepancy, muscle spasm). No vertebral rotation. Rib hump absent or minimizes. PT can address the underlying cause directly.

Sitting Posture — Slouched vs. Erect
Slouched (Sacral) Sitting
  • Pelvis: posterior tilt; sitting on sacrum
  • Lumbar spine: kyphosis (reversal of normal lordosis)
  • Thoracic spine: increased kyphosis
  • Cervical: FHP + capital extension to maintain eye level
  • Short/tight: hamstrings, posterior capsule, upper abdominals
  • Weak/lengthened: hip flexors, lumbar extensors, deep cervical flexors
  • LE: hip IR, knee flexed — posterior capsule loading
Erect Sitting
  • Pelvis: neutral to slight anterior tilt
  • Lumbar spine: maintained lordosis
  • Thoracic spine: neutral kyphosis
  • Cervical: neutral; ear over shoulder
  • Core musculature actively engaged
  • Requires active low back extensors + hip flexor length
Clinical Relevance

Prolonged sacral sitting → posterior disc herniation risk, hip flexor shortening, glute inhibition. Address with ergonomic education and lumbar support.

Standing Postural Deviations — Comparison
Four major sagittal plane postures — know tight/weak muscles and clinical implications for each.
Assessment Framework

For each postural deviation: (a) Name the dysfunction. (b) Identify muscles likely short/overactive in frontal, sagittal, transverse planes. (c) Identify muscles likely weak/elongated. (d) Hypothesize clinical implications.

Deviation 1 · Sagittal Plane

Forward Head Posture (FHP)

Short / Overactive (Tight)

Suboccipital extensors (rectus capitis posterior, obliquus capitis). Cervical extensors (semispinalis, splenius capitis/cervicis). SCM (lower fibers). Scalenes. Pectoralis minor (secondary thoracic contribution).

Weak / Elongated

Deep cervical flexors (longus colli, longus capitis). Capital flexors. Lower cervical extensors. Rhomboids / middle/lower trap (secondary if thoracic kyphosis present).

Clinical Implications

For every inch of FHP, cervical load increases ~10 lbs. Narrows intervertebral foramen → nerve root compression risk. Alters TMJ mechanics. Associated with cervicogenic headache, shoulder impingement (altered scapular kinematics), thoracic outlet syndrome.

Deviation 2 · Sagittal Plane

Excessive Thoracic Kyphosis

Short / Overactive

Pectoralis minor (pulls coracoid anteriorly → anterior scapular tilt). Pectoralis major. Intercostals (anterior). Rectus abdominis (upper). SCM.

Weak / Elongated

Middle + lower trapezius. Rhomboids. Serratus anterior. Thoracic erector spinae. Rotator cuff (secondary — altered glenohumeral alignment).

Clinical Implications

Reduced thoracic mobility → compensatory cervical and lumbar motion. Anterior scapular tilt → subacromial space narrowing → shoulder impingement syndrome. Reduced respiratory capacity. Often co-exists with FHP.

Deviation 3 · Sagittal Plane

Lumbar Hyperlordosis (Anterior Pelvic Tilt)

Short / Overactive

Hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris, TFL). Lumbar erector spinae (particularly iliocostalis). Thoracolumbar fascia. Quadratus lumborum.

Weak / Elongated

Gluteus maximus. Hamstrings. Abdominals (rectus abdominis, transverse abdominis, obliques). Deep stabilizers (multifidus).

Clinical Implications

↑ Posterior element loading → facet joint compression → posterior element pain. Anterior pelvic tilt = hip flexors shorten → limited hip extension in gait → compensatory lumbar extension → chronic LBP. Also creates faulty movement patterns in squat/deadlift.

Deviation 4 · Frontal + Transverse Plane

Scoliosis

Concave Side — Short / Overactive

Ipsilateral (concave side) spinal extensors, quadratus lumborum, scalenes, intercostals. Ipsilateral hip abductors if pelvic obliquity present.

Convex Side — Weak / Elongated

Contralateral spinal extensors and lateral flexors. Contralateral hip abductors. Respiratory compromise if Cobb angle >50°.

Clinical Implications

Cobb angle: <20° = observe, 20–40° = bracing considered, >40° = surgical consult. PT role: trunk stabilization, respiratory management, pain management, functional mobility. Distinguish structural vs. functional scoliosis (functional corrects with forward bend or unloading).

Deviation 5 · Sagittal Plane

Swayback Posture

Short / Overactive

Hamstrings (pelvis posteriorly tilted). Posterior hip capsule. Upper abdominals (pulls thorax posteriorly). Internal obliques.

Weak / Elongated

Hip flexors (lengthened with posterior pelvic tilt). Lumbar erectors. Lower abdominals. Gluteus maximus (positioned passively shortened → weak in function).

Distinguish from Lordosis

Swayback = posterior pelvic tilt + pelvis shifted anteriorly + trunk shifted posteriorly. vs. Lordosis = anterior pelvic tilt + increased lumbar curve. Both are sagittal plane deviations but opposite pelvic mechanics. Confused frequently in clinical assessment.

Deviation 6 · Sagittal Plane

Flat Back Posture

Short / Overactive

Hamstrings (draw pelvis into posterior tilt). Abdominals (rectus, lower obliques). Hip extensors. Posterior hip capsule.

Weak / Elongated

Hip flexors (lengthened). Lumbar erector spinae. Thoracic extensors. Multifidus. One-joint hip extensors may be shortened.

Clinical Implications

Loss of lumbar lordosis = reduced shock absorption (spine functions as rigid column). Posterior pelvic tilt → posterior disc loading → posterior annular stress. Hamstring tightness perpetuates posterior pelvic tilt. Distinguish from swayback: flat back = reduced thoracic kyphosis too; swayback = increased thoracic kyphosis. Both have posterior pelvic tilt but different thoracic posture.

Dynamic Postural Control — Balance Strategies
The nervous system deploys three reactive strategies in response to perturbation — selected based on the speed and magnitude of the CoM displacement relative to the BoS. Both reactive (after perturbation) and anticipatory (before expected perturbation) postural adjustments exist.
Ankle Strategy

Used for small, slow perturbations on a firm surface. CoM is controlled by ankle muscle torque — gastrocnemius/soleus (forward sway) and tibialis anterior (backward sway). Whole body moves as an inverted pendulum. Requires adequate DF ROM and proprioception.

Muscles Active

Falling forward: gastrocnemius/soleus → hamstrings → paraspinals (distal to proximal). Falling backward: tibialis anterior → quadriceps → abdominals.

Hip Strategy

Used for larger, faster perturbations or unstable/narrow surfaces. Hip flexors/extensors rapidly flex or extend the trunk to shift CoM back over BoS. Faster activation of proximal muscles.

When Hip Strategy Overrides Ankle

Narrow BoS, compliant/unstable surface (foam), large perturbation magnitude, elderly patients with impaired proprioception → ankle strategy insufficient → hip strategy activates.

Step / Reach Strategy

Used when CoM displacement exceeds BoS — a step or reach expands the BoS to regain stability. Last resort before fall. Requires rapid limb advancement and weight transfer.

Clinical Application

Impaired stepping strategy = high fall risk. Test with perturbation in SLS, narrow stance, while walking. Document if step is protective and well-placed vs. absent/misdirected.

Reactive vs. Anticipatory Balance
  • Reactive (feedback): Response occurs AFTER perturbation detected. Latency ~100–120ms. Dependent on sensory input quality. Assess with surprise perturbations.
  • Anticipatory (feedforward): CNS pre-activates postural muscles BEFORE a voluntary movement to counteract predicted CoM displacement. Example: trunk stabilizes before rapid arm movement. Assess by observing pre-movement postural adjustments during functional tasks.
Clinical Balance Assessment Points
  • Perturbation positions: wide → narrow → tandem → single limb stance
  • Surface: firm → foam (removes ankle strategy input)
  • Vision: eyes open → eyes closed (Romberg)
  • Dual task: balance + cognitive load
  • Apply perturbations anteriorly, posteriorly, and laterally
  • Document which strategy used, quality, and latency
  • Note asymmetry between sides

What is the screw-home mechanism and which muscle unlocks it?
tap
Terminal tibial ER (OKC) or femoral IR (CKC) as knee reaches full extension due to medial condyle asymmetry. Unlocked by popliteus (IR tibia / ER femur).
Knee OKC vs. CKC: which direction does the femur glide during CKC flexion?
tap
CKC flexion (femur on tibia): convex on concave → roll and glide OPPOSITE. Posterior roll / Anterior glide of femur.
What talocrural ROM is the minimum required for normal gait?
tap
10° DF minimum for normal gait. 20° needed for stair climbing. Limited DF → compensatory pronation, early heel rise, knee hyperextension.
OKC talocrural DF arthrokinematics?
tap
Convex talus on concave mortise → opposite. Anterior roll / Posterior glide. To mobilize for DF restriction: posterior talar glide.
What are the 6 criteria of the FPI-6?
tap
Rearfoot: (1) Talar head palpation · (2) Curves above/below lateral malleolus · (3) Calcaneal inversion/eversion. Forefoot: (4) TNJ congruence · (5) Medial arch height · (6) Forefoot ABD/ADD on rearfoot.
FPI-6 reference values: what is normal, pronated, supinated?
tap
Normal: 0 to +5. Pronated: +6 to +9. Highly pronated: 10+. Supinated: –1 to –4. Highly supinated: –5 to –12.
What is the windlass mechanism and why does it matter for gait?
tap
Great toe MTP extension tightens plantar fascia → raises medial arch → supinates foot → creates rigid lever for push-off. Loss = impaired propulsion, shortened stride, compensatory limb ER at push-off.
In CKC, what happens to the tibia when the subtalar joint pronates?
tap
Subtalar pronation → calcaneal eversion → talar ADDuction + PF → tibial internal rotation. Cascades up chain: knee valgus, hip IR, patellar stress.
Gait: what percent of the cycle is stance and swing?
tap
Stance ~60% · Swing ~40%. Double support occurs at 0–10% (loading) and 50–60% (pre-swing). Single support = each leg ~40% of cycle.
What causes a Trendelenburg gait and what is the compensatory strategy?
tap
Ipsilateral glute med weakness → contralateral pelvic drop during stance. Compensation: trunk lateral lean toward stance side (gluteus medius lurch) → shifts CoM to reduce abductor demand.
What is the primary mobilization for limited ankle DF?
tap
Posterior talar glide — addresses restricted posterior glide needed for DF (OKC: DF = anterior roll + posterior glide). Also consider distal fibula posterior mob if fibula is restricted.
Which muscles are tight vs. weak in lumbar hyperlordosis?
tap
Tight: Hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris), lumbar erector spinae. Weak: Gluteus maximus, hamstrings, abdominals (rectus, transverse, obliques), multifidus.
What is the VMO and why is it clinically important?
tap
Vastus Medialis Obliquus — medial pull on patella to prevent lateral tracking. Fires in last 15–20° of extension. VMO:VL imbalance → lateral patellar tracking → PFPS. Targeted in PFPS rehabilitation.
Distinguish swayback posture from lumbar lordosis.
tap
Swayback: Posterior pelvic tilt + pelvis shifted anteriorly + trunk shifted posteriorly. Tight hamstrings. Lordosis: Anterior pelvic tilt + increased lumbar curve. Tight hip flexors. Opposite pelvic mechanics — don't confuse.
What gait compensation occurs with foot drop and why?
tap
Tibialis anterior weakness → foot can't DF in swing → steppage gait (exaggerated hip/knee flexion to clear foot), circumduction, or hip hiking. All increase energy cost and fall risk.
How does excessive foot pronation affect the knee?
tap
Subtalar pronation → tibial IR → knee valgus stress → increased lateral patellar pull + medial compartment loading → PFPS, MCL stress, medial meniscus loading. Foundation of "pronation-to-pain" kinetic chain cascade.
Name the 3 bands of the lateral collateral ligament and what each checks.
tap
ATFL — taut in PF; most commonly sprained. CFL — taut in neutral/DF; spans talocrural + subtalar. PTFL — taut in DF; strongest; rarely torn. All check inversion/supination.
What does the deltoid (medial collateral) ligament check and why is it rarely torn?
tap
Checks eversion and pronation. Fan-shaped and extremely strong — when eversion force is sufficient, the medial malleolus avulses before the deltoid tears.
What is the spring ligament and why does it matter clinically?
tap
Plantar calcaneonavicular ligament — sustentaculum tali → inferior navicular. Primary passive stabilizer of the medial longitudinal arch. Laxity → arch collapse (flatfoot). Continuous medially with deltoid ligament.
What is the sinus tarsi and what clinical condition is associated with it?
tap
Large lateral opening of the tarsal canal, located anterior to the fibular malleolus. Sinus tarsi syndrome — lateral hindfoot pain + instability, often after lateral ankle sprains. Contains cervical ligament and nerve endings.
What are the 3 balance strategies in order of perturbation magnitude?
tap
Ankle strategy (small/slow, firm surface) → Hip strategy (large/fast, narrow/unstable surface) → Step/Reach strategy (CoM outside BoS). Ankle uses distal-to-proximal muscle activation; hip uses proximal-to-distal.
Distinguish reactive from anticipatory postural control.
tap
Reactive: Response AFTER perturbation — feedback-based, ~100–120ms latency. Anticipatory: Pre-activation of postural muscles BEFORE voluntary movement — feedforward, predictive. Both required for functional balance. Elderly often lose anticipatory control first.
What are the lateral plumb line landmarks from head to foot?
tap
Ear/mastoid → just anterior to shoulder joint → just posterior to hip/greater trochanter → just posterior to center of knee → just anterior to lateral malleolus.
Flat back vs. swayback — how do you distinguish them?
tap
Both have posterior pelvic tilt. Flat back: reduced lumbar lordosis + reduced thoracic kyphosis; spine appears straight. Swayback: pelvis shifted anteriorly + thorax shifted posteriorly + increased thoracic kyphosis. Different thoracic postures distinguish them.
What are the 3 degrees of angular motion at the tibiofemoral joint?
tap
Flexion/Extension (sagittal, coronal axis) · IR/ER (transverse, longitudinal axis) · ABD/ADD/varus/valgus (frontal, AP axis). It's a double condyloid joint, not a simple hinge.
What is the tibial plateau posterior slope and why does it matter?
tap
7–10° posterior slope of tibial plateau — allows for knee flexion. Clinically relevant in ACL reconstruction: steeper slope → greater anterior tibial shear forces → influences graft tension and re-tear risk.
Meniscal zones — which heals and which doesn't, and why?
tap
Red Zone (outer 25–33%) — vascularized → good healing potential. White Zone (central) — avascular, relies on synovial fluid diffusion → poor healing. After age 50 only periphery vascularized. Determines surgical repair vs. resection decision.
What makes the MCL deep layer clinically important?
tap
Deep MCL is directly connected to the medial meniscus. A valgus stress that tears the MCL often simultaneously tears the medial meniscus. Also explains why medial meniscus is less mobile than lateral.
What is the IT band — is it contractile, and what causes ITB syndrome?
tap
IT band = thick fascia from TFL + glute max → Gerdy's tubercle. NOT contractile — consistently taut regardless of knee/hip position. ITB syndrome: fat pad between ITB and lateral femoral epicondyle becomes irritated at ~30° flexion with repetitive movement.
What is the Insall-Salvati index and what do alta/baja indicate?
tap
Patellar tendon length ÷ patellar length. Normal = 1:1. Patella Alta (>1.2): longer tendon → patella sits high → higher subluxation risk (sits on shallow sulcus longer). Patella Baja (<0.8): shorter tendon → increased PF compressive stress.
What is the MPFL and why is it the most important patellar stabilizer?
tap
Medial PatelloFemoral Ligament — thickest, most clinically important structure resisting lateral patellar subluxation/dislocation. Part of the anterior joint capsule's medial retinaculum. MPFL rupture occurs in ~90% of first-time patellar dislocations.
When is the screw-home mechanism visible and what drives it?
tap
Begins at ~30° but not visible until the final 5° of terminal extension. Driven by bony geometry: medial femoral condyle is larger → lateral condyle exhausts articular surface first → medial side continues → obligatory tibial ER (OKC) or femoral IR (CKC).
What is the physiological angulation of the knee and when is it pathological?
tap
Normal: 180–185° (slight valgus). Genu valgum (knock-knee) = >185°. Genu varum (bow-leg) = <175°. Genu valgum → ↑ Q-angle → lateral patellar pull → PFPS risk. Genu varum → ↑ medial compartment loading → medial OA.
What are the 3 rockers of gait and which phase does each occur in?
tap
Heel rocker (IC/LR) — pivot at calcaneus, forefoot lowers; pretibials eccentric. Ankle rocker (MSt) — tibia advances over foot; calf eccentric. Forefoot rocker (TSt) — heel rises, body moves ahead; calf eccentric + windlass activation.
What are the 3 goals of gait?
tap
(1) Maintain weight-bearing stability, (2) Conserve energy, (3) Absorb shock of floor impact. Forward progression while accomplishing all three simultaneously.
What is the critical event at Loading Response and what muscles are key?
tap
Critical event: hip stability + controlled knee flexion + controlled ankle PF. Quads (E) control knee flexion to 15°. Pretibials (E) control ankle PF. Hip abductors + extensors (E) stabilize pelvis. First period of double support.
What is the stability vs. mobility bucket framework in gait assessment?
tap
Mobility bucket = ROM deficit → compensation above/below (e.g., limited DF → knee hyperextension). Stability bucket = motor control/strength deficit → hanging on ligaments, frontal plane deviations, audible contacts. Identifying which bucket drives the clinical intervention choice.
Where should your eyes start during gait assessment and why?
tap
Start at the pelvis — the hub that reflects what's happening both above (spine/trunk) and below (hip/knee/ankle). Travel cranially or caudally based on excessive motion, lack of motion, or abnormalities. Pelvis tells the whole story.
What squat variation tests ankle DF most specifically and why?
tap
Narrow stance squat — reduces hip compensation and isolates DF demand. Also: heel elevation test — if squat improves with heels raised, DF restriction is driving the compensation. Wide stance tests hip mobility instead.
During midstance, what is the critical event and what muscle group provides it?
tap
Critical event: controlled tibial advancement (ankle rocker). Gastroc/soleus eccentrically control tibial advance over foot. Hip abductors (3 gluteals) isometrically stabilize pelvis. Knee extensors isometric. Body progresses over single stable limb.

↑ Kyphosis → ↓ trunk rotation → ↓ arm swing → less energy-efficient gait. More frontal plane compensation needed. Less thoracic curve = less shock absorption = louder/harder heel contact. Scoliosis → lateral trunk lean preference, asymmetric step length.

Which muscle provides the most consistent dynamic support of the medial longitudinal arch?
tap
Tibialis posterior — passes medial to the subtalar axis → supination moment; wraps around navicular. Also: plantar intrinsics active in quiet stance and gait.
1st ray axis: what motions couple with DF and with PF?
tap
Oblique medial axis. DF couples with IV + ADD. PF couples with EV + ABD. (5th ray is opposite: DF+EV+ABD; PF+IV+ADD. 3rd ray is near coronal: pure DF/PF only.)
What is the metatarsal break and what are the ROM values?
tap
Hinge point at metatarsal heads during terminal stance as heel rises and toes remain WB. 36–65° overall during gait; ~60–70° at 1st MTP alone. Occurs around an oblique axis (~54–73°). Drives windlass mechanism.
During initial single limb stance, what happens at the TT joint even though the subtalar is pronating?
tap
TT joint undergoes relative supination — to maintain forefoot contact with the ground despite subtalar pronation and medial tibial rotation. The TT does NOT simply follow the subtalar into pronation; it adapts to terrain demands independently.
Which muscles are lateral to the subtalar axis (pronators) vs. medial (supinators)?
tap
Medial (supinators): tibialis anterior, tibialis posterior, FHL, FDL, triceps surae. Lateral (pronators): peroneus longus, peroneus brevis, peroneus tertius, EDL (mild). Position relative to subtalar axis determines the moment, not the muscle name alone.
What passive structures support the medial longitudinal arch?
tap
Spring ligament (primary passive stabilizer) + interosseous talocalcaneal ligament + deltoid ligament + plantar aponeurosis. Dynamic: tibialis posterior, FHL, FDL, plantar intrinsics.
Left rib hump on Adam's forward bend test — what does this indicate?
tap
Right thoracic convexity (curve to the right). Vertebrae rotate toward the convex side, carrying the ribs posteriorly on the right — this pushes them up creating a hump on the right. A LEFT rib hump = LEFT-sided vertebral rotation = RIGHT convexity curve.
