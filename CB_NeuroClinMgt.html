Stroke / CVA — Cerebrovascular Accident
Stroke is the sudden loss of neurological function caused by interruption of blood flow to the brain. 800k people per year experience stroke. 80% are ischemic. Hemorrhagic strokes are more deadly. To be classified as a stroke, neurological deficits must persist for at least 24 hours. Motor deficits are characterized by hemiplegia (paralysis) or hemiparesis (weakness), typically contralateral to the lesion.

Epidemiology, Risk Factors & FAST

A-fib = 5x riskFAST = Face, Arm, Speech, Time▼
Risk Factors

Modifiable: Overweight, sedentary life, binge drinking, DM, smoking, HTN (≥140/90 = 2x lifetime risk), high cholesterol, cardiovascular diseases. Non-modifiable: Genetics, age, sex, Asian ethnicity. A-Fib: 5x increased risk for stroke. Irregular and often rapid heart rhythm. Atherosclerosis: Plaque (lipids, fibrin, calcium) narrows vessels → interrupts blood flow. Polycythemia: Increased circulating RBCs — primary (genetic, JAK2 mutation) vs secondary (another cause). Demographics: Women have lower rates until >85. African Americans have 2x risk vs whites. Higher in Mexicans, Native Americans/Alaskans.

FAST & "Brain Attack"

F = Facial drooping. A = Arm weakness. S = Speech issues. T = Time (call 911). "Brain attack" is a marketing term to incite emergency reaction — like "heart attack" for cardiac events. Fear-provoking phrase designed to help people recognize the urgency.

Ischemic Penumbra vs Umbra

Umbra: Actual tissue that has DIED due to lack of oxygen. Irreversible. Penumbra: The area that was without oxygen but WILL eventually recover. This is the salvageable tissue — the target of acute stroke treatment. Goal of emergency treatment = save the penumbra.

tPA (Tissue Plasminogen Activator)

Thrombolytic that dissolves clots. Only for ischemic strokes — contraindicated in hemorrhagic (would worsen bleeding). Time window is critical. Standard window: within 3–4.5 hours of symptom onset. This is why "Time" in FAST is critical — every minute of delay = more penumbra becomes umbra.

TIA vs Stroke

TIA (Transient Ischemic Attack): Temporary neurological dysfunction that resolves within 24 hours — "warning stroke." RIND (Reversible Ischemic Neurological Deficit): Resolves within 72 hours. Stroke: Deficits persist >24 hours. AVM (Arteriovenous Malformation): Tangled blood vessels bypassing normal tissue — usually congenital, can rupture causing hemorrhagic stroke.

Circle of Willis

Anastomotic ring connecting the internal carotid and vertebral arterial systems. Provides collateral circulation — if one vessel is occluded, blood can potentially reach the affected area through alternative routes. Key components: Anterior communicating artery (connects L and R ACA), posterior communicating arteries (connect ICA to PCA). Anatomic variations are common and affect collateral capacity.

Handedness & Dominance

90% of people are right-handed (since prehistoric times). 95% of RH have speech in L hemisphere. 70% of LH show the same pattern. Of the remaining 30%: 50% have R hemi control, 50% bilateral control. 15% with R hemi dominance = "crossed dominance." Familial sinistrality (LH in immediate family): language disturbances after brain lesion occur with similar frequency as RH. No familial sinistrality: Language disturbances are almost non-existent. Disinhibition vs Inhibition: The intact hemisphere becomes disinhibited (more dominant) when the other is damaged. This explains the behavioral presentations — R damage amplifies L hemisphere characteristics and vice versa.

Subluxed Shoulder Post-Stroke

Sulcus sign (2-finger sign): Traction applied inferiorly on relaxed arm — depression below acromion indicates glenohumeral instability. Treatment depends on neuromotor control: Closed-chain exercises to approximate the shoulder joint — retraction, depression, work toward midline. Target traps, rhomboids, levator scapulae. Strengthen in closed chain before open chain.

Stroke Evidence & Recovery

High-intensity task-specific = strong evidenceAerobic training = CV health▼
Strong Evidence for PT Post-Stroke

Higher dose of practice is better (strong evidence). Intensive, high-repetitive, task-oriented and task-specific training is favored in ALL phases post-stroke. Walking training at moderate-to-high intensities or VR-based training improves walking speed/distance in patients >6 months post-CNS injury (strong evidence). Aerobic exercise in subacute stroke: Recumbent stepper 3x/week for 8 weeks → significant improvements in FMD (endothelial function), resting SBP, and 6MWT. Beneficial for cardiovascular health, reducing cardiac risk, and improving physical performance. Weak evidence: Strength training, circuit training, cycling at moderate-high intensities, VR-based balance training may improve walking. Should NOT be performed: Body weight-supported treadmill training, robotic-assisted training, or sitting/standing balance training without VR — for improving walking speed/distance.
