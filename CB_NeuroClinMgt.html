Neurological Clinical Management — Advanced Neuro PT

The clinical application course — taking neuroanatomy and applying it to real patient management. Covers stroke (arterial territories, hemisphere lateralization, tPA), brain tumors (glioma classification, Karnofsky scale), cerebellar disorders (ataxia, dysmetria, PT interventions), SCI (ASIA classification, functional levels, autonomic dysreflexia), HIV/AIDS (stages, neurologic involvement), MS (4 subtypes, fatigue management), ALS (UMN+LMN, therapeutic window), PNF treatment techniques, TBI (GCS, Rancho levels), and differential diagnosis across all neuro conditions.

Stroke / CVA — Cerebrovascular Accident
Stroke is the sudden loss of neurological function caused by interruption of blood flow to the brain. 800k people per year experience stroke. 80% are ischemic. Hemorrhagic strokes are more deadly. To be classified as a stroke, neurological deficits must persist for at least 24 hours. Motor deficits are characterized by hemiplegia (paralysis) or hemiparesis (weakness), typically contralateral to the lesion.

Epidemiology, Risk Factors & FAST

▼
Risk Factors

Modifiable: Overweight, sedentary life, binge drinking, DM, smoking, HTN (≥140/90 = 2x lifetime risk), high cholesterol, cardiovascular diseases.
Non-modifiable: Genetics, age, sex, Asian ethnicity.
A-Fib: 5x increased risk for stroke. Irregular and often rapid heart rhythm.
Atherosclerosis: Plaque (lipids, fibrin, calcium) narrows vessels → interrupts blood flow.
Polycythemia: Increased circulating RBCs — primary (genetic, JAK2 mutation) vs secondary (another cause).
Demographics: Women have lower rates until >85. African Americans have 2x risk vs whites. Higher in Mexicans, Native Americans/Alaskans.

FAST & "Brain Attack"

F = Facial drooping.
A = Arm weakness.
S = Speech issues.
T = Time (call 911). "Brain attack" is a marketing term to incite emergency reaction — like "heart attack" for cardiac events. Fear-provoking phrase designed to help people recognize the urgency.

Ischemic Penumbra vs Umbra

Umbra: Actual tissue that has DIED due to lack of oxygen. Irreversible.
Penumbra: The area that was without oxygen but WILL eventually recover. This is the salvageable tissue — the target of acute stroke treatment.
Goal of emergency treatment = save the penumbra.

tPA (Tissue Plasminogen Activator)

Thrombolytic that dissolves clots. Only for ischemic strokes — contraindicated in hemorrhagic (would worsen bleeding). Time window is critical. Standard window: within 3–4.5 hours of symptom onset. This is why "Time" in FAST is critical — every minute of delay = more penumbra becomes umbra.

TIA vs Stroke

TIA (Transient Ischemic Attack): Temporary neurological dysfunction that resolves within 24 hours — "warning stroke." RIND (Reversible Ischemic Neurological Deficit): Resolves within 72 hours.
Stroke: Deficits persist >24 hours.
AVM (Arteriovenous Malformation): Tangled blood vessels bypassing normal tissue — usually congenital, can rupture causing hemorrhagic stroke.

Anastomotic ring connecting the internal carotid and vertebral arterial systems. Handedness & Dominance

90% of people are right-handed (since prehistoric times).
95% of RH have speech in L hemisphere. 70% of LH show the same pattern. Of the remaining 30%: 50% have R hemi control, 50% bilateral control. 15% with R hemi dominance = "crossed dominance." Familial sinistrality (LH in immediate family): language disturbances after brain lesion occur with similar frequency as RH.
No familial sinistrality: Language disturbances are almost non-existent.
Disinhibition vs Inhibition: The intact hemisphere becomes disinhibited (more dominant) when the other is damaged. This explains the behavioral presentations — R damage amplifies L hemisphere characteristics and vice versa.

Subluxed Shoulder Post-Stroke

Sulcus sign (2-finger sign): Traction applied inferiorly on relaxed arm — depression below acromion indicates glenohumeral instability. Treatment depends on neuromotor control: Closed-chain exercises to approximate the shoulder joint — retraction, depression, work toward midline. Target traps, rhomboids, levator scapulae. Strengthen in closed chain before open chain.

Stroke Evidence & Recovery

▼
Strong Evidence for PT Post-Stroke

Higher dose of practice is better (strong evidence). Intensive, high-repetitive, task-oriented and task-specific training is favored in ALL phases post-stroke. Walking training at moderate-to-high intensities or VR-based training improves walking speed/distance in patients >6 months post-CNS injury (strong evidence).
Aerobic exercise in subacute stroke: Recumbent stepper 3x/week for 8 weeks → significant improvements in FMD (endothelial function), resting SBP, and 6MWT. Beneficial for cardiovascular health, reducing cardiac risk, and improving physical performance.
Weak evidence: Strength training, circuit training, cycling at moderate-high intensities, VR-based balance training may improve walking.
Should NOT be performed: Body weight-supported treadmill training, robotic-assisted training, or sitting/standing balance training without VR — for improving walking speed/distance.

Brain Tumors — Classification, Location & PT Management
Clinical presentation of brain tumors mimics other CNS conditions — location of the tumor determines impairments, just like vascular lesions. Two distinct peaks: children 0–15 yo and adults in the 5th–7th decades. White Americans > Black Americans, M > F. Etiology unclear but associated with toxic/infectious chemical exposure, electromagnetic fields, and ionizing radiation.

Primary Brain Tumors — Glioma Classification

▼
Primary vs Secondary (Metastatic)

Primary tumors originate in the CNS.
Gliomas arise from supportive tissues (glial cells), frequently in cerebral hemispheres. Can also occur in brainstem, optic canal, spinal cord, and cerebellum (especially in children).
Secondary/Metastatic tumors spread from sites outside the brain (lung, breast, melanoma are common primaries).
Key difference: Primary tumors arise IN the CNS; metastatic tumors spread TO the CNS.

Karnofsky Scale, Imaging & PT Intervention

▼
Karnofsky Performance Status Scale

Scale indicating the patient's condition and ability to carry on normal activity. 100% = normal, no complaints. 90% = completes normal activity with minor symptoms. 80% = normal activity with effort. 70% = cares for self but unable to carry on normal activity. 60% = requires occasional assistance. 50% = requires considerable assistance. 40% = disabled. 30% = severely disabled. 20% = very sick. 10% = moribund. FIM (Functional Independence Measure) is useful during eval — provides a means of documenting response to therapy intervention.

PT Intervention Considerations

Treatment must consider: the nature of the tumor, fluctuating neurological function, and likelihood for progressive decline.
For metastatic tumor with mild hemiparesis and gait abnormalities (70 yo, not receiving tumor treatment): Optimize functional capabilities through durable medical equipment to promote safety and independence — NOT aggressive strengthening or neuroplasticity-focused rehab.
For operable primary tumor: More aggressive rehab approach, functional recovery through neural plasticity principles.
SNF patient with inoperable tumor showing long-term memory deficits, cognitive deficits, inability to transfer learning: Likely bilateral frontal or medial temporal involvement.

Cerebellar Disorders — Pathways, Deficits & PT Management
The cerebellum has IPSILATERAL control over the body (2 points of output decussation). Lesion = movement deficits on the SAME side of the body. Vital for motor learning, timing, anticipatory cues, and environmental adaptation. Without it, movements become conscious. The cerebellum compares what IS actually happening to the INTENT of higher-order motor commands.

Cerebellar Pathways & Functional Zones

▼
Afferent & Efferent Pathways

Afferent (input): Receives from cortex, sensory modalities, brainstem nuclei, spinal tracts. Major source: corticopontine fibers from frontal, temporal, parietal, and occipital lobes traveling in the internal capsule and cerebral peduncles. Spinocerebellar fibers provide info on limb movement and spinal cord interneuron activity — transmits unconscious info for rapid feedback allowing fine adjustments. Efferent (output): Projects to cerebrum and brainstem, exerting strong influence on movement. Outputs project to Ventrolateral nucleus of thalamus (VL) → motor cortex, premotor cortex, SMA, and parietal lobe to influence motor planning.

ZoneControlsLesion Effects
Vestibulocerebellum (Flocculonodular)VOR, gaze stabilization, postureVision does NOT improve balance (unlike other zones). VOR cancellation impaired
Spinocerebellum (Vermis)Medial motor system — proximal trunk musculature BILATERALLYTruncal ataxia. Midline lesion = bilateral trunk involvement. Titubation (head/trunk tremor)
Spinocerebellum (Intermediate)Lateral motor system — limb controlAppendicular ataxia. Ipsilateral limb incoordination
Cerebrocerebellum (Lateral)Motor planning, timing, sequencing. Mental rehearsal of movementDysmetria, intention tremor, dysdiadochokinesia. Loss of feedforward control
What the Cerebellum Does — Why It Matters

Vital to motor learning — feedforward based on memory from previous motor and sensory outputs. Stored internal memory of limb dynamics helps generate motor commands for multi-joint movements. Provides precise temporal and spatial control. Ability to remember what has to happen spatially or temporally = activity-dependent plasticity = constant adaptive learning. Without it, movements become conscious — adaptive learning (walking on various surfaces) is lost, patients must use cognitive/conscious control.

Cerebellar PT Assessment & Interventions

▼
PT Assessment

Neuro exam with emphasis on coordination and timing tasks. Assess functional tasks: bed mobility, transfers, elevation from variable surfaces, sitting/standing balance, gait in variable environments, ADLs (dressing, grooming, feeding). Note: If cerebellum AND cerebral cortex are both involved, cortical damage may mask some cerebellar symptoms.

PT Interventions — Emphasis Areas

Will require repetition and task sequencing. Difficulty with faster movements and more complex multi-joint tasks.
Postural stability/core stabilization: Head and trunk control in various positions. Challenge with non-compliant and compliant surfaces. Rhythmic stabilization (stability > strength). Push-pull with PT, joint approximation. Sitting on dynadisc/physioball.
Sitting balance: Maintain trunk posture during UE activity or trunk rotation. Functional activities (supine to sit — optimal strategy for patient's lifestyle).
Sit-to-stand: Practice correct sequence with emphasis on COG placement — ataxic individuals may lean too far forward or extend trunk too early (timing issues). High reps, increase verbal and visual feedback. Progress with unstable surfaces.
Standing balance: May use biofeedback on force plate for COG control. Floor transfers.
Ambulation: Give specific verbal feedback on step length, body rotation, trunk/accessory movements. Walking in tall kneeling (limits limb ataxia, strengthens trunk/hips). Stepping over objects/to targets (hurdles, agility ladder, lines on floor).
AD selection: Bilateral platform walker as starting point (more contact points, trunk stabilization) → progress to FWW → no AD as trunk stability improves. Single cane has sequencing issues. Walking poles for reciprocal UE/LE coordination.

Weighting Controversy & Priming

Weights: Can temporarily decrease amplitude of tremor. However, research shows it can be detrimental since patients cannot adjust to different limb torques — can worsen hypermetria. Distal weights make this MORE difficult. Slows movements down.
Alternative: Non-weighted external compression to improve gait while core/postural stabilization is still poor — watch for compensation with overuse.
Priming (before functional tasks): PNF patterns, rhythmic stabilization, slow reverse hold of LEs prior to gait training. No therapeutic procedure will eliminate dysmetria, but priming the motor system can temporarily reduce amplitude. All tasks performed with visual focus and attention by the patient.

Progressive vs Non-Progressive

Progressive (worsening): Spinocerebellar ataxia (later midlife, autosomal dominant, 30+ types, no pharmacological treatment). MS, neurodegenerative diseases. Need periodic PT over lifetime, family education, caregiver training, equipment needs.
Non-progressive (stable): Stroke, TBI, alcoholism (anterior superior vermis atrophy). More potential for substantial recovery.
Episodic Ataxia: Rare autosomal dominant. Periods of ataxic movement (minutes to hours) induced by stress/excitement. N/V, vertigo, HA, blurred vision, diplopia, tinnitus.
Responds well to medication.

Spinal Cord Injury — ASIA, Syndromes & Functional Goals
SCI results in loss of motor and/or sensory function below the level of injury. The level and completeness of injury determine functional expectations. Upper motor neurons (UMNs) travel from the brain through the spinal cord; lower motor neurons (LMNs) exit between each vertebra and go to the body. Damage to the cord interrupts the "telephone cable" between the main office (brain) and individual offices (body parts).

ASIA Examination

Doctor performs pin prick test (sensory level) and key muscle group testing (motor level) at each dermatome/myotome level. The level of injury = most caudal level with intact motor and sensory function.
SCIM (Spinal Cord Independence Measure): Outcome measure specific to SCI — assesses self-care, respiration/sphincter management, and mobility. Each injury is different — described by level and type (complete vs incomplete).

Autonomic Dysreflexia — MEDICAL EMERGENCY

Occurs in SCI at T6 and above. Massive sympathetic response — widespread peripheral arterial vasoconstriction causing severe hypertension.
S/S: HTN, bradycardia, diaphoresis (sweating), headache, blotchy/red skin, piloerection (goosebumps).
Common triggers (noxious stimuli below lesion level): UTI or fecal impaction (#1 causes), pressure ulcers, tight straps/clothing on LE.
Treatment: Sit or stand patient to lower BP (DO NOT lie them down). Relieve noxious stimuli immediately. Loosen tight clothing.
Avoid increasing BP — risk of hemorrhagic stroke.

Bladder, Bowel & Sexual Function

Bladder: Spastic bladder (UMN — uncontrollable contractions) vs flaccid bladder (LMN — overflow). Bladder dysfunction is most common presentation. Catheter management critical.
Sexual function in males: Reflexive erection (PNS efferents S2–4), psychogenic erection (SNS efferents T11–L2). Emission from T10–L2. Ejaculation from S2–4 somatic efferents. Function depends on level and completeness.
Skin care: Pressure relief every 15–30 minutes. Position changes. Inspect skin daily. Shear forces, moisture, nutrition all affect integrity.

Psychosocial — DABDA & Community Reintegration

DABDA grief stages: Denial, Anger, Bargaining, Depression, Acceptance. Plus anxiety.
Coping with loss: Physical loss (dependence), physique changes (atrophy), perception changes (where am I in space), interaction with environment, financial security, social isolation.
Behavior changes can be from: sensory deprivation, lack of time cues, sleep deprivation, pain, O₂ desaturation, psychoactive medication, anesthesia effects, head injury — figure out the actual problem before assuming it's part of the injury.Community reintegration: Teaching physical skills is NOT enough. Help with self-advocacy. Involve the whole family.
NEVER imply patient is a second-class citizen. Independence not dependence. Positive atmosphere. Educational model not medical model. Hope and truth. Counseling, peer support, social support.

HIV/AIDS & CNS Infections
HIV is the virus that causes AIDS. As viral load increases, CD4+ count decreases (inverse relationship). As immunity is compromised, patients develop AIDS-defining illnesses. Up to 70% of HIV patients have some form of neurological symptom. Progression from HIV to AIDS was linear prior to ART (antiretroviral therapy).

Transmission & ART

Transmission hierarchy: MSM (men who have sex with men) > IDU (injection drug use) > MSM + IDU > Heterosexual > Other (hemophilia, blood transfusion).
ART (Antiretroviral Therapy): Viral load dependent. Changes to regimen due to resistance = salvage therapy. Doctor begins ART when CD4 ≤350.
HIV wasting syndrome: ≥10% baseline body weight loss in 12 months or 5% in 6 months with chronic diarrhea or unexplained weakness/fever. Treatment: nutritional counseling, medications, resistance strength training. ART can compromise nutrition (side effects) and cause lipodystrophy.

PT Implications for HIV/AIDS

Wear gloves for inpatient (outpatient is up to you), mask for sure. Stress decreases CD4 counts. Exercise helps maintain immune function.
Assessment: Balance (standing — Romberg, tandem), coordination (finger-to-nose, pronation/supination), 6MWT, strength (MMT), sensation, CN 10 (vagus), reflexes, hearing extinction, grip strength, mini-mental exam, short-term memory.
Treatment priorities: ADL/functional, gait/balance, strength, coordination, aerobic conditioning.
Education: Medication management, opportunistic infection awareness, motivation for independence. Consider speech/OT referral.
Use it or lose it — neuroplasticity principles apply. Motivation matters. Start with gait belt, AD, parallel bars for stability. Progress walking forward/backward, over obstacles, treadmill with incline, medicine ball, push/pull sled, sit-to-stand.

Multiple Sclerosis — Subtypes, Symptoms & Management
MS is an autoimmune disease where the immune response triggers activation of immune cells that cross the blood-brain barrier, producing cytotoxic effects within the CNS. Disruption of the myelin sheath slows neural transmission and causes nerves to fatigue rapidly. With severe disruption, conduction block occurs with resulting loss of function. MS attacks the myelin sheath of axons in the CNS (usually UMN presentation with spasticity).

Uhthoff's Phenomenon

Heat sensitivity — MS symptoms temporarily worsen with increased body temperature. Even a small rise (0.5°C) can worsen symptoms. Exercise in cool environments. Use cooling strategies. Cold pool > warm pool. Symptoms improve when temperature returns to normal. This is NOT a relapse — it's a temporary conduction block in demyelinated fibers.

MS vs Parkinson's

MS: Myelin sheath of axons is attacked (usually UMN → spasticity). Relapsing-remitting pattern common. Exercise in COLD environments.
PD: Substantia nigra dopamine neurons die (BG → rigidity). Patients are often more aware of their condition. No relapsing-remitting pattern — progressive.
Key distinction: MS = spasticity (velocity-dependent). PD = rigidity (not velocity-dependent, cogwheel/lead pipe).

MS Treatment Approach

Acute care setting: DC safely to next level of care, maximize functional potential. MS-EDGE outcome measures recommended for standardized assessment. Treat based on current presentation (varies with relapses/remissions). Address fatigue management (energy conservation, pacing, rest breaks). Exercise during remission. Avoid overheating. Strength training within tolerance — do NOT push to fatigue (worsens symptoms). Balance and gait training. Fall prevention. Depression screening and referral. Long-term management with periodic reassessment.

ALS — Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis
AKA Lou Gehrig's Disease. Combined UMN + LMN motor neuron death — both corticospinal tract AND anterior horn cells are affected. Sensation is INTACT. Cognition is usually intact. 30,000 individuals in the US have ALS; 15 are diagnosed each day. Average onset: mid-to-late 50s. More rapid decline compared to MS and PD. No definitive diagnostic test exists — diagnosis is made by ruling everything else out.

Etiology, Diagnosis & Clinical Presentation

▼
Etiology & Risk Factors

No single mechanism — multiple/cumulative mechanisms.
SOD (Superoxide dismutase) decrease: Loss of this enzyme → buildup of oxidized free radicals → neuron damage.
Glutamate excess: Excitatory neurotransmitter — excess leads to cell death (excitotoxicity). Increased glutamate found in CSF, plasma, and postmortem tissue.
Autoimmune reaction implicated in several studies.
Risk factors: Disease-causing mutations (SOD1, alsin), male gender, family history, age.
Possible factors: Neurotoxicant exposures, vigorous physical activity, trauma, diet, certain occupations.

Diagnosis Requirements

Diagnosis requires ALL of: (1) LMN signs by clinical, electrophysiological, or neuropathological exam. (2) UMN signs by clinical exam. (3) Progression within a region or to other regions. (4) Absence of other disease processes.
Testing: Lab studies, EMG, NCV, muscle/nerve biopsies, neuroimaging — used to SUPPORT diagnosis (rule out other conditions). One genetic test exists (SOD1 mutation).
DDx to rule out: Brainstem gliomas, central cord syndrome, CIDP, Lambert-Eaton, Lyme disease, MS, neurosarcoidosis, polymyositis, syringomyelia, primary lateral sclerosis, spinal muscular atrophy.

LMN Presentation

Most frequent presenting impairment: focal, asymmetrical muscle weakness beginning in LE, UE, or bulbar muscles. Bulbar dysfunction: dysarthria (speech rate and voice quality), dysphagia. Muscle atrophy, fasciculations, hyporeflexia, decreased tone. Bulbar onset: Dysarthria, dysphagia first — poorer prognosis. Limb onset: Asymmetric distal weakness first — slightly better prognosis.

UMN Presentation

Hyperreflexia, spasticity, pathological reflexes (Babinski, Hoffmann's), clonus. Slow/awkward fine movements. The COMBINATION of both UMN + LMN signs is what makes ALS unique — most other conditions show one or the other. Fasciculations + atrophy (LMN) PLUS hyperreflexia + spasticity (UMN) in the same patient = ALS until proven otherwise.

Strength Curve, Therapeutic Window & PT Management

▼
Strength Curve & Therapeutic Window

ALS patients exist on a declining strength curve. The therapeutic window is 35–80% of maximum (daily activities and mild training). Below 35% = too weak for meaningful exercise benefit. Above 80% = overwork damage risk — can accelerate motor neuron death. Exercise must stay within the therapeutic window. High-intensity/high-resistance exercise is contraindicated — overwork weakness is a real danger. Focus on moderate aerobic conditioning, gentle ROM, functional activities, and energy conservation.

PT Management Priorities

Early stage: Maintain function, moderate exercise, ROM, aerobic conditioning within therapeutic window. Core strengthening, functional activities the patient can do on their own.
Middle stage: Compensatory strategies, assistive devices, energy conservation, fall prevention. Address respiratory decline (diaphragmatic breathing, assistive cough techniques).
Late stage: Positioning, comfort, skin integrity, PROM, respiratory support, caregiver training.
Respiratory failure is the #1 cause of death in ALS. Monitor FVC (forced vital capacity) — decline indicates respiratory muscle involvement. Equipment needs progress over time: cane → walker → WC → power WC → hospital bed → ventilator.
Average survival: 2–5 years from diagnosis. PT focus: maintain function as long as possible, maximize QOL at every stage.

These approaches form the toolbox for neuro PT. The key is knowing WHAT you are doing and WHY — matching the right technique to the patient's specific deficits. Treatment should encourage motor learning by assessing the patient's ability to participate and assisting when needed.

Transfers, Balance & Pre-Gait Progressions

▼
Transfer Types

Lift-Off Transfer: For patients who cannot stand.
Bobath Transfer: NDT-based facilitation of normal movement patterns during transfer.
Squat Pivot: NWB on one leg.
Standing Pivot: THA, TKA, TTWB, FWB.
Sliding Board: SCI, bilateral NWB. Match transfer to WB status, strength, balance, and cognitive ability.

Sitting Balance Re-Establishment

Feet and knees close. Weight even hip to hip. Anterior pelvic tilt. Lumbar and thoracic extension. Head balanced on level shoulders. Early goal = re-establish control in sitting for function: AP upper body initiated movements, AP lower body initiated, lateral lower body, rotational movements. Progress to: dynamic reaching, weight shifting on unstable surfaces, dual-task challenges.

Plantigrade Position

Stable posture with wide BOS and high COM. Progress: flat hand → fingertip support → bilateral → unilateral UE support → free stand. Symmetrical stance → step position. Alternative: hands on large unstable surface for increased challenge. Sideways to wall with shoulder abduction for unilateral WB. Motor control goal: Stability (static postural control). Functional skill: Patient maintains upright standing with UE support independently with minimal sway. This is a lead-up activity to standing without UE support.

Pre-Gait & AD Selection

Pre-gait: Bed mobility (supine to sit) → sitting transfers (bed to chair) → gait training → stairs.
DF wrapping: For foot drop — normal DF is 10–15°.
Pusher Syndrome: Patient actively pushes toward hemiplegic side, resisting correction toward midline. Use visual feedback, weight shifting toward strong side, environmental cues.
AD selection criteria for community independence: Community ambulation of ≥250 ft, can drive, NO balance issues.

TBI, Glasgow Coma Scale, Rancho Levels & Differential Diagnosis
TBI is an alteration in brain function caused by an external force. Shear forces cause axonal damage. The subarachnoid space is highly vascular — could burst and blood can enter the brain. Monitoring ICP is critical: 0–10 is normal, >20 is worrying, 25 is threshold. Knowing the anatomy involved will help you deliver effective treatment — monopolizing on strengths, building upon weaknesses.

TBI Evaluation Approach

You walk in and see: Worried family, lots of tubes.
Assess: A&O (simple commands first), squeeze fingers (motor response), test CNs, skin integrity, PROM, Ashworth scale (rigid vs spastic), proprioceptive awareness, facial expressions (not tolerating movement — could recoil).
Monitor: ICP (≥17–18 = take a break, ≥20 = no therapy), BP, HR. Gradually elevate HOB (20° to 45°) to assess tolerance.
Treatment: Family education (PROM, proprioception). EOB — even 30 seconds is meaningful. Basic position changes. NG tube (nasal gastric — nutrition). Foley catheter (urine drainage).

Client has L MCA stroke. What deficits do you expect and why is L MCA the most common?
tap
L MCA is most common because of anatomically direct line from the aorta and heart. Expected deficits: R hemiparesis/hemiplegia (face and arm > leg — MCA supplies lateral cortex where face/arm homunculus is represented). R hemisensory loss. Broca's aphasia (non-fluent speech production) and/or Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but meaningless speech, impaired comprehension) since L is dominant for language in 95% of RH people. R homonymous hemianopsia. Apraxia. Behavioral: L damage = unmotivated, hard to start, aware of deficits but limited. Subluxed shoulder: Check sulcus sign. Treat with closed-chain approximation exercises.
What is the method to differentiate cerebellar ataxia from sensory ataxia?
tap
Cerebellar ataxia: Ipsilateral deficits. "Drunken gait" — uneven step length, wide BOS, absent rhythm, feet lifted too high, absent arm swing. Falls toward side of lesion. Dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesia, intention tremor, oculomotor deficits. Vision does NOT help if vestibulocerebellum is involved. Romberg may be positive but vision won't compensate. Sensory ataxia: From posterior column–medial lemniscus damage. Loss of joint position sense. Wide-based unsteady gait that IMPROVES with visual feedback (positive Romberg — worse with eyes closed). No dysmetria or intention tremor. Key test: Does vision help? Yes = sensory ataxia. No = cerebellar. Also: ataxia from pons or cerebellar peduncle lesion can mimic cerebellar ataxia even if cerebellum itself is intact.
What makes ALS unique compared to other neuro conditions, and what is the therapeutic window in this scenario?
tap
ALS is unique because it has BOTH UMN + LMN signs simultaneously. Fasciculations + atrophy (LMN) PLUS hyperreflexia + spasticity (UMN) in the same patient. Sensation is INTACT (unlike MS or SCI). Cognition is usually intact. No definitive diagnostic test — diagnosis by ruling everything else out. Therapeutic window: 35–80% of maximum. Below 35% = too weak to benefit. Above 80% = overwork damage risk, can accelerate motor neuron death. Never push ALS patients to fatigue. Focus on moderate activity, energy conservation, respiratory support. Bulbar onset (speech/swallowing first) has poorer prognosis than limb onset. Average survival: 2–5 years. Respiratory failure = #1 cause of death.
T4 complete SCI patient develops sudden severe HTN and bradycardia. In this case, What is happening and what do you do?
tap
Autonomic dysreflexia — MEDICAL EMERGENCY. Occurs in SCI at T6 and above (this patient is T4). Massive sympathetic response causing widespread vasoconstriction and severe HTN. S/S: HTN, bradycardia, diaphoresis, headache, blotchy/red skin, piloerection. Most common triggers: UTI, fecal impaction, pressure ulcers, tight clothing on LE. Immediate treatment: (1) SIT or STAND the patient — do NOT lie them down (lowers BP). (2) Relieve the noxious stimulus immediately (check catheter, loosen clothing, check for skin breakdown). (3) Monitor BP. Risk: Uncontrolled HTN can cause hemorrhagic stroke. This is why we never use tight clothing, always check catheters, and educate patients/caregivers about triggers.
Child with posterior fossa brain tumor has abnormal head posturing, balance deficits, and nausea. In this case, What's the biggest concern?
tap
Tonsillar herniation — the child could die. Posterior fossa tumors (commonly medulloblastoma in children) grow into the 4th ventricle, blocking CSF flow → hydrocephalus → increased ICP. As ICP rises, the cerebellar tonsils can herniate through the foramen magnum, compressing the brainstem → respiratory arrest → death. Medulloblastoma: Always malignant. Arises from primitive neuroectodermal cells. 20% of all childhood brain tumors. 5-year survival 61%. General brain tumor signs: Headache (30%), seizures, altered mental status, papilledema. The nausea is concerning because it can indicate rising ICP. Not: vomiting risk, agitation, hitting a doorframe, or bathroom accident. The life-threatening concern is always herniation with posterior fossa tumors.
What is Rhythmic Initiation and when do you use it in this scenario?
tap
Rhythmic Initiation (PNF technique): Goal is to promote learning of a new movement, improve intra/intermuscular coordination, promote relaxation and independent movement. 4 phases: (1) Passive: "Relax, let me move you." (2) Active-assisted: "Now help me move you." (3) AROM: "Now move on your own." (4) Resisted: "Now push." Indications: Inability to relax, hypertonicity (spasticity AND rigidity), difficulty initiating movement, uncoordinated movement, motor planning or learning deficits (apraxia/dyspraxia), communication deficits (aphasia). Why aphasia? The 4-phase progression provides kinesthetic learning without relying heavily on verbal instructions — the patient FEELS the movement pattern before being asked to produce it. Key: This is for learning NEW movements. For ROM limitations, use Contract-Relax or Hold-Relax instead.
MS patient asks about exercising. In this case, What do you tell them about heat, fatigue, and exercise type?
tap
Uhthoff's phenomenon: MS symptoms temporarily worsen with ANY increase in body temperature — even 0.5°C. This is NOT a relapse. It's a temporary conduction block in demyelinated fibers that resolves when temperature normalizes. Exercise guidelines: Cool environment (cold pool > warm pool). Stay hydrated. Use cooling strategies (pre-cooling, cooling vest). Fatigue is the #1 complaint in MS — not just physical, also cognitive. Do NOT push to fatigue (worsens all symptoms). Energy conservation, pacing, rest breaks are essential. Exercise within tolerance: moderate aerobic, strength training at submaximal levels, balance training. Time it right: Exercise during remission, not relapse. Morning often better than afternoon. Short frequent sessions > long sessions. MS vs PD reminder: MS = spasticity (velocity-dependent, UMN). PD = rigidity (not velocity-dependent, BG).
Identify the 4 incomplete SCI syndromes and which has the best prognosis?
tap
Anterior Cord: Flexion injuries. Loss of motor function AND pain/temp below level. Proprioception/vibration PRESERVED (posterior columns intact). Central Cord: Hyperextension injuries (most common incomplete). UE affected MORE than LE. Sensory less than motor. Usually recover to ambulate. Posterior Cord: Rare. Loss of proprioception, vibration, touch. Motor and pain/temp preserved. Brown-Séquard: Hemisection. Ipsilateral weakness/paralysis + proprioceptive loss. Contralateral pain/temp loss. BEST PROGNOSIS = Brown-Séquard.Remember: Posterior columns cross in medulla (ipsilateral loss below lesion). Spinothalamic (pain/temp) crosses in cord (contralateral loss). This anatomy explains the split pattern in Brown-Séquard. Also: Cauda equina = LMN pattern (roots, not cord). Conus medullaris = terminal cord.
