Neuroanatomy · Clinical Neuroscience

Neuroanatomy — Complete Clinical Reference

The nervous system controls everything — every movement, every sensation, every thought. For PTs, neuroanatomy is not abstract science; it is the roadmap for localizing lesions, predicting deficits, and designing rehabilitation. A stroke in the MCA territory produces different deficits than one in the PCA territory. A spinal cord lesion at C5 produces different functional outcomes than one at T10. Understanding pathways (corticospinal, dorsal columns, spinothalamic) lets you connect examination findings to anatomy. This module covers neural cells, sensory and motor pathways, cranial nerves, brainstem, cerebellum, basal ganglia, vascular supply, and stroke syndromes.

Cells of the Nervous System & Neural Development
Before you can understand any pathway, you must understand what the pathways are made of. Neurons are the information processors; glia are the support staff. Understanding which glial cell myelinates which type of axon explains why GBS and MS produce different deficits. Development explains why brainstem lesions have sensory tissue lateral and motor tissue medial — the opposite of the spinal cord.
NS Organization — CNS vs PNS
Central Nervous System (CNS) = Brain + Spinal Cord

Brain: Cerebrum (telencephalon + diencephalon) · Brainstem (midbrain + pons + medulla oblongata) · Cerebellum. Spinal cord. Weighs approximately 3–3.5 lbs.

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

Cranial nerves + Spinal nerves + autonomic ganglia. Bundles of nerve fibers in connective tissue (nerves). Clusters of neuronal cell bodies outside CNS (ganglia — vs. nuclei in CNS).

Gray Matter vs White Matter

Gray matter: cell bodies + dendrites. In CNS = nuclei. In PNS = ganglia. (Note: "basal ganglia" is a historical misnomer — they are actually nuclei.) White matter: myelinated axons (myelin appears white). White matter terminology: fasciculus = bundle; funiculus = cord-like column (anterior/lateral/posterior of SC); lemniscus = ribbon (medial/lateral lemniscus in brainstem); peduncle = stalk (cerebellar peduncles); tract = trail (spinal tracts); commissure = fibers crossing midline (corpus callosum, anterior commissure, hippocampal commissure); projection fibers = cortex ↔ lower CNS; association fibers = within cortex.

Neuron Anatomy — Parts & Function
Soma (Cell Body)

Contains nucleus and most organelles. Primary site of synaptic input (afferent). Contains receptors for neurotransmitters on subsynaptic membrane.

Dendrites

Extensions of soma. Primary site of synaptic input. Contain neurotransmitter receptors. Information flows INTO the neuron via dendrites.

Axon Hillock — The Integration Zone

Anatomical transition from soma to axon. Physiological transition from graded potential to action potential. The initial segment has the lowest threshold for AP — this is where the decision to fire is made.

Axon

Long, thin efferent process. Up to 400 mm/day axonal transport. Anterograde transport (soma → knobs): kinesin protein motor. Retrograde transport (knobs → soma): dynein protein motor. [Clinical: tetanus toxin uses retrograde transport to reach inhibitory interneurons.] May be surrounded by myelin with Nodes of Ranvier — AP conduction speed increases with larger diameter and more myelin.

Synaptic Knobs (Terminal Boutons)

Swellings at end of axons. Contain vesicles of neurotransmitter. Presynaptic neuron releases NT into synaptic cleft. Postsynaptic neuron detects NT. EPSP = depolarizes postsynaptic cell (closer to threshold, more likely to fire). IPSP = hyperpolarizes postsynaptic cell (further from threshold, less likely to fire).

Contiguous vs Saltatory Conduction

Contiguous conduction (unmyelinated): AP must be recreated at every point along the membrane. Slower. Saltatory conduction (myelinated): AP "jumps" from one Node of Ranvier to the next. Much faster. This is why demyelinating diseases slow conduction — the insulating myelin sheath is damaged and the AP can no longer jump efficiently.

Neurotransmitters — Clinical Pharmacology Connections
Excitatory Fast — ACh & Glutamate

Acetylcholine (ACh): neuromuscular junction, autonomic NS. Glutamate: estimated 90%+ of CNS synapses — the primary excitatory NT in the brain. Both have fast ionotropic receptors and slower metabotropic receptors.

Inhibitory Fast — GABA & Glycine

GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid): primarily in brain. Target of benzodiazepines and barbiturates (enhance GABA-A activity → sedation/anxiolysis). Glycine: primarily in spinal cord. Target of tetanus toxin (blocks glycine release → spastic paralysis — see below).

Slow / Second Messenger (Catecholamines, Serotonin)

Catecholamines: dopamine (basal ganglia, reward), norepinephrine (sympathetic, arousal), epinephrine. Serotonin: mood, sleep, appetite. Target of SSRIs. Neuropeptides (neuromodulators) also act via second messengers.

Clinical: Diseases & Drugs at the Synapse
  • Curare: blocks nicotinic ACh receptors → flaccid paralysis (used in surgical anesthesia)
  • Botulism: toxin blocks ACh exocytosis at NMJ → flaccid paralysis (therapeutic use: Botox)
  • Tetanus: retrograde axonal transport → inhibitory interneurons of SC → blocks glycine → unopposed excitation → spastic paralysis ("lockjaw")
  • Myasthenia Gravis: autoimmune destruction of nicotinic ACh receptors → weakness, fatigability, double vision (treated with AChE inhibitors)
Glial Cells — The Support Infrastructure
Astrocytes (CNS)

Connective tissue of CNS. Hold neurons in position. Form the blood-brain barrier — chemically protect neurons by regulating what crosses from blood to CNS. React to CNS injury (reactive astrogliosis).

Oligodendrocytes (CNS) — Myelinate CNS Axons

Myelinate axons in CNS. One oligodendrocyte can myelinate multiple segments of multiple axons. Target of autoimmune attack in Multiple Sclerosis.

Schwann Cells (PNS) — Myelinate PNS Axons

Myelinate axons in PNS. Each Schwann cell myelinates a single specific region of one axon. Also phagocytic — clean up myelin debris after injury. Target in Guillain-Barré syndrome. Unlike oligodendrocytes, Schwann cells support peripheral nerve regeneration after injury.

Ependymal Cells (CNS)

Line ventricles and central canal of spinal cord. Specialized ependymal cells within ventricles form the choroid plexus — produce CSF. Secretory, absorptive, and CSF circulatory roles.

Microglia (CNS)

Phagocytosis of degenerative cellular debris in CNS. Primary immune defense of CNS. Become activated macrophage-like cells after CNS injury or infection.

Neural Development
The embryological sequence from ectoderm to neural tube to adult brain structures is clinically important because developmental errors (failure of tube closure) explain specific congenital conditions. The sulcus limitans explains brainstem organization. The primary-to-secondary vesicle sequence explains why brain structures are organized the way they are.
Embryological Sequence
3rd Week — Neural Tube Formation

Ectoderm thickens → neural groove forms → neural folds meet → neural tube closes (begins near future cervical SC, closure spreads rostrally and caudally). Tube cavity → future ventricles + central canal. Neural crest cells escape as tube fuses → form PNS (DRG cells, autonomic ganglia, Schwann cells, etc.).

4th Week — Sulcus Limitans & Vesicles

Sulcus limitans: longitudinal groove dividing tube into dorsal alar plate (sensory) and ventral basal plate (motor). At the 4th ventricle level, alar plate is pushed apart → ends up lateral to basal plate. Result: lateral brainstem = more sensory; medial brainstem = more motor (opposite of spinal cord). Primary vesicles develop: prosencephalon, mesencephalon, rhombencephalon.

Developmental Disorders
Spina Bifida — Caudal Tube Fails to Close

Failure of caudal end of neural tube to fuse. Folic acid supplementation significantly reduces incidence. Myelomeningocele: no vertebrae form; neural tissue continuous with skin — most severe form. Often accompanied by Arnold-Chiari malformation (cerebellum and brainstem pushed into foramen magnum → hydrocephalus).

Anencephaly — Rostral Tube Fails to Close

Failure of rostral end of neural tube to close/fuse. Much or all of the cerebral hemispheres fail to form. Incompatible with survival. Also prevented by folic acid supplementation.

Holoprosencephaly — Prosencephalon Fails to Divide

Spectrum of disorders from failure of the prosencephalon to properly separate into diencephalon and telencephalon. Usually fatal. Typically accompanied by facial midline abnormalities (cyclopia in most severe form). Illustrates the link between facial and brain midline development.

Somatosensory Pathways
Three-neuron chains carry sensory information from receptor to cortex. The key clinical question is always: WHERE did the signal decussate? Dorsal column/medial lemniscus crosses in the medulla → ipsilateral SC deficits. Spinothalamic tract crosses immediately at the level of entry → contralateral SC deficits. This single anatomical fact is the basis of Brown-Séquard syndrome.
Dorsal Column / Medial Lemniscus (DC/ML) Pathway — Discriminatory Touch, Vibration, Conscious Proprioception from BODY
1st Order Neuron — Receptor to Dorsal Column

Receptors: Meissner's corpuscle (light touch), Merkel complex (pressure/texture), Pacinian corpuscle (vibration/deep pressure), hair follicles, Ruffini corpuscles, proprioceptive receptors (muscle spindles, GTOs). Cell bodies in Dorsal Root Ganglion (DRG). Enter spinal cord near Lissauer's tract. Enter dorsal columns (white matter between dorsal horns). Somatotopic: highest spinal levels most lateral, lowest levels most medial. Fasciculus gracilis = lower extremity (below T6), more medial. Fasciculus cuneatus = upper extremity (rostral to T6), more lateral.

2nd Order Neuron — Decussation in Caudal Medulla

Synapse in dorsal column nuclei in caudal medulla. Nucleus gracilis receives LE input. Nucleus cuneatus receives UE input. Axons decussate here (at caudal medulla) and form the medial lemniscus. Somatotopy REVERSES: LE is now lateral, UE is now medial (opposite of dorsal columns).

3rd Order Neuron — Thalamus to S1

Synapse in Ventral Posterolateral nucleus (VPL) of thalamus. Somatotopy: LE lateral, UE medial. Axons travel through internal capsule → Primary Somatosensory Cortex (S1) in postcentral gyrus. Medial to lateral: LE, UE, face. S2 = texture and roughness.

DC/ML Lesion Signs
  • Astereognosis: cannot recognize objects/patterns by touch or manipulation
  • Loss of conscious proprioception and kinesthesia
  • Sensory ataxia: wide-based, unsteady gait from proprioceptive loss
  • Steppage gait: high-stepping, foot-slapping due to loss of proprioception
  • Romberg's sign: sway and fall with eyes closed (eyes were compensating for proprioceptive loss)
Tabes Dorsalis

Late-stage neurosyphilis causing dorsal column degeneration. Classic presentation: lightning pains, profound sensory ataxia, Romberg positive, Argyll Robertson pupils (accommodate but don't react to light). Rarely seen now due to antibiotic treatment of syphilis.

Trigeminal Touch Pathway — Discriminatory Touch from FACE

CN V (Trigeminal) carries discriminatory touch from face. Same receptors as DC/ML. Cell bodies in trigeminal ganglion. Enter brainstem at midpons. Synapse in main sensory nucleus of CN V (ipsilateral). Decussate → join medial lemniscus (most medial position). 3rd order synapse in VPM thalamus (medial to VPL) → internal capsule → S1 (face lateral region of homunculus).

Lateral Spinothalamic Tract (STT) — Pain & Temperature from BODY
The Pain vs. Nociception Distinction

Pain: "An unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage" (IASP). Pain is an emotion. Nociception: Encoding and processing of harmful stimuli — a purely sensory term without emotional connotation. The two are anatomically separated (different cortical targets): posterior insula = pain localization; anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) = emotional affect of pain.

1st Order Neuron — Nociceptors

Aδ fibers (lightly myelinated): fast/sharp/pricking pain. C fibers (unmyelinated): slow/aching/throbbing/burning pain. Both are free nerve endings responding to mechanical, thermal, and chemical stimuli. Cell bodies in DRG. Enter spinal cord via Lissauer's tract.

2nd Order Neuron — Immediate Decussation

Synapse in Lamina I (superficial dorsal horn). Fibers decussate almost immediately at the same spinal level, passing through the anterior gray commissure. Travel up the contralateral Lateral Spinothalamic Tract (anterolateral funiculus). Somatotopy: caudal inputs posterolateral, rostral inputs anteromedial. CLINICAL KEY: pain from the right side of the body travels UP THE LEFT side of the spinal cord.

3rd Order Neuron — VMpo Thalamus to Insula/ACC

Synapse in Ventral Medial posterior nucleus (VMpo) of thalamus. Somatotopy: face anterior, foot posterior (orthogonal to VPL/VPM arrangement). VMpo projects to: posterior insula (pain localization — analogous to S1 for touch), S1 cortex, and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) (emotional/affective component). The anterior insula receives input from all three → "feeling" of pain.

Trigeminal Pain/Temperature — FACE

CN V enters at pons. Fibers travel caudally through the spinal tract of CN V (analogous to Lissauer's tract) → synapse in the spinal nucleus of CN V (analogous to dorsal horn) near the cervico-medullary junction. Decussate immediately → join contralateral STT. 3rd order: VMpo → middle insular cortex, S1, ACC.

Anterior STT — Crude Touch & Deep Pressure

2nd order neurons from Laminae II, V, VII. Decussate at level of origin. Travel in anterior STT. 3rd order synapse in MDvc thalamus → fibers to ACC. Crude touch = poorly localized ("something, but not sure where"). This pathway is NOT C-fiber dependent.

Pain Modulation — Descending Control

Central pain suppression via descending signals from periaqueductal gray (PAG) and rostral medulla. Endogenous opiates (β-endorphins) are released — bind to opioid receptors on dorsal horn neurons → reduce pain signal transmission. Basis for opioid analgesics and some mechanisms of acupuncture/TENS-induced analgesia.

Brown-Séquard Syndrome — Spinal Cord Hemisection

Classic exam question. Damage to ONE HALF of the spinal cord. Results in a "dissociated sensory loss" — different deficits on each side below the lesion.

Ipsilateral to lesion (same side):
· Loss of DC/ML function (discriminatory touch, vibration, conscious proprioception) — because DC/ML stays ipsilateral until medulla
· UMN signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski) — CST crossed at pyramidal decussation above

Contralateral to lesion (opposite side):
· Loss of pain and temperature — because STT crosses immediately at level of entry

Cause: spinal cord tumor, multiple sclerosis, stabbing injury, herniated disc (rarely complete). Classic example of why knowing where each pathway decussates is clinically essential.

Motor Control Systems
Motor control is a hierarchy: cortex sets intention, CST delivers the command, lower motor neurons execute it. The extrapyramidal system shapes tone, posture, and antigravity. The autonomic NS runs the viscera. Knowing whether a lesion is UMN or LMN — and which specific UMN — determines whether you see spasticity or flaccidity, hyperreflexia or areflexia, preserved or lost atrophy.
Corticospinal Tract (CST) — The Pyramidal System
Origin — Motor Cortex Hierarchy

Premotor cortex & Supplementary motor area (SMA): initiation of voluntary movement. Fire BEFORE movement begins — even if movement is only imagined or contemplated. Receive inputs from prefrontal cortex (decision) and somatosensory association areas. Primary motor area (M1): precentral gyrus, frontal lobe. Final cortical output. Motor homunculus = somatotopic map. Lowest threshold to induce movement. ~50% of CST fibers originate here.

CST Pathway Through Brainstem

All three motor areas → posterior limb of internal capsule (dense, somatotopic) → cerebral peduncle (midbrain, stays ipsilateral) → pons → pyramidal decussation at caudal medulla. Lateral CST (~85%): fibers that cross at pyramids → travel in lateral funiculus of contralateral SC → act on lateral ventral horn neurons → distal/limb muscles. Medial/anterior CST (~15%): stay ipsilateral at pyramids → travel in ventral funiculus → usually cross low in SC → influence medial ventral horn neurons → axial muscles. Both pathways project to both α- and γ-motor neurons (alpha/gamma coactivation).

Corticobulbar Pathway — Motor CNs V, VII, X, XI, XII

Controls facial muscles (expression, mastication), laryngeal/pharyngeal muscles, SCM/trapezius, tongue. Upper face: bilateral cortical control (both hemispheres supply). Lower face: unilateral control (contralateral hemisphere only). Clinical significance: UMN lesion (above facial motor nucleus) → ONLY lower face weakness ipsilateral to lesion side. LMN lesion (facial nerve itself = Bell's palsy) → entire ipsilateral face weak (upper + lower). CN XII: tongue → damage causes tongue to deviate TOWARD the weak side (weak side pushes less).

Lower Motor Neurons (LMN) & Spinal Reflexes
α-Motor Neurons — Organization of Ventral Horn

Innervate skeletal muscle (extrafusal fibers). Organization: Medial = axial muscles; Lateral = peripheral/limb muscles. Dorsal = flexors; Ventral = extensors. Motor unit = one α-motor neuron + ALL muscle fibers it innervates. Small motor units (3:1 ratio, e.g., ocular muscles) = precise control. Large motor units (1000+:1, e.g., gastrocnemius) = powerful but imprecise.

Spinal Reflexes

Stretch reflex: muscle spindle → Ia afferent → monosynaptic excitation of α-motor neuron + reciprocal inhibition of antagonist (via Ia inhibitory interneuron). Basis of DTRs (patellar, Achilles). Flexor withdrawal reflex: nociceptors → withdrawal from painful stimulus. Crossed extensor reflex: when one limb flexes, contralateral limb extends — signals must decussate. Basis of locomotion (central pattern generators).

Extrapyramidal Pathways — Posture, Tone, & Reflexive Movement
Vestibulospinal Tracts — Anti-Gravity & Balance

Lateral vestibulospinal tract: from lateral vestibular nucleus → anti-gravity muscles throughout body → compensation for tilt and body movement. Primary pathway for postural changes to tilts/movements. Medial vestibulospinal tract: bilateral projection to cervical SC → stabilizes and coordinates head and eye movements (via medial longitudinal fasciculus, MLF).

Reticulospinal Tract — Postural Control

From reticular formation. Major alternative to pyramidal pathway. Postural control during locomotion. Medullary (lateral) reticulospinal: inhibits trunk extensors. Pontine (medial) reticulospinal: activates trunk extensors (anti-gravity). Damaged in many UMN conditions — contributes to spastic patterns.

Tectospinal & Rubrospinal

Tectospinal: from superior/inferior colliculus → reflexive head turning toward visual and auditory stimuli. Rubrospinal: from red nucleus → minor in adult humans. Major in infants' crawling (before CST myelination). Red nucleus is a critical relay in cerebellar→cerebral motor pathways.

Decorticate vs Decerebrate Rigidity

Decorticate rigidity (lesion ROSTRAL to red nucleus): Flexion of elbows, wrists, fingers + extension and internal rotation of legs. The red nucleus drives arm extension; with cortex disconnected, it dominates in legs but not arms. Mnemonic: "cor" → corTEX → arms go to CORE (curl in).

Decerebrate rigidity (lesion CAUDAL to red nucleus): Extension AND internal rotation of ALL four limbs. Without red nucleus input, the pontine reticulospinal (extensor-activating) dominates everything. Decerebrate posturing is a more ominous neurological sign than decorticate.

Clinical: CST Damage at Different Levels
  • Cerebral cortex (stroke): focal contralateral motor deficit (face, hand, limb depending on location)
  • Internal capsule (lacunar stroke): widespread contralateral hemiparesis
  • Brainstem: often bilateral, associated with cranial nerve and sensory deficits
  • Spinal cord: increased tone (spasticity) + sensory deficits (other pathways also damaged)
Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)
Sympathetic Division — "Fight or Flight" (Thoracolumbar T1–L2)

Preganglionic neurons in lateral horn (IML) of T2–L1 SC. Short distance to paravertebral ganglia. Preganglionic: myelinated, cholinergic. Postganglionic: unmyelinated, adrenergic (NE). Target tissues: heart (↑ HR), lungs (bronchodilation), gut (↓ peristalsis), pupil dilation, sweating, piloerection, adrenal medulla (epinephrine release).

Parasympathetic Division — "Rest & Digest" (Craniosacral)

Cranial: CN III (pupil constriction, lens accommodation), CN VII (lacrimal + salivary glands), CN IX (parotid gland), CN X (heart, lungs, GI — from esophagus to mid-transverse colon). Sacral: S2–S4 (distal GI, bladder, genitalia). Preganglionic: myelinated, cholinergic. Postganglionic: unmyelinated, cholinergic (ACh on muscarinic receptors).

Enteric Nervous System — The "Second Brain"

Neurons and plexuses controlling the GI tract independently. Myenteric plexus (motility) + submucosal plexus (secretion/absorption). Has as many or more neurons than the spinal cord. Functions independently of CNS — explains why GI function persists in high spinal cord injuries. Influenced by sympathetic/parasympathetic but operates autonomously.

ANS Clinical: Horner's Syndrome

Disruption of descending sympathetic pathway to the eye and face (hypothalamus → brainstem → C8-T2 SC → superior cervical ganglion). Classic triad: Ptosis (drooping upper eyelid — loss of superior tarsal muscle), Miosis (pupil constriction — unopposed parasympathetic), Anhidrosis (decreased sweating ipsilateral face). Also: apparent enophthalmos (eye looks sunken). Seen in: Wallenberg syndrome (lateral medullary), Pancoast tumor (lung apex), carotid artery dissection, syringomyelia, or central cord injury at C8–T2.

Cerebellum — Coordination, Tone & Motor Learning
"Little brain" — 10% of brain volume, 50% of brain neurons. The cerebellum DOES NOT initiate movement and DOES NOT cause paralysis. It is the comparator and coordinator: comparing intended movement with actual movement, correcting errors, timing sequences, and building motor programs through learning. Cerebellar deficits are ipsilateral due to the double-decussation effect of cerebellar output and the CST.
Anatomy — Regions & Deep Nuclei
Anatomical Divisions

Vermis (medial strip): axial motor function, trunk coordination. Paravermis/medial hemispheres: ipsilateral limb coordination (feedback system). Lateral hemispheres: largest region; motor planning and learning (feedforward system). Flocculonodular lobe: receives vestibular input; balance and VOR.

Deep Cerebellar Nuclei — Primary Output

Fastigial nucleus: receives from vestibulocerebellum and vermis. Output → vestibular/reticular nuclei → postural control. Interposed nucleus (globose + emboliform): receives from spinocerebellum (medial hemispheres/paravermis). Output → contralateral motor thalamus → motor cortex → limb control (feedback). Dentate nucleus (largest, most lateral): receives from cerebrocerebellum. Output → contralateral motor thalamus → motor and premotor cortex → skilled, planned movements (feedforward). Most common site of cerebellar damage.

Cerebellar Cortex Cells

Purkinje cells: ONLY fiber type exiting cerebellar cortex. Project to deep cerebellar nuclei. Enormous dendritic trees — up to 200,000 synapses per cell. Inhibitory (GABAergic). Granule cells: most numerous (~50 billion, ~75% of all brain neurons). Their axons = parallel fibers. Excitatory. Climbing fibers: from inferior olivary nucleus via ICP. One climbing fiber synapses with one Purkinje cell. Mossy fibers: all other inputs. Synapse on granule cells, which relay to Purkinje cells. Most mossy fibers are from cerebral cortex via pontine nuclei.

Three Cerebellar Peduncles — Input/Output Routes
Superior Cerebellar Peduncle (SCP)

Primarily OUTPUT (efferents from dentate, interposed nuclei → decussate in midbrain → contralateral motor thalamus). Also carries anterior spinocerebellar tract input (proprioception from LE). The SCP DECUSSATES — combined with CST decussation at pyramids, this is why LEFT cerebellum controls LEFT limbs (double decussation = net ipsilateral effect).

Middle Cerebellar Peduncle (MCP)

Largest peduncle. Primarily INPUT from pontine nuclei (which receive from cerebral cortex). This corticopontocerebellar pathway is 10–12× larger than the corticospinal tract — reflecting the massive scope of cerebellar involvement in all movements. Input to cerebrocerebellum (lateral hemispheres).

Inferior Cerebellar Peduncle (ICP)

Mixed input/output. INPUT: proprioception from dorsal spinocerebellar tract (trunk/LE), climbing fibers from inferior olive, vestibular inputs (direct CN VIII). OUTPUT: from fastigial nucleus → vestibular and reticular nuclei → vestibulospinal tract (balance/posture). Output of vestibulocerebellum exits via ICP (most fibers decussate).

Three Functional Zones — Pathways & Clinical Signs
Vestibulocerebellum

Region: Flocculonodular lobe + vermis (uvula). Input: CN VIII and vestibular nuclei via ICP (only direct sensory nerve input to cerebellum). Deep nucleus: Fastigial. Output: via ICP → vestibular/reticular nuclei → vestibulospinal tracts. Function: VOR, smooth eye movements, postural control. Lesion signs: Disequilibrium (truncal/gait ataxia), hypotonia of trunk/proximal LE, nystagmus (involuntary rhythmic eye movements), strabismus (eyes don't align).

Spinocerebellum

Region: Vermis (axial) + medial hemispheres/paravermis (limbs). Input: proprioception via spinocerebellar tracts via ICP. Deep nuclei: Fastigial (vermis/trunk) + Interposed (limbs). Output: via SCP → decussate → contralateral motor thalamus → motor cortex. Function: Ongoing feedback correction during movement. Neurons fire DURING movement. Lesion signs: Truncal ataxia ("drunken sailor" wide-based gait), rebound phenomenon (loss of check reflex — arm overshoots when resistance suddenly released), scanning dysarthria (irregular emphasis on syllables).

Cerebrocerebellum

Region: Lateral hemispheres (largest, most common damage site). Input: cerebral cortex → pontine nuclei → via MCP. Deep nucleus: Dentate (largest). Output: via SCP → decussate → contralateral motor thalamus (VL/VA) → motor and premotor cortex. Function: Motor planning and learning; feedforward correction. Neurons fire BEFORE movement. Lesion signs: Dyssynergia (jerky sequential movements), dysmetria (misjudges distance — overshoot/undershoot), intention tremor (worsens as target approached), hypotonia, reduced spinal reflexes, difficulty timing/stopping/changing direction.

The Double Decussation Rule — Why Cerebellar Deficits Are Ipsilateral

Cerebellar output crosses via the superior cerebellar peduncle (decussation #1) → reaches contralateral motor thalamus → motor cortex. That cortex then sends CST fibers which cross at the pyramidal decussation (decussation #2) → reach the ipsilateral spinal motor neurons. Two crossings cancel out: left cerebellum → left limbs. This is why cerebellar lesions cause ipsilateral deficits — and why Wallenberg syndrome, though a brainstem stroke, produces cerebellar signs on the SAME side as the lesion.

Inferior Olivary Nucleus & Cerebellar Learning
Inferior Olivary Nucleus (IO)

Located in rostral medulla. Receives widespread inputs (spinal cord, cerebral cortex, red nucleus, cerebellar feedback). Sends climbing fibers via ICP to Purkinje cells (contralateral). Forms a circuit: IO → cerebellar cortex → dentate nucleus → red nucleus → IO. Function unclear — but lesions of IO produce symptoms similar to lateral cerebellar damage. Believed to be involved in motor error signaling and cerebellar long-term depression (a cellular mechanism of motor learning).

Basal Ganglia — Motor Gating, Tone & Movement Disorders
The basal ganglia don't initiate movement — they modulate the motor cortex's drive via the thalamus. Too little thalamic drive = hypokinesia (Parkinson's). Too much = hyperkinesia (Huntington's). Understanding the direct vs. indirect pathway gives you a principled model for predicting what dopamine depletion does and why levodopa treats Parkinson's.
Anatomy of the Basal Ganglia
Striatum — Primary Input Structure

Caudate nucleus: cognitive/associative loop (from association cortex → prefrontal cortex). Putamen: motor loop (from somatosensory/motor cortex → motor cortex). Nucleus accumbens: limbic loop (motivation, reward, addiction). Together = striatum (+ ventral striatum).

Output Structures

Globus Pallidus internal (GPi): primary output of basal ganglia → GABAergic (inhibitory) → thalamus. Substantia Nigra reticularis (SNr): co-output with GPi. Both tonically INHIBIT the thalamus (motor thalamus VA/VL).

Modulatory Structures

Globus Pallidus external (GPe): part of indirect pathway; inhibitory. Subthalamic Nucleus (STN): part of indirect pathway; excitatory. Target of deep brain stimulation (DBS) for Parkinson's. Substantia Nigra compact (SNc): dopaminergic neurons that modulate striatal activity. Degenerate in Parkinson's disease.

Direct vs. Indirect Pathway — Thalamic Drive
Direct Pathway — FACILITATES Movement (↑ thalamic drive)
→

Motor cortex (excites) → Striatum → (inhibits) GPi/SNr → (disinhibits = allows) Thalamus → (excites) Motor cortex → movement

Dopamine from SNc excites direct pathway striatal neurons (D1 receptors). Net effect: MORE thalamic drive = MORE movement. Damage = hypokinesis.

Indirect Pathway — INHIBITS Movement (↓ thalamic drive)
→

Motor cortex (excites) → Striatum → (inhibits) GPe → (disinhibits) STN → (excites) GPi → (inhibits) Thalamus → (less excitation to) Motor cortex = less movement

Dopamine from SNc inhibits indirect pathway striatal neurons (D2 receptors). Net effect: LESS STN→GPi drive = MORE thalamic drive = MORE movement. Damage here = hyperkinesis.

Dopamine Summary

Dopamine FACILITATES movement — both by exciting direct pathway (which inhibits GPi → releases thalamus) AND by inhibiting indirect pathway (which would otherwise excite GPi → inhibit thalamus). Loss of dopamine (Parkinson's) → both pathways shift to inhibit thalamus → bradykinesia/hypokinesia.

Rigidity vs. Spasticity — Critical Distinction

Rigidity (basal ganglia/extrapyramidal): NOT speed-dependent. Resistance is the same whether you move the limb fast or slow. Bidirectional (flexion and extension both resist). Lead-pipe rigidity: constant, uniform resistance. Cogwheel rigidity: periodic "ratcheting" resistance (tremor + rigidity). Spasticity (pyramidal/UMN): speed-dependent ("clasp-knife"). Moves slow → less resistance; moves fast → high resistance. Often unidirectional.

Parkinson's Disease — SNc Degeneration

Prevalence: 100–200/100,000 over age 60. Pathology: cell loss in substantia nigra compact (SNc) → ↓ dopamine. Direct pathway inhibited + indirect pathway dominant → ↓ thalamic drive → bradykinesia/hypokinesia.

Primary Motor Signs:
· Bradykinesia — slowing of voluntary movement
· Difficulty initiating movement — use center of gravity to start walking; festinating gait
· Rigidity — lead-pipe or cogwheel
· Resting tremor — classic "pill-rolling" at 3–6 Hz, decreases with intentional movement
· Flexed posture (lean forward) + micrographia (small handwriting) + masked facies + monotone speech

Non-Motor: dysautonomia (urge incontinence, constipation), depression, potential dementia, REM sleep behavior disorder.

PT: LSVT (Lee Silverman Voice Treatment), cueing strategies, treadmill training, balance training, fall prevention.

Huntington's Disease — GPe Degeneration

Prevalence: 5/100,000. Genetics: autosomal dominant (CAG repeat in HTT gene); each child has 50% chance. Onset typically 30–50 years. Diagnosis is a life-changing event for entire family.

Pathology: Loss of GABAergic neurons in GPe → indirect pathway inhibited → ↑ thalamic drive → hyperkinesis. Later: striatal + cortical loss also develops → dementia and eventual slowing of movements.

Clinical Signs:
· Chorea — initially restlessness/fidgeting; progresses to face → arms → whole body
· Dementia — attention/executive function/short-term memory deficits
· Mood changes, depression, psychosis

No cure. PT focuses on safety, fall prevention, assistive devices, dysphagia management as disease progresses.

Basal Ganglia Functional Regions — Motor, Cognitive, Emotional
Motor Loop — Putamen

From somatosensory cortex → putamen → GPi/SNr → motor thalamus → motor cortex. Controls execution and scaling of movements. Lesions: movement disorders (Parkinson's, chorea). Putamen is the primary motor arm of basal ganglia.

Cognitive Loop — Caudate

From association cortical areas → caudate nucleus → GPi/SNr → mediodorsal thalamus → prefrontal cortex. Involved in executive function, habit learning, and procedural memory. Caudate degeneration in Huntington's → executive dysfunction and attention deficits precede motor signs.

Limbic Loop — Nucleus Accumbens (Ventral Striatum)

From limbic areas (amygdala, hippocampus, orbital/cingulate cortex) → nucleus accumbens (+ head of caudate + ventral putamen) → ventral pallidum → mediodorsal thalamus → anterior cingulate. Motivation, reward, pleasure, positive reinforcement. Central to addiction pathophysiology.

Special Senses — Vision, Vestibular, Auditory, Olfactory, Gustatory
The special senses are clinically tested in virtually every neurological examination. Visual field deficits localize lesions along the optic pathway. The vestibular system drives eye movements, balance, and postural control — lesions produce nystagmus, vertigo, and falls. Auditory processing is heavily bilateral, which is why unilateral cortical damage rarely causes deafness. Smell is the only sense with direct cortical access, bypassing the thalamus entirely.
Visual System — Three Parallel Pathways
Geniculostriate Pathway — Primary Visual Pathway

Receptors: Rods (night/grayscale) and cones (daylight/color) in retina. Retina is an extension of the diencephalon — optic nerve is technically a CNS tract, not a peripheral nerve. Optic chiasm: hemidecussation — nasal retinal fibers (representing temporal visual fields) cross; temporal retinal fibers (nasal visual field) stay ipsilateral. Result: ALL information from RIGHT visual field → LEFT cerebral cortex. Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN): thalamic relay with 2 components: parvocellular layers (4) = color/form → ventral "what" pathway; magnocellular layers (2) = motion/contrast → dorsal "where" pathway. Optic radiation: splits into superior path (inferior visual field) and inferior path (superior visual field) through Meyer's loop in temporal lobe. Primary visual cortex (V1, area 17) in occipital lobe along calcarine sulcus.

Ventral "What" Pathway vs. Dorsal "Where" Pathway

Ventral "what": LGN parvocellular → V1/V2 → inferior temporal cortex. Processes color, form, object and face recognition. Lesion: visual agnosia (cannot recognize objects by sight), prosopagnosia (cannot recognize faces, even own), achromatopsia (color blindness from cortical damage). Dorsal "where": LGN magnocellular → V1/V2 → posterior parietal cortex. Processes motion, depth, spatial location. Lesion: motion blindness. Note: significant cross-communication exists between streams.

Tectal Pathway — Pupillary Reflex & Eye Tracking

Bypasses LGN. → Pretectal nucleus → Edinger-Westphal nucleus (bilateral) → pupillary light reflex (both pupils constrict to light in either eye). → Superior colliculus → visual tracking, saccades, orienting responses; also tectospinal tract (reflexive head turning). Basis for clinical pupil exam.

Hypothalamic Pathway — Circadian Rhythms

Special light-sensitive ganglion cells (not rods/cones) → suprachiasmatic nucleus of hypothalamus → entrains internal circadian clock to light-dark cycle. Internal clock runs ~25.3 hours without light cues. Explains jet lag and sleep disruption in visually impaired patients.

Vestibular System — Balance, Head Orientation & Eye Stabilization
Peripheral Receptors

Semicircular canals (3): anterior, posterior, horizontal — orthogonal planes. Sense angular acceleration (rotation). Hair cells in crista ampullaris within gelatinous cupula. Rotation in one direction increases AP firing; opposite direction decreases firing. Utricle: horizontal linear acceleration (e.g., walking forward). Saccule: vertical linear acceleration (e.g., gravity, jumping). Both use maculae with otolithic membranes (calcium carbonate crystals/otoliths). Inertia of otoliths bends hair cells.

Central Processing — Vestibular Nuclei (4)

Inferior, medial, lateral, superior vestibular nuclei in brainstem. Integrate: vestibular sensory input + cerebellar (flocculonodular) feedback + spinal cord somatosensory + visual inputs. Efferents: lateral vestibulospinal tract (anti-gravity postural muscles, all levels), medial vestibulospinal tract (cervical SC, head/eye coordination via MLF).

Vestibuloocular Reflex (VOR)

Gaze stays fixed even when head is moving. Faster than visual tracking (shake your head vs. shake your book). 3-neuron arc: CN VIII → vestibular nuclei → CNs III, IV, VI motor nuclei. Extremely fast latency. Coordinated via the Medial Longitudinal Fasciculus (MLF) — bilateral tracts connecting vestibular nuclei to extraocular CN nuclei. MLF lesion (e.g., MS): internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO) — impaired conjugate lateral gaze with nystagmus in abducting eye and incomplete adduction ipsilateral to lesion.

Nystagmus — Clinical Application

Physiological nystagmus: normal response when head rotation exceeds VOR capacity. Keeps visual image stable on retina. Pathological nystagmus: from vestibular system dysfunction (peripheral or central). Caloric nystagmus: warm/cold water in ear creates convection current in endolymph → artificial semicircular canal stimulation → nystagmus. Clinical use: COWS mnemonic (Cold Opposite, Warm Same = direction of fast phase). Tests brainstem function in unconscious patients.

Auditory Pathway — Bilateral Representation
Pathway Overview

Sound → auricle → external canal → tympanic membrane → ossicles (malleus → incus → stapes) → oval window → cochlea → organ of Corti (basilar membrane + hair cells in tectorial membrane) → spiral ganglia (CN VIII bipolar neurons) → cochlear nuclei (ipsilateral, dorsal and ventral) → superior olivary nuclei (bilateral — first binaural processing) → inferior colliculus → medial geniculate nucleus (MGN) of thalamus → primary auditory cortex (area 41) in superior temporal gyrus (Heschl's gyrus). Tonotopic organization throughout (frequency map).

Why Unilateral Lesions Rarely Cause Deafness

After the cochlear nuclei, auditory information spreads bilaterally via the superior olivary nuclei. Both temporal lobes receive input from both ears. Unilateral temporal lobe damage = very subtle auditory changes (some trouble localizing sound; auditory agnosia only with more complete lesions). Bilateral temporal lobe damage required for deafness. This explains why coup-contrecoup brain injuries can cause deafness — bilateral temporal contusions.

Olfactory & Gustatory Systems
Olfactory — Direct Cortical Access (NO Thalamic Relay)

Unique among all senses: olfactory information reaches the cerebral cortex WITHOUT synapsing in the thalamus. Olfactory nerve (CN I) → olfactory bulb → olfactory tract → primary olfactory cortex (prepyriform cortex, amygdala, hippocampus) → orbitofrontal cortex (conscious smell perception). Direct limbic access explains powerful emotional and memory associations with smell (Proust phenomenon). Anosmia (CN I damage) = loss of smell; also one of the earliest signs of Alzheimer's disease.

Gustatory (Taste) — Uncrossed Pathway

CN VII = anterior 2/3 of tongue (chorda tympani branch). CN IX = posterior 1/3 of tongue. CN X = epiglottis and pharynx. All converge on nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) in medulla. → thalamus (VPM, medial portion) → gustatory cortex (anterior insula / frontal operculum). Pathway is predominantly uncrossed (ipsilateral). Important: CN VII carries taste from anterior tongue via the chorda tympani — this branch separates from the facial nerve before the stylomastoid foramen, so proximal Bell's palsy damage may include taste loss; distal damage does not.

Limbic System & Hypothalamus
The limbic system controls emotion, memory, and behavior — "feel, feed, fight, flee, and sexual behavior." Its primary outputs go through the hypothalamus to the ANS. The hypothalamus is the homeostasis hub. The amygdala is the fear/aggression center. The hippocampus converts short-term to long-term memories. Damage to these structures produces some of the most striking and complex clinical syndromes in neurology.
Hypothalamus — The Homeostasis Hub
Anatomy & Location

Part of diencephalon. Inferior to thalamus. Within floor and walls of 3rd ventricle. Surrounds infundibulum (pituitary stalk). Sits directly above the optic chiasm and the circle of Willis. Small but controls: temperature, water balance, food intake, pituitary hormones, emotion, circadian rhythms.

Anterior pituitary: controlled by releasing hormones from arcuate and other nuclei via portal blood system (CRH → ACTH, GHRH → GH, TRH → TSH, GnRH → FSH/LH, etc.). Korsakoff Syndrome — Mammillary Body Damage

Caused by chronic alcoholism + Vitamin B1 (thiamine) deficiency. Damage to mammillary bodies and mediodorsal thalamus. Classic presentation: profound anterograde amnesia (cannot form new memories) + confabulation (unconsciously fabricates memories to fill gaps) + temporal/spatial disorientation. Called "amnesic confabulatory syndrome." The confabulation is NOT intentional lying — the patient genuinely believes their fabricated memories. Often coexists with Wernicke encephalopathy (confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia) — together = Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome.

Amygdala — Fear, Aggression & Emotional Memory
Function & Connections

Critical hub connecting sensory association input with hypothalamus. Receives wide range of sensory inputs: visual, auditory, somatosensory, gustatory, pain — especially from insula, anterior cingulate cortex, and hypothalamus. Processes: fear, rage/aggressiveness, and feeding behavior. Connects to mammillary bodies (via fornix) and directly to hypothalamus. Projects to brainstem → ANS responses to emotional stimuli (heart racing with fear).

Amygdala Lesion — Placidity & Loss of Fear

Bilateral amygdala damage → loss of fear, rage, and aggressiveness. Patient becomes placid, fearless, hypersexual, puts everything in mouth (hyperorality), shows visual agnosia. This is Klüver-Bucy Syndrome (bilateral amygdala + hippocampus + anterior temporal lobe damage). Note: damage to different sub-regions of amygdala may produce different emotional dysregulation — some lesions may promote certain behaviors rather than suppress them.

Hippocampus — Memory Consolidation
Function — Short → Long Term Memory

Converts short-term memory (<1 hour) into long-term memory storage. Large output via the fornix (lies within lateral ventricles). Works with surrounding entorhinal cortex for memory encoding and retrieval. Also involved in spatial navigation and the formation of cognitive maps (place cells).

Hippocampal Lesions — Anterograde Amnesia

Must be bilateral to produce significant deficits (some preservation with unilateral). Anterograde amnesia: inability to form NEW long-term memories after the lesion. Previously stored long-term memories remain intact. Patient can hold a conversation normally (working memory intact) but will not recall the conversation later (cannot consolidate). Classic case: H.M. (Henry Molaison) — bilateral hippocampal removal for epilepsy → could not form any new episodic memories for the rest of his life.

Other Limbic Components
Nucleus Accumbens — Reward & Addiction

Part of ventral striatum. Involved in motivation, reward, pleasure, and positive reinforcement. The neurobiological substrate of addiction. Dopamine release here produces the "reward" sensation — drugs of abuse all ultimately increase dopamine in nucleus accumbens. Target of current addiction pharmacology research.

Cingulate Gyrus — Surrounds Corpus Callosum

Part of limbic lobe. Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC): pain affect (emotional response to pain), motivation, attention. Damage/lesion → akinesia, mutism, apathy, indifference to pain. Posterior cingulate: spatial orientation, episodic memory.

Mediodorsal Thalamus — Memory & Affect

Role in affective behavior and memory. Connects to amygdala, prefrontal cortex, and cingulate. Damaged in Korsakoff syndrome. Also involved in working memory and emotional processing. One of the thalamic nuclei activated during emotional experiences.

Brainstem & Cranial Nerves
The brainstem is the crossroads — every ascending and descending pathway passes through it, and 10 of the 12 cranial nerves originate here. Brainstem lesions produce crossed deficits: ipsilateral CN signs + contralateral motor/sensory long tract signs. Know the 3 classic brainstem stroke syndromes and every CN cold.
Brainstem Organization — 3 Levels
Midbrain

Superior colliculus (visual reflexes, saccades), inferior colliculus (auditory reflexes), cerebral peduncles (CST passing through), substantia nigra (dopamine), red nucleus (rubrospinal/cerebellar relay), Edinger-Westphal nucleus (CN III parasympathetics → pupil constriction). Aqueduct of Sylvius (CSF conduit — obstruction = hydrocephalus).

Pons

Basilar pons (descending CST, corticopontine fibers). Tegmentum (ascending sensory tracts, reticular formation). CN nuclei: V (trigeminal — main sensory + motor), VI (abducens), VII (facial), VIII (vestibulocochlear). MLF (medial longitudinal fasciculus) coordinates conjugate gaze — bilateral tracts connecting CN III/IV/VI nuclei.

Medulla

Pyramids (CST) + pyramidal decussation (caudal medulla — where ~85% of CST fibers cross). Dorsal column nuclei (nucleus gracilis + cuneatus → medial lemniscus). Inferior olive. CN nuclei: IX (glossopharyngeal), X (vagus), XI (spinal accessory), XII (hypoglossal). Nucleus ambiguus (IX/X motor). Nucleus tractus solitarius (taste/visceral afferents).

Sulcus Limitans Rule — Lateral = Sensory, Medial = Motor

In the brainstem, sensory nuclei are LATERAL and motor nuclei are MEDIAL. At the 4th ventricle the alar plate (sensory) is pushed laterally, so lateral brainstem = more sensory, medial = more motor. This explains Wallenberg: lateral medulla lesion = sensory/vestibular/cerebellar deficits, NO paralysis (pyramids = medial, spared).

Wallenberg Syndrome — Lateral Medullary / PICA Syndrome

▼
Vascular Supply & Anatomy

PICA (posterior inferior cerebellar artery) supplies the lateral medulla and inferior cerebellum. PICA depends entirely on the vertebral artery (VA) — VA occlusion can produce the same syndrome. ~10% of population has one underdeveloped VA (higher risk). Most common brainstem stroke syndrome.

Structures Damaged → Signs
  • Descending sympathetic fibers → Ipsilateral Horner's syndrome
  • Spinal nucleus/tract CN V → Ipsilateral face pain & temp loss
  • STT (already crossed) → Contralateral body pain & temp loss
  • Vestibular nuclei (CN VIII) → Nystagmus, vertigo (bilateral)
  • Nucleus ambiguus (CNs IX/X/XI) → Dysphagia, hoarseness
  • Inferior cerebellar peduncle + cerebellum → Ipsilateral ataxia, hypotonia
  • CN XII usually SPARED (hypoglossal nucleus is medial)
Horner's Syndrome — Complete Sign Set

Ipsilateral: Ptosis (drooping lid — superior tarsal muscle), Miosis (constricted pupil), Anhidrosis (absent facial sweating), Enophthalmos (sunken eye appearance). Loss of ciliospinal reflex. Caused by interruption of descending sympathetic fibers: hypothalamus → lateral brainstem → IML at T2.

What Is PRESERVED (why no paralysis)

Corticospinal tract runs in the MEDIAL medulla (pyramids) — not touched by lateral medullary infarct. Medial lemniscus (discriminatory touch/proprioception) is also medial — preserved. Key memory: Lateral medulla = everything EXCEPT motor paralysis and fine touch.

Locked-In Syndrome — Basilar Pons Infarct

▼
Mechanism

Infarct in the base of the pons from lower paramedian basilar artery occlusion. Basilar pons contains the descending CST. Bilateral destruction of both CST here → quadriplegia. Pontine tegmentum (reticular activating system) is SPARED → patient remains fully conscious.

Preserved — Vertical Gaze & Blinking

Vertical gaze controlled by midbrain (CN III/IV nuclei, superior colliculus) — above the pons infarct. Blinking controlled by CN VII nucleus in CAUDAL pons — may be below infarct. Horizontal gaze lost (CN VI nucleus is in pons). All voluntary communication relies on eye movement coding (e.g., eye-tracking AAC devices).

Full Clinical Picture
  • Quadriplegia: all 4 limbs + lower CN motor nuclei — can't move, speak, or swallow
  • Conscious and aware: reticular formation (tegmentum) intact
  • Can feel pain: STT intact (pain/temp information from body)
  • Bilateral loss: discriminatory touch/vibration/proprioception (medial lemniscus damaged)
  • Preserved: vertical gaze + blinking (midbrain/caudal pons intact)
  • Prognosis: poor — most patients do not recover significantly

Parinaud Syndrome — Dorsal Midbrain

▼
Cause & Location

Typically pineal gland or midbrain tumor compressing dorsal midbrain (non-vascular cause most common). Vascular: superior cerebellar artery or PCA/basilar branches. Also: hydrocephalus (dilated aqueduct pushes on dorsal midbrain), MS, trauma. Structures damaged: superior colliculus, pretectal area, cerebral aqueduct.

4 Classic Signs
  • Paralysis of upgaze: can't look up (vertical gaze center in dorsal midbrain)
  • Light-near dissociation: pupil does NOT constrict to light but DOES constrict to near stimulus (convergence intact)
  • Convergence-retraction nystagmus: eyes retract and converge on attempted upgaze
  • Eyelid retraction (Collier's sign): wide-eyed appearance. In infants with hydrocephalus: "sunset sign" — eyes forced downward
Cerebral Cortex — Lobes, Language & Lesion Syndromes
The cortex is organized into primary areas (direct sensory/motor), unimodal association areas (one modality), and multimodal areas (integrate multiple modalities). The dominant hemisphere (left in 95% of right-handers) controls language production and comprehension. The non-dominant hemisphere controls spatial attention. Damage is predictable — know which lobe, which side, which exact area.

Frontal Lobe — Motor Control, Language Production & Executive Function

▼
Primary Motor Area (Area 4) — M1

Precentral gyrus. Motor homunculus — medial = leg, lateral = face. Lowest threshold to induce movement. Half of CST fibers from here. Lesion: initial contralateral flaccid paralysis → evolves to spastic hemiparesis over days-weeks. More obvious in distal limb/skilled movements. Combined M1+premotor (areas 4+6) damage = full spastic hemiplegia.

Broca's Area (Areas 44, 45) — Dominant Hemisphere

Left inferior frontal gyrus. Motor programs for language generation ("premotor for speech"). Broca's aphasia (nonfluent/expressive): halting, telegraphic, effortful speech; missing function words. Comprehension largely intact. Often right-sided arm/leg weakness (adjacent motor cortex involved). Agraphia. Non-dominant areas 44/45 → prosody expression (melody of speech). Lesion = motor aprosodia (flat, robotic speech).

Frontal Eye Fields (Area 8)

Saccade initiation and guidance. Destructive lesion = conjugate gaze TOWARD lesion (can't look contralateral). Irritative/seizure lesion = conjugate gaze AWAY from lesion. Memory: destruction → eyes roll toward the "dead" side.

Prefrontal Cortex (Areas 10–12)

Dorsolateral PFC: working memory, planning, sustained attention. Lesion: poor planning, attention deficits. Ventromedial PFC (orbitofrontal + anterior cingulate): emotion regulation, behavior switching, reward-based decisions. Connected to amygdala. Lesion (Phineas Gage): impulsiveness, poor decisions, perseveration, inability to suppress emotions, akinetic mutism (conscious but won't move or speak).

Parietal, Temporal & Occipital Lobes — Sensation, Language & Vision

▼
Primary Somatosensory (S1, Areas 3,1,2)

Postcentral gyrus. Sensory homunculus. Cutaneous and proprioceptive input from DC/ML and STT. Lesion: contralateral anesthesia/hypesthesia. Does NOT eliminate pain (pain → posterior insula via VMpo).

Non-Dominant Parietal (Right) — Neglect

Right parietal controls spatial attention for BOTH sides. Left parietal controls only right side. Right parietal lesion → profound left hemispatial neglect: ignores left visual field/left side of objects, motor neglect (won't use left limbs), anosognosia (denies the deficit). Extinction: bilateral simultaneous stimuli → perceives only right. Clinical: patient doesn't dress left side, doesn't eat left side of plate — not non-compliance.

Dominant Parietal (Left) — Apraxia & Gerstmann's

Ideomotor apraxia: can't execute complex motor actions on command despite intact motor strength and comprehension. CAN do it spontaneously (scratch nose) but CANNOT when asked. Gerstmann's syndrome (angular gyrus, area 39): agraphia + acalculia + finger agnosia + right-left disorientation.

Wernicke's Area (Area 22) — Dominant Temporal

Left posterior superior temporal gyrus. Production AND interpretation of written and spoken language. Wernicke's aphasia (fluent/receptive): fluent (talks too much), paraphasias and neologisms (wrong/invented words), severely impaired comprehension. Patient doesn't know speech is wrong. No weakness. Non-dominant area 22: prosody comprehension. Lesion = sensory dysprosodia.

Arcuate Fasciculus — Conduction Aphasia

White matter tract connecting Wernicke's to Broca's. Lesion: conduction aphasia — fluent speech + intact comprehension + severely impaired REPETITION. Signature: patient cannot repeat a sentence just spoken to them. The disconnection prevents auditory representation from reaching motor speech area.

Occipital Lobe

Area 17 (primary visual): retinotopic, large foveal representation. Areas 18/19 (visual association): begin dorsal ("where") and ventral ("what") streams. Lesions: visual agnosia (can't recognize objects), prosopagnosia (can't recognize faces), achromatopsia (color blindness), alexia (can't read). Homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing from V1 lesion.

The 3-Question Algorithm

Ask: (1) Fluent? Nonfluent = anterior (Broca's). (2) Comprehension intact? Impaired = posterior (Wernicke's). (3) Repetition intact? Impaired despite fluency + intact comprehension = arcuate fasciculus (conduction). Intact repetition with other deficits = transcortical (arcuate spared).

Stroke & Cerebrovascular Anatomy
Stroke is the #3 cause of death in the US — 700,000 cases/year, 20% mortality. FAST: Face, Arm, Speech, Time. Ischemic (thrombus/emboli, ~85%) vs. hemorrhagic (bleeding, ~15%). Every clinical sign maps to a vascular territory. Know the Circle of Willis, the 3 main cerebral arteries, the brainstem vessels, and the lacunar stroke patterns cold.

Flow: 2 lateral ventricles → foramina of Monro → 3rd ventricle → aqueduct of Sylvius → 4th ventricle → foramina of Magendie (midline) + Luschka (lateral) → subarachnoid space. Total volume: 150 mL. Ischemic vs. Hemorrhagic & Risk Factors
Ischemic (~85%) — Thrombus or Emboli

Decrease in blood flow → neuron death within minutes. Medical priority: tPA (thrombolytic) within 3–4.5 hours. "Time is brain" — ~1.9 million neurons die per minute of ischemia. Sources: cardiac emboli (atrial fibrillation most common), carotid atherosclerosis, intracranial thrombosis.

Hemorrhagic (~15%) — Vessel Rupture

Bleeding into brain parenchyma. NO tPA (contraindicated — worsens bleeding). Primary destruction from hemorrhage + secondary from rising ICP. Causes: hypertension (most common small vessel), anticoagulation therapy, cocaine use. Treatment: BP control, surgical hematoma evacuation if indicated.

Risk Factors

Hypertension (>140/90) · Age >55 · Family history · Elevated cholesterol · Smoking · Diabetes · Obesity (BMI >30) · AF and other cardiac disease · Prior stroke or TIA. TIA: symptoms resolve within 24 hours (usually <1 hr). Major warning sign — markedly increased stroke risk in days following TIA.

Circle of Willis — ACA, MCA, PCA Territories

▼
Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA)

Supplies medial hemisphere: motor + sensory cortex for the LOWER EXTREMITY and foot (medial homunculus), prefrontal cortex. Small perforators → internal capsule/basal ganglia. ACA infarct: contralateral LE > arm weakness and sensory loss. Behavioral/personality changes (prefrontal). Urinary incontinence (medial frontal lobe — bladder control area).

Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) — Most Common

Largest territory. Lateral frontal/parietal/temporal lobes — motor + sensory for trunk and UPPER EXTREMITY (face > arm > leg), association areas. MCA infarct: contralateral face/arm weakness + sensory loss. Dominant: Broca's/Wernicke's/global aphasia. Non-dominant: contralateral neglect, anosognosia, aprosodia. Lenticulostriate branches → internal capsule → lacunar strokes.

Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA)

Occipital lobe + inferior temporal lobe. Small branches to thalamus and midbrain. PCA infarct: contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing (V1 lesion — dual blood supply + large foveal representation). Visual agnosia, prosopagnosia, achromatopsia. If thalamic branches → contralateral hemisensory loss. If midbrain → CN III palsy.

Lacunar Strokes — Small Vessel Perforators

▼
Mechanism

Small perforating arteries supply: internal capsule, thalamus, pons, basal ganglia. Pathology: micro-deposits of connective/fatty tissue block arteries → small necrotic "lacune." Macrophages clear the tissue over weeks-months — literally leaves a small hole on MRI. NOT caused by atherosclerosis (different mechanism). Risk factors: hypertension (most critical), diabetes, smoking.

4 Classic Lacunar Patterns
  • Pure motor: contralateral hemiparesis, face/arm/leg — internal capsule (posterior limb)
  • Pure sensory: contralateral hemisensory loss — thalamus (VPL/VPM)
  • Mixed motor + sensory: both — thalamus + adjacent internal capsule
  • Ataxic hemiparesis: cerebellar + motor signs — pons or internal capsule (cerebellar tract + CST both involved)

Vertebral & Basilar Arteries — Posterior Circulation

▼
Vertebral Artery Branches

Anterior spinal artery: anterior spinal cord. Occlusion = bilateral motor loss + pain/temp loss but PRESERVED vibration/proprioception (posterior columns have separate posterior spinal arteries). PICA (posterior inferior cerebellar artery): lateral medulla + inferior cerebellum. Depends entirely on VA — VA occlusion can produce Wallenberg syndrome. ~10% of population has one underdeveloped VA.

Basilar Artery

R+L vertebral arteries merge at pontomedullary junction → basilar artery ascending anterior to pons → bifurcates into R+L PCAs (part of Circle of Willis). Branches: AICA (anterior/inferior cerebellum + lateral pons), pontine perforators (CST in pons), SCA (superior cerebellum + midbrain), PCAs. Complete basilar occlusion: catastrophic — bilateral pontine infarction, extreme poor prognosis.

AICA Syndrome — Lateral Pontine

Anterior inferior cerebellar artery supplies lateral pons + anterior/inferior cerebellum. Infarct: ipsilateral face pain/temp loss (CN V spinal nucleus), CN VI (lateral gaze palsy), CN VII (facial weakness), CN VIII (sensorineural hearing loss + vestibular), Horner's syndrome (descending sympathetics), ipsilateral limb ataxia. Contralateral body pain/temp loss (STT).

SCA Syndrome — Superior Cerebellar

Superior cerebellar artery supplies superior cerebellum + lateral midbrain portions. Infarct: Parinaud syndrome (dorsal midbrain), ipsilateral cerebellar signs (ataxia, dysmetria). Contralateral pain/temp loss if STT involved. May include CN IV palsy if midbrain affected. SCA is the most rostral cerebellar artery — its occlusion affects the newest (cerebrocerebellum) regions.

List the 5 types of glial cells, their locations (CNS vs. PNS), and their primary functions?

Trace the complete DC/ML pathway from the left foot to the cortex. In this case, Name every synapse, decussation, and tract name.

1st order neuron: receptor (Meissner's, Merkel, Pacinian, muscle spindle, GTO) → DRG → enters spinal cord near Lissauer's tract → travels IPSILATERALLY up the dorsal column as fasciculus gracilis (LE info, below T6, most medial position). Where does pain/temperature decussate, and why is this the basis of Brown-Séquard syndrome in this scenario?
tap
Pain and temperature 1st order neurons (Aδ and C fibers) enter the spinal cord via Lissauer's tract → synapse in Lamina I (superficial dorsal horn) → 2nd order axons decussate IMMEDIATELY (within 1–2 spinal levels) via the anterior gray commissure → travel up the contralateral lateral spinothalamic tract. In Brown-Séquard (hemisection at, e.g., right T6): Right DC/ML fibers are cut → ipsilateral (right) loss of proprioception and discriminatory touch below T6. Right lateral STT is cut — but those fibers already crossed from the LEFT side → contralateral (left) loss of pain and temperature below T6. Motor (right CST) is cut → ipsilateral (right) UMN signs below T6. Classic mnemonic: "Ipsilateral paralysis and proprioception loss; contralateral pain and temperature loss."
Compare the 3 cerebellar zones: anatomy, input peduncle, deep nucleus, output, and what lesion signs each produces.
tap
Vestibulocerebellum (flocculonodular lobe): Input = ICP from CN VIII/vestibular nuclei. Deep nucleus = Fastigial. Output = ICP → vestibular/reticular nuclei → vestibulospinal tracts. Lesion: disequilibrium, truncal hypotonia, nystagmus, strabismus. Spinocerebellum (vermis + medial hemispheres): Input = ICP (spinocerebellar tracts — proprioception). Fires DURING movement (feedback). Deep nuclei = Fastigial (trunk/vermis) + Interposed (limbs). Output = SCP decussates → contralateral VA/VL thalamus → motor cortex. Lesion: truncal ataxia (drunken sailor gait), rebound/check reflex loss, scanning dysarthria. Cerebrocerebellum (lateral hemispheres): Input = MCP from contralateral cortex via pons (10–12× CST size). Fires BEFORE movement (feedforward). Deep nucleus = Dentate. Output = SCP decussates → contralateral thalamus → motor/premotor cortex. Lesion (IPSILATERAL): dyssynergia, dysmetria, intention tremor, hypotonia. Double decussation (SCP + CST pyramids) = left cerebellar lesion affects left limbs.
Detail the direct and indirect basal ganglia pathways and predict: what does dopamine loss do (Parkinson's) and what does GPe neuron loss do (Huntington's)?
tap
Direct pathway: Cortex → striatum → GPi/SNr (inhibited) → thalamus (disinhibited) → cortex. Facilitates movement (↑ thalamic drive). Dopamine EXCITES direct pathway. Indirect pathway: Cortex → striatum → GPe (inhibited) → STN (disinhibited) → GPi (excited) → thalamus (inhibited) → cortex. Inhibits movement (↓ thalamic drive). Dopamine INHIBITS indirect pathway. Parkinson's (↓ dopamine from SNc loss): Direct pathway is inhibited (less movement facilitation) AND indirect pathway is released (more movement suppression) → ↓ thalamic drive → HYPOKINESIA. Signs: bradykinesia, rigidity, resting tremor, difficulty initiating. Huntington's (GPe neuron loss): GPe can no longer inhibit STN → STN still excites GPi → but wait — GABAergic GPe neurons are lost, so GPe can't inhibit STN → indirect pathway inhibited → less GPi output → thalamus disinhibited → ↑ thalamic drive → HYPERKINESIA. Chorea results.
What happens at the optic chiasm in this scenario? What visual field defect does a pituitary tumor cause and why?
tap
At the optic chiasm: nasal (medial) retinal fibers cross; temporal (lateral) fibers stay ipsilateral. Result: all right visual field information (from nasal retina of left eye AND temporal retina of right eye) converges and travels to the left hemisphere. Pituitary tumor (grows upward from sella turcica, compresses chiasm from below at midline): destroys the crossing nasal fibers → bitemporal hemianopia — loss of BOTH temporal (lateral/peripheral) visual fields. Patient loses lateral vision bilaterally, keeping only central tunnel vision. Each eye loses its temporal field because the nasal retinal fibers that would carry temporal visual field information are destroyed at the crossing. Lateral chiasm compression (internal carotid aneurysm) → destroys uncrossed temporal fibers → nasal hemianopia of ipsilateral eye (monocular defect, rare).
Name all the signs of Wallenberg syndrome and explain the vascular anatomy.
tap
Wallenberg = PICA or vertebral artery occlusion → lateral medullary infarct. IPSILATERAL signs: (1) Horner's syndrome (ptosis/miosis/anhidrosis/enophthalmos) — descending sympathetic fibers traverse lateral medulla. (2) Loss of face pain + temperature — spinal nucleus/tract of CN V in lateral medulla. (3) Dysphagia + hoarseness — nucleus ambiguus (CNs IX/X/XI). (4) Cerebellar signs (ataxia/hypotonia/dysmetria) — inferior cerebellar peduncle/cerebellum. CONTRALATERAL signs: (5) Loss of body pain + temperature — STT already crossed before reaching lateral medulla, traveling contralaterally. BILATERAL: (6) Nystagmus/vertigo — vestibular nuclei. PRESERVED (medial medulla structures): Motor function (CST = pyramids, medial), discriminatory touch/proprioception (DC/ML = medial lemniscus, medial), CN XII (hypoglossal nucleus, medial). Memory: "Everything except paralysis and fine touch."
UMN vs. In this case, LMN facial weakness — how do you tell them apart and what anatomy explains it?
tap
UMN (cortex/internal capsule/corticobulbar lesion): Only CONTRALATERAL LOWER face weak. Forehead and eye closure SPARED bilaterally. Reason: upper face (forehead, orbicularis oculi) receives BILATERAL UMN innervation from both hemispheres via corticobulbar — the intact hemisphere compensates. Lower face receives only CONTRALATERAL UMN innervation. Bedside test: "Raise your eyebrows" — can raise both with UMN lesion. LMN (CN VII nucleus or nerve — Bell's palsy): Entire IPSILATERAL face weak — forehead, eye, AND lower face all fail. Cannot raise brow, cannot close eye fully, cannot smile on that side. Reason: damage to the final common pathway — the CN VII nucleus/nerve itself controls all ipsilateral facial muscles regardless of UMN input. Other CN rules: Tongue deviates TOWARD weak side (LMN CN XII). Uvula deviates AWAY from lesion (CN X). Jaw deviates TOWARD weak side (CN V motor, lower motor neuron).
Name the 3 primary neurotransmitters at the synapse and what disease/toxin targets each?
tap
Acetylcholine (ACh) — excitatory, fast: NMJ + CNS. Curare = blocks nicotinic ACh receptors → flaccid paralysis (no receptor activation). Botulism toxin = prevents ACh exocytosis at NMJ → flaccid paralysis (no transmitter release). Myasthenia gravis = autoimmune destruction of ACh receptors → weakness + fatigable muscle contractions (weakness worsens with repeated use) + double vision. GABA — inhibitory, fast (mainly brain): BZDs/alcohol enhance GABA → sedation. Loss of GABA interneurons in GPe (Huntington's) → hyperkinesia. Deficiency → seizures (GABA maintains inhibitory tone). Glycine — inhibitory, fast (mainly spinal cord): Tetanus toxin = retrograde transport to spinal cord interneurons → blocks glycine release → motor neurons can't be inhibited → spastic paralysis/lockjaw/risus sardonicus. Strychnine also blocks glycine → similar syndrome. Glycine receptors: important for reciprocal inhibition in spinal reflexes.
Name the developmental stages of the nervous system? Name the primary vesicles, secondary vesicles, and adult structures they become.

Week 3: Ectoderm thickens → neural groove → neural folds meet → neural tube (begins at future cervical SC). Neural crest cells left behind → form PNS (DRG, autonomic ganglia, Schwann cells). What is the difference between rigidity and spasticity, and which basal ganglia disorder produces each in this scenario?
tap
Rigidity (extrapyramidal/basal ganglia): Resistance to passive movement that is NOT speed-dependent — present whether you move the joint slow or fast. Bidirectional — resists both flexion and extension. Types: Lead-pipe rigidity = continuous uniform resistance throughout ROM. Cogwheel rigidity = periodic "catches" throughout ROM (Parkinson's). Found in: Parkinson's disease (dopamine loss → abnormal basal ganglia output → increased thalamic drive for rigidity). Spasticity (pyramidal/UMN system): Speed-dependent resistance — moving the joint slowly = little or no resistance; moving quickly = high resistance (velocity-dependent catch and release). Often unidirectional (typically resists only flexion or only extension). Caused by loss of cortical inhibition of spinal stretch reflexes. Found in: stroke, spinal cord injury, cerebral palsy, MS. Clinical test: move joint slowly then quickly — if resistance increases with speed = spastic; if resistance is the same regardless of speed = rigid. This distinction determines treatment approach (e.g., different medications target each).
What is the VOR, what is its circuit, and why is it faster than visual tracking in this scenario?
tap
VOR (vestibuloocular reflex): Maintains gaze stability on a target during head movement — eyes rotate opposite to head rotation to keep image stable on retina. Why faster than visual tracking: The VOR is a direct 3-neuron arc. Visual tracking requires the cortex to process the retinal error, plan a corrective eye movement, and send commands — takes ~200ms. The VOR bypasses cortical processing — it's purely brainstem: CN VIII → vestibular nuclei → CN III/IV/VI → extraocular muscles. Response time ~15ms. This is why you can read while walking or shake your head while reading a sign — the VOR stabilizes your gaze faster than vision can. Clinical relevance: VOR testing (head impulse test, caloric testing) distinguishes peripheral (vestibular end-organ/CN VIII) from central (brainstem) causes of vertigo. In peripheral vestibular damage: VOR is abnormal (corrective saccade seen on head impulse test). In central (cerebellar/brainstem): VOR may be intact or abnormally enhanced.
Detail Klüver-Bucy syndrome: cause, 5 signs, and which structures are damaged.
tap
Cause: Bilateral damage to amygdala, hippocampus, and anterior temporal lobe. Classic etiology: herpes simplex encephalitis (predilection for temporal lobes), also anterior temporal lobe surgical resection. 5 signs: (1) Placidity/docility — loss of fear and aggression (amygdala). (2) Hypersexuality — inappropriate sexual behavior toward objects or people. (3) Hyperphagia + oral tendencies — puts everything in mouth; can't stop eating. (4) Visual agnosia ("psychic blindness") — can see objects but cannot recognize or identify them. (5) Amnesia — both anterograde (can't form new memories) AND retrograde (lost past memories) — from hippocampal damage. Mnemonic for H.K-B: Hypersexual, Keeps eating, Blindly handles everything, Placid, Amnesic. Contrast with isolated hippocampal damage: only anterograde amnesia, no hypersexuality or placidity.
What is locked-in syndrome in this scenario? What is preserved, what is lost, and what vessel is occluded?
tap
Locked-in syndrome = infarct in the base of the pons from occlusion of lower paramedian branches of the basilar artery. The basilar pons contains the descending CST bilaterally. LOST: Quadriplegia (bilateral CST destruction — no voluntary movement of face, limbs, or trunk). Cannot speak or swallow (lower CN motor nuclei also in pons). Bilateral loss of discriminatory touch/vibration/proprioception (medial lemniscus at medullary level lost). Horizontal eye movements (CN VI nucleus in pons). PRESERVED: Full consciousness and cognition (reticular activating system in pontine tegmentum = SPARED). Pain and temperature sensation (STT intact — "can't move but CAN feel pain"). Vertical eye movements (midbrain CN III/IV — above the pons lesion). Blinking (may be preserved). Communication: entirely through vertical gaze and blinking. Eye-tracking AAC devices. Prognosis: poor. Important distinction from vegetative state: the locked-in patient is fully aware and experiences everything — they just cannot express it.
