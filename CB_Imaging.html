X-ray Fundamentals & Fracture Description
PTs must understand imaging to interpret reports, communicate with physicians, identify red flags, and make appropriate referrals. Understanding what images show directly influences clinical decision-making, treatment planning, and patient safety.

How X-rays Work — Production & Tissue Density

Radiopaque = white = boneRadiolucent = dark = air▼
X-ray Production

X-rays are produced within a tube after a beam of electrons is heated. Emitted through a collimator and projected onto the object. Attenuation: Reduction in X-ray photons as beam passes through patient (scattering or photoelectric absorption). Remnant beam projected onto image receptor and processed.

Key Terms

Radiopaque: Not easily penetrated → appears WHITE (bone, metal). Radiodense: Structures that block significant X-rays (metal implants = most radiodense). Radiolucent: Easily penetrated, low attenuation → appears DARK/BLACK (air, gas).

Density Spectrum (White to Black)

Metal (whitest) > Bone > Soft Tissue/Fluid > Fat > Air (blackest). Denser structure = more X-rays blocked = whiter on film.

Projections

AP: Beam enters front, exits back. PA: Beam enters back, exits front (preferred for chest — heart closer to film, less magnification). Lateral: Side view. Oblique: Angled view. Most joints need minimum 2 views (AP + lateral).

Fracture Types Defined

Transverse: Horizontal, perpendicular to shaft. Oblique: Angled ~45°. Spiral: Wraps around bone (rotation). Comminuted: ≥3 fragments. Avulsion: Chip pulled off by tendon/ligament. Buckle (Torus): Cortex indentation (children). Greenstick: One cortex breaks, other bends (children). Stress: Repetitive loading — may not be visible on X-ray initially (edema on MRI is the early sign).

Common Sense Treatment Plan (Sports Medicine Approach)

This approach applies to most chronic knee conditions: (1) Decrease inciting activities — "if it hurts, don't do it." (2) Encourage appropriate dosing of anti-inflammatories and pain meds (dosing based on weight). (3) ICE. (4) If indicated, least restrictive orthosis (more is not better). (5) PT and gradual progression back to full activity. (6) Participation with pain-free ambulation and minimal pain with activity — typically less than pain level of 3.

Chronic Knee Conditions — Often Missed on Imaging

Patella Tendinopathy: More common in teenagers+ (growth plate closed). Pain with running and jumping. NOT associated with traumatic event (tendon tear is rare in adolescents). Palpate ENTIRE patella tendon, not just central. Differentiate from fat pad impingement. If >3 months of symptoms, need different approach: eccentric training, Graston, dry needling, US-guided needle tenotomy for refractory cases.

ITB Friction Syndrome: Lateral knee pain with running or dance. Gradual onset, can't pinpoint location. Pain over femoral condyle, crossing joint, or Gerdy's tubercle. Figure-4 position may help pinpoint. May have false (+) McMurray's — if McMurray's is positive, make sure the story makes sense. In younger patients, there really isn't much pathology laterally for chronic pain. Snapping ITB = popping with no pain.

Medial Plica Syndrome: Thin vascularized intraarticular synovial fold over medial knee. Present in everyone but more prominent in some. Dynamically controlled by quads (more common with poor quad tone). Dull ache increasing with activity, proximal to joint line. Stairs, squatting, bending give significant pain. May report "locking." Exam: patient relaxed, palpate space between medial condyle and patella — ribbon-like structure rolled against condyle. Key: pain is PROXIMAL to joint line (vs meniscus which is AT joint line).

Pes Anserine Bursitis: Attaches close to tibial tuberosity — distinguish on exam. Consider resisted SLR in external rotation to elicit pain from sartorius.

Fat Pad Impingement: Typically from hyperextension injury (gymnasts, skateboarding). Localized swelling BELOW patella with NO effusion. Palpation medially and laterally to patella + hyperextension helps diagnosis.

Multipartite Patella: Can predispose to avulsion injury. Patient may be sprinting and feel a pop. Treat with rest, WBAT, should improve in 4 weeks. Tip: You can always X-ray the other side for comparison.

Traumatic Knee History — Don't Rush to Touch the Knee

History is EXTREMELY important in acute knee injury. Ask these questions BEFORE examining: (1) Contact vs noncontact mechanism? (2) Did you hear or feel a pop? (3) What were the exact circumstances? (4) Did you feel your kneecap move out of place? (5) Did it swell immediately (like a grapefruit)? Tibial tuberosity fracture: Running with hard stop or jump from height, acute onset, pop felt, pain directly at tibial tuberosity, swelling, fear of knee extension against resistance. Management: knee immobilizer or locked hinged brace, crutches → WBAT over 4 weeks, PT after immobilization. Tip: Swelling pattern can be more diffuse than OSD.

Evidence: Patella Dislocation — Conservative Often Wins

Palmu et al (JBJS 2008): 74 acute patella dislocations in patients <16 yo. Nonoperative: 75% good/excellent subjective outcome. Operative: 66%. Recurrent dislocation: 71% nonop vs 67% op. Nikku et al (Acta Orthop 2005): 127 primary dislocations (mean age 20), 5+ year follow-up. Nonoperative: 81% good/excellent. Operative: 67%. Recurrent dislocation: 39% nonop vs 31% op. Bottom line: Operative treatment of primary dislocation does NOT improve medium-term outcome. Conservative management with early PT is the standard approach.

MRI Indications for Knee

History/exam not consistent with diagnosis. Fracture or osteochondral fragment on X-ray. Chronic traumatic patella dislocation. Failure of conservative treatment. Mechanical symptoms. Pre-surgical planning.

Knee MRI Anatomy

Sagittal: ACL (taut dark band), PCL, menisci (dark triangles — white line through = tear). Coronal: MCL, LCL, meniscal body. Axial: Patellofemoral joint, effusion. Bone bruise patterns: Medial patella + lateral femoral condyle = patella dislocation. Posterior tibia + central femur = pivot shift (ACL mechanism). Effusion = bright on T2.

Tips from Sports Medicine

Meniscus: "Modified" McMurray's most accurate. Don't chase the click — it's rare to elicit one consistent with meniscal tear. MCL, LCL, ITB, fat pad all can have joint line tenderness. ACL: Lachman done properly is most accurate. External rotation of hip helps patient relax. Support knee with towel roll. If patient contracting muscles = not a valid test.

Spine Imaging Additional Findings

Flexion/Extension views: Used to assess spinal instability — dynamic views show abnormal translation between segments. Essential for spondylolisthesis assessment and post-surgical evaluation. Open-mouth (odontoid) view: Specifically to view the dens — used to assess for odontoid fractures, Jefferson fracture (lateral mass overhang), and atlantoaxial instability (RA, Down syndrome). Oblique views: Show neural foramina and the "Scotty dog" (pars articularis). CT vs MRI for spine: CT is better for bony detail (fracture lines, facet morphology, 3D reconstruction for surgical planning). MRI is better for soft tissue (disc, cord, nerve roots, ligaments, edema). Red flags requiring immediate spine imaging: Cauda equina symptoms, progressive neurological deficit, suspected spinal cord compression, trauma with neurological signs.

Critical Imaging Pearl

Never treat the image. 20–30% of asymptomatic people have abnormal spinal imaging findings (disc bulges, herniations, degenerative changes). A disc bulge in someone with no radicular signs, negative neural tension tests, and full pain-free ROM is an incidental finding — not a diagnosis. Telling a pain-free patient they have a "herniated disc" risks unnecessary fear, catastrophizing, decreased activity, and chronic pain behavior. Always correlate imaging with clinical presentation. The image shows anatomy, not pain. This is central to the biopsychosocial model and PNE.

MRI & Diagnostic Ultrasound
MRI is the gold standard for soft tissue evaluation. Diagnostic ultrasound is increasingly used for real-time, dynamic assessment. PTs should understand what these modalities show, their advantages, limitations, and how findings change treatment.

MRI Fundamentals — T1 vs T2

T1 = anatomy (fat bright)T2 = pathology (fluid bright)STIR = bone marrow edema▼
T1 vs T2 — The Key Distinction

T1 (aNaTomy): Fat = bright/white. Fluid = dark. Best for normal structures, bone marrow, fat planes. T2 (fLUid/paTHology): Fluid = bright/white. Edema, inflammation, tears, effusions all "light up." STIR: Fat-suppressed T2 — edema stands out maximally. Best for stress fractures and bone bruises.

MRI Planes

Sagittal: Side view. Best for ACL, PCL, spine disc herniations. Coronal: Front view. Best for MCL, LCL, meniscal body, hip labrum. Axial: Top-down cross-section. Best for patellofemoral joint, spinal canal stenosis.

What MRI Shows That X-ray Cannot

Soft tissue injuries (ligament/tendon/meniscal tears), bone marrow edema (stress Fx before cortical break), disc herniations, labral tears, cartilage defects, bone bruises, tumors, infections, nerve root compression.

What does radiopaque vs radiolucent mean, and what is the density spectrum?
tap
Radiopaque = blocks X-rays = WHITE (bone, metal). Radiolucent = lets X-rays through = DARK/BLACK (air). Spectrum white to black: Metal > Bone > Soft Tissue/Fluid > Fat > Air. Denser = more attenuation = whiter. Fractures show as disruptions in the white cortical line. Pneumothorax = absent lung markings (too much black where there should be lung).
What is the 5-point fracture description system?
tap
1. WHERE: Bone, location, side. 2. INTRA/EXTRA-ARTICULAR: Does it enter the joint? Intra = worse. 3. TYPE: Transverse, oblique, spiral, comminuted, avulsion, buckle, greenstick, stress. 4. OPEN/CLOSED: Skin laceration? Open = infection risk. 5. DISPLACEMENT/ANGULATION: Distal relative to proximal. This system ensures complete, consistent communication with physicians.
What is the ABCDEFGH approach to chest X-ray?
tap
Airway (trachea midline? deviation = mass or pneumothorax). Bones (fractures, scoliosis, metastases). Cardiac (ratio ≤0.5; >0.5 = cardiomegaly). Diaphragm (dome = normal; flat = COPD). Effusions (sharp costophrenic angles = normal; blunted = effusion). Fields (consolidation, ground-glass, TB). Gastric bubble (free air not in stomach = perforated viscus = emergency). Hardware (check all device positions).
What are the only 5 causes of acute traumatic knee effusion?
tap
If the knee swells within 24 hours of trauma: (1) ACL/PCL. (2) Meniscus. (3) Fracture. (4) Patella subluxation/dislocation. (5) Bone bruise. Nothing else causes immediate traumatic effusion. Massive rapid swelling = ACL until proven otherwise. Segond fracture (lateral tibial avulsion) = highly associated with ACL tear. Bone bruise pattern on MRI: medial patella + lateral femoral condyle = patella dislocation. Posterior tibia + central femur = ACL pivot shift mechanism.
How do T1 and T2 MRI differ and when does each matter?
tap
T1 = aNaTomy: Fat bright, fluid dark. See normal structures. T2 = fLUid/paTHology: Fluid bright. Edema, tears, effusions all light up — this is where you see pathology. STIR: Fat-suppressed T2, edema stands out maximally. Best for stress fractures and bone bruises. Bright signal in normally dark meniscus on T2 = tear. Bright bone marrow on STIR = bone bruise/stress reaction.
What is the Salter-Harris classification and why is Type V dangerous?
tap
SALTR: Slip (I: physis only). Above (II: metaphysis, ~75% most common). Lower (III: epiphysis, intra-articular). Through all (IV: all three layers). Rammed (V: crush). Type V = worst prognosis because growth plate cells are destroyed. X-ray may look NORMAL initially. Diagnosis often retrospective when growth arrest appears. Always X-ray contralateral side in pediatric patients for comparison.

What is the "common sense treatment plan" for chronic knee conditions, and when does it change?
tap
The sports medicine "common sense" approach: (1) Decrease inciting activities — "if it hurts, don't do it." (2) Appropriate dosing of anti-inflammatories/pain meds (weight-based). (3) ICE. (4) Least restrictive orthosis if indicated (more is NOT better). (5) PT and gradual progression back to full activity. (6) Participate with pain-free ambulation and minimal pain (typically <3/10). When it changes: Patella tendinopathy >3 months of symptoms = need different approach (eccentric training, Graston, dry needling, US-guided tenotomy). OCD = restrict sports 3 months, PT after 6 weeks pain-free. PFS = be VERY clear patient needs dedicated PT, 4–6 weeks minimum for meaningful improvement. Hip & gluteal strengthening is KEY for PFS, not just quad work.
How do you differentiate medial plica syndrome from meniscus tear on exam?
tap
Medial plica: Pain is PROXIMAL to joint line (between medial condyle and patella). Dull ache increasing with activity, worse with stairs/squatting/bending. May report "locking." Palpate with patient relaxed — ribbon-like structure rolled against femoral condyle. More common with poor quad tone. Meniscus tear: Pain is AT the joint line. McMurray's may be positive (but it's not very specific — MCL, LCL, ITB, fat pad can all have joint line tenderness). Don't chase the click — it's rare to elicit one consistent with meniscus. Also distinguish from: pes anserine (pain near tibial tuberosity, resisted SLR in ER reproduces), fat pad impingement (hyperextension injury, swelling BELOW patella with NO effusion), and ITB syndrome (lateral pain, gradual onset, may have false (+) McMurray's).
What evidence supports conservative vs operative management for primary patella dislocation?
tap
Two landmark studies show operative treatment does NOT improve outcomes: Palmu (JBJS 2008): 74 dislocations in patients <16 yo. Nonoperative: 75% good/excellent. Operative: 66%. Recurrent dislocation: 71% nonop vs 67% op — essentially equal. Nikku (Acta Orthop 2005): 127 primary dislocations, 5+ year follow-up. Nonoperative: 81% good/excellent. Operative: 67%. Conservative management with early PT is the standard. Protocol: patella stabilizing brace → early PT (quad isometrics, SLR) → progress out of brace → walking → running → sport-specific. RTP typically 4–6 weeks. Always get sunrise X-ray. MRI only if: exam inconsistent with dislocation, fracture/osteochondral fragment on film, chronic or recurrent, failure of conservative, mechanical symptoms, or pre-surgical planning. Most common mechanism: noncontact, LE internal rotation, knee valgus on planted foot.
What does tendinopathy look like on ultrasound vs normal tendon?
tap
Normal tendon on US: Tightly packed hyperechoic (bright) lines in long axis representing fibrils. In short axis (transverse): multiple bright dots in a tightly packed bundle. Overall appearance is organized, bright, and uniform. Tendinopathy findings: (1) Loss of normal fibrillar pattern — organized bright lines become disrupted. (2) Hypoechoic (dark) areas within the normally bright tendon = degeneration. (3) Tendon thickening (compare to contralateral). (4) Neovascularization on Doppler = increased blood flow in normally avascular tendon. (5) Calcifications = hyperechoic foci with acoustic shadowing. Clinical pearl: "Tendinitis" is being replaced by "tendinosis/tendinopathy" because histology shows chronic tendon pain involves degeneration (collagen disorganization, ground substance changes), NOT active inflammation. This changes treatment — anti-inflammatories may be less useful than eccentric loading, which stimulates collagen remodeling.
What does the posterior fat pad sign on a lateral elbow X-ray mean?
tap
A posterior fat pad sign is ALWAYS abnormal. On lateral elbow X-ray, the posterior fat pad is normally hidden within the olecranon fossa. When it becomes visible (displaced by intra-articular effusion), it indicates an occult fracture — even if no fracture line is seen on the X-ray. The anterior fat pad may show a "sail sign" (elevated and triangular). If you see a posterior fat pad sign: treat as fracture, immobilize, and arrange follow-up imaging. This is one of the most commonly tested imaging concepts for PTs.
