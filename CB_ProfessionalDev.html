Professional Development — From Student to Licensed PT

Everything they don’t teach you enough of in school — PT-PTA supervision rules by setting, Medicare billing and coding (CPT codes, 8-minute rule), NPTE licensure process, resume and interview strategies, specialty board certifications, and the legal framework for practice. This is end-of-program content designed to prepare you for Day 1 as a licensed clinician.

PT-PTA Supervision — Rules by Setting
The PT is responsible for ALL elements of patient management: examination, evaluation, diagnosis, prognosis, intervention, and outcomes. The PTA assists in components of intervention and collection of selected examination and outcomes data. The PT is DIRECTLY responsible for the actions of the PTA in ALL practice settings. Aides can NEVER be the provider of a skilled service.

8 Responsibilities Borne SOLELY by the PT

▼
PT-Only Responsibilities (Cannot Be Delegated)

(1) Interpretation of referrals.
(2) Evaluation, diagnosis, and prognosis.
(3) Development or modification of POC (based on initial exam or re-exam, includes goals and outcomes).
(4) Determination of when PT must personally render services vs when PTA is appropriate.
(5) Revision of the POC.
(6) Conclusion of episode of care.
(7) Responsibility for any "hand-off" communication.
(8) Oversight of ALL documentation for services rendered.
Interventions exclusive to PT: Spinal and peripheral joint mobilization/manipulation, dry needling, and sharp selective debridement.

Key Supervision Rules — Updated 2025

MAJOR 2025 CHANGE: CMS changed PTA supervision in private practice/outpatient from DIRECT to GENERAL supervision effective January 1, 2025. PTAs now practice under general supervision in ALL Medicare settings. PT must still be available by telecommunication.
PTA Payment Differential (CQ Modifier): Since January 1, 2022, services furnished in whole or in part by a PTA are paid at 85% of the otherwise applicable rate (15% reduction). Must use CQ modifier on claims. De minimis rule: if PTA provides ≤10% of the service, the CQ modifier is not required.
PTA cannot: Perform initial evaluation. Change the POC. Initiate treatment on a patient with no POC. Supervise an aide.
PTA can: Collect selected examination data. Modify elements of intervention to progress patient AS DIRECTED by PT or to ensure safety. Provide skilled interventions within the POC.
State practice acts always apply — if state is MORE stringent than Medicare, state rules prevail (49 states already permitted general supervision before the 2025 rule change).

Billing & Coding — Medicare, CPT Codes & the 8-Minute Rule
Proper billing and coding protects your license, your employer, and your patients. Therapeutic activities (97530) generally reimburse higher than therapeutic exercise (97110). Know the difference between timed and untimed codes, and always document to support the codes you bill.

The 8-Minute Rule (Medicare Timed Codes)

Medicare requires a minimum of 8 minutes of a timed service to bill 1 unit.
Billing chart: 8–22 min = 1 unit. 23–37 min = 2 units. 38–52 min = 3 units. 53–67 min = 4 units.
You must provide at least 8 minutes of a specific service to bill for it. If you provide 7 minutes of manual therapy, you CANNOT bill for it.
Total treatment time determines total units — remainder minutes can only be billed to a service that received the most minutes.
Documentation must support every code billed — time, specific interventions, skilled rationale, and patient response.

Billing Example from Course

Patient: 27yo M, R hip FAI, follow-up visit.
Manual Therapy (97140): 15 min = 1 unit. Lateral hip joint mobs grade 3/4, Mulligan lateral hip glides.
Ther Ex (97110): 30 min = 2 units. Upright bike 10 min, wall ball squat with band 4x8, SL press 4x8, sidelying banded hip abduction 2x12.
Ther Act (97530): 15 min = 1 unit. Lateral banded walks 2x20ft, lateral lunge onto bosu 4x8, multi-directional lunges with slider 3x5.
Total: 60 min = 4 units billed (1 MT + 2 Ther Ex + 1 Ther Act).

NPTE & Licensure — Getting Your License
The NPTE (National Physical Therapy Examination) is administered by the FSBPT (Federation of State Boards of Physical Therapy). Each state has its own licensure requirements, fees, and timelines. Some states allow you to take the exam before graduation. You will also need to pass a jurisprudence exam in most states.

Pros/Cons of Taking NPTE Before Graduation

Pros: Can work immediately after passing. Can apply to positions sooner → pay off loans faster. Motivated by being “almost done.” Cons: Significant study load on top of clinical rotations = more stress. Less energy for clinical experiences. Must also prepare for jurisprudence exam. School/life balance suffers.
State-specific: Not all states allow pre-graduation testing — check your state board. Arizona allows it if the university determines eligibility.

NPTE Prep Strategy

Resources: FSBPT Practice Exam and Assessment Tool (PEAT). Review courses (Scorebuilders, TherapyEd, O’Sullivan). Study groups. Program-specific board prep.
Content domains: Physical Therapy Examination (tests and measures), Foundations for Evaluation/Differential Diagnosis/Prognosis, Interventions, Equipment/Devices/Technologies, Safety/Protection, Professional Responsibilities/Research.
Key: Start studying early. Practice under timed conditions. Focus on weak areas identified by practice exams. Clinical rotations reinforce clinical reasoning that appears on the exam.

Resume, Cover Letter & Interview Strategies
Your resume is your first impression. Make it employer-specific, concise, and skills-focused. The interview is where you sell yourself — preparation is everything.

Resume & Cover Letter Best Practices

▼
Resume Rules

1 page maximum. Employer-specific — look at their website for cultural fit. Use keywords from the job posting. 5–7 bullets per clinical rotation section. List skills relevant to the position. Include volunteer work and professional involvement.
No pictures. Clean formatting, consistent fonts. Highlight clinical experiences, patient populations, and settings. Include any certifications (BLS, specialty courses).
Cover letter: Half a page maximum. Specific to the position and organization. Show you’ve researched them.

Interview Do’s, Don’ts & Questions to Ask

▼
DO

Research the company — look for cultural fit (prescreening). Reread and know the job posting and expectations. Prepare by conducting mock interviews. Sell yourself. Make eye contact. Answer concisely. Show respect and manners. Come prepared with specific questions.

DON’T

Divert your attention (phone away). Try to fill dead air (silence is OK). Hesitate to focus on strengths. Be underprepared. Be too familiar (maintain professionalism). Badmouth previous employers or clinical sites.

Questions to Ask the Interviewer

Culture: “How would you describe the company culture?” Day-to-day: “What are the day-to-day responsibilities this role entails?” Onboarding: “What does the onboarding program look like?” Mentorship: “How is mentorship handled in the clinic?” Success: “How would you describe the characteristics of someone who succeeds in this role?” Performance: “How would my performance be measured and how often?” Collaboration: “What departments or teammates does this team interact with regularly?” Challenges: “What are the current challenges therapists face in the practice?” CE: “What continuing education opportunities are supported?” Salary: You CAN ask about salary — it’s professional and expected.

Specialty Board Certifications — ABPTS
Board certification through the American Board of Physical Therapy Specialties (ABPTS) demonstrates advanced clinical knowledge and skills. There are 10 specialty areas. Certification requires current PT license, direct patient care hours, and passing a specialty examination.

Minimum Requirements (All Specialties)

Licensure: Current permanent/unrestricted PT license in the US.
Direct patient care: Minimum 2,000 hours in the specialty area within the last 10 years (varies by pathway).
Application: Online through abpts.org.
Fees: APTA member: $525 application + $810 exam = $1,335 total. Non-member: $870 + $1,535 = $2,405 total.
APTA membership saves over $1,000. Exam is offered annually. Application deadlines vary by specialty (October for most). Files remain active for 2 consecutive exam administrations. After that, must reapply with full fees.

PTA in a SNF has treated a patient on 5 consecutive visits since the PT last saw your patient. What needs to happen?
tap
The PT must perform a re-evaluation. Under Medicare SNF rules, the PT must re-evaluate every 4th treatment visit OR every 30 days, whichever occurs first. This patient has been seen 5 times since the PT last treated — the re-eval is overdue. The PT must personally provide each therapeutic intervention on the day of the re-evaluation. In home health: Same rule applies. PT must also make an onsite supervisory visit at least once every 30 days. Key rules: PTA works under general supervision in SNF (PT not required on site but must be available by telecom). PTA CANNOT change the POC, evaluate, or discharge. PTA cannot supervise an aide — aides require line-of-sight supervision from the PT only.
You treated a patient for 12 minutes of manual therapy and 25 minutes of therapeutic exercise. Outline how to bill?
tap
Manual Therapy (97140): 1 unit (12 min). Therapeutic Exercise (97110): 2 units (25 min). Total: 3 units for 37 minutes. Under the 8-minute rule: 8–22 min = 1 unit, 23–37 min = 2 units. MT at 12 min qualifies for 1 unit (≥8 min threshold met). Ther Ex at 25 min qualifies for 2 units (in the 23–37 range). Total treatment time = 37 min. Total units = 3. If you had done 7 minutes of MT: You could NOT bill for it (below 8-minute minimum). Key: Therapeutic activities (97530) generally reimburse higher than therapeutic exercise (97110). If your intervention is task-oriented and functional, consider whether 97530 is more appropriate. Always document time, specific interventions, skilled rationale, and patient response to support every code billed.
What interventions can ONLY be performed by a PT and never delegated to a PTA in this scenario?
tap
Spinal and peripheral joint mobilization/manipulation, dry needling, and sharp selective debridement. These are the specifically named interventions that must be performed exclusively by the PT per APTA position statement (HOD P06-18-31-36). Beyond specific interventions, the PT exclusively performs: initial examination, all re-examinations, evaluation, diagnosis, prognosis, development/modification/revision of the POC, conclusion of episode of care, hand-off communication, and oversight of all documentation. The PTA may assist with components of intervention and collection of selected examination and outcomes data — but the PT determines when PTA utilization is appropriate based on PTA education/training/experience, patient acuity/complexity, setting, federal/state statutes, liability concerns, and needed frequency of re-examination.
You want to pursue an OCS. Identify the requirements and costs?
tap
OCS (Orthopaedic Certified Specialist) is the most commonly pursued PT specialty certification through ABPTS. Requirements: Current permanent/unrestricted PT license. Minimum 2,000 hours of direct patient care in orthopaedics within the last 10 years. Application through abpts.org. Costs: APTA member = $1,335 total ($525 application + $810 exam). Non-member = $2,405 ($870 + $1,535). APTA membership saves over $1,000. Application deadline for OCS is October 31. Exam offered annually in early spring. Application file active for 2 consecutive exam cycles. After 2 attempts, must reapply with full fees. 10 specialty areas available: OCS, SCS, NCS, GCS, PCS, CCS, WCS, ECS, ONC, WCC. All require licensure + direct patient care hours + passing the specialty exam.
