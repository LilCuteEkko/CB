Integumentary System & Wound Care

The integumentary system is critical for every PT. You will encounter wounds in every setting — SNF, acute care, outpatient, home health. Understanding skin anatomy, wound healing phases, wound types, infection, dressings, and modalities directly impacts how safely and effectively you mobilize and treat patients.

Skin Anatomy & Physiology
"Look at the whole patient, not just the hole in the patient." — Carrie Sussman. The skin is the largest organ: 15–20% body weight, 1.5–2 m² surface area, 9.5 million cells per sq inch. It is often the most neglected organ — yet as a PT you assess it every session when you mobilize and touch your patient.

Epidermis — Layers, Cells & Disease Processes

Stratified squamous keratinized epitheliumThick skin = 5 layers; Thin = 4 layersDermis does NOT regenerate — it REPAIRS▼
Epidermal Cells
  • Keratinocytes — predominant cell; produce keratin; arise via mitotic division in stratum basale, migrate upward, shed at surface (every 27 days)
  • Melanocytes — pigment-producing cells in basal layer; protect against UV; produce melanin granules
  • Langerhans cells — dendritic immune cells; bone marrow-derived; bind and process foreign bodies; present antigens to T-lymphocytes; microorganisms cannot penetrate epidermis without triggering immune response
  • Merkel (tactile) cells — mechanoreceptors in stratum basale; high-sensitivity areas; associated with afferent myelinated nerve fiber terminals
5 Layers (Thick Skin) — Deep to Superficial
  1. Stratum basale — single layer of stem cells for keratinocytes; contains keratins; site of melanocytes and Merkel cells
  2. Stratum spinosum — thickest layer; actively synthesize keratin filaments (tonofibrils); desmosomes resist friction (Node of Bizzozero)
  3. Stratum granulosum — most superficial non-keratinized portion; 3–5 layers; keratohyaline granules
  4. Stratum lucidum — thick skin only (palms, soles); thin translucent layer; densely packed keratin
  5. Stratum corneum — 15–20 layers; flattened non-nucleated keratinized cells; continually shed
Epidermal Disease Processes
  • Basal cell carcinoma — slow growing; does NOT invade blood or lymph; causes significant local destruction; from stratum basale
  • Squamous cell carcinoma — from stratum spinosum; can be in situ or invasive
  • Malignant melanoma — invasive tumor of melanocytes; penetrates basal lamina, invades dermis, blood and lymphatics; easily distributes throughout body
  • Psoriasis — autoimmune; increased cell proliferation in stratum basale + spinosum; decreased cycle time; increased epidermal thickness; abnormal keratinization
  • SJS/TEN — severe cutaneous hypersensitivity; keratinocyte death + epidermal sloughing
  • Bullous pemphigoid — autoimmune; blisters at epidermal/dermal junction
Nail Changes as Systemic Clues
  • Clubbing — COPD, emphysema, cystic fibrosis
  • Half-and-half nails — chronic renal failure (proximal white, distal darker)
  • Cyanotic changes — congenital heart disease or pulmonary disease
  • Longitudinal linear lesions (melanonychia) — 50% of melanomas in dark-skinned populations are nail melanomas; changing or irregular nail pigment warrants biopsy; normal variant in >50% of Black people but age-progressive

Dermis, Hypodermis & Sensory Receptors

Papillary layer = loose CT, type VII collagen anchorsReticular layer = dense CT, type I collagen + elastinElastin decreases with age → skin tears▼
Dermis Layers

Papillary layer: loose connective tissue; fibroblasts, mast cells, macrophages; embedded in gelatinous matrix (ground substance); anchoring fibrils = Type VII collagen insert into basal lamina. Reticular layer (thicker): dense CT Type I collagen + elastin network; site of hair follicles, sebaceous glands; rich blood, lymphatic and nerve supply. Key: collagen accounts for >70% of skin's dry weight. 3 major cell types: fibroblasts (produce collagen and elastin), macrophages (fight infection), mast cells (chemical mediators of inflammation).

Collagen Types — Know These
  • Type I — dermis, bone, tendon, fascia, sclera, intervertebral disk
  • Type II — hyaline and elastic cartilage
  • Type III — smooth muscle, arteries, lung, uterus, kidney; produced FIRST during proliferative phase, then replaced by Type I in remodeling
  • Type VII — anchoring fibrils at basement membrane
Sensory Receptors
  • Root hair plexus — detects hair movement
  • Meissner corpuscles (tactile) — light touch
  • Pacinian corpuscles — deep pressure, vibration
  • Krause end bulb — cold temperature; mechanoreceptor
  • Ruffini corpuscles — stretch
Skin Aging — Critical for PT Practice

Skin thickness decreases 6.4% per decade. Two independent processes: normal aging (slow irreversible degeneration from reactive oxygen species) and extrinsic aging (photoaging from UV, pollution, smoking). Changes: decreased dermal thickness, subcutaneous fat, vascularity, cellularity, mast cells, fibroblasts, GAG/HA/ground substance, collagen turnover; elastin becomes more calcified. Consequences: increased rigidity; decreased elasticity; increased vulnerability to skin tears; recovery time from mechanical depression = minutes in young skin, >24 hours in aged skin. Two most common problems in nursing home patients: xerosis and pruritus. 80% of adults >65 have 1+ chronic conditions. Most major aging changes occur in the dermis.

Non-Caucasian Skin — Clinical Variations

Cyanosis, erythema, and pallor are more difficult to detect in non-Caucasian skin — look and feel for temperature discrepancies and localized tissue discoloration. Dark skin: larger pores, sweat and sebaceous glands; more sebum; lower pH; more prone to scarring from acne; less sensitive to some irritating chemicals. Post-inflammatory hyper/hypopigmentation: melanocytes respond in an exaggerated way to trauma or inflammation in Black skin. Mongolian spots (blue-gray lumbosacral in infants) — do NOT mistake for abuse; disappear in early childhood. Dermatosis papulosa nigra: benign brown-black papules on neck/face/trunk — no treatment needed.

Braden Scale — Pressure Injury Risk

6 indicators: sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, friction/shear. All rated 1–4 except friction/shear (1–3). Total score 6–23. Lower score = higher risk.

  • <18 — at-risk status
  • 15–18 — mild risk (50–60% chance Stage I)
  • 12–14 — moderate risk (65–90% chance Stage I–II)
  • <12 — high risk (90–100% chance Stage II or deeper)
Skin Threats — Enemies of Skin Integrity

More pronounced in adults >60. Threats: pressure, friction, shear (cellular deformation enemies); moisture (causes maceration, increases shear); malnutrition and dehydration; immobility; impaired cognition; medications (topical and systemic). Support surfaces do NOT substitute for proper skin care, turning, and repositioning.

Wound Healing Phases
Wound healing is a dynamic, highly regulated cascade of overlapping events that normally occurs in a predictable fashion. The dermis does NOT regenerate — it REPAIRS — scar formation. Three overlapping phases: inflammatory (0–25 days), proliferative (days 3–21), remodeling (6 months – 3 years). Mature scar = 80% as strong as original tissue.

Inflammatory Phase — Days 0–25

Step 1 — Hemostasis

Epidermal barrier disrupted → keratinocytes release IL-1 (first signal). Platelets activated by exposed collagen → trigger vasoconstriction. Clot forms: cytokines, GFs, fibrin, fibronectin, vitronectin, thrombospondins — serves as scaffolding for leukocytes, keratinocytes, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells; reservoir of growth factors. Platelets secrete EGF, PDGF, TGF-β, IL-1 → attract neutrophils.

Wound Space Hypoxia — Key Signal

Vasoconstriction → decreased O₂ → hypoxia = key signal that controls wound healing. Stimulates endothelial cells for angiogenesis. Causes shift to anaerobic glycolysis → increased lactate production → wound becomes hyperlactic and acidotic. Necrotic tissue will not allow the wound to move into the next phase.

Neutrophils — Primary Wound Cleanser

Arrive within 24 hours of injury. Phagocytic cells that clean debris and bacteria. Most active in the hypoxic environment — work best without oxygen. First responders to infection.

Macrophages — Most Important Cell Throughout ALL Phases

Activities during inflammatory phase initiate angiogenesis and granulation tissue formation for proliferation. Phagocytosis of debris → excrete lactic acid. Secrete collagenases (debridement for new collagen). Secrete growth factors: VEGF, TNF-β, IL-1 — promotes endothelial migration and proliferation. Essential in every phase of healing.

Current of Injury — Clinical Pearl for PT

All body cells possess their own electrical currents (transport of ions through ion pumps). Current flowing between normal and injured tissue is a stimulus for the repair process. (-) polarity attracts fibroblasts; increases fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis. Absence of current may lead to chronic wound healing. Electrical stimulation may mimic the current of injury and accelerate or restart the repair process — this is the rationale for HVPC in wound care.

Mast Cells & Temperature

Mast cells: promote fibroblast proliferation (TNF-α); release histamine → vascular dilation + permeability + slight edema; produce heparin → stimulates endothelial cells. Temperature: vasodilation + injured nerve endings cause reflex hyperemia → increased temperature. If no temperature decrease by day 4 — possible sign of infection.

Proliferative Phase — Days 3–21

Cells: fibroblasts, myofibroblasts, endothelial cells, keratinocytesGranulation tissue = fragile, bleeds with traumaType III collagen → replaced by Type I in remodeling▼
Angiogenesis & Granulation Tissue

Macrophages release TNF-α, VEGF → induce angiogenesis. New capillary buds arise from intact blood vessels; endothelial cells proliferate into wound space. New capillary bed fills wound space → supplies oxygen and nutrients. Capillary loops have the appearance of granules = granulation tissue. Very fragile — trauma may induce bleeding. Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) produced by neutrophils, macrophages, fibroblasts, and keratinocytes degrade inflammatory debris.

Fibroblast Activity — ECM Building

Macrophages release GFs and cytokines → attract fibroblasts. Fibroblasts produce collagen and elastin — building blocks of connective tissue; act as scaffolding for blood vessel growth by endothelial cells. Elastin: network of fibers that stretch and recoil; gives ECM its elasticity; decreases with age (skin tears). Collagen: rope-like macromolecule that resists stretching forces. Ground substance: fills space, high water content, glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), proteoglycans, multi-adhesive glycoproteins.

Contraction & Re-epithelialization

Contraction (dermis): myofibroblasts connect to wound margin → pull dermal layer inward → closes the wound. Myofibroblasts contain actin/myosin contractile system similar to smooth muscle. Re-epithelialization (epidermis): keratinocytes near basal lamina migrate across granulation tissue; epithelial migration also from intact hair follicles and sebaceous glands — this is why partial-thickness wounds heal faster (epidermal appendages preserved).

Key Growth Factors in Wound Healing
  • EGF — stimulates angioblast, fibroblast, and keratinocyte proliferation
  • PDGF — chemotactic for macrophages and PMNs; stimulates matrix production and angiogenesis
  • TGF-β — regulates matrix and collagen synthesis; involved in scar formation
  • VEGF — powerful stimulator of angiogenesis; granulation tissue formation
  • FGF — stimulates angiogenesis, epithelialization, and collagen production
  • TNF-α — stimulates fibroblasts; activates neutrophils; levels elevated in chronic wounds

Collagen Remodeling — Wolff's Law Applied to Scar

Collagen laid down during proliferation = Type III. Type III lysed by tissue collagenases and replaced with Type I (stronger fibril). Finely tuned balance between collagen synthesis and lysis. Stress has a significant effect on scar remodeling — shape, strength, and pliability. Collagen, elastin, and ground substance are affected by the direction and magnitude of mechanical stress applied to the scar (Wolff's Law). Connective tissue is laid down in response to lines of stress. Too much stress = newly formed scar pulls apart. Scar heals centripetally; contracts at forces measured in tens to hundreds of pounds. Most endothelial cells, macrophages, and myofibroblasts undergo apoptosis.

Scar Types

Flat pliable scar: collagen breakdown ≥ rate of production. Hypertrophic scar: collagen breakdown < rate of production = RED, RAISED, ITCHY; confined to original injury area; 50–60% incidence; peaks 3–6 months; myofibroblasts persist causing further hypertrophy. Keloid: type of hypertrophic scar that EXTENDS BEYOND the injury area; tumor-like appearance. Contracture: scar tissue continues to contract unless met with opposing force — 24 hours/day until fully mature.

Chronic Wound — When Healing Stalls

A wound that deviates from expected sequence of repair (time, appearance, response to treatment). Characteristics: senescent cells (cease to divide); higher MMPs, lower TIMPs (tissue inhibitors of matrix metalloproteinases → ECM degradation); greater inflammatory cytokines; arrested current of injury. PT trigger: if no 20–40% decrease in wound surface area within 2–4 weeks — reassess and consider adjunctive interventions.

Factors Affecting Wound Healing — Know for Every Patient You Treat
Intrinsic (patient status): age, chronic disease, impaired perfusion, immunosuppression, neuropathy
Extrinsic (environmental): medications (anticoagulants, immunosuppressants, steroids), malnutrition, irradiation (injures fibroblasts + endothelial cells), chemotherapy (stops angiogenesis), psychophysiologic stress, wound bioburden
Iatrogenic (how PT manages it): local ischemia from pressure/compression, inappropriate wound care, trauma during dressing change, improper sharp debridement, time to heal
Wound Assessment & Lab Values
"If you want to heal a wound, you need to address not only the wound, but the underlying cause. This means treating the whole patient. It is a team effort." Lab values tell you WHY a wound is not healing. ABI tells you about perfusion before you apply compression. The Braden scale predicts pressure injury risk. Document everything — your notes support reimbursement, treatment decisions, and litigation defense.

Wound Measurement & Characteristics

Length = vertical (head to foot = 12 to 6 o'clock)Width = horizontal (3 and 9 o'clock)Tunneling: clock method (e.g., 1.9 cm at 3 o'clock)▼
Measurement Protocol
  • Length: vertical (12 = head, 6 = feet)
  • Width: horizontal (3 and 9 o'clock)
  • Depth: cotton-tipped applicator into deepest visible portion
  • Tunneling (sinus tract): narrow passageway within wound bed — document depth and clock position (e.g., "Wound tunnels 1.9 cm at 3-o'clock position")
  • Undermining: area of tissue under wound edges that becomes eroded resulting in a large wound with small opening — document as "Undermining 1.2 cm from 9-o'clock to 1-o'clock positions"
Wound Bed Tissue Types
  • Granulation tissue: red/pink, moist; healthy; percentage present
  • Eschar: black/brown necrotic tissue; dry, hard; full-thickness damage
  • Slough: yellow, stringy consistency; nonviable tissue
  • Exposed structures: document bone, tendon, muscle, vessels, nerves, fascia, hardware
Drainage Documentation
  • Serous: clear/pale yellow = normal
  • Serosanguinous: blood-tinged = normal

Wound Types & Etiology
Most wounds you will see in PT have a specific vascular, pressure, or neuropathic etiology. Knowing the difference between arterial, venous, and neuropathic ulcers is essential — the wrong treatment (e.g., compression on a severely ischemic limb) can cause serious harm. Burns require understanding depth to predict healing time.

Arterial Insufficiency
  • Location: dorsal foot, toes, distal
  • Shape: regular; may conform to trauma
  • Wound bed: black eschar; pale granulation or gangrene
  • Drainage: little or none
  • Pain: severe; increases with elevation; relieved by dependency; patient may sleep sitting up in recliner
  • Periwound: loss of hair growth; thickened/yellow toenails; pale, dusky, cyanotic skin; decreased/absent pulses; decreased limb temperature

  • Location: medial malleolus; medial lower leg
  • Shape: superficial; irregular
  • Wound bed: red/ruddy; fibrous, glossy coating
  • Drainage: moderate to copious
  • Pain: mild to moderate; decreases with elevation and/or compression

  • 50% have contralateral ulcer within 18 months
  • Monofilament: if unable to perceive 5.07 monofilament (10g pressure) = at risk for ulceration

Pressure Injuries — Etiology, Prevention & PT Management

95% over sacrum, GT, ischial tuberosity, posterior calcaneus, lateral malleolusMalnutrition = 2nd most common risk factorInverse pressure-time relationship▼
Etiology & Risk Factors

Inverse pressure-time relationship: higher pressure = shorter time to ulceration. Muscle is MORE sensitive to pressure than skin — deep damage may exist before skin changes. Greatest risk: SCI patients, hospitalized patients, long-term care. Risk factors: shear (force parallel to soft tissue), excessive moisture (maceration + increased shear), impaired mobility, malnutrition (2nd most common), impaired sensation, advanced age, history of prior pressure ulcer (scar = only 80% original strength).

Support Surface Selection
  • Stage I: mattresses and mattress overlays
  • Stage II: specialty mattresses (pressure-reducing foam, alternative air, low air loss)
  • Stage III: air-fluidized beds
  • Consider: pressure redistribution, shear reduction, continence, temperature/moisture control, patient mobility, body weight, deformities
  • Support surfaces do NOT substitute for proper skin care, turning, and repositioning
PT Interventions for Pressure Injuries
  • Address pressure, friction, shear, moisture — the etiology
  • Educate on bandaging, cleansing, pain control, skin care
  • Determine decline in ulcer status early
  • Dietary consult recommended for all pressure injuries
  • Culture wounds that fail to progress or show infection signs
  • Assess for osteomyelitis with exposed bone or purulent/malodorous drainage from deep wounds
  • Early surgical consult for deep pressure ulcers
  • You must minimize pressure to heal these wounds — consider ALL surfaces patient will be on: bed, commode, chair, car seat
Neuropathic Ulcer — PT Responsibility

Visually inspecting and touching the hands and feet at EVERY session is essential. 50% of patients are unaware they have lost protective sensation. Autonomic neuropathy → dry, cracked skin; increased callus formation; uncontrolled vasodilation → osteopenia. Motor neuropathy → intrinsic muscle weakness → deformities → increased pressure and shear. Charcot foot: neurogenic arthropathy; metatarsals and tarsals most affected; minor traumas go undetected and untreated.

Wound Infection — Biofilm, MRSA, Wound Cultures

Biofilm = protected from antibiotics and antisepticsGold standard: tissue biopsyMRSA: can live hours to days on surfacesSilent infection risk: immunocompromised + poor perfusion▼
Wound Biofilm

Communities of microorganisms attached to wound surface encased in a glycocalyx. Protected against harsh environments, antiseptics, and antimicrobials. May increase bacterial virulence and resistance. Generally found on devitalized tissues, implanted devices, and within gastric mucosa. Biofilm is a primary reason chronic wounds fail to progress — PLWS and sharp debridement are effective disruption strategies.

Diagnosing Wound Infection
  • Gold standard: tissue biopsy
  • Swab cultures: quantify number and type of bacteria; rotate over 1 cm² of viable wound surface for 5 seconds with enough pressure to express tissue fluid
  • Fluid aspiration and blood tests also used
  • Gram stain: Gram-positive (purple) vs Gram-negative (pink); cocci vs bacilli; intracellular bacteria
  • Sensitivity and resistance testing determines appropriate antibiotic
MRSA — Nosocomial & Community Acquired

Can live hours to days on surfaces. Spread by direct contact, contaminated equipment, hands. Nosocomial risk factors: increased age, diabetes, immunosuppression, malnutrition, recent surgery, immobility/debility, large burns, prior antimicrobial use. Community acquired: prisons, contact sports teams, military. Premature cessation of antimicrobials and misuse/overuse of antibiotics increase resistance. Physician must prescribe correct antibiotic for the specific microorganism.

Factors Increasing Infection Risk

Host factors: break in skin integrity, diabetes, malnutrition, obesity, steroid use, immunocompromised, increased age. Local factors: ischemia, necrotic tissue, wound debris, chronic wounds. Silent infections: patients who are immunocompromised or have inadequate perfusion are at greater risk — look for systemic signs (malaise, tachycardia, altered mental status, hypotension) when suspected.

Dressing Types — Properties, Uses & Contraindications

Gauze

Woven (cotton) or nonwoven (synthetic, more absorbent). Highly permeable, relatively non-occlusive. Primary or secondary dressing. Inexpensive short-term. Limitations: costly over time; may adhere to wound bed; higher infection rate than occlusive dressings; require frequent changes. Uses: infected and non-infected wounds of all sizes/shapes/depths; packing; frequent changes; highly exudating wounds; fragile skin (roll gauze keeps adhesives off).

Semipermeable Film

Thin transparent polyurethane with adhesive. Permeable to water vapor, O₂, CO₂ but impermeable to bacteria and water. Little absorptive capability. Allows wound visualization. Highly elastic. Leave in place 5–7 days; change if channel develops. Cannot use on: infected wounds; moderate to heavy drainage; fragile skin. Uses: superficial/partial-thickness, skin tears, donor sites, areas of friction, over IV catheters (allows bathing), over wounds for ultrasound.

Hydrogels (Sheet & Amorphous)

80–90% water- or glycerin-based. Donate moisture to dry wounds — "put moisture back." Non-adherent. Can reduce pain. May decrease pain in wound. Cannot use on: heavily draining wounds; infected wounds. May dehydrate. Require secondary dressing. Uses: minimally/moderately exudating, burns, radiation wounds, donor sites, blisters/abrasions. Amorphous hydrogel = coupling agent for ultrasound; softens eschar.

Semipermeable Foam

Polyurethane foam; hydrophilic wound side, hydrophobic outside. Permeable to gas but NOT bacteria. Provides thermal insulation and cushioning. Adhesive and non-adhesive forms. NOT for: dry/eschar-covered wounds; exposed tendons (caution); arterial ulcers; heel ulcers (tends to roll); infected wounds (unless changed daily). Uses: minimally to heavily exudating; neuropathic ulcers; venous insufficiency ulcers; ostomy sites; minor burns.

Hydrocolloids

Hydrophilic colloidal particles with strong film/foam adhesive backing. Absorb fluid slowly into gel-like mass. Impermeable to water, O₂, and bacteria — effective barrier against urine, stool, MRSA, hepatitis B, HIV-1, Pseudomonas. Wide absorption range. NOT for: bleeding/heavily draining; infected; dry/arterial/third-degree burns; exposed tendons; poor skin integrity; immunocompromised (caution). May cause hypergranulation. Leaves residue in wound bed. Uses: partial to full-thickness, venous ulcers, minor burns, pressure ulcers (sacral/coccygeal designs available).

Alginates

Salts of alginic acid from brown seaweed. React with exudate to form hydrophilic gel. Absorbs 20× its weight. Highly permeable and non-occlusive. Require secondary dressing. NOT for: full-thickness burns; dry/minimally draining wounds; exposed tendon/joint capsule/bone; neonates <38 weeks. Change daily on infected wounds. Uses: highly exudating, venous insufficiency ulcers, tunneling wounds; swabs to probe/fill/measure wound depth.

Antimicrobial Dressings (Silver & Cadexomer Iodine)

Incorporated into all dressing classes. Broad-spectrum antimicrobial. Decreases wound bioburden. Silver cautions: blue-black wound bed discoloration; use short-term; monitor for hypersensitivity; do NOT moisten with saline (deactivates silver); cannot use with electrical stimulation; remove before MRI; cytotoxic to fibroblasts and epithelial cells. Cadexomer iodine cautions: thyroid disease; iodine sensitivity; large cavity wounds. Uses: infected/colonized chronic wounds, burns, pressure ulcers, neuropathic ulcers.

Specialty Dressings
  • Impregnated gauze (Petrolatum/Adaptic): non-adherent contact layer; burn wounds; exposed tendons; granulating wounds; prevents desiccation of tendons
  • Xeroform (bismuth tribromophenate): cytotoxic to inflammatory cells; caution in venous insufficiency ulcers
  • Honey (Manuka/Leptospermum): antimicrobial; controls odor; may stimulate growth factors
  • Collagen dressings: Stage III–IV pressure ulcers; partial/full-thickness; contraindicated if sensitive to collagen
  • Charcoal dressings: control wound odor — improve quality of life

Skin Sealants

Wipe or spray for intact skin. Makes skin tacky for better edge seal. Prevents maceration from adhesives.

Moisture Barriers

Ointments or creams. Prevents perineal rashes and skin breakdown. Can apply to macerated skin. Use whenever incontinence is present or skin folds are involved.

Moisturizers — Basic Skin Care

Key component of basic skin care. Restores barrier function and skin hydration. Avoid perfume or alcohol (possible reaction). Apply to intact skin only. Petrolatum = penetrates best (can be greasy). Ointments > creams > lotions for occlusiveness. Do NOT apply between toes — increases fungal infection risk. Especially important in aging skin (xerosis prevention).

Moist Wound Healing Evidence

Winter (1962, Nature): wounds covered with occlusive dressings heal twice as rapidly as wounds exposed to air. Occlusive dressings have lower infection rates than gauze — they serve as bacterial barriers and facilitate neutralization of microorganisms. Common fear: "moist environment facilitates colonization" — FACT: occlusive dressings have lower infection rates compared to non-occlusive. Change if barrier becomes compromised. Most should be discontinued if infection is present (exception: alginates and foams).

Effects: increases local blood flow, capillary filling, and lymphatic flow; may approximate wound edges and facilitate contraction; decreases wound/periwound edema; increases granulation tissue, angiogenesis, and epithelialization; decreases wound bioburden; promotes cell proliferation; maintains moist, warm wound environment.   • Irrigate and debride as needed first

Advantages: may cost less over time; can use in any setting; moist wound environment. As PT in SNF: you will often see patients on VAC — confirm order for mobility and understand mobility limitations from tubing and device.

Ultrasound, Hyperbaric Oxygen & Whirlpool

US: enhances all 3 phases; 3 MHz superficial, 1 MHz deepHBO: 100% O₂ at >1.5 ATM; controversial evidenceWhirlpool: one of most OVERUSED interventions — APTA Choosing Wisely▼
Therapeutic Ultrasound for Wounds

Mechanisms: cavitation (stable = changes in cell membrane diffusion); acoustic microstreaming (fluid movement across membranes from pressure wave). Effects: enhances all 3 phases of wound healing; increases collagen deposition, granulation tissue, angiogenesis; enhances wound contraction; improves scar pliability. Low frequency (25–40 kHz) + saline mist (MIST Therapy): assist with debridement of fibrin; reduce wound bioburden; disrupt biofilm; reduce MMP production; increase vasodilation and early release of growth factors. Parameters: 3 MHz for superficial wounds; 1 MHz for deep wounds; pulsed 0.5–1.0 W/cm²; 2–3 min/zone; 2×/day or 3×/week. Contraindications: untreated osteomyelitis; active profuse bleeding; severe arterial insufficiency; DVT.

Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBO)

100% oxygen at >1.5 ATM in mono- or multiplace chamber. 14× greater oxygen dissolved in plasma. Tissue O₂ must be >40 mmHg for normal wound healing. Effects: increases O₂ concentration gradient; may reduce bacterial growth; may enhance angiogenesis, granulation tissue, and epithelialization; may reduce edema. Method: 90–120 min; 2×/day to 3×/week; 10–60 sessions. If 50% decrease in wound surface area NOT noted after 10 HBO treatments = wound unlikely to respond. Controversial — American Diabetes Association and NPUAP do NOT recommend for neuropathic or pressure injuries. Revascularization should be considered first.

Whirlpool — APTA Choosing Wisely: Don't Use

Nonselective mechanical debridement. Risks: bacterial cross-contamination; damage to fragile tissue from high turbine forces; complications in extremity edema (dependent positioning in warm water). Use more selective hydrotherapy: directed wound irrigation or PLWS instead. If used: 92–98°F (smaller) or 98–102°F (larger); 10–20 minutes. Absolute contraindications: venous insufficiency, edema, lymphedema; incontinence with wounds requiring perineal submersion; active profuse bleeding; uncontrolled seizures; tunneling wounds; significant undermining; clean granulating wounds; skin folds; combative/confused patients.

Scar Management & Nutrition
Scar management is PT's domain — especially after burns, surgeries, and prolonged wounds. The goal is to maximize function and minimize contracture. Nutrition directly impacts wound healing: you cannot heal without adequate protein, vitamin C, zinc, and hydration. Always consider a dietician referral for any patient with a wound.

Scar Management — Massage, Pressure Garments, Silicone, Splinting

Pressure garments: 23 hrs/day; distal to proximalSilicone: apply as soon as wound re-epithelializesScar contracts 24 hrs/day until mature — oppose with constant forceMassage: 3–4×/day; blanch the scar▼
Scar Massage Protocol

Press hard enough to make the scar blanch. Techniques: circular, vertical, horizontal motion; pinch and roll. Any lotion that makes skin soft (avoid perfumes). Frequency: 3–4 times per day. Evidence: studies have failed to demonstrate positive effects on height, pliability, and vascularity, but decreases in itching and pain have been shown, along with improvements in anxiety and depression levels.

Pressure Garments

Controls collagen synthesis by limiting blood, oxygen, and nutrients to scar tissue. Reduces collagen production to normal scar tissue levels. Encourages realignment of collagen bundles. Must fit snugly. Apply distal to proximal. Need to be worn 23 hours a day. Concave areas filled with inserts (silicone, elastomere, foam padding). Apply sunblock before donning. Effective for moderate to severe scarring (12-year study evidence). Recommended for: deep dermal wounds healed spontaneously over weeks (>21 days); grafted wounds; children and young adults; darker pigmented skin.

Stretching & Exercise

Stretch to point of blanching. Grade I–II joint mobs for inpatients and post-op; grades I–IV once scar is maturing. Supervised exercise: improves muscle strength, power, lean body mass, and aerobic capacity; decreases number of surgical interventions. Practice guidelines: begin as early as immediately post-discharge from acute care; programs should last 6–12 weeks for adults, up to 12 weeks for children. Evaluate strength and cardiovascular endurance for individuals 7 years and older.

Silicone Gel & Sheets

Possible mechanisms: controls scar hydration; increased pressure; absorption of silicone; increases scar temperature. Practice guidelines: apply to burn scars with high probability of hypertrophic scarring (≥21 days to heal) as soon as wound re-epithelializes. Only treat immature scars — mature burn scars do NOT respond. Silicone gels have fewer adverse reactions than gel sheets. At 4 months: treatment group had reductions in pigmentation, vascularity, pliability, and pruritus (not pain).

Splinting

Splinting must be utilized if daily therapy cannot be provided — scar contracts 24 hours a day until mature. Extenders and positioning MUST be used continuously. When to initiate: full ROM = preventative; loss of ROM = therapeutic (nearly always). Splint in position of comfort opposes typical scar contracture direction. HEP splinting compliance is critical. Burns: be creative; keep exercises focused; give most important exercises; emphasize function; don't give HEP at the last minute.

Other Wound Types — Skin Tears, Surgical Wounds, Radiation Burns

Skin tears: elderly at highest risk; improper lifting and dressing removalSurgical wounds: epithelialized 7–10 days; dehiscence 4–14 days post-opRadiation: avoid adhesives, heat, ice, heavy detergents▼
Skin Tears

Traumatic wounds from shear or friction separating epidermis from dermis. Partial-thickness wound. Elderly at highest risk (aging changes = vulnerability to tear-type injuries). Most common causes in healthcare: improper turning and lifting procedures; removing dressings and adhesives; healthcare provider fingernails or jewelry. Presentation: linear tear or flap; slight serous drainage; minimal to significant bleeding; minimal pain. Interventions: irrigate with saline; approximate wound edges + skin sealant; moisture-retentive dressing (film or hydrogel + nonadherent gauze + roll gauze); caution with adhesives; generally heal without complications; watch for infection.

Surgical Wounds

Primary closure (sutures, staples, tissue adhesives): keep clean and dry 24–48 hours; expect minimal bleeding/drainage first day; observe for infection (redness, induration); epithelialized in 7–10 days. Sutures more likely to cause inflammation than staples. Dehiscence: occurs 4–14 days post-op; 0.5–3.4% of abdominopelvic surgeries; high mortality rate. Causes: too much tension on wound edges or underlying infection. Risk factors: malnutrition, diabetes, steroids, smoking, COPD, anemia. Management: treat the cause — decrease tension (binders, Montgomery straps, splinting) or treat infection (antibiotics, irrigation, debridement).

Radiation Burns & Radiation Fibrosis

~50% of cancer patients receive radiation. Radiation directly destroys tissue and causes indirect damage from free radical production. Inhibits inflammatory response and proliferative phase. Presentation: mild inflammation, slight erythema, local edema progressing to dry/scaling/itchy/hyperpigmented skin; bullae and fibrinous exudate if radiation continues; late injury = skin dry, discolored, hairless, atrophied, fibrotic, inelastic, translucent (radiation fibrosis). Interventions: protect from mechanical forces; avoid adhesives; avoid superficial heat and ice; decrease bathing frequency and pat dry; avoid heavy detergents or perfumes; amorphous hydrogel to soothe; moisturizing ointment; oatmeal baths or topical steroids. DO apply sunscreen if direct sun exposure expected.

Common Skin Disorders — PT Clinical Recognition
  • Atopic dermatitis (eczema): chronic inflammatory; flexor surfaces; xerosis + pruritus; treat with moisturizing + topical agents; avoid irritants
  • Contact dermatitis: in pattern of contact with allergen; EKG electrode adhesives are common cause in clinical settings — always check adhesive sites
  • Stasis dermatitis: lower extremity; varicose veins/DVT history; significant LE edema; treat with UNNA boot, ambulation with pressure garments, LE elevation
  • Intertrigo: skin fold inflammation from friction, heat, and moisture; common in obese patients; treat with moisture barrier and breathable fabric

What does Braden Scale measure, what's the scoring, and what score puts someone at high risk?
tap
Braden Scale = pressure injury risk assessment. 6 subscales: Sensory Perception, Moisture, Activity, Mobility, Nutrition, Friction/Shear. All rated 1–4 except friction/shear (1–3). Total range: 6–23. Lower score = higher risk.
• 15–18 = Mild risk (50–60% chance Stage I PI)
• 12–14 = Moderate risk (65–90% chance Stage I–II)
• <12 = High risk (90–100% chance Stage II or deeper)
• <18 = at-risk status (trigger preventative interventions)
Name the 6 debridement methods, which are selective vs. nonselective, and what's the one absolute contraindication to all debridement?
tap
Selective (remove only necrotic): Sharp, Autolytic, Enzymatic, Biological (maggots). Nonselective (remove both viable and nonviable): Mechanical (wet-to-dry, PLWS, scrubbing), Surgical.
Sharp: fastest; PT only, NOT PTA; where allowed by law. Autolytic: least invasive; 72–96 hr dressing changes. Enzymatic: collagenase (only FDA-approved); requires physician Rx. Biological: FDA-approved; seldom used. Surgical: physician/podiatrist.
Absolute contraindication to ALL debridement: PYODERMA GANGRENOSUM. Deep ulcer with violet/blue border; 50% associated with inflammatory bowel disease; treatment = immunosuppression. Also: do NOT debride stable heel eschar without edema/erythema/fluctuance.
What is the NPWT application protocol and its contraindications?
tap
Protocol: (1) Irrigate and debride as needed; (2) Cut foam to size, place within wound bed; (3) Protect periwound with hydrocolloid or skin sealant; (4) Place tubing in wound, connect to canister; (5) Cover with film drape for air-tight seal; (6) Set pressure 50–175 mmHg, constant or intermittent; (7) Change every 12–72 hours.
Contraindications: Do NOT place foam directly on exposed blood vessels, anastomotic sites, organs, or nerves. Malignancy in the wound. Untreated osteomyelitis. Non-enteric and unexplored fistulas. Necrotic tissue with eschar present.
Effects: increases local blood flow, granulation tissue, angiogenesis, epithelialization; decreases edema and bioburden; maintains moist warm environment.
What are the NPIAP pressure injury stages and key distinguishing features?
tap
DTI: persistent non-blanchable deep red/maroon/purple; intact or non-intact; blood-filled blister; pain/temp precede color changes; bone-muscle interface injury.
Stage 1: non-blanchable erythema, intact skin; changes in sensation/temperature/firmness may precede visual changes.
Stage 2: partial-thickness, exposed dermis; pink/red/moist; serum blister; NO adipose, NO granulation, NO slough/eschar.
Stage 3: full-thickness; adipose visible; granulation + epibole (rolled edges); NO fascia/muscle/tendon/bone.
Stage 4: full-thickness; fascia/muscle/tendon/bone EXPOSED or directly palpable.
Unstageable: covered by slough or eschar; stable heel eschar = do NOT remove.

What dressing do you choose for each wound scenario: (A) full-thickness granulating wound with heavy drainage; (B) necrotic non-draining wound; (C) highly exudating venous ulcer?
tap
(A) Full-thickness, granulating, heavy drainage: Alginate (absorbs 20× weight), semipermeable foam, or hydrocolloid. Protect wound bed from trauma. Observe for infection. Change daily if infected; can extend to 3–4 days if clean.
(B) Necrotic, non-draining wound: First consider debridement (enzymatic collagenase, autolytic with hydrocolloid, or surgical/sharp). Dressing: amorphous hydrogel to soften eschar; hydrocolloid; impregnated gauze or transparent film. Goal: soften and remove eschar while maintaining moist environment.
(C) Highly exudating venous ulcer: Alginate is ideal (highly absorptive, non-adherent). Semipermeable foam is also appropriate. Ensure ABI >0.7 before applying compression over the dressing. Address venous HTN as the root cause.
What are scar management treatments and who is pressure therapy most recommended for?
tap
Scar management tools: massage (3–4×/day, blanch the scar; circular/vertical/horizontal/pinch-and-roll); pressure garments (23 hrs/day, distal to proximal, sunblock before donning); silicone gel/sheets (apply as soon as re-epithelialized; immature scars only); stretching (to blanching; Grade I–II early; I–IV as scar matures); splinting (preventative with full ROM; therapeutic with ROM loss; needed 24 hrs if no daily therapy); therapeutic exercise (6–12 weeks; may start immediately post-acute discharge).
Pressure therapy most recommended for: wounds taking >21 days to heal; grafted wounds surrounded by deep dermal wounds; children and young adults; darker pigmented skin. Effectiveness supported by 12-year study for moderate to severe scarring.
Why is the whirlpool on the APTA Choosing Wisely list, and when is it truly indicated vs. contraindicated?
tap
Why "Don't use whirlpool for wound management" (Choosing Wisely #5): Nonselective debridement; predisposes to bacterial cross-contamination; damage to fragile tissue from high turbine forces; complications in extremity edema from dependent positioning in warm water. More selective alternatives (PLWS, directed wound irrigation) are superior.
If used, indications: infected wounds to reduce bacterial load; non-draining wounds to rehydrate; thick eschar to soften; loosely adherent necrotic tissue/thick exudate to facilitate debridement.
Absolute contraindications: venous insufficiency/edema/lymphedema; incontinence requiring perineal submersion; active profuse bleeding; uncontrolled seizures; clean granulating wounds; tunneling; significant undermining; skin folds; multiple open areas (not all infected).
What are the 5 burn depths, their appearance, pain level, and expected healing time?
tap
Superficial (1st degree): epidermis only; reddened skin; painful; 3–7 days; seldom clinically significant (sunburn).
Superficial partial thickness (2nd degree): epidermis + papillary dermis; moist, red, weepy; good capillary refill; extremely painful; 7–21 days.
Deep partial thickness: epidermis + papillary + reticular dermis; mottled white/pink/deep red; some hair follicles remain; less sensation than SPT; variable healing; often needs grafting.
Full thickness (3rd degree): entire epidermis + dermis including appendages; white/charred/yellow/brown; dry, leathery; insensate; heals by contracture and epithelial ingrowth from edges or skin graft.
4th degree: subcutaneous structures including ligaments, tendon, bone; surgical intervention; possible amputation.
What is the "current of injury" and how does it relate to electrical stimulation in wound care?
tap
All body cells possess their own electrical currents (ion transport through ion pumps). When a wound occurs, a measurable electrical field is created between normal and injured tissue. This endogenous wound current is confirmed in ALL wounds (discovered by Du-Bois Reymond). The current of injury is a stimulus for the repair process: (-) polarity attracts fibroblasts and increases fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis. Absence of this current = chronic wound (arrested current of injury). Rationale for E-stim (HVPC): electrical stimulation mimics the current of injury to accelerate or RESTART the repair process. Also causes galvanotaxis (guided cell migration), stimulates angiogenesis, reduces edema, and combats infection. Indications: Stage 3–4 PI, chronic wounds failing 30 days of standard care. Cannot combine with silver dressings.
What is the PLWS infection control protocol, and why is it so strict?
tap
Why so strict: In 2003, an outbreak of multi-drug resistant bacteria occurred in patients treated with PLWS. PLWS was NOT being used with proper PPE and infection control techniques — some patients died. This makes PPE compliance non-negotiable.
Protocol: Private room with ventilation and doors CLOSED; all exposed items in room covered; all tubes/ports covered; consider masking the patient; NO family or visitors during treatment; standard precautions + hand hygiene; appropriate PPE throughout; dispose of all disposables properly; do NOT re-use single-use items; after treatment, disinfect ALL environmental surfaces touched.
Dosing: once/day standard; twice/day if >50% necrotic + purulent/foul odor; 2–3×/week if full granulation base with no odor/purulence.
What skin changes make elderly patients high-risk for wounds, and what are the 2 most common skin problems in nursing homes?
tap
Skin changes with aging (6th decade and beyond): skin thickness decreases 6.4%/decade; decreased dermal thickness, subcutaneous fat, vascularity, cellularity, mast cells, fibroblasts, GAG/HA/ground substance, collagen turnover; elastin becomes calcified. Consequences: increased rigidity; decreased elasticity; increased vulnerability to skin tears; inflammatory response decreases; tissue regeneration slows; recovery time from mechanical depression = minutes in young skin vs. >24 hours in aged skin. 80% of adults >65 have 1+ chronic conditions.
2 most common problems in nursing homes: XEROSIS and PRURITUS. Important for PT: always check skin every session, especially in nursing home patients — skin tears from transfers, positioning, and equipment are preventable.
