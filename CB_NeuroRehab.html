Neural Plasticity & The Limbic System
Rehabilitation IS the process of maximizing learning. Neural plasticity is the mechanism by which the brain encodes experience, learns new behaviors, and by which a damaged brain relearns lost behavior in response to rehabilitation. Without understanding these principles, you are doing exercises — not neurorehabilitation.

Neuroanatomical Changes with Plasticity

Decreases, increases, and shifts in brain activation to facilitate behavioral improvement. Reallocation of hemispheric resources (L/R dominance changes). Recruitment of additional brain regions. Strengthening of anatomical structures (increased white-matter density). Increased coherence in neural pathways — "Neurons that fire together, wire together." Example: Occipital lobe in a blind person activating when hearing sounds (cross-modal plasticity).

What Plasticity Requires in Therapy

(1) Great repetition of practice. (2) A strong desire by the patient to gain functional ability (compelled to learn). (3) The potential for the CNS to change. (4) A therapist who knows how to intentionally drive the nervous system. Cortical reorganization is dependent on goal-directed, task-specific practice — not mere repetition of movements. Motor cortex reorganization was NOT a consequence of spontaneous recovery in the absence of training. Knowledge does not mean understanding — you must be able to apply these principles.

5 Levels of Limbic Function

Level 1 — State of Alertness: Brainstem reticular activating system → thalamus → limbic → cerebral cortex. Information moving to and from these structures. Level 2 — Subconscious Drives & Innate Instincts: Survival-oriented drives. If a person is functioning in survival mode, they will NOT be able to learn. Dealing with the emotions of fear first will enable better therapy outcomes. Level 3 — Abstract Conceptualization: Somatosensory and motor cortices work together to develop motor programs. Motor program development is abstract thinking until it becomes concrete with action. Level 4 — Social Expression: Limbic closely tied to frontal lobe. A brain insult at ANY level results in expression on ALL levels. Level 5 — The Unexplained: People who believe they will get better often do, and those who don't, don't. Conscious intent for health drives hypothalamic and autoimmune function. "Mind, Body, Spirit." A patient's beliefs, what they say, how they identify themselves, makes a difference in how they progress.

Critical Limbic Takeaway

If limbic decides NOT to act, motor response will be ABSENT. Limbic connects with cerebellum, basal ganglia, frontal lobe, and brainstem motor generators. Limbic drives behavior through willingness to participate and forms the belief that the outcome will lead to some quality of life that has been lost. Intuition: humans bring to consciousness about 10% of incoming information, but the brain uses 100% of input subconsciously. The non-conscious decision-making capacity IS intuition.

Motor Learning Theory — ASAP, OPTIMAL & Treatment Roles

ASAP = 3 elementsPositive FB > negative FBCIMT = constraint good limb▼
Accelerated Skill Acquisition Program (ASAP) — Winstein

A hybrid of task-specific and skill-based/impairment-mitigating training with embedded patient-centered motivational enhancements. 3 essential elements: (1) Skill acquisition — targeted task-specific training is the single most important approach. (2) Impairment mitigation — addressing underlying deficits. (3) Motivational enhancements — patient-centered behavioral change strategies. "Practice is more than mere repetition — it is repetition with a purpose." (Cross). The therapist acts as a facilitator-instructor providing instructions, guidance, feedback about errors, suggestions for modifications, and encouragement. Patients are engaged in repeated attempts to produce motor behaviors beyond their present capabilities — learning to use the damaged motor system in a NEW way to accomplish environmental goals.

OPTIMAL Theory of Motor Learning

Optimizing Performance Through Intrinsic Motivation and Attention for Learning. Key findings: (1) Positive feedback: Providing learners with feedback after "good" trials (vs poor trials) results in more effective learning. (2) Social comparative feedback: False positive feedback ("you're performing better than average") enhances motor learning MORE than negative or even neutral feedback. (3) Expectations carry personal histories forward in time — they are NOT motivationally neutral. They anticipate rewarding properties of significance. Practice-related techniques like drills and frequent feedback can have a performance-enhancing effect BUT some practice-related interventions may slow progress (such as randomly ordered tasks — which can be beneficial for generalization but slows initial acquisition).

Therapist Roles & Treatment Strategies

Compensation (Internal Support): Patient uses different internal strategies to accomplish tasks. Substitution (External Support): Teaching patient to use a different sensory system or muscle group, using assistive devices. Functional Training: Where neuro rehab is focused. Select procedures using patient's strengths. Avoid activities too difficult that elicit abnormal movement patterns. "Shrink" the environment to allow normal motor programs to run. Impairment Training: Correcting sub-system impairment during activity. Augmented Feedback: External cues (verbal, visual, kinesthetic) and perimeter control over motor programs. OK initially but patient MUST eventually learn tasks without cues — otherwise you create dependence, not learning. CIMT: Constrains good limb and forces use of stroke-affected limb. Drives neuroplasticity through intensive task-specific practice. Sometimes you address several aspects at once — knowing what you are doing and WHY enables you to reproduce results.

Core Neuro Outcome Measures

Berg Balance Scale (BBS): 56 pts. Sitting/standing, static/dynamic balance. PD: ≤45 = recommend AD; ≤42 = higher fall risk. Functional Gait Assessment (FGA): Modified 8-item DGI with improved reliability, reduced ceiling effect. Deviation from midline, speed changes, positional effects. Activities-Specific Balance Confidence (ABC): Self-report. PD <69% = predictive of recurrent falls. Vestibular <67%. 10-Meter Walk Test: Gait speed (m/s) — a vital sign for neuro patients. 6-Minute Walk Test: Walking endurance and aerobic capacity. 5× Sit-to-Stand: Functional LE strength and transfer strategies. MoCA: Cognitive screening — if impaired, may need SLP referral. MMSE: Mental status screening. Practice these as outcome measures.

Neurologic Exam Part 1 — Mental Status & Cranial Nerves
The neuro exam tests function. Each part should titrate the patient's level of function from easiest to most difficult. Record what the patient can AND cannot do — this allows comparison over time. The neuro exam has 6 subdivisions: Mental Status, Cranial Nerves, Motor Exam, Reflexes, Coordination & Gait, Sensory Exam. CN abnormalities with any other neurologic symptom strongly suggests brainstem dysfunction.

Critical CN Rule for PTs

Perhaps more than any other part of the neuro exam, cranial nerve testing can raise "red flags" that suggest specific neurologic dysfunction rather than a systemic disorder. Many medical conditions cause lethargy, unsteadiness, headaches, or dizziness. However, ANY of these symptoms together with cranial nerve abnormalities strongly suggests brainstem dysfunction as the cause. CN testing IS NOT the same as CNS testing — functionally CN is part of the peripheral nervous system, but structurally it's part of the CNS.

Neurologic Exam Part 2 — Motor, Reflexes, Coordination & Sensory
The motor exam distinguishes UMN from LMN lesions — this is one of the most critical clinical skills in neuro rehab. Reflexes, coordination, and sensory testing further localize lesions and guide treatment planning. Patterns of weakness, tone changes, and reflex abnormalities point to specific levels of the nervous system.

Motor Exam — Observation Through Strength Testing

UMN: hyperreflexia, spasticity, BabinskiLMN: atrophy, fasciculations, hyporeflexia▼
UMN vs LMN — The Fundamental Distinction

Upper Motor Neuron Signs: Weakness, hyperreflexia (3–5+), increased tone (spasticity — velocity-dependent), (+) Babinski (upgoing toe + fanning), clonus, (+) Hoffmann's sign, slow/awkward fine finger movements or toe tapping without weakness. IMPORTANT: Acute UMN lesions often show initial flaccid paralysis with decreased tone and decreased reflexes. Hyperreflexia and spasticity develop over hours to weeks. Lower Motor Neuron Signs: Weakness, atrophy, fasciculations (spontaneous quivering), hyporeflexia (0–1+), decreased tone. Best muscles to look for fasciculations: intrinsic hand muscles, shoulder girdle, thigh.

Coordination & Gait — Cerebellar & Gait Pattern Localization

Appendicular ataxia = cerebellar hemispheresTruncal ataxia = cerebellar vermisRomberg = proprioceptive/vestibular▼
Coordination Tests

Finger-nose-finger: Best with examiner's finger at extreme of patient's reach and occasionally moved suddenly. Heel-shin test: Touch heel to opposite knee, drag straight down shin — ALWAYS do supine to eliminate gravity. Rapid alternating movements: Wipe palm alternately with palm and dorsum of other hand. Dysdiadochokinesia = abnormal alternating movements. Ataxia = medium-to-large amplitude involuntary movements with irregular oscillatory quality interfering with smooth trajectory. Past pointing = overshoot on target-oriented movements. Dysmetria can be tested with eye movements OR finger-to-nose.

Appendicular Ataxia

Affects extremity movements. Caused by cerebellar hemisphere lesions and associated pathways. Abnormal finger-nose-finger, heel-shin, rapid alternating movements.

Truncal Ataxia

Affects proximal musculature and gait stability. Caused by cerebellar vermis (midline) lesions. Wide-based, unsteady gait. Worse with tandem gait (heel-to-toe). These patients have particular difficulty keeping feet close together.

Romberg Test

Stand with feet together, then close eyes (stay close to guard). 3 sensory systems provide balance input: Vision, proprioception, vestibular. If mild proprioceptive or vestibular lesion, patient compensates with eyes open but becomes unstable with eyes closed (visual input removed). Severe proprioceptive/vestibular lesion or midline cerebellar lesion: Unstable even with eyes OPEN. Note: instability can also occur with UMN, LMN, or BG lesions — test these separately.

Sensory Exam — Primary & Cortical Sensation

Position/vibration = posterior columns (cross in medulla)Pain/temp = anterolateral (cross in cord)Cortical sensation = contralateral sensory cortex▼
Primary Sensation Testing

Light touch: Cotton-tipped swab or light finger touch. Pain: Alternate randomly between sharp and dull end of safety pin (new pin for each patient). Temperature: Cool tuning fork. Vibration: Vibrating tuning fork (128 Hz) on ball of great toe or fingers — report when vibration stops. Do NOT place on bone (conducts to distant sites). Joint position sense: Move finger or toe up/down, patient reports direction. Hold digit by sides to prevent tactile clues. Move very slightly (normal individuals detect barely perceptible movements). Two-point discrimination: Calipers or bent paper clip, record minimal separation in mm.

Sensory Pathway Anatomy — Critical for Localization

Posterior column pathway: Position sense, vibration, fine touch, two-point discrimination. Ascends ipsilaterally in posterior columns → CROSSES in the medulla → thalamus → somatosensory cortex. Anterolateral pathway (spinothalamic): Pain, temperature, crude touch. CROSSES shortly after entering the spinal cord → ascends contralaterally → thalamus → cortex. This means: A RIGHT-sided spinal cord lesion causes ipsilateral loss of position/vibration BUT contralateral loss of pain/temperature (Brown-Séquard pattern).

Cortical Sensation

Graphesthesia: Trace letters/numbers on palm or fingertip with eyes closed — patient identifies them. Stereognosis: Identify objects by touch with eyes closed, one hand at a time. Tactile extinction: Double simultaneous stimulation. These CANNOT reliably be tested unless primary sensation is intact bilaterally. Intact primary sensation + impaired cortical sensation = contralateral sensory cortex lesion. Note that severe cortical lesions can also impair primary sensation. Always look for a sensory level (sudden change corresponding to a spinal segment) — may indicate spinal cord lesion requiring emergency intervention.

Basal Ganglia Disorders
The basal ganglia are critical for movement initiation, automatic movement, postural adjustments, and perceptual/cognitive functions. Dysfunction produces either hypokinetic disorders (PD — too little movement) or hyperkinetic disorders (HD — too much movement). Key BG functions: movement and posture, automatic movement, movement initiation and preparation, postural adjustments, perceptual and cognitive functions.

Key Movement Disorder Terminology

Hypokinetic: Too little movement (PD). Hyperkinetic: Too much movement (HD). Spasticity: Velocity-dependent resistance to passive stretch (UMN — chronic lesion). Rigidity: Resistance in BOTH directions, NOT velocity-dependent (BG). Cogwheel rigidity: Ratchety resistance (PD). Lead pipe rigidity: Smooth constant resistance through full range (PD). Athetosis: Slow writhing movements. Chorea: Rapid irregular involuntary movements (HD). Akinesia: Lack of movement initiation. Bradykinesia: Slowness of movement. Resting tremor: Present at rest, decreases with intentional movement (PD). Intention tremor: Appears with goal-directed movement, absent at rest (cerebellar). Tardive dyskinesia: Involuntary repetitive movements (face/tongue) from long-term antipsychotic use or dopamine medication.

Parkinson's Disease — In-Depth Management
PD is the condition PTs in neuro rehab will see most frequently. Understanding staging, medication timing, the on-off phenomenon, and the extensive exercise evidence base is essential. PT can enhance or delay symptoms, increase strength, improve coordination and balance — but cannot cure the disease or stop progression.

PD Cardinal Signs, Non-Motor Symptoms & Staging

Cardinal Motor Signs — TRAP

Tremor: Resting, 4–6 Hz, "pill-rolling." Bilateral UE (one side typically more pronounced — contralateral brain more affected). Decreases with intentional movement. Rigidity: Cogwheel (ratchety) or lead pipe (constant in both directions). NOT velocity-dependent (vs spasticity). Akinesia/Bradykinesia: Difficulty initiating movement, pathological pausing with active motion. Flat affect — difficulty making facial expressions AND difficulty interpreting YOUR expressions if cognition declining. Postural Instability: Flexed posture (kyphotic → decreased lung compliance → restrictive pulmonary pattern), forward BOS, retropulsion, high fall risk. Berg ≤45 = recommend AD.

Gait Analysis in PD

Festinating gait: Short shuffling steps with progressively increasing speed (forward AND backward festination). Decreased step length, increased step speed. Forward BOS. Freezing with transitional movements and threshold changes (visual-perceptive impairments noted — changes in surface, doorways, crowded spaces). Decreased heel strike. Turning is extremely difficult — be there to guard patient during TUG. Backward walking (retrogait) may be terrifying due to fear of falling. Patients may lean on shopping cart or walker for forward stability.

Non-Motor Symptoms — Often Overlooked

Depression: Very high prevalence — assess in every PD patient. Cognitive decline: Difficulty with decision-making and planning (frontal lobe). PD dementia with prolonged disease. MoCA for screening — may need SLP referral. Autonomic dysfunction: Orthostatic hypotension (very common — always check BP seated AND standing), constipation, urinary urgency. Patients may avoid water intake to reduce bathroom trips → worsens dehydration → worsens orthostatic hypotension. Sleep disturbances. Olfactory dysfunction (can be early sign). Dysphagia (SLP referral). Respiratory: Kyphotic posture → decreased lung compliance → restrictive pattern. Bradykinesia can affect autonomic functions including digestion.

Medications & the On-Off Phenomenon

Carbidopa/Levodopa: Dopamine supplement. Carbidopa helps levodopa cross the blood-brain barrier to reach the BG (especially the substantia nigra). Typical dosing: 8AM, 4PM, 12AM. On-Off Phenomenon: Medication effects fluctuate — "on" periods (medication working, improved movement) vs "off" periods (medication wearing off, symptoms return). SCHEDULE THERAPY DURING "ON" PERIODS (typically 1–2 hours after dose). Tardive Dyskinesia: Involuntary facial/body movements — can occur immediately after dose (wife reports "funny facial movements after morning dose"). Artane (Trihexyphenidyl): Anticholinergic sometimes added to help with tremor. PD Diagnosis: Rule out everything else, then give levodopa — if patient responds well, confirms PD.

Hoehn & Yahr Staging

Stage 1: Unilateral involvement only. Stage 2: Bilateral involvement without impairment of balance. Stage 3: Bilateral involvement with mild-moderate disability and impaired postural reflexes — physically independent. Stage 4: Severe disability, still able to walk and stand without assistance. Stage 5: Wheelchair-bound or bedridden unless aided. UPDRS (Unified PD Rating Scale): Gold standard assessment — subscales for cognition, speech, functional movement, motor (Motor III most used by PTs), and ADLs.

What PT Can and Cannot Do for Progressive Disease

PT CAN: Enhance or delay symptoms, increase strength/coordination/balance, improve gait quality and endurance, reduce fall risk, provide education, improve QOL, address psychosocial factors, prescribe exercise programs, manage orthostatic hypotension, train compensatory strategies, improve respiratory function through postural correction. PT CANNOT: Cure the disease, stop progression, replace dopamine (PD), rebuild myelin (MS), regenerate motor neurons (ALS). The goal: maximize function at every stage and maintain it as long as possible. Effects of exercise on psychological AND physical symptoms are positive for progressive diseases.

Vestibular Anatomy & Diagnoses
>15 million people per year develop dizziness. Prevalence increases with age. >70% who have dizziness will NOT resolve within 2 weeks. 63% have recurrence beyond 3 months. The vestibular system uniquely identifies self-motion as different from environmental motion — it cannot be tricked like the visual system. Although working, you typically only notice your vestibular system when it fails.

Peripheral Vestibular Anatomy — SCCs & Otolith Organs

SCCs = angular/rotationalOtoliths = linear & gravityPush-pull dynamic▼
Semicircular Canals (SCCs)

3 canals arranged orthogonally (at right angles) in each temporal bone: Horizontal, Posterior (Inferior), Anterior (Superior). Respond to angular (rotational) acceleration. Each SCC has a contralateral coplanar mate. Filled with endolymph that moves freely in response to angular head rotation. At the end of each canal is the ampulla containing the cupula (gelatinous barrier with sensory hair cells). Kinocilia = mechanosensing cilia for movement detection. Stereocilia = mechanosensing organelles seated in the crista ampullaris (sensory organ of angular rotation). Deflection of stereocilia TOWARD kinocilia = excitation (depolarization). AWAY from kinocilia = inhibition (hyperpolarization).

Push-Pull Dynamic

Each SCC responds best to motion in its own plane with coplanar pairs exhibiting a "push-pull" dynamic. Example: As head turns RIGHT, hair cells in the RIGHT horizontal SCC are excited while hair cells in the LEFT horizontal SCC are inhibited. This bilateral difference in firing rate is how the brain detects direction and speed of head rotation. Vestibular afferents fire at 50–100 spikes per second at rest (tonic firing rate with no motion).

Otolith Organs

Saccule and Utricle make up the otolith organs. Respond to linear acceleration, static head tilt, and gravity. Sensory hair cells project into a gelatinous material with embedded crystals called otoconia. Motion toward = excitation, away = inhibition. Utricle: Senses horizontal linear acceleration and lateral head tilt. Saccule: Senses vertical linear acceleration and forward head tilt. Activated during: elevator rides, airplane takeoff, rising from chair. Do NOT respond to constant velocity. When otoconia are displaced INTO the semicircular canals = BPPV.

Peripheral vs Central — Critical Distinction

Peripheral: Movement/position dependent. Nystagmus suppressed with visual fixation. Obstructive (something physical causing obstruction — BPPV is classic). Easier to treat — repositioning, adaptation. Central: NOT position dependent. Nystagmus NOT suppressed with fixation. Timing deficit (push-pull relationship disrupted). Above level of vestibular nuclei — lateropulsion, head tilt, visual perceptual difficulties. Harder to treat — use substitution strategies. Ask questions about central — not really changed by position.

Retina Slip

Retina slip = motion of the visual image on the surface of the retina. Slip across large portions of retina is the stimulus for optokinetic eye movements and the stimulus that produces adaptation of the optokinetic system. When VOR is impaired, retina slip increases during head movements → blurry vision → this is what adaptation exercises (X1/X2 viewing) address.

Vestibular Treatment — 4 Categories
Vestibular rehabilitation uses 4 treatment categories: Repositioning (for BPPV), Adaptation (strengthen VOR), Habituation (reduce dizziness response), and Substitution (strengthen alternative systems). Treatment selection depends on diagnosis and whether the lesion is peripheral or central.

Adaptation, Habituation & Substitution — Progressions

X1 viewing = head moves, target stableHabituation = last resortSubstitution = central lesions▼
Adaptation — Strengthen VOR

Goal: bring the weak side UP to the good side as much as possible. Strengthen or rebalance the VOR and push-pull relationship. X1 Viewing: Move head horizontally and vertically as quickly as possible while maintaining gaze on a stable target (hold thumbs in front). If target becomes blurry, patient MUST slow down (indicates VOR is not keeping up). X2 Viewing: Patient quickly turns head H or V while the target moves in the OPPOSITE direction. Eye-then-Head: Eyes move to new target first, then head follows. Progression parameters (easier → harder): Plain background → busy background. Target far away → closer. Larger target → smaller. Slow head speed → faster. Firm surface → foam surface. Seated → standing → walking.

Habituation — Reduce Dizziness Response

Warranted when patient with UVH has continual complaints of dizziness AND doesn't fit other treatment categories. Habituation = last resort when other categories don't fit. Defined as reduction in response to a repeatedly performed movement. Use MSQ (Motion Sensitivity Quotient) to identify provoking positions. Literally put patient in position that elicits mild-to-moderate response and have them hold that position for 30 seconds or until abolition of symptoms. HEP: 3–5 repetitions for each position, 2–3×/day. Dose matters — needs consistent daily exposure.

Substitution — Alternative Systems

Used when vestibular system CANNOT be fixed (central lesions, bilateral vestibular loss). Utilizing alternative methods or senses to make up for the deficits. Strengthen different systems to compensate. May include AD use. Examples: Balance exercises with eyes closed and walking toward object (challenges proprioception, forces patient off visual system). Walking on foam surface (challenges vestibular, forces reliance on other systems). Gaze stability exercises. Visual system is dominant (70%) — taking away vision forces reliance on somatosensory and vestibular, which is the therapeutic goal when those systems need strengthening.

Concussion Management
1.6–3.8 million sport-related TBIs annually. Concussion is a FUNCTIONAL (not structural) brain injury — it alters how the brain functions but not the anatomy. MRI/CT show physical abnormalities, not functional ones. 80–90% recover in 7–10 days. Those requiring >10 days have delayed recovery. Only 47.3% of concussed athletes reported the event (McCrea, 2004). Reasons: didn't think serious enough (66%), didn't want to leave game (41%), didn't know it was a concussion (36%).

Second Impact Syndrome — Why RTP Protocols are Non-Negotiable

A second concussion BEFORE the previous one has healed. Can be catastrophic or fatal due to loss of cerebrovascular autoregulation → massive cerebral edema. This is why return-to-play/return-to-duty protocols exist and are non-negotiable. 100% of rugby players who received RTP guidance failed to comply with 3-week stand-down regulation (Hollis, 2011). >25% of hockey players thought it was OK to play with concussion if "careful" (Cusimano, 2009). Education is critical.

Blast Injuries — Military TBI

Over 2.5 million US troops deployed to OEF/OIF/OND (2001–2013). >1/3 deployed more than once. Estimated 320,000 sustained at least a concussion during deployment. 25–40% have psychological and neurological injuries. Prevalence of concussion: 4.2–23%. Symptoms overlap significantly with PTSD — headaches, cognitive dysfunction, attention difficulties, impaired balance (all have poor diagnostic specificity). Potential for bias: under-reporting in theater (to remain on duty), over-reporting post-deployment (for secondary gain). Concussion and PTSD frequently co-occur in this population.

When is PT Recommended for Concussion?

Patients with physical symptoms at 10 days post-concussion may benefit from PT. Sports medicine physicians should consider individually prescribed progressive PT intervention (manual techniques, vestibular rehab, oculomotor training, neuromotor retraining) as preferred treatments MUCH EARLIER than once a patient becomes chronic. Early PT intervention for dizziness after sport-related concussion is feasible and beneficial (Reneker et al., 2017). PT has specific expertise in assessment and treatment of persistent headache, dizziness, poor balance, and neck pain post-concussion.

Neuro Rehab Treatment Strategies
Neuro rehab follows a continuum from acute care through inpatient rehab to outpatient and home-based therapy. 3–6 months is when the most progress is seen. Selection of strategies should use the patient's strengths, avoid activities that elicit abnormal movement patterns, and "shrink" the environment to allow normal motor programs to run.

Continuum of Care

Acute Care: Medical stabilization, early mobilization, prevent complications. Inpatient Rehabilitation: Intensive therapy (3+ hours/day), functional recovery, transition planning. Outpatient: Patient has progressed to where they can easily leave home. Receive fine-tuning to maximize functional potential. Gradually decreasing frequency with increasing emphasis on independent HEP. May be used throughout life in periods of change or functional decline. Home-Based: Independent program with periodic skilled check-ins. How many reps to drive neuroplasticity? A LOT.

Key Treatment Principles

CIMT (Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy): Constrains good limb, forces use of stroke-affected limb. Drives neuroplasticity through intensive task-specific practice. First 3 months post-injury is THE critical window for developing good movement patterns. 3–6 months is when most progress is seen. Overload principle applies — stronger load = stronger neuroplasticity effect. Shrink the environment to allow normal motor programs to run (reduce distractors, simplify space). Then gradually expand the environment as function improves.

Augmented Feedback Progression

Patient initially needs external cues (verbal, visual, kinesthetic — moment to moment feedback) and perimeter control over the motor program. This is OK initially — cues help the patient participate and learn the movement. BUT the patient MUST eventually learn tasks without cues. If you never remove the cues, you create dependence, not learning. Example: using therapist's hands to enhance normalized movement patterns during gait — hands are augmented feedback. Gradually reduce until patient can perform independently. Think of it like training wheels — essential early, detrimental if never removed.

Prevention Framework for Progressive Disease

Primary prevention (general health): Exercise programs, cardiovascular health, nutrition, smoking cessation, alcohol reduction. Secondary prevention (decrease duration of illness): Early detection, early intervention, medication compliance, aggressive early rehabilitation. Tertiary prevention (limit degree of disability): Compensatory strategies, adaptive equipment, fall prevention, caregiver education, home modifications, community resources. Normal aging: Gait slows, compensated balance patterns used, decreased muscle tone. Disease: Symptoms FORCE patient to utilize compensations that older people normally use — but earlier and more severely.

How do you distinguish UMN from LMN lesions, and what is the key CN VII distinction?
tap
UMN: Weakness, hyperreflexia (3–5+), spasticity (velocity-dependent), (+) Babinski (upgoing toe — ALWAYS abnormal in adults), (+) Hoffmann's, clonus. IMPORTANT: acute UMN may initially show flaccidity before hyperreflexia develops. LMN: Weakness, atrophy, fasciculations, hyporeflexia (0–1+), decreased tone. CN VII key distinction: UMN lesion (stroke) = contralateral face weakness SPARING the forehead (bilateral UMN innervation to upper face). LMN lesion (Bell's palsy) = entire ipsilateral face INCLUDING forehead. Brown-Séquard: Ipsilateral loss of position/vibration (posterior columns cross in medulla) + contralateral loss of pain/temperature (anterolateral crosses in cord).

What are the 4 categories of vestibular treatment and when do you use each?
tap
1. Repositioning: For BPPV (otoconia in canals). Epley (posterior/anterior), Semont (posterior — head 45° AWAY from affected, rapidly to affected side), BBQ Roll (horizontal), Brandt-Daroff (HEP). Can resolve in 1–3 sessions. 2. Adaptation: Strengthen VOR. X1 viewing (head moves, target stable — slow down if blurry). X2 viewing (head and target opposite). Progress: speed, background complexity, target size/distance, surface (firm → foam). 3. Habituation: For UVH with continual dizziness. LAST RESORT. MSQ identifies provoking positions. Hold 30 sec, 3–5 reps, 2–3×/day. 4. Substitution: When vestibular can't be fixed (central, bilateral loss). Strengthen other systems. Eyes-closed walking (proprioception), foam surfaces, AD. Peripheral = easy to fix. Central = substitution.
What are the 6 concussion phenotypes and why is "process not protocol" the approach?
tap
6 phenotypes: (1) Vestibular — dizziness with movement, busy environments. (2) Migraine — HA on waking, nausea, visual/verbal memory deficits. (3) Ocular — blurred vision, difficulty reading, HA with visual tasks. (4) Anxiety/Mood — worry, rumination, limited social interaction. (5) Cognitive/Fatigue — memory problems, often patients who played through injury. (6) Cervical — neck pain, cervicogenic HA (clear TMJ!). Process not protocol because concussion is heterogeneous — same injury, different presentations. Phenotypes frequently co-occur (vestibular → migraine, ocular → cognitive fatigue). Pre-existing risks predispose to specific profiles. Push too hard on vestibular = migraine. Don't push enough = anxiety. Concussion is a moving target.
