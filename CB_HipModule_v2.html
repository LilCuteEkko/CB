Hip Anatomy & Kinesiology
The hip is a ball-and-socket joint built for both stability and mobility — more stable than the shoulder due to deep acetabular coverage, thick capsule, and strong ligaments. The coxofemoral joint transmits 1.8–3.8× body weight during normal gait; any force >3× BW raises risk of early degeneration. Hip dysfunction has downstream effects on gait, posture, and the entire kinetic chain.

Hip Muscles — Action Groups

▼
Flexors

Prime: Iliopsoas (most powerful), Sartorius, TFL, Rectus femoris, Pectineus, Adductor longus. Assist: Adductor brevis, Gracilis, Gluteus minimus (anterior fibers). Key: TFL anteromedial fibers active during running/sprinting — overactivation leads to compressive bursa/tendinopathies at greater trochanter and proximal lateral tibia.

Extensors

Prime: Gluteus maximus (most powerful, most active in hip flexion position), Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus, Biceps femoris (long head), Adductor magnus (posterior head). Assist: Gluteus medius (posterior fibers). Glute max is active with trunk extension, lateral sway control, and bipedal locomotion — greatest activity during sprinting.

Abductors

Prime: Gluteus medius (deltoid of the hip — primary dynamic stabilizer, active ~60% of gait cycle, decelerates hip adduction), Gluteus minimus, TFL. Assist: Sartorius, Rectus femoris, Piriformis.

Adductors

Prime: Adductor magnus (both heads), Adductor longus, Adductor brevis, Gracilis, Pectineus. Assist: Biceps femoris (long head), Gluteus maximus (posterior fibers). Adductor longus and magnus are the most common muscles involved in groin-region pain. Test adductor strength at 0° (gracilis), 45° (longus/brevis), 90° (pectineus).

External Rotators

Prime: Gluteus maximus, Gemelli inferior & superior, Obturator internus, Quadratus femoris, Piriformis (at <60° hip flexion). Assist: Gluteus medius posterior fibers (ER in ext, IR in flexion), Biceps femoris, Sartorius, Obturator externus. Short ER muscles function like the rotator cuff — compressing the hip joint for stability.

Internal Rotators — No Primary Muscle

NO muscle has IR as its primary function. Secondary IR: anterior gluteus medius, gluteus minimus, TFL, adductor longus/brevis, pectineus, posterior head of adductor magnus. Semimembranosus, semitendinosus, gracilis, piriformis (at 90° flexion) assist.

Pelvic & Femoral Osteology — Complete Landmark Reference

▼
Pelvis — Three Bones Fuse at Acetabulum

Ilium: body, ASIS/AIIS (sartorius/rectus femoris origins), PSIS/PIIS, iliac crest & tubercle (IT band origin), iliac fossa (iliacus origin), gluteal lines (posterior/anterior/inferior — gluteal muscle zones), auricular surface (SI joint).

Ischium: body, ischial tuberosity (hamstring origin — “sit bones”), ischial spine (sacrospinous lig, pudendal N landmark), greater sciatic notch (sciatic N exits pelvis), lesser sciatic notch, ischial ramus (joins with inferior pubic ramus → ischiopubic ramus).

Pubis: body, pubic symphysis (fibrocartilaginous joint), pubic crest & tubercle (inguinal lig attachment), superior ramus with pecten pubis (pectineus origin), inferior ramus, obturator foramen (formed with ischium).

Acetabulum: deep socket facing inferolaterally. Lunate surface = articular (horseshoe-shaped). Acetabular fossa = non-articular (fat pad + ligamentum teres). Acetabular notch = inferior gap bridged by transverse acetabular ligament. Acetabular labrum = fibrocartilage rim deepening socket by ~50%, increasing contact area.

Femur — Longest, Strongest Bone

Proximal: Head (2/3 sphere, fovea for ligamentum teres), neck (fracture-prone zone, connects head to shaft), neck-shaft angle ~125° (angle of inclination — coxa vara <120°, coxa valga >130°), anteversion ~12° (head faces slightly anterior). Greater trochanter (glut med/min, short ERs insert), lesser trochanter (iliopsoas inserts). Intertrochanteric line (anterior — iliofemoral lig), intertrochanteric crest (posterior — quadrate tubercle).

Shaft: Slight anterior bow. Linea aspera = prominent posterior ridge with medial & lateral lips (adductor/vastus origins). Gluteal tuberosity (glut max). Pectineal line. Medial & lateral supracondylar lines.

Distal: Medial condyle (larger, MCL attachment at epicondyle), lateral condyle (LCL at epicondyle), intercondylar notch (cruciate ligaments), patellar groove (trochlea — patella tracks here), adductor tubercle (superior to medial epicondyle — hamstring portion of adductor magnus inserts).

Fascia Lata, IT Band & Thigh Compartments

▼
Fascia Lata & Iliotibial Tract

Fascia lata = dense thick deep fascia of the thigh. Attaches to inguinal ligament, iliac crest, sacrum, coccyx, sacrotuberous ligament, ischial tuberosity, and bony prominences around the knee. Functions with contracting muscles to pump blood and lymph toward thorax (like compression stockings).

Iliotibial tract (IT band): lateral thickening of fascia lata. Conjoint aponeurosis of TFL + gluteus maximus. Extends from iliac tubercle → Gerdy's tubercle on lateral tibial condyle. Crosses both hip and knee → stabilizes both joints laterally. IT band syndrome: friction over lateral femoral epicondyle during repetitive knee flexion/extension → lateral knee pain in runners/cyclists.

Three intermuscular septa divide thigh into compartments: Lateral (strongest, from IT band to lateral lip of linea aspera), Medial (to medial lip), Posterior (to linea aspera posteriorly).

Saphenous Opening & Cribriform Fascia

Deficiency in fascia lata ~4cm distal to pubic tubercle. Medial margin is smooth; superior/lateral/inferior form the falciform margin (sharp crescent edge). Allows great saphenous vein to connect to femoral vein. Covered by cribriform fascia (sieve-like membrane allowing vessels/lymphatics to pass through).

Femoral Triangle, Femoral Sheath & Adductor Canal

▼
Femoral Triangle

Boundaries: Superior = inguinal ligament. Medial = adductor longus. Lateral = sartorius. Apex = where sartorius and adductor longus meet. Floor = adductor longus, pectineus, iliopsoas. Roof = fascia lata + cribriform fascia.

Contents (lateral → medial): Femoral Nerve, femoral Artery, femoral Vein — mnemonic: NAVeL (Nerve, Artery, Vein, empty space/Lymphatics). Also: deep femoral artery, inguinal lymph nodes.

Femoral Sheath

Funnel-shaped fascial tube (continuation of extraperitoneal fascia). Contains 3 compartments: Lateral = femoral artery. Intermediate = femoral vein. Medial = femoral canal (lymph vessels, nodes, loose CT). Femoral canal ring boundaries: lateral = vein partition, posterior = pecten pubis/pectineus, medial = lacunar ligament, anterior = inguinal ligament. Femoral hernia enters through femoral ring — more common in females.

Adductor Canal (Subsartorial Canal)

Passage for femoral vessels from femoral triangle to popliteal fossa. Boundaries: Lateral = vastus medialis. Posterior = adductor longus + adductor magnus. Anterior/roof = sartorius + subsartorial fascia.

Contents: Femoral artery, femoral vein, saphenous nerve, nerve to vastus medialis. Vessels exit through adductor hiatus (gap in adductor magnus) to become popliteal vessels behind the knee.

Hip Region Bursae

Trochanteric bursa: between superior glut max and greater trochanter — trochanteric bursitis/greater trochanteric pain syndrome. Ischial (sciatic) bursa: between inferior glut max and ischial tuberosity — “weaver's bottom.” Iliopsoas (iliopectineal) bursa: between iliopsoas tendon and hip capsule — may communicate with joint cavity; largest bursa in body. Obturator internus bursa: between tendon and lesser sciatic notch.

Blood Supply to Hip Region

Femoral artery (continuation of external iliac) enters thigh under inguinal ligament → gives off deep femoral (profunda femoris) = chief artery of thigh. Medial femoral circumflex (most important for femoral head blood supply — disrupted in femoral neck fracture → AVN) + lateral femoral circumflex (ascending, transverse, descending branches) + perforating branches (supply posterior compartment). Obturator artery (branch of internal iliac) supplies ligamentum teres → femoral head (minor supply in adults, critical in children).

Gluteal region: Superior gluteal artery (largest branch of internal iliac — exits above piriformis) + inferior gluteal artery (exits below piriformis) + internal pudendal artery (exits below piriformis, re-enters via lesser sciatic foramen — supplies NO gluteal structures).

Key Anatomical Structures

Femoral triangle: Boundaries = inguinal ligament (superior), adductor longus (medial), sartorius (lateral). Floor = adductor longus, pectineus, iliopsoas. Contents medial → lateral: Vein, Artery, Nerve (VAN). Femoral sheath: Funnel-shaped fascial tube. Lateral = artery; intermediate = vein; medial = femoral canal (lymph). Adductor canal (subsartorial): Lateral = vastus medialis; posterior = adductor longus/magnus; anterior/roof = sartorius. Contains femoral artery, femoral vein, saphenous nerve, nerve to vastus medialis. Saphenous opening: Deficiency in fascia lata ~4cm distal to pubic tubercle; allows great saphenous vein to connect to femoral vein. Adductor hiatus: Gap in adductor magnus where femoral vessels pass to become popliteal vessels.

Hip Ligaments & Bursae

▼
Iliofemoral Ligament (Y Ligament)

Strongest ligament in the body. Reinforces anterior hip capsule. AIIS to intertrochanteric line. Function: limits hip extension and ER; prevents anterior translation; maintains upright posture, reducing need for hip extensor contraction in balanced stance. Hip adduction tightens its superior portion. Taut in extension.

Pubofemoral Ligament

Obturator crest to intertrochanteric line. Function: tightens in extension AND ER; reinforces medial capsule; prevents over-abduction. Works with iliofemoral to limit extension.

Ischiofemoral Ligament — Most Commonly Injured

Posterior capsule thickening, winds posteriorly. Function: tightens IR in flexion AND extension; tightens with adduction of flexed hip. Most commonly injured hip ligament. Posterior surgical approach to THA can disrupt this — careful repair reduces posterior dislocation risk.

Ligamentum Teres (Capitis Femoris)

Acetabular notch to fovea capitis. Prevents subluxation when hip is ER in flexion or IR in extension. Tightens with adduction, flexion, and ER. Injury sign: deep posterior hip pain with hip flexion activities. Blood supply (internal iliac artery) passes through — injury disrupts vascularity → risk of AVN. Tears lead to microinstability → labral and cartilage damage.

Bursae

Iliopsoas bursa: Largest (up to 7×4 cm), present in 98% of adults. Deep to iliopsoas tendon between inguinal ligament and hip capsule. Inflamed with overuse, OA, RA, infection.
Trochanteric bursae: Subgluteus medius (superoposterior GT) and subgluteus maximus (between GT and gluteus maximus/ITB).
Ischiogluteal bursa: Between ischium and gluteus maximus. "Weaver's Bottom" — compressed by sitting on hard surfaces.

Femoral Triangle — VAN (Medial → Lateral)

Borders: Superior = inguinal ligament; Medial = adductor longus; Lateral = sartorius. Contents (medial → lateral): Femoral Vein, Femoral Artery, Femoral Nerve (VAN). Use caution palpating here or applying soft tissue techniques.

Osteology, Load, & Angles of Inclination

▼
Angle of Inclination

Normal adult: 125–139°. Coxa Vara (<125°): decreased limb length, increased load on femoral neck → predisposes to fracture. Coxa Valga (>125°): increased limb length, decreased stability → predisposes to dislocation/subluxation. Ward triangle: area of weakness in femoral neck trabecular system — common osteoporotic fracture site.

Hip Joint Loading

Coxofemoral joint peak forces during gait: 1.8–3.8× body weight. Forces >3× BW increase risk of early degeneration — driven by higher body mass and impact loading (running, jumping). Any force >3× BW → focus on mechanical weight acceptance. Femoral anteversion increases load more than retroversion.

Arthrokinematics (OKC)

OKC: convex femoral head moves on concave acetabulum → femoral head glides opposite to distal femur motion (rule of convex on concave). Anatomical flexion = parasagittal plane = Flex + Abd + IR. Extension = Ext + Add + ER. Abduction = Extension + ER of femoral head. Adduction = Flexion + ER.

Key Osteological Avulsion Sites

ASIS: Sartorius — avulsion in adolescent runners. Pubic tubercle: Rectus abdominis, adductor brevis/longus — tendinopathy site, groin pain referral. Ischial tuberosity: Hamstring origin — avulsion in athletes, longer healing; hamstring syndrome = sciatic N compression mimicking hamstring strain.

Hip Assessment Framework — Subjective, Lumbar Screen, SIJ Screen

▼
Location-Based DDx
  • Groin pain: Ask MOI (hockey, ballet, gymnastics). OA, labral, lumbar referral, inguinal hernia, adductor strain, iliopsoas, gynecological/vascular
  • Posterior/buttock: Rule out SIJ, gluteal bursitis, hamstring syndrome, lumbar spine, labrum, posterior pubic symphysis
  • Posterolateral: Trochanteric bursitis, gluteal insertional tendinopathy (Trendelenburg gait), THA component loosening
  • Groin pain with sitting: Ant/superior acetabular labral compromise — posterior/inferior glide helps
  • Posterior pain with sitting: Ischial bursitis, hamstring syndrome, LB spine
  • Night-only pain: Red flag — rule out tumor (osteoid osteoma common in long bones)
Age-Based Clinical Considerations
  • Boys 4–10: LCPD (painful limp, painless initially)
  • Males <6: Transient synovitis (most common sudden hip pain)
  • Adolescent 11–14: SCFE (especially overweight boys, ER gait)
  • Young athletic female: Stress fracture (female triad risk — disordered eating, amenorrhea, osteoporosis)
  • 35–50: Labral lesions (wear & tear, catching/clicking)
  • >40 males: Labral cysts, sacral pathologies, stress fractures
  • >45: OA (groin + knee pain; hip pain often presents first as knee pain)
Lumbar Screening (Clear the Spine)

Subjective: Dermatomal/myotomal distribution; achy/shooting/stabbing; must clear piriformis syndrome; sensation changes. Objective: AROM functionally; lumbosacral roots via dural testing (SLR, SLRD — good sensitivity and specificity). Bilateral knee pain → think lumbar referral.

SIJ Screening — Laslett Cluster

Subjective clues: unilateral pain, feeling of being stuck on one side, one-finger sign (PSIS), postpartum instability. Laslett cluster — 3 or more = highly likely SIJ: Dorsolateral provocation, Thigh Thrust, Gaenslen, Ventromedial provocation, Sacral Thrust. ASLR: perform SLR without assistance then with innominate stabilization — if pain changes, high probability of SIJ dysfunction.

Motion Restrictions

First motions to diminish in OA and labral issues: Flexion and IR. Capsular pattern: IR > Flexion > Extension (most limited = IR). Hard end-feels indicate OA or coxarthrosis. Significant motor function loss → consider subluxation, stress fracture, nerve injury — may need imaging. Foot drop + atrophy = refer out.

Hip Muscles — Organized by Action
The hip has 22+ muscles crossing it. No muscle has internal rotation as its primary action — IR is always a secondary function. The gluteus medius is the “deltoid of the hip” and the most clinically important hip muscle.
Hip Pathologies — Capsular, Noncapsular & Intra-articular
Hip pathologies are organized by pattern of limitation. Capsular pattern (arthritis, synovitis): IR > Flexion most limited. Noncapsular pattern (SCFE, AVN, loose body): any pattern other than capsular. Intra-articular (labral tear, FAI): common report is groin pain with mechanical symptoms.

Capsular Pattern — Osteoarthritis & Synovitis

▼
Hip OA Background

28 million Americans estimated to have OA (2011). Generally >50 years, overweight. Post-traumatic OA presents 10 years later. Pain at hip often presents first as knee pain. X-ray: joint space narrowing + marginal spurring. Conservative: cane, NSAIDs, exercise + weight loss (aquatics), acupuncture, PRP. Movement is critical — avoid immobility.

Traumatic Arthritis

Age >20. Repetitive hyperextension + rotation from work or sports (kickers, soccer, horse riders). Pain: groin or anterior thigh with sitting, walking, ascending stairs. Objective: PROM limited in capsular pattern.

Coxarthrosis — "Frozen Shoulder of the Hip"

Disorder of articular cartilage/bone balance — slow progressive course. Primary: genetic, older, female more common and more severe. Secondary: underlying pathology (instability, dysplasia, intra-articular fractures). Progression: pain with WB → constant pain at rest → pain subsides but movement limited (capsular pattern). Treatment: manual therapy, joint mobilization, ther ex for function (not pain), neuromuscular re-education + HEP.

OA Clinical Prediction Rule — 3 of 5
  1. Squatting is an aggravating factor
  2. Active hip flexion causes lateral hip pain
  3. Scour test with adduction causes lateral hip pain or groin pain (catching may occur)
  4. Active hip extension causes pain
  5. Passive IR <25°
OA PT Treatment Protocol

Mobilization/manipulation: ventral + lateral + caudal directed force at femur. Traction (long-axis): applied perpendicular; contraindicated with joint instability, degenerative bone disorders, anticoagulant therapy. Soft tissue: adductors typically hypertonic (wanting stability), abductors weaker. NMR: functional mobility focus — use UE reaching in decompressed mode to activate hip muscles during WB activities.

Transient Synovitis

Males under age 6 — most common cause of sudden hip pain and limp. Viral, autoimmune, or microtrauma origin; 40% have preceding illness. Limited IR (capsular pattern). Antalgic gait. Imaging may be normal. Lasts 2–3 weeks; typically full recovery. Differentiate from sciatica. In some cases progresses to juvenile arthritis.

Noncapsular Pattern — AVN, SCFE, LCPD

▼
Avascular Necrosis (AVN) of Femoral Head

Disruption of blood supply to proximal femur (circumflex arteries). Adult causes: fracture, dislocation, surgery, alcohol, steroids, radiation. Presentation: gradual groin pain, possible hip movement limits, crepitation and gluteal pain with increased severity. Dx: CT scan or MRI (X-ray late to show changes). Treatment: Early = nonsurgical (stem cells, magnetics). Surgical = core decompression or osteotomy (PWB 3–6 months, AROM to tolerance). End-stage = THA (cemented shows most improvement in pain and function).

SCFE — Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis

Background: femoral head slips posteriorly off femoral neck at growth plate. Adolescents 11–14; more common in boys; overweight (80–100th percentile); diabetes; cancer treatments. Presentation: hip/medial thigh/knee pain; antalgic gait; tends to walk with ER foot; positive log roll sign and SL raise against resistance; pain with IR relieved with ER. Radiograph: femoral head displaced posteriorly and inferiorly. Internal fixation ASAP — goal is to prevent AVN.

Legg-Calvé-Perthes Disease (LCPD)

Femoral head blood supply disrupted → AVN → collapse and flattening. Blood supply returns over months; bone replacement takes 2–3 years. Boys 4–10, unilateral. Presentation: painless limp (comes and goes), hip stiffness (flex/IR/abd), constant thigh/groin pain, limb length discrepancy, upper thigh atrophy. X-ray/MRI: small femoral epiphysis; sclerosis and flattening if chronic. Conservative: short bed rest, limited WB, possible casting, PT for strength and soft tissue. Surgery only if conservative fails. Prognosis: good if <6 years; high deformity/arthritis risk if >6 years.

Trauma & Fractures — Red Flags
  • Fall → think fracture first: inability to bear weight = femoral hip fracture until proven otherwise
  • Stress fractures: pelvic rami and femoral neck most common; X-ray only 10% sensitive early — bone scan better
  • High risk: female triad, amenorrhea, low aerobic fitness, smoking, steroid use
  • Myositis ossificans: bone forms inside muscle from blunt trauma — ask about mechanism and time since injury
  • Hip pointer: contusion to iliac crest — lasts a long time; treat with soft tissue + ROM

Intra-articular — Labral Tears, FAI, Loose Bodies

▼
Labral Tears

Background: degenerative or repetitive (torsion/twisting, LCPD hx, dysplasia). Ages 35–50. Anterior labrum: greatest force in hip flexion + adduction. Presentation: anterolateral/anterosuperior groin pain; catching/locking/clicking in 50%; dull pain with intermittent sharp pain in IR/ER; prolonged sitting; antalgic or Trendelenburg gait. Exam: +FADIR, +FABER; loss/apprehension of IR; catching (mechanical symptoms). Imaging: MRA 90% SN, 100% SP. Conservative PT: glute max stabilization, quadriceps/hamstring co-contraction, myofascial work (adductor insertion, glute med insertion, piriformis, ITB), MWM for capsular restriction, weak hip abductors in CKC. Goal: full ROM with rotation in full WB at velocity.

Loose Bodies

Ossified or non-ossified fragments (osteochondral, chondral, fibrous, foreign). Usually secondary to other processes or trauma. Presentation: hx of dislocation or low-energy trauma; popping/catching/clicking/giving way; sudden leg pain "twinges" when WB; possible stiffness; springy end feel during passive extension. Imaging: X-ray, MRI with contrast, MRA. Surgery: arthroscopic removal — gold standard.

Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI)

Types (Ganz): CAM — bony abnormality of femoral head (blocky, non-spherical); young male athletes; impingement in flex + ADD + IR. Pincer — increased acetabular rim coverage; middle-aged women in athletics. Mixed: most common. Associated with labral and chondral damage; significant cause of hip OA. Cardinal sign: sharp anterior hip pain with deep flexion, deep squat, IR, or abduction. "C sign." Objective: limited IR <20° at 90° flexion; +FADIR, +FABER. Common in hockey, dancers, soccer, football. Conservative PT: discontinue sport temporarily; joint mobs (create joint space); dry needling; hip abductor strengthening; CARS exercises; banded squats for valgus collapse control. Surgical: arthroscopic osteoplasty; 93% return rate (Philippon 2008).

Hip Microinstability

Shallow acetabulum, excessive femoral anteversion, Ehlers-Danlos/Marfan/Down syndrome. Repetitive hip rotation + axial loading in dancers, ballet, martial arts, tennis, baseball. Presentation: "loosey-goosey" feeling, apprehension at end range; no motion loss but excessive motion (especially IR + ER); insidious anterior/groin pain. Treatment: core + global hip strengthening, biomechanical analysis; avoid repeated subluxations. Surgical: arthroscopic capsulorrhaphy if conservative fails.

Extra-articular Hip Pathologies
Extra-articular pathologies involve tendons, bursae, muscles, and nerves surrounding the hip joint — not the joint surface itself. They are often overlooked when a patient presents with "hip pain." Snapping hip, bursitis, adductor/hamstring strains, piriformis syndrome, and stress fractures all require distinct examination and management approaches.

Coxa Saltans — Snapping Hip Syndrome

▼
Mechanisms of Snapping
  • Internal (anterior): Iliopsoas tendon snapping over iliopectineal prominence or lesser tubercle — most common cause of groin pain in runners
  • External (lateral): IT band snapping over greater trochanter — trochanteric bursitis
  • Iliofemoral ligament over femoral head
  • Long head biceps femoris over ischial tuberosity
Internal Snapping Hip (Iliopsoas Tendinitis)

Pain or discomfort with internal snapping = indication of iliopsoas tendinitis. More common in ballet dancers (often not painful). Ask if activities were ramped up when pain started — overuse pattern. Chronic degeneration → tendinopathy (chronic pain + mental pain component). Presentation: anterior groin pain moving from hip extension to flexion; popping/clicking/catching; can be associated with LBP (hip flexors tied to pelvic and lumbar position).

ITB/Greater Trochanteric Bursitis & External Snapping

Thickened ITB or glute max tendon snapping over greater trochanter = irritation and friction. Primary cause: increased hip adduction + knee IR. Presentation: pain over greater trochanter radiating down lateral thigh; painful with sleeping and direct pressure; stair climbing often painful; +Trendelenburg; associated hip abductor weakness; snapping with sidelying hip moved from flex to ext and IR to ER. More common in middle-aged women; growing prevalence in young runners. Diabetes, OA, and thyroid issues = increased risk. Treatment: STM + hold/relax stretching (ITB, rectus femoris, iliopsoas); manual + self-MFR to ITB; hip abductor strengthening in SLS; gait training; dry needling + KT tape (star pattern); address pronated foot or poor mechanical control.

Gluteus Medius/Minimus Injury & Piriformis Syndrome

▼
Gluteus Medius/Minimus Injury

Gmed and Gmin = "rotator cuff of the hip." More common in women; recently active. Triggered by falls, prolonged single-leg WB, overuse, sports. Presentation: dull lateral hip/groin/buttock pain; focal tenderness at insertion; leg length discrepancy; weak hip abduction; pain with PROM/MMT of ER at 90° hip flexion; +pain with SLS >30 seconds. Tears → constant pain that feels better in therapy but doesn't improve overall. Intervention — 3 Phases: Phase 1: protection, low-load exercises, minimize dynamic influence, NO hip abd strength training yet. Phase 2: standing sagittal plane focus, static → dynamic, add planes one at a time. Phase 3: dynamic multiplanar with reaction, stress length-tension (posterolateral lunge).

Piriformis Syndrome

Background: compression of sciatic nerve as it exits pelvis under piriformis muscle. Pain in sciatic distribution. Three contributing conditions: (A) myofascial referred pain from trigger points in piriformis (most common); (B) adjacent muscle/nerve/vascular entrapment at greater sciatic foramen (12%); (C) SIJ dysfunction altering piriformis position. Presentation: chronic buttock pain, numbness, paresthesia; pain with sitting, squatting, standing, bowel movements, and dyspareunia in women; tenderness over sciatic notch; exacerbated by active hip ER; pain may lessen with lying down or bending knee. Tests: +Pace, +Freiberg, +Lasegue, +log roll, +FAIR test. DDx from radiculopathy: sciatic N entrapment pain increases with IR during SLR; lumbosacral radiculopathy doesn't change with hip rotation; sciatic N entrapment spares gluteal muscles (radiculopathy causes palpable atrophy).

Adductor Strain, Hamstring Strain & Stress Fractures

▼
Adductor Strain

MOI: forced push-off or kick in frontal plane, repetitive kicking, overuse during fatigue. Hockey, skating, soccer, rugby. Adductor longus and magnus most frequent. Testing by position: 0° = gracilis; 45° = adductor longus/brevis; 90° + adduction = pectineus. Grades: 1 = pain, minimal strength loss; 2 = compromised strength, no complete loss; 3 = complete disruption + complete function loss. Predisposing factors: adductor weakness, adductor-abductor imbalance, restricted hip ROM. Treatment: non-surgical; collagen deposition management; return to sport with full strength/ROM; address adductor-abductor-abdominal imbalance.

Hamstring Strains

Biceps femoris most commonly involved (short sprint). MOI: short sprint, jump/kick, fall or collision. Increased anterior pelvic tilt (tight hip flexors or restricted anterior capsule) → increased hamstring tension → higher strain risk. Hamstring syndrome: mimics hamstring strain but is actually sciatic N compression at ischial tuberosity. Biceps femoris may be contributing.

Stress Fractures

Two types: Fatigue fracture (normal bone + abnormal stress — runners) and Insufficiency fracture (normal stress + abnormal bone — osteoporosis). Most common sites: pelvic rami, femoral neck. Risk factors: female triad, amenorrhea, low aerobic fitness, smoking, steroid use, nutritional compromise. Exam: diffuse radiating groin/hip pain; +fulcrum test; X-ray only 10% sensitive early — bone scan is better. Night pain, inability to absorb shock during gait. Treatment: Tension-sided = surgical pinning to prevent AVN. Compression-sided = 6–8 weeks limited WB, PT for weakness/myofascial/neuromotor imbalances, cross-training (aquatics).

Iliopsoas Strain

MOI: forced extension while hip is actively flexed. Presentation: pain with acceleration and high-stepping; RIMS — increased pain with resisted hip flexion, adduction, and ER. Recovery can be lengthy; reaggravation common.

Special tests at the hip are organized by clinical purpose. No single test is definitive — combine with subjective history and functional screening. FADIR has high sensitivity (rule out); valgus stress and specific cluster tests have higher specificity. Always test bilaterally and note quality vs. quantity of response.

The 5 Laslett Tests

SI Distraction • SI Compression • Thigh Thrust • Gaenslen's • Sacral Thrust (Spring). FABER is often added as a 6th. Thigh Thrust: supine; flex hip 90°, adduct slightly; apply force through long axis of femur creating A-P shear on SIJ. Distraction: supine; cross-armed pressure to ASIS gapping the SIJ. Sacral Spring: prone; vigorous downward pressure on sacrum at S3 (up to 6 times).

Hip Post-Operative Rehabilitation
Key post-op protocols for THA, hip arthroscopy, and hip fracture ORIF.

Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) — Posterior Approach

▼
Posterior Precautions

No hip flexion >90°. No adduction past midline. No internal rotation. Duration: 6–12 weeks (surgeon dependent). These precautions protect the posterior capsule repair. Anterior approach: fewer precautions (avoid ER + extension). Use elevated toilet seat, reacher, sock aide, long-handled shoe horn. Sleep with pillow between legs.

Review Cards — Hip
Tap any card to flip. High-yield clinical pearls for exams and practice.
What is the hip capsular pattern, and what is the OA Clinical Prediction Rule?
tap
Capsular pattern: IR > Flexion > Extension (IR is most limited). Most limited = IR. OA CPR — 3 of 5: (1) squatting aggravates; (2) active hip flexion causes lateral hip pain; (3) scour test with adduction causes lateral hip/groin pain (±catching); (4) active hip extension causes pain; (5) passive IR <25°. These three things drive OA treatment: mobilization with ventral+lateral+caudal force at femur, long-axis traction, and soft tissue (hypertonic adductors, weak abductors).
What are the THA posterior approach precautions, and why?
tap
Posterior approach cuts: capsule and short external rotators (released to gain access). Protect sciatic nerve and superior gluteal nerve/artery. Precautions ×3 months: No hip flexion >90°; no hip adduction past midline; no IR of operated hip. Reason: these combined motions recreate the position that would dislocate the hip posteriorly — through the cut posterior capsule/ER tendons before healing is complete. Careful repair of posterior capsule and ER tendons significantly reduces dislocation risk. Anterior approach spares short ER muscles — different precautions (no posterior dislocation risk).
What are the hip arthroscopy post-op ROM and WB restrictions by procedure?
tap
All procedures: Flexion restricted to 0–90°. Labral repair: TTWB ×3–4 weeks; ER <30°; ABD <30°. Microfracture: NWB ×0–6 weeks; ER <30°; ABD <30°. Osteoplasty: PWB ×0–4 weeks; ER <30°; ABD <30°. Capsular repair (most restrictive): TTWB ×3–4 weeks; ER = 0°; Extension = 0°. Critical: Avoid long lever arm exercises early (SLR, prone planks) — can spike inflammation via hip flexor overactivation. Hip flexor tendinopathy is the most common post-arthroscopy complication.
SCFE vs. LCPD — demographics, presentation, and clinical distinguishing features?
tap
SCFE (Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis): Adolescents 11–14, more common in boys, overweight (80–100th percentile), diabetes, cancer treatment. Femoral head slips posteriorly off femoral neck. Presentation: hip/medial thigh/knee pain; tends to walk with ER foot; pain with IR relieved with ER; positive log roll and SL raise against resistance; antalgic gait. Treatment: internal fixation ASAP to prevent AVN. LCPD (Legg-Calvé-Perthes): Boys 4–10, unilateral. Blood supply disrupted → AVN → femoral head flattens. Presentation: painless limp (comes and goes); hip stiffness (flex/IR/abd); constant thigh/groin pain; limb length discrepancy; upper thigh atrophy. Prognosis: good if <6 yo; high deformity/arthritis risk if >6 yo.
What is the FADIR test, what does it indicate, and what's its diagnostic accuracy?
tap
FADIR (Flexion, ADduction, Internal Rotation): Patient supine; PT passively flexes hip to 90°, adducts, and internally rotates. Positive: pain reproduced in the groin. Indicates: labral tear or femoroacetabular impingement (FAI). Diagnostic accuracy: SN:.99 / SP:.25. Interpretation: very high sensitivity (excellent rule-OUT test — if negative, labral/FAI very unlikely), but very low specificity (positive result alone is not diagnostic — many people without labral tears will be positive). Must combine with other tests (FABER, scour, Fitzgerald anterior/posterior) and clinical history. In FAI: also expect limited IR <20° at 90° flexion and possible C-sign (patient cups hand over lateral hip/waist area to describe pain location).
What is the Laslett Cluster for SIJ, and at what threshold do you treat?
tap
Laslett Cluster (5 tests): SI Distraction, SI Compression, Thigh Thrust, Gaenslen's, Sacral Spring Test. FABER often added as 6th. Clinical threshold: 3 or more positive tests = highly likely SIJ — treat accordingly. Sensitivity/specificity at each threshold: 1+ = SN:1.00 (rules out perfectly if all negative); 3+ = SN:.94 SP:.78 (best clinical balance); 5+ = SN:.27 SP:.88 (very specific, too insensitive to require all 5). Subjective clues for SIJ: unilateral pain; feeling of being stuck on one side; one-finger pointing to PSIS; postpartum instability. ASLR: if pain changes when you provide innominate stability, high probability of SIJ dysfunction.
