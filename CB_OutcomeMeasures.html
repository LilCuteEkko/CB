Balance & Fall Risk Assessment
Balance testing is the cornerstone of fall prevention programs. The ANPT Core Set recommends BBS, FGA, and ABC for all neuro patients. Know the cutoffs — they drive your plan of care and justify skilled services.

Berg Balance Scale (BBS)

▼
What It Tests

Static and dynamic balance through 14 functional tasks of increasing difficulty. Assesses ability to maintain balance during sitting, standing, transferring, reaching, turning, and single-leg stance. The gold standard balance assessment in PT.

DetailInformation
Scoring14 items, each scored 0–4. Total: 0–56 points. Higher = better balance
Fall Risk Cutoffs≤45/56 = increased fall risk. 41–56 = low risk (independent). 21–40 = medium risk (assist needed). 0–20 = high risk (wheelchair level)
MDCStroke: 4.6–7 points. Older adults: 3.3–5 points. PD: 5 points
MCIDStroke: 4–7 points. MS: 3 points. Older adults: 3–4 points
Time15–20 minutes
EquipmentStopwatch, ruler, step/stool, 2 chairs (one with arms, one without)
PopulationsStroke, PD, MS, TBI, SCI, older adults, vestibular disorders, hip fracture
ReliabilityExcellent inter-rater (ICC 0.95–0.98) and intra-rater (ICC 0.97)
14 Items (In Order)

(1) Sitting to standing (2) Standing unsupported (3) Sitting unsupported (4) Standing to sitting (5) Transfers (6) Standing with eyes closed (7) Standing with feet together (8) Reaching forward with outstretched arm (9) Retrieving object from floor (10) Turning to look behind (11) Turning 360° (12) Alternating foot on step (13) Standing with one foot in front (14) Standing on one foot

Clinical Tips

Ceiling effect: high-functioning patients can score 56/56 and still fall — consider the Mini-BESTest or FGA for these patients. 1-point change per item = clinically meaningful within that task. Each 1-point drop below 56 is associated with 3–4% increase in fall risk. Use same chair height and testing conditions each time for reliable re-testing.

Functional Reach Test (FRT)

▼
What It Tests

Dynamic standing balance and limits of stability in the anterior direction. Measures the maximum distance a person can reach forward beyond arm’s length while maintaining a fixed base of support (feet flat, no stepping).

DetailInformation
ProtocolPatient stands with feet shoulder-width apart, dominant arm raised to 90° shoulder flexion, fist closed. Ruler/yardstick mounted on wall at shoulder height. Record position of 3rd metacarpal. Patient reaches forward as far as possible without stepping. Record final position. Score = difference in cm/inches. Best of 3 trials
Fall Risk Cutoffs≤6 inches (15 cm) = significant fall risk. 6–10 inches = moderate risk. >10 inches = low risk. Unable to reach or needs to step = unable to perform
NormsMales 20–40: 16.7″. Males 41–69: 14.9″. Males 70–87: 13.2″. Females 20–40: 14.6″. Females 41–69: 13.8″. Females 70–87: 10.5″
MDCOlder adults: ~3.3 cm (1.3″). Stroke: 4.6 cm
Time<3 minutes
PopulationsOlder adults, stroke, PD, vestibular, hip fracture, general orthopedic
Clinical Tips

Ensure patient does NOT step, pivot, or use external support. Watch for trunk rotation substitution or hip hiking — these inflate the score. The modified FRT can be done seated for wheelchair-bound patients. Correlates with center of pressure excursion during standing. Quick screen for anterior limits of stability.

Activity-Specific Balance Confidence Scale (ABC)

▼
What It Tests

Self-reported confidence in maintaining balance during 16 specific daily activities. Measures balance self-efficacy — the patient’s perceived confidence, not actual balance ability. Low confidence predicts activity avoidance and future falls.

DetailInformation
Scoring16 items, each rated 0–100% confidence. Average all 16 = total score (0–100%)
Cutoffs<67% = fall risk in older adults. <50% = low level of functioning (may need AD). 50–80% = moderate. >80% = high functioning
MDCStroke: 12–13 points. PD: 11 points. Older adults: 10–13 points
MCIDVestibular: 11 points. Older adults: varies 10–15 points
Time5–10 minutes (self-report questionnaire)
PopulationsOlder adults, stroke, PD, MS, vestibular, TBI, lower extremity amputation
Sample Items

Walking around the house, going up/down stairs, reaching on tiptoes, walking in a crowd, walking on icy sidewalks, stepping on/off an escalator. Activities range from easy (walking in house) to challenging (icy surfaces, escalators).

Tinetti Performance-Oriented Mobility Assessment (POMA)

▼
DetailInformation
What It TestsBalance AND gait in a single assessment. Two sections: Balance (16 points) + Gait (12 points)
ScoringTotal: 0–28 points. Balance section: 0–16. Gait section: 0–12. Higher = better
Fall Risk Cutoffs≤18 = high fall risk. 19–23 = moderate risk. ≥24 = low risk

PopulationsOlder adults (originally designed for nursing home residents), PD, stroke
Gait & Endurance Tests
Gait speed is the “6th vital sign” in geriatrics. The 6MWT is the standard for functional endurance. Both are ANPT Core Set measures.

10-Meter Walk Test (10MWT) — Gait Speed

▼
What It Tests

Walking speed over a short distance. Measures comfortable and/or fast gait speed. Gait speed is the single best predictor of functional status in older adults.

DetailInformation
Protocol14-meter walkway: 2m acceleration + 10m timed + 2m deceleration. Time the middle 10 meters. AD permitted. Test both comfortable and fast speed. Speed = 10m ÷ time (sec) = m/s
Key Cutoffs<0.4 m/s = household ambulator. 0.4–0.8 m/s = limited community ambulator. >0.8 m/s = community ambulator. >1.0 m/s = full community ambulator. <0.6 m/s = increased fall risk and mortality
NormsHealthy 60–69: ~1.3–1.4 m/s. 70–79: ~1.1–1.3 m/s. 80+: ~0.9–1.1 m/s
MDCStroke (acute): 0.16 m/s. Stroke (chronic): 0.06 m/s. PD: 0.18 m/s. Older adults: 0.05 m/s
MCID0.10–0.16 m/s (small meaningful change) across most populations
Time<5 minutes
PopulationsStroke, PD, MS, SCI, TBI, older adults, hip fracture, total joint, cardiac/pulmonary rehab
Clinical Tips

Gait speed <1.0 m/s is required to safely cross a standard intersection (walk signal timing). 0.1 m/s improvement is associated with a meaningful reduction in mortality risk and disability. Always report with the assistive device used. Record both comfortable and fast speed — the difference tells you about reserve capacity.

6-Minute Walk Test (6MWT)

▼
What It Tests

Submaximal exercise capacity and functional endurance. Measures total distance walked in 6 minutes on a flat, hard surface. Reflects ability to perform daily activities requiring sustained walking.

DetailInformation
Protocol30-meter (100-ft) hallway with cones at each end. Patient walks at own pace for 6 minutes. Rest breaks allowed (timer continues). Standardized encouragement at each minute (“You are doing well, you have X minutes left”). Record total distance in meters. Record HR, BP, SpO2, RPE before + after
Key Cutoffs<300m = limited community ambulation. <200m = severe functional limitation (consider wheelchair for community). Normal healthy: 400–700m depending on age/sex. <350m post-cardiac surgery = poor prognosis
NormsMales 60–69: ~572m. 70–79: ~527m. 80+: ~417m. Females 60–69: ~538m. 70–79: ~471m. 80+: ~392m
MDCCOPD: 54–80m. Heart failure: 36m. Stroke: 34–61m. Older adults: 20–50m
MCIDCOPD: 25–33m. Heart failure: 32m. Stroke: 34.4m. Older adults: 20–50m
Time6 minutes test + setup/vitals = ~15 min total
PopulationsCardiac, pulmonary (COPD), stroke, MS, PD, SCI, post-surgical, heart failure, PAD
Precautions & Tips

Stop if: chest pain, intolerable dyspnea, leg cramps, staggering, diaphoresis, pale/ashen appearance, SpO2 <88%. Rest breaks allowed but timer does NOT stop. Do NOT walk with the patient or set pace. Use standardized encouragement phrases only. Record number of rest breaks and reasons. Testing learning effect: perform 2 tests ≥30 min apart, use the better distance.

Dynamic Gait Index (DGI) / Functional Gait Assessment (FGA)

▼
TestItemsScoringFall Risk CutoffTests
DGI8 items0–24 (each item 0–3)≤19/24 = fall riskGait on level surface, gait with speed changes, head turns horizontal/vertical, gait over/around obstacles, pivot turn, stairs
FGA10 items0–30 (each item 0–3)≤22/30 = fall risk in older adultsSame as DGI + narrow BOS walking, backward walking, eyes closed walking. Developed to address DGI ceiling effect
DGI vs FGA

FGA was developed because the DGI has a ceiling effect in higher-functioning patients. FGA adds more challenging tasks (narrow BOS, backward walking, eyes closed) and is now the ANPT Core Set recommended measure over the DGI. Use the FGA for community-dwelling adults; DGI remains useful for lower-functioning patients.

Functional Mobility Tests
These tests assess the ability to perform transfers, sit-to-stand, and basic mobility tasks. Essential for SNF, acute care, and home health practice.

Timed Up and Go (TUG)

▼
What It Tests

Functional mobility, balance, and fall risk in a single timed task. Combines sit-to-stand, walking, turning, and stand-to-sit. The most widely used quick mobility screen in PT.

DetailInformation
ProtocolStandard armchair. Cone/mark at 3 meters (10 feet). Patient starts seated, back against chair. On “Go”: stand up, walk to cone at comfortable safe pace, turn around, walk back, sit down. Timing starts on “Go,” stops when buttocks touch chair. AD permitted. Record device used
Cutoffs≤10 sec = normal mobility. 10–12 sec = emerging risk. >12 sec = increased fall risk. >14 sec = high fall risk per CDC STEADI. >30 sec = severe mobility impairment, likely needs AD
MDCStroke: 2.9 sec. PD: 3.5 sec. Hip fracture: 2.4 sec. Older adults: 2.1–4.1 sec
MCIDFrail older adults: 0.8–1.4 sec. Stroke: 2.9 sec. Hip OA: 1.1 sec
Time<3 minutes
PopulationsOlder adults, stroke, PD, MS, hip fracture, total joint, vestibular, general rehab
Variations

TUG-Cognitive (TUG-C): perform TUG while counting backwards by 3s from a random number. Dual-task cost >20% increase in time suggests attention-demanding balance and fall risk in real-world environments. TUG-Manual: carry a cup of water during TUG.

Clinical Tips

Watch the transitions — the stand-up and sit-down reveal the most. Observe: use of arms to push up (LE weakness), hesitation at turn (vestibular or fear of falling), wide BOS, shuffling. The turn is the most balance-challenging part — stepping turn vs pivot turn matters. Always record the same chair and AD for re-test.

5 Times Sit-to-Stand (5TSTS)

▼
What It Tests

Functional lower extremity strength, dynamic balance, and transfer ability. Measures the time to complete 5 full sit-to-stand cycles. Part of the ANPT Core Set of outcome measures.

DetailInformation
ProtocolStandard chair (43–45 cm seat height). Arms crossed over chest (stroke patients may have impaired arm at side or in sling). Chair NOT against a wall. Instructions: “Stand up and sit down 5 times as quickly as you can when I say Go.” Timing starts on “Go,” stops when buttocks touch seat after 5th stand. Patient must fully stand and fully sit each time
Fall Risk Cutoffs>12 sec = increased fall risk. >15 sec = recurrent falls risk in community-dwelling adults ≥65. Unable to complete = significant functional impairment
Norms (Age-Matched)60–69 years: 11.4 sec. 70–79 years: 12.6 sec. 80–89 years: 14.8 sec. 14–19 years: 6.5 sec
MDC3.6–4.2 sec (older adults). 2.5 sec (older females)
MCID2.3 sec (general). Stroke limited community ambulators: ~3 sec
ReliabilityIntra-rater ICC 0.91–0.93. Test-retest ICC 0.99. Excellent reliability
Time<2 minutes
PopulationsOlder adults, stroke, PD, MS, hip/knee OA, post-surgical (TKA/THA), SCI, LBP, vestibular
Clinical Tips

Observe quality, not just time: asymmetric weight shift (leans to one side), excessive trunk flexion momentum (“rocking”), use of hands on thighs (“climbing up legs” = quad weakness), feet placement asymmetry (stroke patients place uninvolved foot forward). Correlates strongly with quad strength (r = -0.75 to -0.83), TUG (r = 0.64), and fall history. Excellent for tracking strength gains in post-TKA/THA patients.

MDC vs MCID — What’s the Difference?

Minimal Detectable Change (MDC): the smallest change that exceeds measurement error. If a patient improves by more than the MDC, you can be confident the change is REAL (not just test-retest variability). Minimal Clinically Important Difference (MCID): the smallest change that is meaningful to the PATIENT. A change exceeding the MCID means the patient would notice and care about the improvement. Always report both — a change can be real (exceeds MDC) but not meaningful (doesn’t reach MCID), or vice versa.

Patient scores 42/56 on the Berg Balance Scale. What does this mean?
tap
Medium fall risk. BBS ≤45 = increased fall risk. 21–40 = medium, 41–56 = low. At 42, this patient is just below the fall-risk threshold. Each 1-point drop below 56 = 3–4% increase in fall risk. Intervention indicated.
Patient’s TUG is 15 seconds. What’s your interpretation?
tap
High fall risk. TUG >12 sec = increased fall risk. >14 sec = high fall risk (CDC STEADI). >30 sec = severe impairment. This patient likely needs an assistive device and a targeted balance/strength program.
What’s the difference between the 5TSTS and 30-sec STS?
tap
5TSTS: time to complete 5 reps (measures speed/power). 30-sec STS: count reps in 30 seconds (measures endurance + strength). 5TSTS is better for fall risk screening. 30-sec STS is part of the Senior Fitness Test and better for tracking LE endurance gains.
Gait speed is 0.65 m/s. What functional level is this?
tap
Limited community ambulator (0.4–0.8 m/s). Cannot safely cross intersections (<1.0 m/s required). Not a household ambulator (>0.4). Needs gait training to reach community ambulation level (>0.8 m/s). A 0.1 m/s improvement is associated with meaningful reduction in mortality.
Patient’s functional reach is 5 inches. What does this indicate?
tap
Significant fall risk. FRT ≤6 inches (15 cm) = significant fall risk. 6–10 = moderate. >10 = low. This patient has severely limited anterior limits of stability and needs balance training with emphasis on weight shifting and ankle strategy development.
A patient on your caseload improves their ODI from 48% to 38%. Is this a meaningful change?
tap
The change is 10 points. ODI MDC = 10.5 points, MCID = 6–12 points. The change meets the MCID (meaningful to the patient) but may not exceed the MDC (could be measurement error). Clinically: the patient reports improvement, which matters. Statistically: borderline. Continue treatment and re-measure.
Which balance test should you use if the BBS has a ceiling effect for your patient?
tap
Mini-BESTest or FGA (Functional Gait Assessment). Both were developed specifically to address the BBS ceiling effect. The Mini-BESTest also identifies WHICH balance system is impaired (anticipatory, reactive, sensory, dynamic gait), which directs treatment planning.
