The Takeaway

Don't Just Look at the Number

Travel PT and home health show the biggest raw numbers, but they're like comparing gross vs. net ROM — the real number is what you keep. Travel roles typically have no PTO, no employer-match 401K, no health insurance, and no job security. When you add benefits back in, a salaried outpatient or hospital role at $95K with full benefits often beats a travel contract at $115K. Always calculate total compensation = salary + employer health insurance contribution + retirement match + PTO value + CEU reimbursement.

The Hidden Variable

The biggest predictor of salary growth isn't just years — it's whether your employer has a structured advancement pathway. A clinic paying $72K with a defined 24-month track to $90K with clear milestones is a better financial bet than one paying $80K with no articulated path forward. Ask in interviews: "What does the compensation progression look like at 1, 3, and 5 years?" If they can't answer clearly, that's your answer.

Practice Settings — In-Depth Guide
Every PT Setting Explained
Choosing a practice setting is like choosing a joint mobilization grade — each one has its place depending on what you're trying to accomplish. There's no universally "best" setting, only the best fit for your clinical interests, lifestyle, and career goals. Here's an honest breakdown.
🏫 Outpatient Orthopedic Clinic Most Common

What it is: The bread and butter of PT. You treat patients with musculoskeletal conditions — post-surgical knees, frozen shoulders, low back pain, rotator cuff repairs. Patients come to you, get treated for 45–60 minutes, and go home. Think of it as the "primary care" of physical therapy.
Typical caseload: 8–16 patients/day depending on model.
Schedule: M-F, some evenings/Saturdays.
Salary range: $75K–$110K depending on region and experience. Mill clinics (high-volume, 1-PT-to-3-patients) pay less; 1-on-1 specialty clinics pay more.

Pros

Deepest MSK clinical reasoning development See patients improve over weeks — satisfying continuity Regular hours, predictable schedule Specialization opportunities (sports, pelvic floor, hand therapy) Most residency/fellowship programs are outpatient-based

Cons

"Mill" clinics push 2–3 patients simultaneously — burnout risk New grad salaries can be low ($65–75K) at high-volume chains Documentation after hours is common at busy clinics Insurance reimbursement declining — pressure to see more patients Repetitive diagnoses can feel monotonous after years

🏛 Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF) Geriatric Focus

What it is: You're the movement expert for residents recovering from hip fractures, strokes, surgeries, deconditioning, and falls in a long-term care or sub-acute rehab facility. Governed by the PDPM (Patient-Driven Payment Model) reimbursement system under Medicare Part A. Think of it as the "ICU step-down" of rehab — patients are medically complex but your job is getting them functional enough to go home or to a lower level of care.
Typical caseload: 6–10 patients/day (30–60 min each).
Productivity expectations: 85–92%.
Salary range: $85K–$110K.

Pros

Medically complex patients — forces holistic clinical thinking Strong job security (aging population = massive demand) Often better new grad salary than outpatient Autonomy — you're often the primary rehab decision-maker No patient cancellations — they live there Consistent M-F schedule in most facilities

Cons

Productivity pressure can feel like a treadmill (85-92% expectation) Documentation burden is heavy (MDS, progress notes, D/C planning) Ethical tension — pressure to keep patients on caseload for revenue Limited equipment compared to outpatient Emotional toll — end-of-life patients, cognitive decline, family dynamics Facility conditions vary widely — some buildings are rough

🏥 Acute Care Hospital Fast-Paced

What it is: You're the first PT to see patients after major surgery, ICU stays, strokes, cardiac events, or trauma. Your primary job is early mobilization, fall risk assessment, and determining discharge disposition — can this patient go home, or do they need SNF/IRF/LTACH? Think of it as triage for function. You see patients for 15–30 minutes, sometimes just to get them sitting at the edge of bed.
Typical caseload: 8–14 patients/day.
Salary range: $85K–$105K + excellent benefits.

Pros

Best institutional benefits in PT (pension, insurance, tuition reimbursement) Incredible variety — ortho, neuro, cardiac, burns, trauma in one day Develops strongest medical screening and red flag skills Interdisciplinary collaboration with MDs, nurses, OT, SLP Flexible scheduling options (weekends = differential pay) No-show rate is zero — patients are admitted

Cons

Short treatment sessions — limited time to build rapport Rarely see long-term outcomes (patients discharge in days) Physically demanding (lifting, transfers, fall risk management) Weekend/holiday rotations are standard Salary often lower than outpatient despite higher acuity COVID/infectious disease exposure risk higher

🏠 Home Health High Autonomy

What it is: You drive to patients' homes and treat them where they actually live. This is PT in the real world — navigating stairs with no handrails, thick carpet, pets, family dynamics, and whatever creative equipment you can fit in your car. Reimbursed per visit (Medicare Part A for homebound patients). Think of it as outpatient PT in the wild.
Typical caseload: 5–8 visits/day.
Salary range: $85K–$120K+ (per-visit model can be very lucrative).

Pros

Highest autonomy — you're the captain of the ship Top-tier pay, especially per-visit models Flexible schedule — manage your own route Treat patients in their actual environment (most functional setting) No productivity clock — paid per completed visit

Cons

Isolation — you work alone, no coworker interaction Driving time is unpaid and wears on your car Safety concerns in some neighborhoods/homes Limited equipment — you bring what fits in a bag Patient cancellations = lost income (per-visit model) Documentation is heavy and done at night or between visits No mentorship — terrible for new grads learning clinical skills

🏁 Inpatient Rehabilitation Facility (IRF) Intensive Rehab

What it is: A dedicated rehabilitation hospital or unit where patients receive minimum 3 hours of therapy per day, 5 days/week. Patients are post-stroke, TBI, SCI, amputation, polytrauma, or major surgery who need intensive rehab but are medically stable. Think of it as the "boot camp" of rehab — you're pushing patients harder than acute care, for longer, with a multidisciplinary team.
Salary range: $85K–$105K.

Pros

Most time with patients — deep therapeutic relationships See dramatic functional improvements over weeks Strong team approach (PT/OT/SLP/nursing/MD round together) Best setting to develop neuro rehab skills Structured mentorship programs at many facilities

Cons

Physically exhausting — 3-hour therapy days with heavy patients Weekend rotations required (to meet the 3hr/5day rule) Documentation for FIM/IRF-PAI is complex Insurance denials and premature discharges are frustrating Fewer positions available than outpatient or SNF

✈ Travel Physical Therapy Adventure + Pay

What it is: 13-week contracts at facilities nationwide that need temporary staffing. You're essentially a hired gun — show up, treat, and move on. Agencies handle placement, housing stipends, and licensure logistics.
Pay: $1,800–$2,800+/week gross (varies wildly by location, setting, and urgency). Think of it as being a PT mercenary — high reward, high flexibility, but no roots. The PT Compact (30+ states) makes this dramatically easier now.

Pros

Highest raw earning potential in PT See the country — explore different cities, states, settings Housing stipend is often tax-free (if you maintain a tax home) Exposure to diverse clinical environments builds adaptability PT Compact now covers 30+ states — easy multi-state practice Can stack contracts to "try before you buy" a permanent move

Cons

No PTO, no employer 401K match, no employer health insurance Job security = zero. Contract can end or not renew Constantly adjusting to new EMRs, protocols, and coworkers Tax complexity (stipends, tax homes, state taxes) Isolation — you're the outsider at every facility Not ideal for new grads — you're expected to hit the ground running Housing quality varies — agency-provided housing can be rough

🎓 Pediatric Physical Therapy Early Intervention

What it is: You work with infants, children, and adolescents with developmental delays, cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, torticollis, scoliosis, sports injuries, and genetic conditions. Settings include schools (IEP-based), outpatient peds clinics, early intervention programs (birth-3), and children's hospitals. Salary range: $75K–$100K (school-based often lower but with summers off).

Pros

Emotionally rewarding — helping kids achieve milestones Creative treatment — play-based interventions, games, fun School-based positions get summers and holidays off Strong job security (IDEA mandates school-based services) Less physically demanding than adult acute/SNF

Cons

Lower salary than most adult settings Managing parents can be more challenging than managing patients Progress can be very slow with severe developmental delays Heavy paperwork for IEPs and early intervention documentation Emotional toll with terminal/degenerative conditions in children

⚡ Sports / Performance Physical Therapy Elite Athletes

What it is: Treating competitive and recreational athletes — ACL post-ops, throwing athletes, runners, CrossFit injuries, and return-to-sport programming. Can range from a sports-focused outpatient clinic to sideline coverage for a college or professional team. Salary range: $80K–$125K+ (pro sports team PTs can earn significantly more but positions are rare).

Pros

Highly motivated patient population Cutting-edge rehab (blood flow restriction, force plates, biomechanics) Prestige and networking with sports teams/athletes Board certification (SCS) significantly increases earning potential Growing demand for performance-based PT beyond injury

Cons

Extremely competitive — residency/fellowship often expected Pro sports positions are rare and often low-paying initially Evening/weekend hours for game coverage Pressure from coaches, agents, and athletes to rush return-to-play Requires ongoing investment in advanced certifications

💻 Telehealth / Virtual PT Growing Fast

What it is: Evaluating and treating patients via video — exercise instruction, pain education, movement screening, home program progression. Exploded during COVID and has stayed. Works best for chronic pain management, post-surgical follow-up, wellness, and patients in rural areas. The PT Compact makes multi-state telehealth much more feasible. Salary range: $70K–$100K (still maturing as a market).

Pros

Work from anywhere (home, coffee shop, beach) No commute, no overhead, no physical space needed Scalable — can see patients across multiple states with PT Compact Excellent for cash-based practice models Lower burnout — no physical demands of manual therapy all day

Cons

Can't perform manual therapy or hands-on assessment Insurance reimbursement for telehealth PT is inconsistent Patient engagement harder through a screen Not suitable for all diagnoses (post-surgical, high acuity) Licensing still complex in non-Compact states

💰 Cash-Based / Private Practice Entrepreneurial

What it is: You own your practice and patients pay out-of-pocket (no insurance). You set your rates ($150–$300+/session), your schedule, and your treatment approach with zero insurance interference. Think of it as going from being a staff PT to being the attending physician of your own clinic. Highest ceiling, highest risk.
Earnings: wildly variable — $60K while building to $200K+ when established.

Pros

Complete clinical autonomy — treat how you want, for as long as needed No insurance authorization, no denied claims, no visit caps 1-on-1 care model — premium experience for patients Highest income ceiling in PT (clinic owners can earn $150K–$300K+) Build equity in a business you can sell

Cons

Business risk — overhead, marketing, payroll, liability insurance No guaranteed income, especially first 1–2 years Need business skills PT school doesn't teach (marketing, accounting, sales) Patient base limited to those who can afford out-of-pocket PT Solo practice = you do everything (treat, bill, clean, market)

🇺🇸 VA (Veterans Affairs) Physical Therapy Federal

What it is: Federal employment with the Department of Veterans Affairs — the single largest employer of PTs in the US (3,100+ PTs, 670+ PTAs across 1,400+ facilities in all 50 states). You treat veterans across the full continuum: acute care, inpatient rehab, outpatient, polytrauma/TBI, amputee care, SCI, mental health integration, and telehealth. The VA operates on a mission-driven model — patient care over productivity units.
Salary: ~$100K–$130K+ (GS pay scale, varies by locality). The real value is the total compensation package which is among the best in healthcare.

Pros

Federal pension (FERS) — guaranteed retirement annuity for life (1% × high-3 avg salary × years of service)
TSP (Thrift Savings Plan) — federal 401(k) with 5% employer match + automatic 1% = 6% free money EDRP (Education Debt Reduction Program) — up to $200,000 in tax-free student loan repayment over 5 years PSLF eligible — federal employment qualifies for Public Service Loan Forgiveness after 120 payments 1-hour evals, 30–45 min treatments — better patient ratios than private sector FEHB health insurance (continues into retirement), FEGLI life insurance 26 paid vacation days/year after 3 years + 13 sick days + 11 federal holidays = ~50 days off/year Direct access throughout VA system (recent legislation) Job security — extremely low turnover. Many PTs stay 20–40 years Research opportunities, residency programs, continuing education support Unique patient population: polytrauma, combat injuries, PTSD, amputees, SCI

Cons

Competitive entry — new grads rarely hired unless prior military service or HPSP scholarship recipient Bureaucratic — federal hiring process is slow (USAJobs, can take months) 8:00–5:00 rigid schedule at many facilities (less flexibility than private sector) May need to relocate for openings (positions don’t turn over frequently) Complex patient comorbidities: PTSD, substance abuse, TBI, chronic pain — emotionally demanding GS pay scale may be lower base salary than private sector in some markets

How to Get In

Best path in: (1)
HPSP Scholarship — VA pays remaining DPT tuition in exchange for service commitment. (2)
VA clinical rotations during school. (3)
Network — volunteer at VA, join APTA Federal Section, attend Wounded Warrior events. (4)
Prior military service gives hiring preference (veterans’ preference points). Apply via USAJobs.gov. When you calculate total compensation (pension + TSP match + EDRP + health insurance + 50 days off), a VA PT earning $105K base often out-earns a private sector PT making $120K.

🩹 PACU / Perioperative PT (ERAS) Emerging Role

What it is: Physical therapy in the Post-Anesthesia Care Unit (PACU) and perioperative setting as part of Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocols. This is an emerging and growing role where the PT mobilizes patients within hours of surgery — sometimes in the PACU itself before transfer to the floor. Evidence shows early mobilization (within 24 hours) reduces complications, LOS, costs, and improves functional outcomes. The PT works alongside anesthesiologists, surgeons, and PACU nurses as part of the perioperative team.
Salary: acute care hospital PT range ($90K–$115K); may include weekend/shift differentials.

Pros

Cutting-edge, evidence-based role — ERAS is transforming surgical recovery High-acuity, high-impact — your intervention directly reduces complications (DVT, pneumonia, ileus) Interdisciplinary collaboration with surgeons, anesthesiologists, and nursing Growing demand — ERAS adoption is expanding rapidly across surgical specialties You see measurable outcomes fast — patient walks same day, discharges sooner Strong evidence base: early mobilization reduces LOS by 1–3 days, complication rates by 30–50%

Cons

High-acuity patients — lines, drains, catheters, hemodynamic monitoring, fresh incisions Requires strong vital sign interpretation and acute care competency Must be comfortable with post-anesthesia patients (groggy, nauseous, hemodynamically labile) Not all hospitals have formalized PT-in-PACU programs yet Weekend/holiday coverage often required Emotionally intense — surgical complications, patient pain, rapid clinical decisions

What PACU PT Looks Like

Typical PACU PT session: assess vitals + surgical precautions + pain level → bed mobility (supine → sit) → sitting balance at edge of bed → standing with assist → ambulation (even a few steps) → seated in chair for meals. Common surgical populations: total joint (TKA/THA), spine surgery, cardiac (CABG/valve), abdominal, orthopedic trauma. Evidence shows patients who take ≥1,000 steps on POD 1 have significantly shorter LOS. The goal is meaningful mobilization within 4–24 hours of surgery.

State Practice Laws & Regulations
PT Practice Laws by State
As of July 2025, all 50 states + DC + US Virgin Islands allow some form of direct access to physical therapy. That's a historic milestone after 50 years of advocacy. But "some form" is doing a lot of heavy lifting — like saying "all patients can ambulate" without mentioning that some need a FWW and contact guard assist. The details matter.

Direct Access — Unrestricted vs. Provisional

▼
Unrestricted Direct Access (~21 States)

PTs can evaluate and treat patients without any referral, time limit, or visit cap. If the condition is beyond PT scope, the PT must refer out. States include: Alaska, Arizona, Colorado, Hawaii, Idaho, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, Maryland, Montana, Nebraska, North Dakota, Oregon, South Dakota, Utah, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin, Wyoming. Arizona (your home state) is unrestricted — no referral needed, no time limit.

Provisional Direct Access (~29 States + DC)

Patients can see a PT without referral, but with restrictions that vary by state. Common restrictions include: time limits (typically 30 days), visit limits (e.g., 12 visits in California), requirement to obtain a physician POC after a threshold, workers' comp carve-outs, and condition-specific restrictions. Examples: California = 12 visits or 45 days, whichever comes first.
Florida = 30 days or 8 visits.
Texas = 30 days (expanded from 10 days in Sept 2025).
Michigan = 10 visits within 21 days.
New York = 10 visits or 30 days.

Medicare Exception

Regardless of state law, Medicare allows direct access for evaluation, but once you determine therapy is medically necessary, you must: (1) send the POC to a physician/NPP within 30 days, and (2) obtain physician certification. As of January 2025, the physician signature requirement has been eliminated for referred patients, but direct access Medicare patients still need POC certification. This is the payer-level restriction that persists even in unrestricted states.

PT Compact — Interstate Practice Simplified

▼
What is the PT Compact?

An interstate agreement that lets eligible PTs and PTAs practice across state lines without getting a separate license in each state. Instead, you purchase a "compact privilege" through ptcompact.org. Think of it as TSA PreCheck for your PT license — same background check, faster access, one home base. As of 2026, 30+ states + DC are members. Privileges are typically issued within minutes of purchase. Cost: $45 commission fee + state fee ($0–$150 depending on state).

Eligibility Requirements

• Hold a current, valid PT or PTA license in your home state
• Your home state must be a Compact member and actively issuing privileges
• The remote state must also be a Compact member
• Meet home state CE requirements (no separate CE for remote states)
• Complete jurisprudence requirements for the remote state
• You practice under the scope of practice of the state where the patient is located

Notable Non-Compact States (as of early 2026)

California, New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts, Illinois, Pennsylvania (PA implemented July 2025), Michigan, Rhode Island — many of the largest states are still not members. If you plan to practice in these states, you'll need traditional licensure. Michigan, Massachusetts, and Rhode Island have introduced compact legislation but haven't passed it yet. This matters enormously for travel PTs and telehealth practitioners.

Scope of Practice Variations That Matter

▼
Dry Needling

Not legal in all states. Some states (e.g., California, New York) do not allow PTs to perform dry needling. Others require specific continuing education hours (e.g., 54 hours in many states). Some states require certification through APTA or equivalent. Always verify with your state practice act before performing dry needling. Arizona: PTs can perform dry needling with appropriate training.

PTA Supervision (2026 Change)

In 2026, a major shift is happening: "direct" supervision is changing to "general" supervision for PTAs in outpatient private practices. This means PTs can oversee a larger caseload more efficiently, which can increase productivity-based bonuses. However, the PT remains responsible for all evaluations, re-evaluations, and discharge summaries. The PTA-to-PT ratio and specific supervision requirements still vary by state.

Imaging Referral

Most states do not allow PTs to order diagnostic imaging (X-ray, MRI). A few states allow PTs to refer for imaging under specific circumstances. This is an active area of APTA advocacy. As a clinician, you should be proficient in clinical prediction rules and screening to determine when imaging referral to a physician is indicated.

Is It Worth It?

Board certification costs ~$2,000–$3,500 (exam + prep materials) and requires 2,000 hours of direct patient care in the specialty area. The financial ROI depends on your setting — an OCS in a hospital system may get a $5K bump, while the same OCS opening a cash-based ortho clinic could mean a $50K+ difference. Beyond salary, certification signals expertise to patients, referral sources, and employers. It's like having a fellowship on your CV — it opens doors that otherwise stay closed.

Non-Clinical Career Paths for PTs

▼
Clinical Education / Academia

DPT faculty positions ($85K–$130K). Teach, research, and mentor. Typically requires PhD or residency/fellowship. Work-life balance can be excellent (summers, sabbaticals).

Healthcare Administration

Rehab director, VP of rehab services, hospital administrator. PTs who understand both clinical and business sides are valuable. Salary: $100K–$180K+.

Medical Device / Pharma

Clinical specialist, sales rep, or medical science liaison for companies like Stryker, DJO, or Bioventus. Salary: $90K–$160K + commission/bonuses. Your clinical credibility is the differentiator.

Health Tech / AI

Clinical product manager, UX researcher, or clinical AI consultant for companies building PT-related software. Tech companies desperately need clinicians who understand workflows. Salary: $100K–$200K+.

Utilization Review / Insurance

Reviewing therapy claims for medical necessity. Work from home, predictable hours. Salary: $80K–$110K. Less fulfilling clinically, but excellent work-life balance.

Legal / Expert Witness

Medical-legal consulting, depositions, IMEs. Experienced PTs with specialty certifications can earn $150–$500/hr for expert witness work. Part-time supplement to clinical practice.

Student Loan Strategy for New Grads

▼
The Reality

The average DPT graduate carries ~$127,000 in student loan debt (APTA 2023 data). With a starting salary of $68K–$82K, the debt-to-income ratio is concerning. This isn't a "grind harder" problem — it requires strategic repayment planning from day one.

PSLF (Public Service Loan Forgiveness)

If you work for a 501(c)(3) nonprofit hospital, university, government agency, or qualifying nonprofit, you can have remaining federal loan balance forgiven after 120 qualifying payments (10 years) on an income-driven repayment plan. Most hospitals, academic medical centers, and many SNFs qualify. This is the single most impactful financial decision a new PT can make — it can save $50K–$100K+ over the life of the loan.

Income-Driven Repayment (IDR)

SAVE, PAYE, REPAYE, and IBR plans cap payments at 10–20% of discretionary income. If pursuing PSLF, enroll in IDR immediately — lower payments = more forgiven. If not pursuing PSLF, consider whether aggressive payoff (standard/refinanced) saves more in interest than IDR forgiveness after 20–25 years. Run the numbers with studentaid.gov's repayment simulator.
