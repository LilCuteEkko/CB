SIJ & Pelvis — Pelvic Girdle Pain & Assessment

The sacroiliac joint is where the spine meets the pelvis — it transmits the entire weight of the trunk to the lower extremities. Despite having only 2–4mm of motion, the SIJ is a significant pain generator accounting for 15–30% of mechanical low back pain. Its stability depends on two complementary systems: form closure (bony anatomy and ligaments) and force closure (muscles generating compression). SIJ dysfunction is especially common in pregnancy (50% of pregnant women) and post-lumbar fusion. This module covers pelvic osteology, SIJ ligaments, biomechanics, muscles with OINA, special tests (Laslett’s cluster), pelvic conditions, and management.

32-year-old female, 30 weeks pregnant, reports right-sided posterior pelvic pain worse with rolling in bed, stairs, and prolonged standing. Fortin finger test: points to within 1 cm of right PSIS. Laslett's cluster: positive thigh thrust ✓, positive compression ✓, positive sacral thrust ✓ (3/5 positive = SIJ source, +LR 4.0). ASLR: rates right leg as 3/5 difficulty. With manual iliac compression: difficulty drops to 1/5. Clinical reasoning: Confirmed SIJ-origin pain (Laslett's 3/5) with insufficient force closure (ASLR improves with compression). Treatment: SIJ belt at GT level (replicates the manual compression that helped), TrA activation in hooklying (draw-in), pelvic floor engagement (refer to pelvic floor PT if needed), side-sleeping with pillow between knees, avoid asymmetric loading (stairs one at a time). NO manipulation in pregnancy. She needs stabilization, not mobilization.

45-year-old male with left buttock and posterior thigh pain. Is this lumbar radiculopathy or SIJ? Step 1 — Lumbar exam: Repeated extension and flexion testing → no centralization or peripheralization. SLR negative bilaterally. No dermatomal sensory loss. Reflexes intact. Step 2 — SIJ exam: Fortin finger test positive (left PSIS). Laslett's: distraction ✓, thigh thrust ✓, compression ✓, sacral thrust negative, Gaenslen's negative (3/5 positive). Conclusion: SIJ origin. The lumbar exam was negative (no centralization, negative SLR, no neuro deficits), AND the Laslett's cluster is positive 3/5. If the lumbar exam had shown centralization, you'd treat the lumbar spine first regardless of SIJ findings — centralization trumps SIJ testing because lumbar pathology can refer to the SIJ region.

Pelvic Osteology
The pelvis is a bony ring formed by 3 bones (2 innominates + sacrum) connected at 3 joints (2 SIJs + pubic symphysis). It transmits trunk loads to the lower extremities and serves as the foundation for the axial skeleton. Each innominate is formed by the fusion of 3 bones at the acetabulum (triradiate cartilage fuses by age 15–17).

Pelvic Ring Concept

The pelvis functions as a closed ring — disruption at any point affects the entire ring. Fracture at one site nearly always has a second injury (Malgaigne’s rule). The 3 joints (2 SIJs + symphysis) must work as a coordinated unit. Pelvic ring fractures are graded by stability: Type A (stable — single break in ring), Type B (rotationally unstable but vertically stable), Type C (rotationally AND vertically unstable — life-threatening).

Sacroiliac Joint Structure & Ligaments
The SIJ is unique: it’s both a synovial joint (anterior 1/3 with fibrocartilage on sacral side + hyaline on iliac side) and a syndesmosis (posterior 2/3 bound by the strongest ligaments in the body). Motion is limited to 2–4mm and 1–4° — but even this small motion is critical for shock absorption and load transfer.

SIJ Biomechanics — Form & Force Closure
SIJ stability depends on two complementary mechanisms: form closure (anatomy/passive) and force closure (muscles/active). When either mechanism fails, the SIJ becomes vulnerable to shear forces and pain.
Form Closure (Passive)
Anatomical Stability

Stability from the shape and fit of the joint surfaces. No muscle force needed. Contributors: (1) Wedge shape of sacrum driven between ilia (wider superiorly). (2) Complementary ridges and grooves on articular surfaces — highest coefficient of friction of any diarthrodial joint. (3) Dorsocranial wedging during nutation compresses posterior ilia together. (4) Ligamentous binding (interosseous = strongest in body). Form closure alone is insufficient for dynamic stability — needs force closure.

Force Closure (Active)
Dynamic Muscular Stability

Stability from muscles, fascia, and ligaments generating perpendicular compression across the SIJ. Contributors: (1) TrA (approximates anterior ilia — “corset effect”). (2) Multifidus (compresses sacrum into ilia posteriorly). (3) Gluteus maximus (pulls via STL attachment). (4) Pelvic floor (base of “canister”). (5) Thoracolumbar fascia (TLF) interconnects these muscles. Failure of force closure = SIJ instability, excessive shear, and pain.

Clinical Implication

Patients with tight hamstrings are forced into early sacral counternutation during forward bending (“unlocks” the SIJ prematurely). This increases SIJ shear forces and reliance on muscular force closure. If force closure is also insufficient (weak TrA, inhibited multifidus), the SIJ is vulnerable to injury. This explains why hamstring tightness + weak core + forward bending = common mechanism for SIJ dysfunction. Nutation (lordotic posture) is the position of maximum SIJ stability and is advocated for lifting and load transfer.

Muscle Sling Systems

Posterior oblique sling: Lat dorsi (R) + TLF + Glute max (L) — active during contralateral gait. Transfers force across SIJ diagonally. Anterior oblique sling: External oblique (R) + internal oblique (L) + adductors — rotational power, stabilizes pubic symphysis. Deep longitudinal sling: Erector spinae + sacrotuberous ligament + biceps femoris — controls nutation during forward bending. Lateral sling: Glute med/min + adductors + contralateral QL — frontal plane pelvic stability during single-leg stance.

SIJ Special Tests
No single test diagnoses SIJ dysfunction. Use clusters. Laslett’s cluster (2005) is the gold standard — 3 of 5 pain provocation tests positive rules in SIJ as pain source. Diagnosis of SIJ pain requires exclusion of lumbar spine pathology first.

Laslett’s Pain Provocation Cluster (Laslett et al., 2005)

▼
Diagnostic Rule

3 of 5 positive = SIJ as pain source (+LR 4.0). If all 5 negative AND no centralization during lumbar exam = SIJ ruled out. Reference standard: SIJ diagnostic injection block. These tests PROVOKE pain — a positive test recreates the patient’s familiar pain pattern.

Evidence-Based SIJ Management
Treatment depends on whether the SIJ is hypomobile (stiff) or hypermobile (lax). Most PGP patients have insufficient force closure (too lax) and need stabilization, not mobilization.

Hypomobility: Mobilization & Manipulation

▼
Techniques

SIJ manipulation: sidelying (Chicago technique) — gapping force through the posterior SIJ. Muscle energy technique (MET): patient actively contracts (isometric) against PT resistance to correct positional dysfunction. Example: posterior innominate rotation dysfunction → patient pushes into hip extension against PT resistance (activates glute max to rotate innominate posteriorly). Important: If positive Laslett’s cluster for pain provocation, manipulation/mobilization is indicated for that joint. BUT if the problem is hypermobility, manipulation will make it worse.

Hypermobility: Stabilization Program

▼
Progressive Stabilization (O’Sullivan Model)

Stage 1 — Local stabilizers: TrA activation (draw-in maneuver), multifidus recruitment (isometric setting), pelvic floor activation. Supine, hooklying, 4-point kneeling. Target: hold 10 sec × 10 reps without global muscle substitution. Stage 2 — Integrated control: Add limb loading while maintaining local stabilizer activation (leg slides, heel drops, bridging). Stage 3 — Global integration: Functional loading: squats, lunges, single-leg activities, sport-specific tasks. SIJ compression belt: external force closure support. Worn at level of greater trochanters (NOT iliac crest). Evidence supports use in pregnancy-related PGP and post-surgical SIJ fusion rehab.

Clinical Decision-Making

▼
Algorithm

(1) Rule out lumbar spine (lumbar assessment + centralization testing). (2) Rule out hip (FABER, log roll, resisted hip tests). (3) Confirm SIJ (Laslett’s cluster 3/5 positive + Fortin finger test). (4) Assess force closure (ASLR — does manual compression help?). (5) If ASLR improves with compression = force closure deficit = stabilization program. (6) If ASLR does NOT improve with compression = consider joint restriction (hypomobility) = mobilization/MET. (7) Always address contributing factors: hip ROM (especially IR — femoral IR drives innominate motion), hamstring length, lumbar mobility, motor control.

What is the difference between form closure and force closure in this scenario?
tap
Form closure: passive stability from joint anatomy (sacral wedge shape, articular ridges/grooves, ligaments). No muscle force needed. Force closure: active stability from muscles generating perpendicular compression (TrA, multifidus, glute max, pelvic floor via TLF). Both required for full SIJ stability. PGP patients typically have insufficient force closure.
Nutation = close-packed or loose-packed? Name the ligaments tighten?
tap
Nutation = close-packed (self-locking). Sacral base tilts anterior-inferior. ALL dorsal ligaments tighten EXCEPT the long dorsal ligament (LDL). Interosseous, short dorsal, sacrotuberous, and sacrospinous all tighten. Counternutation = loose-packed (unlocked) — ONLY the LDL tightens.
Laslett’s cluster: which tests, how many positive, and what does it mean in this scenario?
tap
5 tests: distraction, compression, thigh thrust (P4), sacral thrust, Gaenslen’s. 3 of 5 positive = SIJ as pain source (+LR 4.0). Thigh thrust is the most sensitive individual test (SN 88%). If all 5 negative AND no centralization during lumbar exam = SIJ essentially ruled out.
ASLR test: what does it assess and what does improvement with manual compression indicate in this scenario?
tap
ASLR assesses functional pelvic stability (force closure). Patient rates difficulty of SLR 0–5. If difficulty improves when PT manually compresses ilia together = insufficient force closure. Treatment: stabilization exercises (TrA, multifidus, pelvic floor) + SIJ belt. The belt simulates what the manual compression does.
What is the strongest ligament in the body and what does it do in this scenario?
tap
Interosseous sacroiliac ligament. Located between sacral and iliac tuberosities (posterior to auricular surfaces). Prevents anterior and inferior movement of the sacrum. Primary SIJ stabilizer. Deep to all other posterior ligaments. This is where the SIJ axis for nutation/counternutation is located.
FABER test is positive. Describe how to differentiate SIJ from hip as the pain source?
tap
Location of reproduced pain: Posterior pelvic pain (near PSIS) = SIJ. Groin/anterior hip pain = hip pathology (FAI, labral tear, OA). FABER stresses BOTH the hip and SIJ. Can be positive for both simultaneously. If groin pain: add hip-specific tests (log roll, scour, resisted hip flexion/IR). If posterior: add Laslett’s cluster to confirm SIJ.
Why might aggressive hamstring stretching worsen SIJ pain in this scenario?
tap
Biceps femoris long head inserts on ischial tuberosity and has a direct connection to the sacrotuberous ligament (STL). STL resists nutation and stabilizes the SIJ. Excessive hamstring stretching can reduce STL tension → reduced SIJ stability. In hypermobile SIJ patients (especially pregnancy-related PGP), aggressive stretching removes a stabilizing force and can increase pain.
