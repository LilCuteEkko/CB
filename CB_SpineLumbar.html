Lumbar Spine — Comprehensive Anatomy
The lumbar spine carries the most load and is the most common site of spinal pathology. Five large vertebrae with thick discs (1:3 disc:vertebral height ratio — same as cervical). Facet orientation favors flexion/extension as the cardinal plane of motion. Understanding disc anatomy, ligament function, and the relationship between facets, discs, and neural structures is fundamental to treating lumbar pathology.
Osteology & Intervertebral Disc
Lumbar Vertebrae
  • Body — kidney-shaped; largest of all vertebrae (supports most body weight)
  • Vertebral foramen — triangular; larger than thoracic, smaller than cervical
  • Transverse processes — have accessory processes (tubercles at base of TP). In L5 shorter and stouter
  • Articular facets — superior facets face posteromedially; inferior facets face anterolaterally. Orientation = sagittal plane → facilitates flex/ext, limits rotation. Mammillary processes on superior articular processes — attachment site for multifidi
  • Spinous processes — sturdy, hatchet-shaped, project nearly horizontally (vs. thoracic which angle inferiorly)
  • Pars interarticularis — region of lamina between superior and inferior facets. Site of spondylolysis (stress fracture) → may progress to spondylolisthesis (anterior slippage)
Intervertebral Disc (IVD)
  • ~25% of total spine length. No disc between occiput–C1 or C1–C2
  • Annulus fibrosus — concentric layers of obliquely-oriented collagen fibers. Alternating direction (cross-woven) provides tensile strength. Outer layers blend with ALL and PLL. Outer annulus is innervated → potential pain source (discogenic pain)
  • Nucleus pulposus — proteoglycan-rich center that attracts water. Acts as hydraulic shock absorber. Gradual transition from annulus (no sharp boundary). With aging: decreased proteoglycan → decreased water content → decreased disc height
  • Disc pathology: Annular fissures → discogenic pain. Protrusion/herniation → nerve root compression → radicular symptoms (dermatome + myotome patterns). Posterolateral herniation most common — PLL narrows caudally, less posterior support
  • Cartilaginous endplates — hyaline cartilage on superior/inferior vertebral surfaces. Primary route for disc nutrition (diffusion). Disc is largely avascular

Deep Back Muscles — OINA (Erector Spinae & Transversospinalis)

▼
Organizational Framework

Back muscles are arranged in 3 layers: Superficial (extrinsic — trap, lats, rhomboids, levator scap — covered in shoulder module), Intermediate (extrinsic — serratus posterior sup/inf), Deep (intrinsic — TRUE back muscles). All intrinsic back muscles are innervated by dorsal primary rami (DPR). The deep layer is further divided into superficial (splenius), intermediate (erector spinae), and deep (transversospinalis) sub-layers.

Splenius Muscles (Superficial Deep Layer)

Fiber direction is superolateral (like a bandage wrapping the deeper muscles). Name from Greek splenion = bandage.

Sacrum, Coccyx & Lumbosacral Variations

▼
Sacrum

Large triangular wedge-shaped bone of 5 fused vertebrae. 4 pairs of sacral foramina for dorsal and ventral nerve roots. Key landmarks: Promontory (anterior edge of S1 — the ledge), Ala (wings — superior lateral), Median sacral crest (fused SPs), Sacral hiatus (laminae of S5 fail to fuse — access for caudal epidural), Sacral cornua (horns from S5 pedicles), Sacral canal (contains cauda equina nerve roots), Apex (inferior tip), Base (superior S1). Lumbosacral angle between L5 body and sacral base.

Clinical Variations

Sacralization of L5: L5 partially or fully fused to sacrum — may appear to have only 4 lumbar vertebrae. Can alter biomechanics at L4–L5. Lumbarization of S1: S1 partially separates from sacrum — appears to have 6 lumbar vertebrae. Spondylolysis: stress fracture of pars interarticularis (defect in lamina). MC at L5. “Scottie dog” sign on oblique X-ray. Spondylolisthesis: anterior slippage of one vertebra on another. Graded I–V (Meyerding). MC at L5 on S1. Spondylosis: general degenerative changes of the spine (OA of vertebral column).

Spinal Cord, Nerve Roots & Dermatomes

▼
Spinal Cord Organization

The spinal cord ends at L1–L2 level as the conus medullaris. Below this, the vertebral canal contains only nerve roots called the cauda equina (“horse's tail”). The cord is anchored inferiorly by the filum terminale to the coccyx.

31 spinal nerve pairs: 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal. Each spinal nerve has a dorsal root (sensory) and ventral root (motor). Immediately after exiting the intervertebral foramen, the spinal nerve divides into DPR (dorsal primary ramus) — supplies posterior trunk muscles and skin, and VPR (ventral primary ramus) — supplies lateral/anterior trunk, limbs, and forms the plexuses.

Key distinction: Cervical nerves exit above their numbered vertebra (C5 exits above C5). Exception: C8 exits below C7 (there are 8 cervical nerves but only 7 cervical vertebrae). From T1 down, nerves exit below their numbered vertebra.

Cauda Equina vs. Conus Medullaris Syndrome

Cauda equina syndrome (CES): Compression of nerve roots below L2. RED FLAG — surgical emergency. Signs: bilateral leg pain/weakness, saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction (retention → overflow incontinence), decreased rectal tone. LMN signs (flaccid, areflexic). Refer immediately.

Conus medullaris syndrome: Compression at cord termination (L1–L2). UMN + LMN mixed signs. Sudden onset, bilateral, symmetric. Saddle anesthesia + sphincter dysfunction but less pain than CES.

Lumbar Coupled Motion & ROM Factors

▼
Lumbar Coupled Motion

In the lumbar spine, lateral flexion and rotation couple to OPPOSITE sides (contralateral). When you side-bend right, the vertebral body rotates left (SPs rotate to the right/ipsilateral/concave side). This is the opposite of cervical coupling.

Cardinal motion: Flexion/extension — facilitated by sagittally-oriented facets. Rotation is significantly limited by facet orientation (only ~5° total per segment).

Four Factors Limiting Spinal ROM

(1) IVD elasticity and compressibility — disc height and hydration status. (2) Facet joint orientation — cervical = rotation-friendly, lumbar = flex/ext-friendly. (3) Joint capsule tension of facet joints. (4) Ligament and muscle resistance — the ligament system provides passive restraint, muscles provide active restraint. Disc:vertebral height ratio is the key mobility predictor: cervical 1:3 (mobile), thoracic 1:6 (least mobile), lumbar 1:3 (mobile).

Red Flags & Spine Screening
The spine is the most common site for serious pathology masquerading as MSK pain. Red flag screening must be completed before any classification or treatment approach. Every PT must know when to refer out immediately versus when to treat.

CPR for Compression Fracture

Female + Age >70 + Trauma (fall/MVA) + Corticosteroid use. ¾ criteria = 100% specificity (+LR 218). If these fit, refer for imaging. Key populations: adolescents (backpack) and elderly females (osteoporosis). Fractures due to osteoporosis most common at T9–T11.

Inflammatory Back Pain Screening (Under 45 + Sx >3 months)

All patients under 45 with symptoms >3 months must be asked: (1) Back stiffness >30 min? (2) Night pain awakening? (3) Alternating buttock pain? (4) Pain relieved by rest? 2/4 = 70% SN, 81% SP. 3/4 = 33% SN, 100% SP for inflammatory back pain (ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic SpA, reactive SpA).

SINSS Framework & Classification Approach

▼
SINSS

Severity (0–10 VAS, rate min/mod/severe). Irritability (how long to provoke? how long to relieve? reflects tissue stress tolerance). Nature (bone, muscle, ligament, joint, nerve). Stage (acute/subacute/chronic). Stability (improving, stable, worsening?).

LOCIDAA

Location. Onset. Characteristics. Intensity. Duration. Aggravating factors. Alleviating factors. Plus: 24-hour behavior.

Treatment-Based Classification (TBC) — 4 Categories

De-emphasizes identifying specific anatomical lesions after red flag screening. 1. Mobilization/Manipulation. 2. Specific Exercise. 3. Traction. 4. Stabilization. Match intervention to classification, not to pathoanatomy.

Symptom Modulation Staging

High SINSS / High pain: Manual therapy (careful), directional preference exercise, modalities (heat), Grade 1–2 mobs, manipulations if acute (<30 days, no symptoms past knee). Mod SINSS: Sensorimotor exercises, stabilization, flexion exercises, mobility. Low SINSS: Strength/conditioning, sport/work-specific tasks, aerobic exercise, general fitness. Injury prevention phase.

Lumbar Spine — Examination & Patterns
LBP is the leading cause of activity limitation and work absence. 60–70% lifetime prevalence. Peaks at 5th–6th decade. Chronic LBP (>3 months): if >6 months, less than half return to work. 25–33% will still have LBP after 1 year. $100 million work days lost per year.

Lumbar History — Pain Patterns & Clinical Clues

▼
Pain Location Clues

Central back pain: Usually not unilateral (not SIJ or zyga joint). Bilateral pain: Rare to have central origin — exception is central IVD protrusion. Buttock pain as referral: Can be zyga joint inflammation. Groin pain: Common with high lumbar IVD herniation — dull ache, deep, hard to localize, some NBT. Leg pain: May indicate radiculopathy or pseudo-radiculopathy.

Dejerine's Triad

Abdominal activation, coughing, and sneezing (Valsalva maneuver) make symptoms worse. Three-part test for nerve compression. Increases CSF pressure. Seen in IVD pathology and sacroiliitis. If (+) → suspect disc involvement.

Palliative & Provocative Factors

Supine with knees flexed / side lying: ↓ spinal column pressure, ↓ nerve tension. Pts with neurocompressive disc herniation will NOT like this. Standing/extension: Less provoking for disc pathologies. Sitting/flexion: Less provoking for spinal stenosis — opens vertebrae. "Shopping cart sign" = leans forward to open canal.

Key Aggravation Patterns

Flexion relief + walking aggravation: Zyga joint, spondylolisthesis, spinal stenosis. Sitting/stooping/lifting aggravation + recumbency relief + standing/walking OK: IVD lesion (protrusion or annular tear). Constant pain: Inflammatory process. Pain with movement: Mechanical cause. Morning stiffness: Inflammatory. Worse during evening: Mechanical symptoms.

Centralization & Peripheralization

Centralization: Progressive retreat of most distal symptoms back toward LB spine — GOOD sign, indicates reducible lesion. Peripheralization: Opposite — symptoms moving distally — BAD sign, lesion is increasing in size. Once a motion/position that reduces symptoms is identified, focus should be to encourage this motion or posture.

Age-Related DDx

10–20 yo: Spondylolisthesis. 15–40 yo: IVD and inflammatory conditions. 45+: OA, spondylosis. 65+: Cancer, stenosis, AAA, compression fracture. Back pain in a young child is NOT normal — think spondylolysis.

Observation & Postural Assessment

▼
Lateral Pelvic Shift

Common in pts with disc-related LBP and sciatica. Herniation LATERAL to nerve root: Deviation AWAY from side of irritated nerve (draws nerve root away from disc fragment). Herniation MEDIAL to nerve root: Deviation TOWARDS side of lesion (decompresses nerve root). Listing is protective — can also be from zyga joint irritation, spinal nerve irritation, spasm of QL or iliacus.

Excessive Lordosis Indicates

Pelvic crossed syndrome (erector spinae + iliopsoas shortened; abs + glute max weak). Shortened PLL, lengthened ALL. Spondylolisthesis. Anterior pelvic tilt (weak abs, shortened TFL/iliopsoas, lengthened hamstrings/glutes).

Flat Back Indicates

Spinal stenosis or lateral recess stenosis. Lateral shifting. Posterior pelvic tilt. Short hamstrings. Disc protrusion. Weak hip flexors. Reversed lordosis (sway back) = thoracic kyphosis + posterior pelvic tilt.

Lumbar Pathologies
Lumbar pathologies range from soft tissue dysfunction to spondyloarthropathies. Understanding the spectrum from ligament sprains to spondylolisthesis to stenosis is critical for accurate classification and treatment.

Ankylosing Spondylitis & Spondyloarthropathies

▼
Ankylosing Spondylitis

Inflammatory arthritis of spine — "Bamboo Spine." Affects facets, tendons, ligaments. Enthesitis (inflammation at disc-vertebrae attachment) → bone erosion → new bone (calcification) at facets, costotransverse, costovertebral joints. AF can calcify, fusing segments. Sacroiliitis usually first to appear → SIJ fuses → progressive immobility → stress fractures → excessive kyphosis → may crowd internal organs. 90% are HLA-B27 positive. Men 3:1. Age of onset 15–40. Can affect eyes, knees, hips, ankles, shoulders. Chest expansion <2.5 cm is pathologic (normal = 5 cm at nipple line).

Other Spondyloarthropathies

Psoriatic Arthritis: Small joints (hands/feet), "sausage digit," psoriasis skin rash. Reactive Arthritis: Joint inflammation from infection (intestinal, UTI). IBD-associated: Crohn's, UC — ask about B&B, stress-related. Juvenile SpA: Childhood onset, enthesitis dominant.

Degenerative Spinal Stenosis — Central vs Lateral

▼
Central Stenosis

Narrowing of spinal canal around thecal sac (contains cauda equina). Can present symptoms similar to cauda equina. Causes: facet arthrosis, ligamentum flavum thickening/bulging, spondylolisthesis.

Lateral Stenosis

Encroachment of spinal nerve in lateral recess. Causes: facet joint hypertrophy, loss of IVD height, IVD bulging, spondylolisthesis.

Presentation

Long history of LBP. Unilateral or bilateral leg pain. 65% present with neurogenic claudication (compression affecting LE innervation, affects gait). ↓ LB lordosis. Aggravation with extension and side bend. Extension closes canal, flexion opens canal. Shortened hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris), lengthened hip extensors (glute max, hamstrings) → early recruitment of LB extensors → excessive LB extension → more stenotic compression.

Intervention

William flexion exercises. Manual therapy (thrust and non-thrust LB mobs/manips). Hip flexor, paraspinal, rectus femoris stretching. Glute and core strengthening/stabilization. Posterior pelvic tilt positioning.

Zyga Joint Dysfunction, Instability & Soft Tissue

▼
Zygapophyseal Joint Dysfunction

Result of lesion to joint and pain-sensitive structures. Can result from hypomobility OR hypermobility/instability. Presentation: Unilateral LBP, aggravated with certain movements (flexion OR extension, especially end range). Extra-articular: ↓ PPIVM, normal PPAIVM. Periarticular: Restriction in both, hard capsular end-feel. Pathomechanical: Restriction in both, abrupt slightly springy end-feel. Treatment: specific joint mobs, postural education, correct muscle imbalances, core stabilization.

Clinical Instability

Recurrent, constant, catching, or locking pain. Feeling of giving way. Aggravated by sustained sitting, prolonged standing, semi-flexed postures, forward bending, sudden movements, lifting, sneezing. Returning to upright from forward bend is key complaint. Posterior creases, fulcrum point, excessive AROM. Treatment: remove abnormal joint stress, ↑ global musculature strength (core and trunk).

QL Dysfunction

Broad stiffness, very tight. ↓ lateral side bend away from dysfunction. Referred pain to glutes. Treatment: put QL on stretch (sidelying, affected side up, glutes on stretch), dry needling, trigger point release.

Psoas Syndrome

Big contributor to LBP that lasts. Anterior pelvic tilt, ↑ lordosis, muscle spasms, forward flexed posture. Treatment: stretch (thunderdome, mod Thomas — not 2–3×/day, too much stretch is bad), STM (likes firm pressure), MFR, KT tape.

Thoracolumbar Fascia & LBP

↑ thickness of TLF with LBP >1 year. ↓ shear plane motion by 20–50%. Fibrosis (fibroblasts don't turn off, continue laying down fibrin). Adhesion of LB muscles → poor movement patterns. Treatment: MFR on stretch, dynamic fascial stretching (TLF runs obliquely — L lats to R glute to R hammy and vice versa), yoga, postural/ergonomic education.

Lower Crossed Syndrome

Erector spinae and iliopsoas are tight. Abs and glutes are weak. Results in anterior pelvic tilt + ↑ LB lordosis. Mirror image of upper crossed syndrome.

Disc Pathology & Radiculopathy
Disc degeneration is a normal aging process beginning around the 3rd decade. The nucleus pulposus starts to be replaced by fibrocartilage and dehydrates. Understanding the 3-stage degeneration cascade and compression by root level is essential for every PT.

Disc Degeneration Cascade & Herniation Types

▼
3 Stages of Disc Degeneration

Stage 1 — Early Dysfunction: Outer annular tears, endplate separation, some cartilage destruction, facet inflammation. Stage 2 — Intermediate Instability: Loss of disc space and height, laxity of posterior joint capsule and AF. "Giving way and catching" through AROM. Abnormal movement patterns. Stage 3 — Final Stabilization: Osteophyte formation, stenosis, laying down extra tissue for stabilization, loss of disc material, radial tears of AF, fibrosis of posterior joint capsules.

Cord Compression Concerns

Extrusion and sequestration can put pressure on spinal cord → myelopathy (RED FLAG) with UMN symptoms and hyperreflexia (3–4+). Pressure on nerve roots distal to conus medullaris → cauda equina syndrome. Conus medullaris = tapered lower end of spinal cord at L1–L2.

DDx for LE Symptoms (Not Disc)

Hip or SIJ pathology. Claudication (iliac/iliofemoral arteries). Spondylolisthesis. Muscle strain, fracture, or CA. Peripheral nerve injury. Diabetic amyotrophy (lumbosacral neuropathy — asymmetric painful muscle wasting of quads/adductors/hip flexors, poor glycemic control). Meralgia paresthetica: Entrapment of lateral femoral cutaneous nerve under inguinal ligament — lateral/anterior paresthesias, sensation only (NO motor or reflex), ↑ symptoms with hip extension, relief with sitting.

Clinical Pearls

Pain worse in LE than LB = nerve root irritation. Pain worse in LB than LE = probably referred from spinal structure. 20–30% of asymptomatic people have abnormal imaging findings. Do not perform full spinal motions shortly after rising from bed (disc is most hydrated). Repetitive contact of spinous processes during max extension → periostitis ("kissing spine" / Baastrup's disease).

What is the difference between centralization and peripheralization, and why does it matter clinically?
tap
Centralization: Progressive retreat of the most distal symptoms back toward the LB spine — this is a GOOD prognostic sign and indicates a reducible lesion. Peripheralization: Symptoms moving distally — BAD sign, lesion is increasing in size. Once a motion/position that centralizes is identified, encourage that motion/posture. This is the foundation of the McKenzie approach: find the directional preference that centralizes symptoms and use it therapeutically. Derangement syndrome responds to this approach.
How do you differentiate a lateral pelvic shift from a disc herniation lateral vs. medial to the nerve root?
tap
Herniation LATERAL to nerve root: Patient deviates AWAY from the side of the irritated nerve root. This draws the nerve root away from the disc fragment. Herniation MEDIAL to nerve root: Patient deviates TOWARDS the side of the lesion. This decompresses the nerve root. The listing is a protective position. Can also be caused by zyga joint irritation, spinal nerve irritation, or QL/iliacus spasm. This is why observation of lateral shift direction combined with neural tension testing is critical for classification.
What distinguishes spondylosis, spondylolysis, and spondylolisthesis?
tap
Spondylosis (OA/DJD): Wear and tear of spine. ↓ disc hydration → bony contact → inflammation → osteophytes → ↓ foraminal space. "Shopping cart sign." Extension/rotation painful, flexion relieves. Spondylolysis (pars Fx): Defect/fracture at pars articularis ("Scotty dog"). 5–7 yo. Back pain in young child is NOT normal. Hyperextension + rotation + loading. Spondylolisthesis (slippage): Pars fracture + anterior slippage. Graded 1–5 (≥3 = surgery). Step-off deformity. 10–15 yo. ↑ lordosis, waddle gait, ask about B&B.
What is the SLR test interpretation at different angles?
tap
Pain <70°: Neurological irritation or disc herniation (usually lateral disc protrusion). Pain >70°: L-spine or SI joint pain. Pain that does NOT increase with neck flexion: Hamstring tightness or SIJ. If >70°, repeat bilaterally: Pain <70° bilateral = SIJ. Pain >70° bilateral = lumbar. Sensitize by adding: ankle PIP (peroneal nerve), TED (tibial nerve), SID (sural nerve). Crossed SLR: Positive when raising the asymptomatic leg reproduces pain in the symptomatic leg — indicates disc herniation MEDIAL to the nerve root, often with extruded fragment.
What is the CPR for lumbar manipulation and what makes a patient a good candidate?
tap
A patient is likely to benefit from lumbar manipulation if they meet these criteria: (1) LBP <16 days (some sources say 30 days). (2) No symptoms distal to the knee. (3) FABQ score <19 (low fear avoidance). (4) At least 1 hypomobile segment in the lumbar spine. (5) At least 1 hip with IR >35°. The more criteria met, the higher the likelihood of improvement. This CPR helps classify patients into the manipulation category of the Treatment-Based Classification system, which de-emphasizes specific pathoanatomy in favor of matching treatment approach to patient presentation.
