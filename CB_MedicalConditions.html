Medical Conditions & PT Practice

These conditions are in your patient's chart every day in SNF, outpatient, and acute care. They change your exercise prescription, your precautions, your monitoring protocols, and how you talk to your patients. This is not background noise — it is clinical practice.

Type 2 Diabetes Hypertension Heart Disease COPD Osteoporosis Obesity Oncology Renal Disease
Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus
Chronic metabolic disease characterized by hyperglycemia from insulin resistance. Complications include cardiovascular disease, peripheral vascular disease, retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy, and loss of physical function. People with T2DM have significantly less muscular strength and exercise capacity than those without — both of which predict mortality and loss of physical function. Exercise is an evidence-based intervention (ADA + ACSM position statements). PT-directed exercise counseling combined with fitness center training produces outcomes equivalent to directly supervised sessions (Taylor et al., Phys Ther 2009).
Complications That Directly Affect PT
  • Cardiovascular disease — increased cardiac risk during exercise; vitals monitoring essential; atypical angina presentations common
  • Peripheral vascular disease (PVD) — reduced perfusion to extremities; wound healing impaired; claudication limits exercise tolerance
  • Peripheral neuropathy — loss of protective sensation; foot inspection mandatory; increased fall risk; balance impairment
  • Autonomic neuropathy — blunted HR response to exercise; unable to reach age-predicted max HR; use RPE instead of HR; orthostatic hypotension risk
  • Retinopathy (proliferative) — high-intensity exercise can increase intraocular pressure → retinal hemorrhage; contraindication to vigorous exercise
  • Nephropathy — renal function impaired; fluid balance considerations; exercise contraindication if severe
  • Accelerated muscle strength loss — older adults with T2DM lose muscular strength significantly faster than peers (Park et al., Diabetes Care 2007)
  • Reduced exercise capacity — inverse relationship between exercise capacity and mortality in men with diabetes (Church et al., Diabetes Care 2004)
Exercise Contraindications & Precautions
Absolute Stop — Glucose Thresholds (ADA)

Exercise is contraindicated if fasting plasma glucose >300 mg/dL, or >250 mg/dL with ketosis present. Exercising with marked hyperglycemia can worsen ketoacidosis.

Exclude from Exercise Program If:

Uncontrolled hypertension · Proliferative retinopathy · Severe peripheral neuropathy · Nephropathy · Autonomic neuropathy · AHA absolute contraindications to exercise testing present · Physical impairment precluding participation.

Hypoglycemia Risk — Active Management

Patients must carry a glucose food source during all exercise sessions. Hypoglycemia risk is greatest with insulin or sulfonylurea medications. Signs: shakiness, sweating, confusion, weakness. Action: stop exercise immediately, provide 15g fast-acting carbohydrate, recheck glucose in 15 minutes.

Monitoring During Exercise

Blood pressure monitored throughout graded exercise testing. Heart rate continuously monitored. Stop test at 85% age-predicted max HR (220 − age) or patient voluntary cessation. With autonomic neuropathy: HR unreliable — use RPE exclusively.

Exercise Prescription — ADA/ACSM Protocol
From Taylor et al. (Phys Ther 2009), Sigal et al. (Diabetes Care 2006 — ADA position), Albright et al. (Med Sci Sports Exerc 2000 — ACSM position). Combined resistance + aerobic training is the evidence-based standard.
Resistance Training Protocol
  • Intensity: 80% of 1-RM (heavy enough to improve strength; adequate dose for glycemic benefit)
  • Volume: 4 sets × up to 8 repetitions per exercise
  • Frequency: 2 nonconsecutive days per week (e.g., Mon/Thu)
  • Progression: When 4×8 at current weight is achieved → increase by 2.27–4.54 kg (5–10 lbs) next session
  • Rest: 1–3 minutes between sets
  • Exercises: Multi-joint, multi-muscle group — chest press, row, leg press as minimum; add others as appropriate
  • Equipment: Range-of-motion limiting machines preferred over free weights for safety in this population (Taylor et al.)
  • Duration: 2-month program demonstrated significant improvements; ongoing maintenance essential
Aerobic Training Protocol
  • Mode: Walking or jogging on treadmill (any low-impact continuous aerobic activity)
  • Intensity: RPE 12 ("somewhat hard") on Borg RPE Scale — important because HR may be unreliable (autonomic neuropathy, beta-blockers)
  • Duration: 20 minutes per session (start here; build to 150 min/week per ADA recommendation)
  • Frequency: Same days as resistance training (2×/week minimum; goal 5×/week)
  • Progression: Increase speed or grade to patient tolerance; do not exceed RPE 12
  • Functional testing: Modified Bruce treadmill protocol for exercise capacity; duration in minutes = exercise capacity measure
  • Timing: Avoid exercise at peak insulin action; best 1–3 hours after a meal
Key Finding — Taylor et al. RCT (n=24)

PT-directed exercise counseling + independent fitness center training = equivalent outcomes to directly supervised exercise for muscular strength and exercise capacity. Determining factor: dose and adherence — not whether a PT is in the room. This validates a cost-effective counseling model for T2DM management.

PT Counseling Model — 5 A's Framework
Evidence-based health behavior counseling strategy for PT-directed exercise counseling. Used in Taylor et al. (2009) and Pinto et al. (Prev Med 1998). Tailored to the patient's current Stage of Exercise Behavior (Prochaska transtheoretical model).
The 5 A's — Exercise Counseling
  • Address the Agenda — establish what the patient wants to discuss; set the session focus collaboratively; align exercise goals with patient priorities
  • Assess — current physical activity level, barriers to exercise, readiness to change, stage of exercise behavior, medical status, relevant contraindications
  • Advise — evidence-based recommendation: benefits of exercise for T2DM, specific exercise prescription (type/intensity/frequency/duration), safety guidelines
  • Assist — help the patient overcome barriers; review the exercise program; problem-solve schedule conflicts; provide written exercise program; ensure equipment familiarity
  • Arrange Follow-Up — schedule face-to-face and phone check-ins; weekly phone calls (~10 min) to monitor adherence and troubleshoot; adjust program as needed

Clinical Application

Tailor counseling to the stage — a Precontemplator needs motivational conversation, not an exercise program handout. A Maintainer needs program advancement. Assess stage at each visit; patients move forward and backward. Stage-matched counseling improves adherence.

PT-Specific Clinical Considerations
Foot & Wound Care
  • Inspect feet before and after every session — loss of protective sensation = patient may not feel injury
  • Proper footwear required — no barefoot exercise; cushioned socks
  • Any wound, blister, or skin breakdown = PT documents and notifies nursing/physician — diabetic wounds → ulcers → infection → amputation risk
  • Monofilament testing: 10-g Semmes-Weinstein monofilament at plantar sites — inability to detect = loss of protective sensation
  • Avoid exercises that increase shear forces on plantar surface in active neuropathic foot
Medication Effects on PT
  • Insulin: Hypoglycemia risk during and after exercise; time exercise 1–3 hrs post-meal; patient carries glucose source always
  • Sulfonylureas (glipizide, glyburide): Also cause hypoglycemia with exercise — same precautions as insulin
  • Metformin: Does not cause hypoglycemia; no direct exercise interaction; most common first-line agent
  • SGLT-2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, canagliflozin): Euglycemic DKA risk; monitor for unusual fatigue; hypotension risk
  • Beta-blockers (if on for CV comorbidity): Blunts HR — use RPE not target HR for exercise intensity
  • ACE inhibitors / ARBs: May cause hypotension, especially post-exercise; monitor BP and transitions
SNF / Inpatient Clinical Pearls
  • Check glucose level before therapy session — many SNFs have glucose log in chart; do not treat if fasting glucose >300 or <70 mg/dL
  • Glucose <70 mg/dL (hypoglycemia): hold therapy, notify nursing, initiate hypoglycemia protocol, re-check in 15 min
  • Document pre/post glucose, symptoms, any adverse events in PT notes
  • Balance and fall risk: neuropathy + vision changes (retinopathy) + orthostatic hypotension = high fall risk; assess Berg/TUG; alert bed alarm/fall precautions
  • Skin integrity rounds: check heels, sacrum, ankles every visit — pressure injury prevention is shared PT responsibility
  • Nutrition timing: coordinate with dietary for meal/therapy scheduling to optimize glucose management
  • Energy levels: T2DM patients may have fatigue from hyperglycemia or hypoglycemia episodes — adapt intensity accordingly
Diabetes — Deep Pathophysiology & Clinical Distinctions
Pancreatic Cell Types & T1 vs T2 DM
Islets of Langerhans — Cell Types

Alpha cells: secrete glucagon (raises blood glucose). Beta cells: secrete insulin (lowers blood glucose; transports glucose into cells). Delta cells: secrete somatostatin (inhibits glucagon and insulin). T1DM = autoimmune destruction of beta cells.

T2DM Pathogenesis Sequence

(1) Increased blood glucose → pancreas secretes insulin → (2) Muscle/liver cannot use glucose due to insulin resistance (receptor problem — "lock changed") → (3) Glucose accumulates in blood → hyperglycemia → (4) Kidney excretes excess glucose → glucosuria → (5) Beta cells eventually fail to compensate → insulin secretion also impairs → progressive decline. Diet and exercise are MORE effective than drugs for T2DM prevention and management (most modifiable factors).

Cardinal Signs & Complications
The "3 Polys" + Other Signs
  • Polyuria — abnormally large volumes of dilute urine (kidneys excreting excess glucose)
  • Polydipsia — abnormally great thirst (from fluid loss)
  • Polyphagia — excessive hunger (cells starving despite high blood glucose)
  • Weight loss — glucose unavailable to cells; body breaks down fat and protein; dehydration
  • Ketonuria — abnormal ketone bodies in urine from fat catabolism
  • Weakness / fatigue / dizziness
  • Recurrent blurred vision
  • Many T2DM patients are initially asymptomatic — discovered incidentally on labs
Macro & Microvascular Complications — Healing Impact

Macrovascular: Hyperglycemia + increased fat metabolism → atherosclerosis → poor tissue perfusion → impaired wound healing → ischemic heart disease. Microvascular: Ischemia from small vessel disease → wounds fail to get blood/O2/cells needed to heal. Increased glucose in body fluids/tissues fosters bacterial growth → increased infection risk. Diabetic neuropathy foot ulcer sites: plantar areas of metatarsal heads, toes, plantar hallux — all high-pressure zones with poor sensory feedback.

T2DM Risk Factors — Most Modifiable

Most modifiable: Diet + Exercise (more effective than medications for prevention). Others: BMI >25, sedentary lifestyle, smoking, HTN (>140/90), HDL <35 mg/dL, triglycerides >250 mg/dL. Adipose tissue: local low-grade inflammatory process → excess adipose tissue → insulin resistance. Leptin (from fat cells) activates killer cells, contributing to chronic inflammation in obesity.

DKA vs. HHS vs. Alcoholic Ketoacidosis
DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis) — Type 1 Classic

Insufficient insulin → biological starvation → counterregulatory hormones (glucagon, catecholamines, cortisol) → hyperglycemia + lipolysis → incomplete fat metabolism → ketone production → metabolic acidosis. Hallmarks: high glucose + high ketones + acidosis. Life-threatening. Exercise contraindicated if glucose >250 mg/dL with ketosis.

HHS — Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic Syndrome

Extreme hyperglycemia; mild or undetectable ketonuria; NO acidosis. Occurs in T2DM because residual insulin prevents fat mobilization/ketosis. Triggers: infection, inadequate fluid intake, medications, stress. Dehydration is severe. Medical emergency.

Hypoglycemia vs. Hyperglycemia — Clinical Recognition
Hypoglycemia (<70 mg/dL) — Recognize and Act

Symptoms (onset rapid — minutes): Shakiness, sweating (diaphoresis), tachycardia, pallor, anxiety, confusion, weakness, hunger, headache. Severe: seizure, loss of consciousness. During PT: stop exercise immediately → 15g fast-acting carbohydrate (OJ, glucose tablets, glucose gel) → recheck in 15 min → do not resume therapy until ≥80 mg/dL and asymptomatic. Always document and notify nursing. Dizziness + diaphoresis during exercise in DM patient = hypoglycemia until proven otherwise.

Hyperglycemia — Signs and PT Thresholds

Symptoms (onset gradual — hours/days): Polydipsia, polyuria, fatigue, blurred vision, slow wound healing, recurrent infections, fruity breath (if ketotic). PT thresholds: Hold therapy if fasting glucose >300 mg/dL (absolute) or >250 mg/dL with ketosis present. With hyperglycemia 180–299 and no ketosis: PT can proceed with monitoring and glucose source available. Hyperglycemia blunts immune response → even minor PT-related skin irritation warrants documentation.

DM → Cardiomyopathy Pathway

Hyperglycemia → atherosclerosis (fat metabolism byproducts → plaque) → poor tissue perfusion → impaired wound healing → ischemic heart disease → ischemic cardiomyopathy. This is why DM patients in cardiac rehab need careful glucose monitoring: silent ischemia is common in DM (autonomic neuropathy blunts anginal pain signal).

Hypertension
The most common cardiovascular condition PT encounters. Present in the majority of SNF patients and a large percentage of outpatient orthopedic patients. HTN changes exercise prescription, vital sign monitoring protocols, and requires recognition of hypertensive urgency/emergency. Understanding antihypertensive medications is essential because they directly alter the exercise response.

Hypertensive Emergency — Symptoms Requiring Immediate Action

Severe headache · Chest pain · Shortness of breath · Vision changes · Neurological symptoms (confusion, slurred speech, weakness) · Nausea/vomiting. These symptoms + severely elevated BP = end-organ damage. Do NOT wait — call for emergency help immediately.

Normal Exercise BP Response vs. Hypertensive Response
Normal Response to Aerobic Exercise

SBP increases progressively with intensity (expected: +10–50 mmHg during maximal exercise). DBP remains relatively stable or decreases slightly. SBP should not exceed 220 mmHg in males or 190 mmHg in females during exercise. Post-exercise: BP returns to resting values within minutes.

Exertional Hypertension — Stop Exercise If:

SBP >250 mmHg during exercise · DBP >115 mmHg during exercise · SBP drops >10 mmHg from resting despite increasing workload (= exercise-induced hypotension — sign of cardiac compromise) · Any cardiac symptoms develop.

Post-Exercise Hypotension

BP drops after exercise — especially common in HTN patients on antihypertensives. Can persist 4–9 hours. Always supervise transfers and position changes post-exercise. Have patient sit 3–5 minutes before standing. SNF: document and communicate to nursing if significant.

Exercise Prescription for HTN
Exercise IS Treatment for HTN

Regular aerobic exercise reduces resting SBP by an average of 5–8 mmHg and DBP 3–5 mmHg in hypertensive individuals. This is equivalent to medication effect for Stage 1 HTN. PT-directed exercise is a first-line non-pharmacological intervention.

  • Aerobic: 5–7 days/week; moderate intensity (RPE 12–13); 30–60 min/day (can accumulate); walking, cycling, swimming
  • Resistance: 2–3 days/week; 60–80% 1-RM; avoid Valsalva maneuver (breath holding drives acute BP spikes — instruct to exhale on exertion)
  • Isometrics: Bilateral handgrip isometrics 4×2 min at 30% MVC, 3×/week has good evidence for BP reduction
  • Avoid: High-intensity isometric exercises at >80% MVC; sustained overhead positions; Valsalva in all patients
Vital Signs Monitoring Protocol in PT
  • Pre-exercise: Always measure BP and HR before initiating exercise — establish resting baseline; document in notes
  • During exercise: Monitor HR continuously (pulse ox or HR monitor); check BP at each intensity change in cardiac or Stage 2 HTN patients
  • Post-exercise: Check BP + HR 3–5 minutes after stopping; watch for post-exercise hypotension before allowing ambulation to discharge home
  • SNF: Pre/post vitals documentation required in skilled notes; any BP >180/110 → hold therapy, notify nurse, document
  • Orthostatic check: Supine → sitting → standing with 1–2 min between positions; drop ≥20 mmHg SBP or ≥10 mmHg DBP = orthostatic hypotension → document, notify MD, fall precautions
Orthostatic Hypotension — Definition & Action

Drop of ≥20 mmHg SBP or ≥10 mmHg DBP within 3 minutes of standing. Symptoms: dizziness, lightheadedness, syncope risk. Action: sit/lie patient immediately, elevate legs, monitor until BP normalizes, document, notify MD. Highest risk: morning, post-meal, first stand after prolonged bed rest, in patients on alpha-blockers or diuretics.

Heart Disease & Cardiac Conditions
Coronary artery disease, heart failure, post-MI, post-cardiac surgery — these patients are in SNF and outpatient PT daily. Exercise is therapeutic but requires precise monitoring. Know the contraindications, warning signs, medication effects, and cardiac rehabilitation framework before putting these patients on a treadmill.
Absolute Contraindications to Exercise (AHA)
  • Unstable angina (not yet stabilized)
  • Uncontrolled cardiac arrhythmia with hemodynamic compromise
  • Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis
  • Uncontrolled symptomatic heart failure
  • Acute pulmonary embolism or pulmonary infarction
  • Acute myocarditis or pericarditis
  • Acute aortic dissection
  • Recent MI (<2 days) without physician clearance
Stop Exercise Immediately — Warning Signs
  • Angina or anginal equivalent — chest pressure/pain, jaw pain, L arm ache during exercise
  • SBP drops >10 mmHg despite increasing workload
  • SBP >250 mmHg or DBP >115 mmHg during exercise
  • Significant arrhythmia — new onset, irregular rhythm, palpitations with hemodynamic symptoms
  • Signs of poor perfusion — pallor, cyanosis, cold/clammy skin, mottling
  • Neurological symptoms — ataxia, dizziness, confusion, syncope
  • Severe dyspnea — out of proportion to exercise level
  • Patient requests to stop — always honor this

Rate Pressure Product (RPP) & MET Levels
Rate Pressure Product (Double Product)

RPP = HR × SBP. Estimates myocardial oxygen demand. If patient had angina during stress testing: note the RPP at which symptoms occurred — keep exercise RPP below that threshold. Normal at rest: ~7,000–10,000. During moderate exercise: ~20,000–25,000. Ischemic threshold is individual.

Heart Failure — PT Considerations
Signs of Decompensated HF — Hold Therapy

Rapid weight gain (>2–3 lbs overnight / >5 lbs/week) · Increased dyspnea at rest or with minimal activity · Orthopnea (can't lie flat) · Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea · New or increased peripheral edema · S3 gallop · Resting SpO2 <90%. Hold PT and notify nursing/MD immediately.

EF Classification

HFrEF (reduced EF <40%): Systolic dysfunction; can exercise but at lower loads; ACEI/ARB/beta-blocker/diuretic treatment. HFpEF (preserved EF ≥50%): Diastolic dysfunction; exercise tolerance limited by diastolic filling; often older female + HTN + T2DM + obesity. Both benefit from graded exercise but require careful monitoring.

Exercise Prescription for Stable HF

Begin at low intensity (40–50% HRR or RPE 9–11). Build duration before intensity. Interval training often better tolerated than continuous. Resistance training (40–60% 1-RM) is safe and improves functional capacity. Avoid isometric Valsalva. Monitor SpO2 — supplement O2 if needed to keep SpO2 ≥92%.

6-Minute Walk Test (6MWT)

Standard outcome measure in HF. Patient walks as far as possible in 6 minutes on flat surface. Normative values vary by age/sex. MCID: ~30–50 meters. Monitor HR, BP, SpO2, Borg dyspnea before/during/after. Stop test if SpO2 <88%, angina, severe dyspnea, SBP >250, or patient cannot continue.

Cardiovascular Symptom Encyclopedia
These symptoms present in your patients daily. Each one has a specific cardiac vs. non-cardiac differential. Know the pattern, know when to refer, know when to hold PT and call nursing.
Chest Pain, Angina & Palpitations
Chest Pain / Angina Pectoris

Cardiac workload exceeds myocardial O2 supply → ischemia → temporary pain. Location: substernal or retrosternal, radiates to left shoulder/ulnar border of arm into fingers, jaw, neck, upper back. Quality: "squeezing," "burning," "pressing," "heartburn," "choking." Associated: diaphoresis, dyspnea, pallor, nausea. Women: SOB, fatigue, neck/jaw/back pain — often no crushing chest pain. Sublingual nitroglycerin relieves in 1–2 minutes. After angina episode: resume exercise at reduced intensity; wait several hours before resuming. Exercise at <anginal threshold (record vitals at ischemia onset).

Palpitations

Irregular, fast, or "extra" heartbeat. Benign causes: mitral valve prolapse, caffeine, anxiety, exercise, athletic heart. Serious causes: CAD, cardiomyopathy, ventricular arrhythmia, aortic/mitral stenosis. In women: may be caused by drops in estrogen (perimenopause) or hypothyroidism. Associated dizziness or syncope = refer immediately.

Dyspnea, Syncope & Edema — PT Red Flags
Dyspnea — 4× Risk of Sudden Death

Dyspnea alone predicts death from cardiac causes at 4× the rate of angina alone (2× the risk). May be cardiac (impaired LV unable to contract fully → pulmonary congestion) or pulmonary. Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND): awakens from supine sleep gasping — classic CHF. Orthopnea: breathlessness relieved by sitting upright; occurs any time of day.

Cardiac Syncope vs. Vasovagal Syncope

Cardiac syncope: decreased O2 to brain from poor cardiac output; caused by arrhythmia, orthostatic hypotension, aortic dissection, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, CAD. Predictors: prior stroke/TIA, cardiac medications, HTN, obesity, DM. Aortic stenosis = prone to postural hypotension and Valsalva-induced syncope. Vasovagal: strong parasympathetic response → HR slows + vessels dilate → hypotension; after prolonged standing/sitting; donating blood; generally not serious.

Peripheral Edema — Right vs. Left Heart

Right ventricular failure: bilateral dependent edema + jugular venous distention + cyanosis + abdominal distention. Right upper quadrant pain = enlarged liver (hepatomegaly). Left ventricular failure: pulmonary edema (SOB, orthopnea, PND). Non-cardiac: pulmonary HTN, renal dysfunction, cirrhosis, lymphatic obstruction, burns, infection. Pitting edema + leg pain = venous disease (DVT territory).

Claudication — PVD in PT

Cramping/leg pain brought on by consistent exercise; subsides with rest. Result of peripheral vascular disease (arterial or venous). More debilitating than angina or dyspnea but can coexist with both. Pitting edema + leg pain = venous. No edema + leg pain with exertion only = arterial (PAD). Exercise: aerobic encouraged; high-impact jogging/step aerobics avoided in varicose veins; walking programs are primary treatment for PAD claudication.

Common Cardiovascular Pathologies — PT Clinical Framework

Myocardial Infarction (MI) — Zones, Signs, PT Timing

3 zones of injuryExercise testing POD 3Women present differently▼
Pathogenesis — 3 Zones

Coronary artery occlusion (thrombus on ruptured plaque) → deprivation of O2 → progressive tissue damage. Zone of Infarction: cells die → necrosis → fibrous scar (complete in 6–8 weeks). Zone of Hypoxic Injury: surrounding zone; can return to normal in 2–3 weeks. Zone of Ischemia: outermost reversible zone. Electrolyte disturbance: cellular loss of K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺ → reduced contractility. Most common site: left ventricle (highest workload). Scar formation is complete in 6–8 weeks — this is why PT progressions must respect this timeline.

Signs & Symptoms — Men vs. Women

Classic (men): sudden crushing chest pressure, radiates to arms/throat/neck/back, constant pain 30–60 min, pallor, SOB, diaphoresis. Women: SOB, middle-of-night waking, chronic unexplained fatigue — often NO crushing chest pain. Women delay longer seeking help ("decision delay") → worse outcomes. Heart attacks occur more in early morning hours (catecholamine surge) and have seasonal peaks (Thanksgiving/New Year's).

PT Timing After MI

Majority of exercise testing can begin within 3 days of MI with very low complication rate. Phase I cardiac rehab begins in hospital: early mobilization, hall ambulation (1–2 METs), ADLs, education. Post-MI complications to screen: CHF, cardiogenic shock, pericarditis, thromboembolism, recurrent infarct, sudden death. Monitor for: dyspnea, new chest pain, arrhythmia, significant BP changes.

Angina vs. MI — The Clinical Distinction

Angina: narrowed (not blocked) artery → reduced O2 → temporary ischemia → no permanent damage; relieved by rest or nitrates in minutes. MI: complete occlusion → sustained ischemia → irreversible cell death → permanent scar. Duration distinguishes them: angina lasts <15 minutes; MI pain lasts 30–60+ minutes and does NOT respond to rest or nitrates alone.

Atherosclerosis & CAD — Pathogenesis, Risk Factors, Exercise

Pathogenesis Sequence

(1) LDL cholesterol modified by free radicals → arterial wall injury. (2) Injury permits infiltration of macromolecules. (3) Naked collagen acts as "flypaper" → platelet adherence. (4) Platelets release chemicals → proliferation of smooth muscle cells + fibrous clots → lesion grows outward first (positive remodeling) then inward. (5) Fibrous cap forms over plaque; cap rupture → thrombus → MI or stroke. Capped plaques are less likely to rupture. Symptoms only appear when coronary lumen narrows >75%.

Non-Modifiable vs. Modifiable Risk Factors

Non-modifiable: Age (men ≥45, women ≥55), gender (men worse until menopause), family history, ethnicity (Black and Mexican-American highest risk), infections. Modifiable: Smoking (leading preventable cause — nicotine increases fibrinogen + plaque), HTN, DM, obesity, physical inactivity, elevated LDL, low HDL, high triglycerides, psychologic/oxidative stress.

Newer Predictors of CAD

CRP (C-reactive protein): acute-phase reactant reflecting low-grade systemic inflammation; elevated levels increase CAD risk regardless of age/gender/other risk factors; high in smokers; predicts response to statins and aspirin. Homocysteine: elevated levels may be as significant as high cholesterol or smoking. Fibrinogen: predicts first heart attacks in unstable CAD. Lipoprotein(a): LDL + extra protein; slows clot breakdown; high levels = greater MI risk.

How Exercise Influences CAD — PT Role

Exercise keeps inner lining of arteries healthy and less prone to injury leading to plaque. Mechanisms: regular arterial expansion/contraction maintains vessel shape; maintains endothelial function; limits plaque attachment; inhibits clot formation (makes platelets less "sticky"); promotes clot-dissolving enzymes. HDL effect: exercise raises HDL by avg 4.6%; every 1% HDL increase = 3.5% reduction in cardiac death risk. Frequency may be more important than intensity for improving HDL. Low-intensity activity (65–70% max HR) 3×/week is as effective as high-intensity for BP reduction. Delayed heart rate recovery (≤12 bpm decrease in first minute post-exercise) is an independent risk factor — exercise improves this.

Women & CAD — Specific PT Knowledge

Women's hearts are smaller, respond differently to hypertrophic stimuli. Women have longer QT intervals → 3× greater risk of fatal arrhythmias from certain medications (QT-prolonging drugs). "Syndrome X" (coronary microvascular dysfunction): doesn't show on angiogram; false-positive stress tests (abnormal result, clear arteries). Women more likely to have diffuse plaque buildup (vs. focal in men). Estrogen cardioprotective: stimulates HDL, acts as Ca²⁺ channel blocker, reduces platelet aggregation. After menopause: CAD incidence matches men within 10 years.

Cardiomyopathy — Types & PT

Dilated cardiomyopathy: impaired contraction/relaxation → LV ejects less efficiently → large blood volume remains post-systole → 4-chamber dilation → CHF. Causes: heavy alcohol (direct toxicity + nutritional deficiency), obesity. Symptoms = same as HF (dyspnea, orthopnea, tachycardia, palpitations, peripheral edema, JVD). 75% die within 5 years of diagnosis if idiopathic. PT: avoid strenuous exercise; assess current activity levels; monitor HR/vitals/rhythm.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) — SCD Risk

Inherited (1:500 athletes); LV wall thickness >13–30mm is pathologic. Normal athletes may have 13–15mm hypertrophy without symptoms. Most common cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes — basketball, football players, African-American athletes. 55% of SCD victims have NO identified risk factors. SCD risk factors: prior cardiac arrest, family history, syncope/near-syncope, ventricular tachycardia on Holter, hypotensive BP response to exercise, LVH >30mm. PT role: follow AHA guidelines for preparticipation screening; recognize murmur on PPE.

Commotio Cordis — Know This

Blunt, non-penetrating chest blow (innocent appearing) that causes virtually instantaneous cardiac death. Mechanism: blow directly over heart + precise timing to vulnerable repolarization phase (just before T-wave peak) → ventricular fibrillation. Average age: 14 years. Typically low energy/velocity projectile (baseball, lacrosse ball). Athletic trainers/PT at sporting events must know: immediate CPR + AED is the only intervention.

Aortic Aneurysm — PT Palpation Rule

Always palpate the abdomen for a pulsating mass before performing any anterior abdominal therapy. AAA: below diaphragm, occurs 4× more often than thoracic; 25–30% have midabdominal or low back pain. Back pain may be only symptom before rupture. Post-op: restrict pushing/pulling/lifting >10 lbs for 6–10 weeks. Throbbing pain that increases with exertion → monitor vitals + palpate pulses. Thoracic aneurysm: above diaphragm, hypertensive men 40–70, more life-threatening. Cerebral aneurysm: 50–80% do not rupture in lifetime.

Mitral Valve Prolapse (MVP)

LV contracts → one or both mitral leaflets prolapse into LA → incomplete seal → arrhythmia. Classic triad: profound fatigue + palpitations + dyspnea with exertion. More common in connective tissue disorders (Marfan's). Exercise is a key management component — not to alter valve function but to improve overall cardiovascular fitness. PT may encounter requests for conditioning programs.

Aortic Stenosis — PT Flag

Progressive valvular calcification (aging); risk factors same as CVD (obesity, sedentary, smoking, high cholesterol). Cardiac output maintained until stenosis is severe. Classic triad indicating poor prognosis: Angina + Exertional Syncope + Left Ventricular Failure. Onset of these symptoms = medical referral immediately. Exertional syncope in an older patient = aortic stenosis until proven otherwise. Prophylactic antibiotics against infective endocarditis for asymptomatic patients.

DVT → PE Pathway

Thrombophlebitis: inflammation of vein + thrombus. DVT: distal = below knee; proximal = popliteal or above (higher PE risk). Most common DVT site: femoral or iliac vein. Superficial thrombosis: saphenous vein. PE: DVT thrombus breaks loose → travels through right heart → lodges in pulmonary artery → can be fatal. Virchow's triad (DVT risk): stasis + hypercoagulability + endothelial injury.

Wells DVT Criteria — PT Must Know

Score-based clinical decision tool. Key components: active cancer, paralysis/immobilization, bedridden >3 days or surgery within 12 weeks, localized deep vein tenderness, entire leg swollen, calf >3cm larger than asymptomatic, pitting edema, collateral superficial veins, alternative diagnosis at least as likely (subtract 2 points). Score ≤0 = low probability; 1–2 = moderate; ≥3 = high probability → refer for Doppler ultrasound. In SNF: any new unexplained calf swelling, warmth, tenderness, Homans' sign (low sensitivity, high false positive) → hold PT and notify MD.

Abnormal dilation of saphenous veins with valve incompetence → tortuous vessels → thrombosis propensity. High venous pressure with prolonged sitting/standing = major risk factor. COPD affects ~16 million Americans and is the third leading cause of death. The hallmark is dyspnea with exertion — which means pulmonary patients are always in PT's caseload. SpO2 monitoring, dyspnea scales, pursed-lip breathing, energy conservation, and inhaler timing are all PT-essential skills.

⚠ SpO2 Thresholds — When to Stop or Supplement

Monitor SpO2 continuously during exercise in COPD. Stop exercise if SpO2 drops below 88%. Supplement oxygen (per MD order) to maintain SpO2 ≥90–92% during activity. Document resting SpO2, lowest SpO2 during exercise, and recovery SpO2 in PT notes.

Pursed-Lip Breathing (PLB)
  • Inhale slowly through nose (2 counts)
  • Purse lips as if blowing out a candle
  • Exhale slowly through pursed lips (4–6 counts — 2× as long as inhale)
  • Mechanism: Increases positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) → prevents airway collapse during exhalation → reduces air trapping → improves gas exchange
  • Use during exertion, stair climbing, transfers — any activity that increases dyspnea
  • Also teaches patient to control breathing rate during anxiety/panic
Energy Conservation Strategies
  • Plan: Schedule high-energy tasks for when energy is best (typically mid-morning)
  • Prioritize: Identify essential vs. optional tasks; eliminate or delegate lower-priority activities
  • Position: Sit to perform tasks; forward lean (elbows on table) offloads accessory muscles and improves diaphragmatic mechanics
  • Pace: Slow down; rest before fatigue onset; work-to-rest ratios (e.g., 2 min work: 1 min rest)
  • ADL modifications: Long-handled tools (reduce bending), seated shower, grab bars, raised toilet seat, clothing selection (avoid tight waistbands)
  • Breathing coordination: Exhale on exertion (exhale when pushing/pulling/lifting)
Inhaler Timing & Exercise
Short-Acting Bronchodilators (SABA)

Albuterol/salbutamol — instruct patient to use SABA 15–30 minutes before exercise session. This maximizes bronchodilation during peak exertion. Document pre-exercise inhaler use in PT notes.

Long-Acting Bronchodilators (LABA/LAMA)

Salmeterol, tiotropium — taken at scheduled times; PT does not time around these. Ensure patient has taken scheduled medications before session.

Inhaler Use During Session

If patient uses rescue inhaler during PT session: stop exercise, allow 5–10 minutes for bronchodilation, reassess SpO2 and dyspnea before considering resuming at lower intensity. Document inhaler use during session. Frequent rescue inhaler use = poorly controlled COPD → communicate to MD team.

Pulmonary Rehabilitation — Exercise Prescription
  • Aerobic: Interval training often better tolerated than continuous (30-sec work: 30-sec rest → build to 3-min work intervals). Walking, cycle ergometer, treadmill. Target: Borg dyspnea 3–5.
  • Resistance: UE + LE strengthening reduces dyspnea burden (stronger muscles = lower O2 demand for same task). 2–3 sets × 10–15 reps, 50–70% 1-RM. Include UE ergometer for upper extremity endurance (functional: carrying, reaching).
  • Inspiratory Muscle Training (IMT): Threshold IMT device at 30% maximal inspiratory pressure (MIP); 5×/week; improves inspiratory muscle strength in COPD with respiratory muscle weakness.
Evidence for Pulmonary Rehab

Pulmonary rehabilitation is Level A evidence for COPD: reduces dyspnea, improves exercise capacity, improves quality of life, reduces hospitalizations. 6MWT + SGRQ (St George's Respiratory Questionnaire) are standard outcome measures. MCID for 6MWT in COPD: 30 meters.

SNF / Acute Care COPD Pearls

Check O2 prescription before mobilizing — confirm flow rate and delivery method. Portable O2 for ambulation. Document resting and exertional SpO2 every visit. Ensure patient uses PLB during transfers. Coordinate with respiratory therapy. Any rapid decline in SpO2 or increased work of breathing → stop, position upright, notify nursing immediately.

Osteoporosis & Bone Health
One in two women and one in four men over 50 will have an osteoporosis-related fracture. In SNF, essentially every older adult is at risk. The PT's role is fracture prevention, safe exercise prescription, and recognizing which movements are contraindicated to avoid vertebral compression fractures — which can occur with activities as minor as bending to pick something up.

WHO Fracture Risk Assessment Tool — estimates 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture (hip, wrist, shoulder, vertebra) using clinical risk factors (age, sex, BMI, smoking, alcohol, prior fracture, family history, steroid use, RA, secondary osteoporosis). Available at frax.shef.ac.uk. Guides treatment decisions alongside DXA T-score.

Contraindicated Movements — Vertebral Fracture Risk
High-Risk Movements to AVOID in Osteoporosis

The vertebral body is most vulnerable to compressive + flexion loading and compressive + rotation under load. These loads can fracture an osteoporotic vertebra even with very light forces.

  • Full trunk flexion — toe touches, sit-ups, crunches, forward bending under load
  • Trunk rotation under load — trunk twists with resistance, golf swing, tennis swing
  • Combined flexion + rotation — most dangerous; avoid in all osteoporosis
  • High-impact activities — running, jumping, step aerobics (unless bone density is adequate)
  • Spinal extension — generally safe and therapeutic, but extreme extension under load avoided
  • Coughing/sneezing caution — educate patient to support spine (hand on thigh, slightly extended) during forceful cough or sneeze
Safe & Therapeutic Exercise for Osteoporosis
Weight-Bearing = Bone-Loading = Treatment

Bone adapts to mechanical loading (Wolff's Law). Weight-bearing exercise stimulates osteoblast activity. Walking, dancing, stair climbing, heel drops — all provide bone-beneficial loads. Goal: 30 min weight-bearing activity most days.

  • Weight-bearing aerobics: Walking, dancing, low-impact aerobics, Tai Chi
  • Resistance training: Hip extensors, hip abductors, spinal extensors — highest bone-loading benefit; 8–12 reps, 2–3 sets, progressive
  • Balance training: Single-leg stance, tandem stance, perturbation training — primary fall prevention mechanism
  • Spinal extension exercises: Prone back extension, standing posture exercises — reduce fracture risk at thoracic spine; counteract kyphosis
  • Hip extension: Bridges, prone hip extension — protects femoral neck
  • Heel drops: Standing heel raise + drop — heel strike sends vibration proximally to hip; evidence for hip BMD benefit
Medications & SNF/Clinical Considerations
Bisphosphonates (Fosamax, Boniva, Reclast)

Inhibit osteoclast activity → reduce bone resorption → maintain or improve BMD. IV infusion (zoledronic acid/Reclast) can cause flu-like reaction 24–48 hrs post-infusion — avoid vigorous PT during this window. Rare: atypical femoral fracture (lateral thigh pain = flag) and osteonecrosis of jaw (dental procedures — PT not directly affected but document).

Corticosteroids — Bone Loss Alert

Long-term oral corticosteroids (prednisone) = most common cause of secondary osteoporosis. Any patient on chronic steroids (≥5 mg prednisone × 3+ months) = high fracture risk regardless of DXA. Treat as osteoporosis even if T-score is not yet below −2.5.

SNF Fall Prevention — Osteoporosis Priority

In SNF, every fall in an osteoporotic patient is a potential hip or vertebral fracture. Bed alarm, call light within reach, non-skid footwear, adequate lighting, clear pathway to bathroom. Document fall risk assessment (Berg, TUG, or facility tool). Hip protectors in high-risk patients.

It changes biomechanics, exercise capacity, equipment requirements, positioning, joint loading, and patient communication. Metabolic syndrome clusters multiple conditions that amplify cardiovascular and orthopedic risk.

Biomechanical & Joint Loading Effects
  • Each pound of body weight = 4 lbs of knee joint force during gait; 6–8 lbs during stair climbing
  • High BMI accelerates OA progression, especially knee and hip
  • Altered gait kinematics: wider base of support, reduced step length, increased hip/knee valgus
  • Increased lumbar lordosis and anterior pelvic tilt → lumbar spine loading
  • Fat distribution (visceral adiposity) impairs diaphragmatic mechanics → reduced respiratory capacity
  • Lymphedema risk from adipose tissue compression of lymphatics
Exercise Modifications
  • Aquatic therapy: Reduces joint loading 40–90% depending on depth; excellent for severe obesity; maintain 84°F water; address skin hygiene
  • Seated or recumbent exercises: Reduce cardiopulmonary demand from weight-bearing; cycle ergometer; seated aerobics
  • Interval training: Often better tolerated than continuous exercise; shorter high-intensity bouts with rest
  • Start low, go slow: 10 minutes/day is a legitimate starting point for severely deconditioned obese patients — build to 150 min/week
  • Monitor HR and RPE — obese patients often have autonomic changes and higher resting HR; use RPE 12–14 for moderate intensity
  • Skin integrity: Check skin folds every visit — moisture, friction = pressure injury risk in obesity
Communication & Patient-Centered Care
Weight-Neutral Language

Focus on function and health — not weight. "Let's work on building your walking tolerance" not "let's help you lose weight." Obesity is not a character flaw or motivation failure — it has genetic, hormonal, social, and environmental drivers. Stigma from healthcare providers is documented and measurable harm.

Set Goals Around Function

Walking tolerance · Stair climbing · Return to ADLs · Pain reduction · Activity endurance. These are measurable, motivating, and patient-centered — not weight-dependent. Celebrate functional gains regardless of scale changes.

Motivational Interviewing

Use open-ended questions, reflective listening, and affirmations. Avoid prescriptive language. Explore ambivalence. Roll with resistance — do not confront. Elicit the patient's own reasons for change (intrinsic motivation = more durable behavior change).

Oncology & Cancer Rehabilitation
PT plays a critical role across the cancer continuum — prehabilitation, during active treatment, post-treatment recovery, and palliative care. Cancer and its treatments cause fatigue, neuropathy, lymphedema, bone metastasis, deconditioning, and muscle loss. Exercise is now recognized as safe and beneficial during chemotherapy and radiation — it is NOT contraindicated for most cancer patients.
Bone Metastasis — Critical Precautions
⚠ Bone Metastasis = No Resistance to Affected Segment

Bone metastases cause pathological fracture risk. Resistance exercise, aggressive PROM, or even standard transfers can fracture a metastatic bone. Always check imaging + oncology notes before treating. Common sites: thoracic spine, lumbar spine, femur, humerus, pelvis, ribs.

Mirels' Scoring System — Fracture Risk

Score 0–12 based on: site (upper limb/lower limb/peritrochanteric), pain (mild/moderate/functional), lesion nature (blastic/mixed/lytic), and size (<1/3, 1/3–2/3, >2/3 of cortex). Score ≤7 = prophylactic fixation not recommended; Score ≥9 = surgical fixation before weight-bearing. PT should know score and restrict WB accordingly.

Clinical Action

If bone metastasis is present: confirm weight-bearing status with oncologist before any WB or resistance exercise to that limb/segment. Document precautions. Use assistive devices per MD-specified WB restrictions. No manual therapy to metastatic sites.

Cancer-Related Fatigue (CRF)
What CRF Is (and Isn't)

CRF is the most common side effect of cancer and treatment (60–96% prevalence). It is NOT the same as normal fatigue — it is not relieved by rest and is disproportionate to exertion. Causes: anemia, cytokines (inflammatory), hormonal changes, poor sleep, deconditioning, psychological distress, malnutrition.

Exercise Is the #1 Intervention for CRF

Multiple meta-analyses: aerobic exercise reduces CRF during and after treatment. Counterintuitive but true — rest worsens CRF; graded activity improves it. Minimum effective dose: 30 min, 3×/week, moderate intensity (RPE 12–14). Start low; progress based on fatigue levels, not just performance.

Fatigue Assessment

Brief Fatigue Inventory (BFI) or 0–10 fatigue NRS. Ask about: severity, pattern (AM vs PM), relationship to activity, impact on function. Fatigue NRS ≥7 = significant; consider activity modification, sleep hygiene referral, nutritional assessment, psychological support.

Lymphedema — PT Role
  • Disruption of lymph nodes (surgery/radiation) → lymphatic insufficiency → protein-rich fluid accumulation in interstitium
  • Most common: post-mastectomy/axillary dissection (UE) or post-prostatectomy/pelvic node dissection (LE)
  • No BP measurement on affected arm (post-axillary dissection)
  • No venipuncture/IV on affected arm
  • Avoid constriction — no tight clothing, watch bands, or jewelry on affected limb
  • Exercise is SAFE and beneficial — previous restriction from exercise was incorrect; gradual progressive resistance training reduces lymphedema severity
  • Complete Decongestive Therapy (CDT): manual lymph drainage + compression bandaging + remedial exercise + skin care
  • Compression garments for air travel and vigorous exercise
Neutropenia — Infection Precautions
  • Neutrophil count <1,500/mm³ = neutropenia; <500/mm³ = severe (febrile neutropenia risk)
  • Check absolute neutrophil count (ANC) in chart before therapy in active chemotherapy patients
  • ANC <500: strict infection precautions; no gym or pool exercise; treat in room; strict hand hygiene; no fresh flowers or fruit in room
  • ANC 500–1,000: proceed with caution; mask; hand hygiene; avoid crowds
  • Fever ≥38.3°C (101°F) in neutropenic patient = medical emergency — hold PT, notify nursing immediately
  • PT must use proper PPE and be vigilant about hand hygiene — you are the patient's biggest infection risk
Chemotherapy-Induced Peripheral Neuropathy (CIPN)
  • Common agents: taxanes (paclitaxel, docetaxel), platinum compounds (cisplatin, oxaliplatin), vincristine, bortezomib
  • Presentation: stocking-glove paresthesias, numbness, burning, loss of proprioception, balance impairment, fall risk
  • Assess: monofilament (protective sensation), proprioception (Romberg, tandem stance), balance (Berg), gait pattern
  • Balance training is primary PT intervention — reduces fall risk
  • Foot inspection: loss of protective sensation = same wound care vigilance as diabetic neuropathy
  • Adaptive equipment: non-skid footwear, shower chair, grab bars, nightlights — fall prevention priority
  • CIPN may be permanent even after chemo cessation — PT manages residual deficits long-term

Dialysis Scheduling — When to Treat
Post-Dialysis Fatigue — Most Important Timing Issue

Hemodialysis sessions (typically 3–4 hours, 3×/week) leave patients profoundly fatigued for 4–6 hours post-session. Scheduling PT immediately post-dialysis is ineffective and potentially unsafe. Best timing: non-dialysis days or the day after dialysis.

Intradialytic Exercise — Growing Evidence

Exercise performed during the last 1–2 hours of hemodialysis (cycle ergometer at bedside or resistance bands) is increasingly supported. Benefits: improved exercise capacity, reduced fatigue, better dialysis adequacy (Kt/V). May be the most feasible window for ESRD patients. Coordinate with dialysis nurse for permission and monitoring.

Dialysis Access — Fistula/Graft/Catheter Precautions

No BP cuff on fistula arm. No IV or venipuncture on fistula arm. No extreme arm positions that could compromise access. Tunneled catheters (chest): no overhead reaching restrictions per MD orders. Document access location and precautions in PT notes.

Fluid & Electrolyte Considerations
  • Fluid restriction: ESRD patients often restricted to 1–1.5 L/day; during exercise sweating = fluid output — monitor; no excessive hydration encouragement
  • Hyperkalemia (K+ >5.5 mEq/L): risk of cardiac arrhythmia; pre-exercise potassium check for high-risk patients; cardiac monitoring if hyperkalemic
  • Hyperphosphatemia: Causes bone fragility (renal osteodystrophy) → fracture risk; treat like CKD-related osteoporosis
  • Anemia of CKD: ESA (erythropoietin) therapy; low Hgb → reduced O2 delivery → fatigue + dyspnea with minimal exertion; use RPE not HR
  • Uremic neuropathy: Peripheral neuropathy from uremic toxins; same precautions as CIPN/diabetic neuropathy
BP Instability in Renal Patients
  • ESRD patients have exaggerated BP swings — can be hypertensive and hypotensive within same session
  • Pre-dialysis: BP elevated from fluid overload
  • Post-dialysis: BP drops as excess fluid removed → orthostatic hypotension risk
  • Always measure pre/post BP; dangle before standing post-dialysis
  • SBP <90 or symptomatic hypotension → hold PT; elevate legs; notify nursing
  • SBP >180/110 → hold PT; notify nursing
  • Document BP trends — wide swings may indicate need for medication adjustment
Exercise Prescription & SNF Pearls
  • Start low intensity: RPE 11–13 for aerobic; 40–60% 1-RM resistance
  • 10–20 minute sessions initially; build duration before intensity
  • Monitor SpO2 — anemia reduces O2-carrying capacity → lower threshold for supplemental O2
  • Resistance training reduces muscle wasting (uremic myopathy) — prioritize in ESRD
  • Avoid exercise if: SBP >180 or <90; K+ >6.0 mEq/L on recent labs; uncontrolled fluid overload (severe edema, respiratory distress)
  • Coordinate dialysis schedule with nursing/physician for optimal PT timing
  • Renal diet restriction affects energy levels — note meal timing relative to PT session
  • Goal: 150 min/week moderate intensity (same as general population — just requires more time to reach)

T2DM — at what glucose level do you hold therapy and why? What do you do for hypoglycemia?
tap
Hold therapy if fasting glucose >300 mg/dL (or >250 mg/dL with ketosis) — exercise worsens ketoacidosis at this level. Also hold if glucose <70 mg/dL (hypoglycemia). Hypoglycemia protocol: stop therapy immediately → notify nursing → administer 15g fast-acting carbohydrate (OJ, glucose tablets) → recheck in 15 min → do not resume therapy until glucose is stable ≥80 mg/dL. All T2DM patients on insulin or sulfonylureas must carry glucose source during exercise. Document pre-therapy glucose in SNF notes.
What did Taylor et al. (Phys Ther 2009) demonstrate about PT-directed exercise counseling for T2DM?
tap
RCT (n=24): PT-directed exercise counseling + independent fitness center training produced equivalent improvements in muscular strength and exercise capacity vs. directly supervised exercise. Protocol: 80% 1-RM resistance training (chest press/row/leg press, 4×8, 2×/wk) + aerobic training (RPE 12, 20 min treadmill, same days). High adherence in both groups; no adverse events. Key conclusion: dose and adherence are the determining factors — not whether a PT is in the room. PT counseling (5 A's + weekly phone calls) is a valid, cost-effective model. PT-directed exercise is evidence-based for T2DM management.
Beta-blockers — why can't you use target heart rate, and what do you use instead?
tap
Beta-blockers blunt the HR response to exercise — the heart cannot increase rate as expected. Age-predicted max HR formulas (220−age) and target HR ranges are invalid for patients on beta-blockers. The patient may never reach a "target" HR. Use Borg RPE Scale instead: moderate intensity = RPE 12–13 ("somewhat hard"). Also consider Talk Test: patient should be able to speak in short sentences but not comfortably hold a full conversation. Document medication class in PT notes; communicate to supervising PT when adapting exercise prescription.
When do you STOP exercise for a cardiac patient — list 5 absolute criteria.
tap
Stop immediately if: (1) Angina or anginal equivalent (chest pressure, jaw pain, L arm ache); (2) SBP drops >10 mmHg from resting despite increasing workload; (3) SBP >250 or DBP >115 mmHg during exercise; (4) Significant arrhythmia — new irregular rhythm, palpitations with hemodynamic symptoms; (5) Signs of poor perfusion — pallor, cyanosis, cold/clammy, mottling. Also stop for: severe dyspnea disproportionate to effort, neurological symptoms (confusion, syncope, ataxia), or patient request. Document, notify nursing/MD, record VS and time.
Hypertensive urgency vs. emergency — definition and PT action for each.
tap
Hypertensive urgency: SBP ≥180 and/or DBP ≥110 mmHg WITHOUT symptoms of end-organ damage. Action: hold therapy, do not exercise, notify nursing/MD immediately, document and recheck in 15–30 min. Hypertensive emergency: SBP ≥180 + symptoms of end-organ damage (severe headache, chest pain, SOB, visual changes, neurological symptoms, nausea). Action: stop all activity, call emergency services (911 or code team), stay with patient, keep patient calm and seated/supine, document time and vitals. Emergency requires immediate medical intervention — BP must be reduced under controlled medical supervision.
COPD — SpO2 threshold for stopping exercise and what you do if SpO2 drops below it.
tap
Stop exercise if SpO2 drops below 88%. Action: stop activity immediately → sit patient upright (tripod position or forward lean) → apply supplemental O2 per physician order → instruct pursed-lip breathing → monitor SpO2 continuously until recovery to ≥90% → document lowest SpO2 reached, activity at time of drop, recovery time. If O2 available and ordered: 2–4 L via nasal cannula. If no O2 order and SpO2 <88% persists: notify nursing/MD urgently. In SNF: all COPD patients should have O2 order reviewed before initiating ambulation. Document resting and exertional SpO2 every session.
Osteoporosis — 3 movements contraindicated and why they fracture the vertebral body.
tap
The osteoporotic vertebral body is most vulnerable to compressive + flexion loading and compressive + rotational loading. Three contraindicated movements: (1) Full trunk flexion — toe touches, sit-ups, forward bending under load; (2) Trunk rotation under load — weighted twists, golf swing, tennis; (3) Combined flexion + rotation — most dangerous; avoidance is absolute. Also avoid high-impact activities in severe osteoporosis. Safe alternatives: spinal extension (therapeutic), weight-bearing (bone-loading), balance training (fall prevention), hip extension (femoral neck protection). Teach coughing/sneezing technique: support spine in slight extension with hand on thigh.
Cancer patient with bone metastasis to femur — what does PT need to know before treating?
tap
Bone metastasis = pathological fracture risk — standard transfers, PROM, or resistance exercise can fracture a metastatic bone. Before treating: (1) Confirm WB status with oncologist; (2) Review imaging for metastatic sites and Mirels' score if available; (3) Mirels' score ≥9 = surgical fixation required before WB — do NOT WB until cleared; (4) No resistance exercise to the affected segment; (5) No manual therapy to metastatic sites; (6) Use assistive device per MD-specified WB restrictions; (7) Document precautions explicitly in PT notes. Most common mets: thoracic/lumbar spine, femur, humerus, pelvis, ribs. When in doubt: call the oncologist before initiating WB.
Dialysis patient — when is the worst time to schedule PT and why? What is the best timing?
tap
Worst time: immediately post-hemodialysis. A 3–4 hour dialysis session causes profound fatigue lasting 4–6 hours post-session — patients are depleted, hemodynamically unstable, and cannot perform meaningful therapy. Best timing: non-dialysis days (patients on MWF or TThSa schedules; schedule PT on off days). Alternative: intradialytic exercise during the last 1–2 hours of dialysis (cycle ergometer, resistance bands at bedside) — evidence is growing that this is the most feasible window. In SNF: coordinate with nursing to identify dialysis schedule; document in plan of care. Also avoid first thing in the AM after dialysis day — orthostatic hypotension risk from volume shifts.
5 A's of exercise counseling — name each and describe the PT's role in each step.
tap
(1) Address the Agenda — set collaborative focus for the session; align exercise goals with patient's priorities. (2) Assess — current activity level, readiness to change (Prochaska stage), barriers, medical contraindications, social support. (3) Advise — evidence-based recommendation: specific type/intensity/frequency/duration + safety guidelines; explain benefits relevant to patient's condition. (4) Assist — overcome barriers; review the exercise program; equipment orientation; provide written program; problem-solve schedule conflicts. (5) Arrange Follow-Up — scheduled face-to-face + weekly phone calls (~10 min); monitor adherence; troubleshoot; adjust program. Tailor to Prochaska stage — Precontemplator needs motivational conversation, not a handout.
Orthostatic hypotension — definition, highest-risk patients, and PT management protocol.
tap
Definition: Drop of ≥20 mmHg SBP or ≥10 mmHg DBP within 3 minutes of standing. Symptoms: dizziness, lightheadedness, syncope risk. Highest-risk patients: alpha-blocker or diuretic medications, post-dialysis, morning (post-overnight recumbence), post-meal, prolonged bed rest, autonomic neuropathy (T2DM, Parkinson's), volume depletion. PT protocol: Always dangle 3–5 min before standing; check orthostatic vitals (supine → sit → stand with 1–2 min between); if positive → sit/lie immediately, elevate legs, monitor until BP normalizes, document, notify MD; reinforce fall precautions; consider compression stockings or abdominal binder (per MD); ensure adequate hydration if not restricted.
Cancer-related fatigue — what is it, what's the #1 PT intervention, and how do you dose it?
tap
CRF is the most common side effect of cancer and treatment (60–96% prevalence). It is NOT relieved by rest — that distinguishes it from normal fatigue. Caused by inflammatory cytokines, anemia, hormonal changes, deconditioning, and psychological distress. #1 PT intervention: aerobic exercise — multiple meta-analyses confirm exercise reduces CRF during and after treatment (counterintuitive: rest worsens CRF). Minimum effective dose: 30 min, 3×/week, moderate intensity (RPE 12–14). Start at patient's current capacity — even 10 min is valid. Progress duration before intensity. CRF NRS ≥7 = significant; adapt session (shorter, lower intensity) rather than canceling — movement helps.
