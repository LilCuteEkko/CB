Hip Joint Anatomy
The hip is a ball-and-socket (triaxial diarthrodial) joint — one of the most stable joints in the body, built for both mobility and load transfer between the trunk and lower extremity.
Acetabulum

Formed by the fusion of three bones at the triradiate cartilage:

  • Ilium — superior 2/5
  • Ischium — posterior–inferior 2/5
  • Pubis — anterior 1/5
Articular Surface

Horseshoe-shaped lunate surface — the central non-articular acetabular fossa contains fat pad and ligamentum teres attachment.

Femur — Proximal

Key landmarks for attachment and orientation:

  • Head — 2/3 sphere; fovea = ligamentum teres origin
  • Neck — connects head to shaft
  • Greater Trochanter — glutes, short ER attachment
  • Lesser Trochanter — iliopsoas insertion
  • Intertrochanteric line/crest — capsule attachment
  • Trochanteric fossa — piriformis
  • Gluteal tuberosity — glute max
Capsule & Labrum
  • Acetabular labrum — fibrocartilaginous rim; deepens socket by ~20%; increases contact area and creates negative intra-articular pressure for stability
  • Transverse acetabular ligament — bridges inferior acetabular notch; converts notch to foramen
  • Joint capsule — attaches to acetabular rim proximally; intertrochanteric line (anterior) and neck (posterior) distally
  • Zona orbicularis — circular fibers forming collar around femoral neck
  • Synovial membrane — lines capsule; wraps ligamentum teres in synovial sleeve
Intracapsular Structures
  • Ligamentum teres (ligament of head of femur) — from fovea to acetabular notch; transmits acetabular branch of obturator artery; relaxed in adduction; of little functional importance in adults
  • Acetabular fat pad — fills acetabular fossa; covered by synovial membrane; cushions contact
  • Obturator artery (acetabular branch) — minor blood supply to femoral head in adults
Clinical Note

Primary femoral head blood supply = medial circumflex femoral artery (branch of profunda femoris). This is why femoral neck fractures risk AVN.

Capsular Ligaments
Three extracapsular ligaments reinforce the hip — they wind around the joint and tighten in extension, creating the closed-packed position.

Clinical Pearl — THA Posterior Approach

Posterior hip approach cuts through ischiofemoral fibers and posterior structures → increased dislocation risk with flexion + IR + adduction. Post-op precautions exist to protect this.

Bursae
Four clinically relevant bursae around the hip — inflamed bursae are frequently misdiagnosed.
Trochanteric

Between glute max and greater trochanter. Lateral hip pain. Often co-presents with gluteal tendinopathy.

Iliopsoas (Iliopectineal)

Between iliopsoas tendon and anterior capsule. Anterior groin snapping. Largest bursa in the body; can communicate with joint.

Gluteus Medius

Between glute med tendon and greater trochanter. Lateral hip pain — distinguished from trochanteric bursitis by palpation point.

Ischiogluteal

Between glute max and ischial tuberosity. "Weaver's bottom" — pain with prolonged sitting.

2. Angle of Torsion (Femoral Version)
Measured in the transverse plane — angle between femoral neck axis and femoral condyle axis. Normal adult: 8–15° anteversion.
Anteversion (>15°)

Femoral head is rotated anteriorly relative to condyles. Femoral neck points forward of the condylar axis.

Clinical Presentation

Toe-in gait (pigeon-toed). Compensatory tibial torsion. Medial femoral torsion. Child will often W-sit. Decreased hip ER.

Associated Chain Effects

↑ anteversion → internal tibial torsion → in-toeing. Compensatory pronation at foot.

Retroversion (<8°)

Femoral head is rotated posteriorly. Condylar axis is medial to femoral neck axis.

Clinical Presentation

Toe-out gait (Charlie Chaplin). Increased hip ER ROM. Decreased hip IR. External tibial torsion → out-toeing.

Craig's Test — Assessing Femoral Version

Patient prone, knee flexed to 90°. Palpate greater trochanter and passively rotate hip IR/ER to find most lateral GT position. Measure angle of hip rotation = femoral anteversion angle.

Normal: 15–25° · >25° = anteversion · <15° = retroversion

Acetabular Orientation
The acetabulum faces anterolaterally and slightly inferiorly. Two key angular measures:
Anteversion (~20°)

Acetabulum opens anteriorly. Combined with femoral anteversion = cumulative influence on version-related pathology. If both antevert excessively → increased FAI and instability risk.

Abduction (~45°)

Acetabulum tilts laterally. Combined with neck–shaft angle determines congruence. Altered in developmental dysplasia (DDH) — shallower, more vertical acetabulum.

Hip Arthrokinematics
Convex femoral head moves on the concave acetabulum (OKC) OR the concave acetabulum moves on the fixed convex femoral head (CKC). In OKC, the head glides opposite to the direction of distal femoral motion.
Rule Reminder

OKC (femur on acetabulum): Convex on concave → roll and slide in OPPOSITE directions. | CKC (acetabulum on femur): Concave on convex → roll and slide in SAME direction.

Lumbopelvic Rhythm in Forward Bending

When bending forward, the first ~40–60° comes from hip flexion (anterior pelvic tilt). The remaining motion comes from lumbar flexion. On return, the reverse occurs — hip extensors initiate, then lumbar extensors complete. Disruption of this rhythm is a major source of low back pain.

In single limb stance (left standing leg example):
R Hip Hike: Spine → ipsilateral (R) lateral flexion
R Hip Drop: Spine → contralateral (L) lateral flexion
The standing leg hip is always the reference — it's a closed chain on the left side when left foot is planted.

Hip Kinetics & Stability
The hip transmits enormous forces — up to 3× body weight in walking, 5× in running. Both static (ligamentous) and dynamic (muscular) stabilizers are essential.
Trabecular Systems
Bone architecture in the femoral head and neck is organized into specific trabecular systems aligned with stress forces. Understanding these explains fracture patterns.
Medial (Compressive) System

Arcs from medial femoral cortex through femoral head. Resists compressive loads from body weight. Strongest system — last to resorb in osteoporosis.

Lateral (Tensile) System

Arcs from greater trochanter through femoral head. Resists tensile forces from abductor muscles pulling laterally. Crosses medial system at femoral head.

Zone of Weakness (Ward's Triangle)

The area between medial and lateral systems contains sparse trabeculae — the "zone of weakness". This is the anatomical basis for femoral neck stress fractures and osteoporotic fractures in the elderly (intertrochanteric + femoral neck Fx patterns).

Joint Reaction Force (JRF)
The resultant compressive force across the hip joint is the sum of body weight and muscle forces. The abductors dominate this calculation.
ActivityApproximate JRF (× BW)
Two-leg stance~0.3× BW each hip
Walking (mid-stance)~3× BW
Running~5× BW
Stair climbing~3.5× BW
Walking with contralateral cane~2× BW (reduced)
Why a Cane on the Opposite Side?

The cane on the contralateral side provides a moment about the hip center that reduces the demand on ipsilateral abductors. Less abductor force required → lower JRF. Ipsilateral cane would not provide this mechanical advantage.

Static Stabilizers (Capsule + Ligaments)
Anterior Capsule

Thick anterosuperior — reinforced by iliofemoral and pubofemoral ligaments. Resists anterior dislocation effectively. Strongest region of capsule.

Posterior Capsule

Loose posteroinferior — reinforced only by ischiofemoral ligament. Weakest region. Explains why posterior hip dislocation (most common type) is the clinical concern.

Dynamic Stabilizers — Principles
Optimal Length-Tension

Muscles produce maximum force at optimal length. Two-joint muscles can be at active insufficiency (too short) or passive insufficiency (too long) depending on position of both joints they cross.

Inversion of Muscle Action

In CKC (standing), proximal muscles that normally move the femur now move the pelvis on the fixed femur. Example: glute max that extends hip now tips pelvis posteriorly.

Trendelenburg Sign — Clinical Application

During single limb stance, ipsilateral abductors (glute med/min) must generate enough force to prevent the contralateral pelvis from dropping. Weakness → pelvis drops to contralateral side (positive Trendelenburg). The body may compensate by lateral trunk lean toward the stance limb (antalgic Trendelenburg / "gluteus medius lurch") to reduce abductor demand by shifting CoM closer to hip axis.

Flexibility Assessment Positions
Position the joint to create or eliminate passive insufficiency of the target muscle.
Hip Flexors

Thomas Test: supine, pull uninvolved knee to chest. Observe opposite hip position. Thigh off table = hip flexor tightness. Knee straightens = RF tightness.

Hamstrings

SLR (hip flex, knee extended) = passive insufficiency position. Measure angle at 90° hip where knee is fully extended = hamstring length.

Quadriceps (Rectus Femoris)

Ely's test: prone, passively flex knee. Normal: heel to buttock. Positive: ipsilateral hip flexes = RF tightness. Confirms passive insufficiency with hip ext + knee flex.

Hip Joint Mobilizations
Mobilization direction is chosen based on the restriction being treated. Rule: glide in the direction of restricted motion when applying Grade III–IV mobs. Always start in open-packed position (30° flex/ABD/slight ER) unless treating specific motion.
Selecting Mobilization Direction (from Arthrokinematics)

To increase a motion, mobilize in the direction of the restricted glide. E.g., to improve flexion → posterior glide (since posterior glide = restricted in extension loss and occurs naturally in flexion).

Mobilizing Femur on Acetabulum (OKC)
Long-Axis Distraction
Promotes: Pain modulation (Grades I–III), general mobility. Patient supine; stabilize pelvis; apply distraction along femoral neck axis. Useful as treatment entry technique.
Posterior Glide
Promotes: Flexion, IR, Adduction. Mob at 90° flexion: posterior force on proximal femur (glide femoral head posteriorly). Hooklying position: stabilize pelvis, posterior glide. 90/90 position: sit on edge, stabilize proximal tibia, apply posterior force.
Inferior Glide
Promotes: Abduction, IR at 90° flexion. At neutral: inferior traction on femur while in ABD. Increases abduction ROM.
Anterior Glide
Promotes: Extension, ER, Abduction. Figure-4 position (sidelying): applies anterior glide to posterior capsule. Sidelying with anterior force: extend hip off table to engage anterior glide direction.
Lateral Distraction
Promotes: General pain modulation, improves all motions. Strap to proximal thigh; distract laterally. Effective as neurophysiological pain modulator Grade I–II.

90° Flexion Position Changes Everything

At 90° hip flexion, the acetabular orientation relative to the femoral head shifts. ABD now requires an anterior glide (vs. inferior at neutral). IR requires inferior glide (vs. posterior at neutral). Always consider position when selecting mob direction.

Clinical Case Studies
Six cases spanning the lifespan — DDH, FAI, OA, instability, THA, and gluteal tendinopathy. Apply anatomy and kinesiology to real clinical presentations.
Case 1 · Pediatric

Eloise — Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)

  • Initial CE angle: 16.5° (below normal for age) → Current: 18.0°
  • Barlow maneuver / Ortolani test — assess for dislocation/reducibility
  • Low CE angle = insufficient acetabular coverage
  • Management may include Pavlik harness (abduction positioning)
Key Concept

CE angle <20° in a child = concern for dysplasia. Femoral head not adequately covered → labrum bears excess stress → early OA risk if untreated.

Case 2 · Young Adult

Tim — Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI)

  • 35 y/o male truck driver: anterior/lateral hip pain with ice hockey + squatting
  • C sign (cupping lateral hip with hand) — classic FAI complaint pattern
  • Initially misdiagnosed as hip flexor strain; no MOI
  • Dull ache with sharp groin pain — suggests labral involvement
  • Cam vs. Pincer vs. Combined type — imaging required
Cam FAI

Bony bump on femoral head-neck junction. During flexion, cam lesion jams into acetabulum → labral shear.

Pincer FAI

Over-coverage of acetabular rim (high CE angle). Labrum pinched between rim and femoral neck in flexion.

Case 3 · Middle-Aged Adult

Harold — Hip Osteoarthritis

  • 68 y/o male: insidious right hip pain
  • Morning stiffness worse than pain with movement (OA pattern — pain worse with activity)
  • Difficulty with ADLs requiring hip ER/flexion: shoes, pants
  • Capsular pattern: IR > ABD > Flexion restriction
  • Clinical Prediction Rule for Hip OA: Pain in groin, pain with hip IR, ↓ hip IR ROM, ≥50 y/o, pain with squatting
Radiographic Findings

Joint space narrowing (superior weightbearing region). Osteophytes. Subchondral sclerosis. Cysts. Compare to contralateral side.

Case 4 · Pediatric/Adolescent

Maddie — Hip Hypermobility & Instability

  • 13 y/o competitive gymnast
  • Pain with split leaps + deep squats bilaterally
  • Decreased hip flexion/extension ROM — despite hypermobility!
  • Generalized hypermobility throughout body
  • Recent increase in competitive level (load spike)
  • Gluteal weakness — key finding
Paradox — Restricted ROM in Hypermobile Patient

Muscle guarding and protective spasm in response to instability. The capsule/ligaments aren't providing adequate passive stability → muscles contract to protect → feel "tight" but are actually guarding. Treat with neuromuscular control + strength, NOT stretching.

Case 5 · Post-Surgical

Karl — Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) — Posterior Approach

  • 72 y/o male, 6'5" / 280 lbs; femoral neck fracture → emergency THA
  • Lateral + posterior hip pain with walking; unable to perform single leg balance
  • Median nerve damage from prolonged assistive device use (carpal tunnel pattern)
  • Posterior approach: ischiofemoral lig + posterior capsule cut + short ERs detached
Posterior THA Precautions (Standard)

No hip flexion >90°, no IR, no ADD past midline (in flexion). These positions recreate the dislocation mechanism: flexion + IR + ADD → posteriorly directed force past now-weakened posterior capsule.

Rehab Priority

Glute med strengthening → restore single limb balance (Trendelenburg correction). Ipsilateral abductor weakness = primary functional deficit post-THA.

Case 6 · Lateral Hip Pain

Carol — Gluteal Tendinopathy (not bursitis)

  • 57 y/o female: lateral hip pain; physician Dx: trochanteric bursitis
  • Significant valgus collapse with step-up and squat
  • Decreased hip extension, abduction, ER strength (MMT)
  • Diagnosis refined: likely gluteal tendinopathy (glute med/min insertional)
Bursitis vs. Gluteal Tendinopathy

Greater trochanteric pain syndrome (GTPS) often involves gluteus medius/minimus tendinopathy rather than isolated bursitis. MRI gold standard. Bursitis diagnosed from MD ≠ PT working diagnosis. Compressive loads (hip ADD crossing midline in standing/sleeping) perpetuate tendinopathy.

PT Approach

Load management (reduce compressive forces), progressive tendon loading, glute med/max strengthening, movement retraining (correct valgus collapse).

Key Mnemonics — Hip
NAVeL
Nerve · Artery · Vein · empty · Lymphatics — femoral triangle contents (lateral → medial)
What is the normal angle of inclination (neck–shaft angle) in adults?
tap
125°. <125° = coxa vara. >125° = coxa valga. Born at ~150° → decreases with weight-bearing.
What is the normal femoral anteversion angle in adults?
tap
8–15°. >15° = anteversion (toe-in). <8° = retroversion (toe-out). Tested with Craig's Test.
Name the three capsular ligaments of the hip and their primary restrictions.
tap
Iliofemoral (strongest, resists extension+ER). Pubofemoral (resists ABD+extension). Ischiofemoral (resists extension+IR; posterior — weakest).
What is the closed-packed position of the hip and why?
tap
Full extension + IR + ABD. All ligaments maximally taut, maximum bony congruence. Used for standing. Opposite = open packed (30°/30° flex/ABD + slight ER).
What is the capsular pattern of hip restriction?
tap
IR > ABD > Flexion — greatest IR loss, then ABD, then flexion. Classic in hip OA.
In OKC neutral, what is the arthrokinematic pattern for hip flexion?
tap
Anterior roll / Posterior glide. Convex femoral head on concave acetabulum → opposite directions.
How do arthrokinematics change for hip ABD when the hip is at 90° flexion vs. neutral?
tap
Neutral ABD = Superior roll / Inferior glide. At 90° flexion ABD = Posterior roll / Anterior glide. Acetabular orientation shifts with hip position.
What happens at each hip during an anterior pelvic tilt (CKC)?
tap
Hip flexion — acetabulum rolls and glides anteriorly on fixed femoral head (concave on convex = same direction). Lumbar spine extends.
During right pelvic shift in bilateral stance, what occurs at each hip?
tap
Right hip: adduction (inferior roll + inferior glide). Left hip: abduction (superior roll + superior glide). Spine: ipsilateral (R) lateral flexion.
Explain the Trendelenburg sign and the compensatory trunk lean strategy.
tap
Positive Trendelenburg = contralateral pelvic drop during SLS → ipsilateral glute med weakness. Compensatory lateral trunk lean toward stance limb = gluteus medius lurch — shifts CoM toward hip to reduce abductor demand.
Why does a cane go in the CONTRALATERAL hand for hip pain?
tap
Creates a moment arm that reduces demand on ipsilateral hip abductors. Less abductor force needed → lower joint reaction force (JRF ~3× → ~2× BW). Ipsilateral cane does not provide this mechanical advantage.
What is the Zone of Weakness in the proximal femur and why does it matter?
tap
Ward's Triangle — sparse trabeculae between medial compressive and lateral tensile systems. First area to lose bone density in osteoporosis → site of femoral neck / intertrochanteric fractures.
For hip mobilization to improve hip IR, in which direction do you glide?
tap
Posterior glide (neutral hip position). Because OKC IR = anterior roll/posterior glide — if IR is restricted, the posterior glide is limited → mobilize posteriorly.
Why are THA posterior approach precautions flexion <90°, no ADD, no IR?
tap
Posterior approach cuts ischiofemoral ligament + posterior capsule + short ERs. These positions (flex+ADD+IR) recreate posterior dislocation mechanism against now-compromised posterior restraints.
What is coxa vara and what are its functional effects?
tap
Neck-shaft angle <125°. Shortened limb. Positive: ↑ abductor moment arm, ↑ joint congruency. Negative: ↑ shear on femoral neck (fracture risk), ↑ ACF on femoral head.
Contents of the femoral triangle from lateral to medial?
tap
NAVeL — Nerve, Artery, Vein, empty space, Lymphatics. Boundaries: inguinal ligament (sup), sartorius (lat), adductor longus (med).
