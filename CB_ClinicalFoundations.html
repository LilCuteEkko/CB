Infection Control
Understanding how infections spread — and how to stop them — is a foundational clinical skill. HAIs are preventable. Every clinician is both a potential source and a potential victim of healthcare-associated infection.

Standard Precautions
Applied to ALL patients, ALL the time — regardless of diagnosis. Based on the principle that all blood, body fluids, secretions, excretions, non-intact skin, and mucous membranes may contain transmissible agents.
5 Components of Standard Precautions

(1)Hand hygiene(2)Personal protective equipment (PPE) — gloves, gowns, masks (3)Respiratory hygiene / cough etiquette(4)Safe handling of contaminated equipment and surfaces (5)Safe injection practices

Hand Hygiene
Hand Rubbing (Preferred when not visibly soiled)

Alcohol-based product: 60–95% alcohol — most effective when washing not required May also use antimicrobial/antiseptic wipe Exception: C. diff and norovirus — alcohol does NOT kill spores. Use soap and water.

Hand Washing (When contaminated or visibly soiled)

Vigorous rubbing all surfaces of lathered hands for 15–30 seconds Rinse under running water from forearms toward fingertips Dry with clean paper towel; use towel to turn off faucet

Hand Hygiene Criteria — Jewelry & Nails

Remove watches and jewelry (especially rings with high settings) · Keep fingernails shorter than fingertip length · No artificial nails · No nail polish. These are requirements, not suggestions — you will be tested on this in practicals.

When to Perform Hand Hygiene

Before AND after patient contact Before AND after contact with wounds, dressings, specimens, linen After contact with secretions or excretions When hands are soiled or visibly dirty

Before and after toileting After sneezing, coughing, or blowing nose After removing gloves (wearing gloves does NOT replace hand hygiene) Before and after eating

C. difficile — Clinician Alert
Critical Facts

94% of C. diff infections are connected to receiving medical care. Linked to ~14,000 deaths/year in the US. Hospital stays tripled in the last decade. Deaths increased 400% between 2000–2007. Risk highest in patients on antibiotics (good protective bacteria destroyed for months).

Hand Sanitizer Does NOT Kill C. diff

Alcohol-based hand sanitizer is ineffective against C. diff spores. Use soap and water. Room surfaces require bleach or EPA-approved spore-killing disinfectant — not standard disinfectant wipes.

6 Steps for Clinicians (CDC)

(1) Prescribe antibiotics carefully (50% given unnecessarily)
(2) Test for C. diff when patient has diarrhea on or within months of antibiotics (3) Isolate C. diff patients immediately(4) Wear gloves AND gown — even during short visits (5) Clean surfaces with bleach or EPA-approved spore-killing disinfectant (6) Notify new facility when patient with C. diff transfers

C. difficile — Special Precautions
Critical SNF/PT Alert — Alcohol Does NOT Kill C. diff

Alcohol-based hand sanitizer does NOT kill C. difficile spores. Soap and water hand washing is required after caring for C. diff patients. Room surfaces must be cleaned with bleach or an EPA-approved spore-killing disinfectant — standard disinfectants are ineffective. C. diff spores survive on surfaces for months.

Scope of the Problem

Linked to 14,000 deaths in the US each year Hospital stays from C. diff tripled over the past decade 94% of infections are connected to receiving medical care 75% first show in nursing home patients or recently-treated outpatients — directly relevant to SNF PT Most dangerous spread source: patients with active diarrhea Patients can arrive with C. diff and spread it within a new facility — notification on transfer is critical

(1) Use antibiotics carefully — ~50% are unnecessary; each course increases risk (2) Test for C. diff when patients have diarrhea while on or within months of antibiotics (3) Isolate patients with C. diffimmediately(4) Wear gloves AND gowns even during short visits (5) Clean room surfaces with bleach or EPA-approved spore-killing disinfectant(6) When patient transfers, notify receiving facility of C. diff status

PPE — Donning & Doffing Sequences
Sequence matters because it determines what is contaminated and when. The fundamental rule: contaminated surfaces must never touch clean skin, mucous membranes, or clothing. Remove all PPE before exiting the patient room, except a respirator (remove after exiting and closing the door).
Donning PPE — Putting On
Always perform hand hygiene FIRST. Sequence: clean → most contaminated.
0
Hand Hygiene

Perform hand hygiene prior to placing any PPE. Start clean.

1
Gown

Fully cover torso from neck to knees, arms to end of wrists, wrap around the back. Fasten at back of neck and waist.

2
Cap (if required)

Cover all hair — front and back of head.

3
Mask or Respirator

Secure ties/elastic bands at middle of head and neck. Fit flexible band to nose bridge. Fit snug to face and below chin. Fit-check respirator (inhale sharply — should collapse inward).

4
Goggles or Face Shield

Place over face and eyes and adjust to fit. Face shield sits over forehead, rests on nose.

5
Gloves

Don last. Extend gloves to cover the wrist of the isolation gown. Gloves go over the gown cuffs.

Safe Work Practices While Wearing PPE

Keep hands away from face · Limit surfaces touched · Change gloves when torn or heavily contaminated · Perform hand hygiene between tasks as needed

Doffing PPE — Taking Off
Gown front, sleeves, and outside of gloves are contaminated. Remove without touching your face, skin, or clean clothing. Contaminated → inside out → waste container.
1
Gloves — Outside is Contaminated!

Grasp outside of one glove with opposite gloved hand and peel off. Hold removed glove in gloved hand. Slide fingers of bare hand under remaining glove at wrist — peel off over first glove. Discard in waste container.

2
Goggles or Face Shield — Outside is Contaminated!

Handle by head band or ear pieces only — never touch the front. Remove from behind. Place in waste container or designated receptacle for reprocessing.

3
Gown — Front and Sleeves are Contaminated!

Grasp gown in front and pull away from body so ties break. Touch inside of gown only. Turn inside out, fold or roll into a bundle. Discard. (Alt: while removing gown, simultaneously peel off gloves — fold gown inside out around gloves.)

4
Mask or Respirator — Front is Contaminated! DO NOT TOUCH!

Grasp bottom ties/elastics first, then top ties. Remove without touching the front. Discard in waste container. If respirator: remove after leaving the patient room and closing the door.

5
Hand Hygiene — Immediately After

Wash hands or use alcohol-based hand sanitizer immediately after removing all PPE. Perform hand hygiene between steps if hands become contaminated.

Critical Rule

If your hands become contaminated at ANY step during doffing — immediately wash hands or use hand sanitizer before continuing. Perform hand hygiene between steps if contamination occurs, and immediately after removing all PPE.

Transmission-Based Isolation Precautions
Standard precautions are the baseline for all patients. Transmission-based precautions are added ON TOP of standard precautions for specific pathogens. Category designations can be combined (e.g., chickenpox = airborne + contact).
Isolation Disadvantages — Clinical Awareness

Isolation adds cost to hospitalization. Forced solitude deprives patients of social interaction and can cause psychological harm. Isolated patients may not be able to access the PT gym — treat in the patient's room. Each facility has its own rules for treating contact-precaution patients outside their room — always check your facility's policy.

Positioning & Draping
Proper positioning prevents pressure injuries, contractures, and deformity while maintaining patient dignity. Patient-centered positioning respects privacy and provides access for treatment — always the minimum exposure necessary for the task.
Goals of Proper Positioning

(1) Make the patient as comfortable as possible (2) Prevent development of deformities and pressure injuries(3) Provide the patient access to the environment(4) Proper positioning for treatment while respecting privacy

General Principles

Body mechanics — protect yourself, protect the patient Lift — do not drag — dragging creates shear forces = skin breakdown Attend to bony prominences — never rest weight directly on them Maintain modesty — expose only the area necessary for intervention

Pressure Relief
Primary Objectives

Disperse pressure over the greatest surface area possible Provide comfort Allow access for care

Specific Techniques

No direct contact on bony prominences — support the area around them · Eliminate sheet wrinkles · Frequent position changes · Use trapeze or linen for transfers to reduce drag/shear · Avoid positions that extend beyond the support surface

Pressure-Dispersing Surfaces & Items

Water flotation mattress — distributes pressure across fluid surface Gel pads — contours to body shape Dynamic air bed — alternating pressure cells cycle automatically Positioning pillows: under legs to elevate heels · between ankles · between knees · behind back · under head · under arm (sidelying)
Limb elevators — heel off-loading devices

Bridging the Gap
What It Is

A technique for relieving pressure around a sensitive area (wound, open area, fragile tissue). Instead of placing padding directly under the area, you support the limb segments proximal and distal to the sensitive area — creating a "bridge" that suspends the problem area off the surface.

Clinical Considerations

Requires more frequent position changes than standard positioning — the suspended area has even less blood flow if the bridge collapses Inspect the area frequently Bridge may need to be rebuilt with each repositioning Consider specialized devices (heel elevators, foam boots) for prolonged situations

Footboards
Purpose

Keep sheets and blankets from pushing the foot into a plantarflexed position. The weight of bedding over the feet is enough to cause PF contracture over time in non-ambulatory patients.

Risk

If the foot is constantly pressed against the footboard, it can cause a pressure injury at the heel or dorsal foot. The footboard prevents contracture from below but can create pressure from above. Monitor regularly.

Skin Changes with Aging — Why This Matters
↓ Thickness

Thinner skin tears and breaks down more easily under pressure or friction

↓ Vascularity

Reduced blood supply = slower healing and less tolerance of ischemia

↑ Rigidity

Less pliable skin is less able to distribute stress; more prone to shear injury

↓ Sensation

Decreased neurosensory perception = patient cannot feel early pressure injury warning signs

Pressure Injuries
Pressure injuries (formerly "pressure ulcers") result from unrelieved pressure causing tissue ischemia and necrosis. 95% develop at 5 classic locations. Staging is based on tissue depth involved and visual appearance. Never reverse-stage a healing wound.
Terminology Note (NPUAP 2016)

Renamed from "pressure ulcer" to "pressure injury" — because an injury can occur without a break in skin (e.g., DTPI), but the old term implied skin disruption. The mechanism is unrelieved pressure causing ischemia → tissue damage at the bone-muscle interface.

95% of all pressure injuries develop at 5 locations — all bony prominences. Know these cold.

Staging — NPUAP Classification
Stages reflect tissue depth involved. Do NOT reverse-stage as wounds heal — a healing Stage 4 stays "healing Stage 4," not "Stage 2."
Stage 1
Non-blanchable erythema
Partial thickness: Epidermis only

Red, swollen intact skin. Does NOT return to normal with pressure relief. No skin break.

Stage 2
Partial-thickness skin loss
Partial thickness: Epidermis + Dermis

Abrasion, blister, peeling, or shallow crater. Wound bed viable, pink/red, moist. May be intact or ruptured serum-filled blister.

Stage 3
Full-thickness skin loss
Full thickness: Epi + Dermis + Subcutaneous

Deep crater. Undermining and tunneling may occur. Fascia/muscle/tendon/bone NOT exposed.

Stage 4
Full-thickness tissue loss
Full thickness: + Fascia, Muscle, Bone

Deep crater with underlying tissue destruction. Fascia, muscle, and/or bone visible or palpable. Highest severity.

Unstageable
Depth unknown
Could be Stage 3 or 4

Covered with slough or eschar — cannot determine severity visually. Do NOT remove stable, dry, adherent heel eschar on ischemic limbs.

Deep Tissue (DTPI)
Bone-muscle interface
Maroon/purple discoloration

Intact or non-intact skin with persistent non-blanchable deep red/maroon/purple. Pain and temp change often precede color changes. May evolve rapidly or resolve without tissue loss. Result of intense shear at bone-muscle interface.

Critical Documentation Rule — Never Reverse-Stage

A healing pressure injury retains its original stage classification. Document as "healing Stage 4" — not "Stage 2" or "Stage 1" as it improves. The tissues that were destroyed in a Stage 4 do not regenerate as original tissue; they fill with scar/granulation tissue. The stage reflects the maximum depth reached, not the current appearance.

Bedrest Complications
Bedrest is not benign. The sequelae of immobility affect nearly every organ system. As PTs, early mobilization is one of the most powerful interventions available — it directly reverses or prevents these complications.
Effects of Bedrest

↓ Muscle strength — up to 1–3% per day; faster in older adults ↑ Fatigue — deconditioning causes disproportionate effort for any activity ↓ Maximal stroke volume, cardiac output, O₂ uptake — cardiovascular deconditioning ↓ Muscle mass (atrophy) — disuse atrophy begins within days ↓ Bone density — disuse osteoporosis; mechanical loading required for bone maintenance DVT riskOrthostatic hypotensionPressure injury risk

Why Mobilization is PT's Most Powerful Tool

Every effect of bedrest listed here is either prevented or reversed by appropriate early mobilization. The question isn't "should we mobilize?" — it's "how early and how aggressively can we safely mobilize given this patient's condition?"

PT Role

Early mobilization reduces hospital length of stay, reduces DVT risk, prevents deconditioning, maintains bone density, prevents pressure injuries, maintains ROM and strength, and improves mood and cognitive function.

Pitting Edema — Grading Scale
Pitting edema is assessed by applying firm pressure with a thumb or finger to the swollen area for 5 seconds, then observing the depth of the pit and how quickly it rebounds. Grade based on both pit depth and rebound time. Document bilaterally and track serially.
1+
2 mm

Barely detectable depression. Immediate rebound — pit disappears as soon as pressure is released. Minimal edema; may only be visible on careful inspection.

2+
4 mm

Slight depression. A few seconds to rebound. Mild edema; visible and palpable. Commonly seen with mild CHF, venous insufficiency, or post-injury.

3+
6 mm

Deep depression. 10–12 seconds to rebound. Moderate edema; extremity appears grossly distorted. Skin may be tight, shiny, or weeping. DVT, significant CHF, lymphedema.

4+
8 mm

Very deep pit. >20 seconds to rebound. Severe edema; extremity severely distorted, weeping. Indicates significant systemic pathology — referral required.

Clinical Documentation

Document location (ankle, pretibial, sacral, bilateral), grade (1+–4+), and rebound time Measure limb circumference at consistent landmarks for serial tracking (e.g., 10 cm above/below medial malleolus) Note if edema is pitting vs. non-pitting — non-pitting edema suggests lymphedema, myxedema, or lipedema (not DVT or CHF) Note if unilateral vs. bilateral — unilateral is a DVT red flag; bilateral suggests systemic cause (CHF, hypoalbuminemia, renal/hepatic disease) Sacral edema in bedbound patients = dependent edema from gravity (not always pathological, but document)

Orthostatic Hypotension
Definition & Causes

Definition: ≥20 mmHg drop in systolic BP or ≥10 mmHg drop in diastolic BP within 3 minutes of standing from sitting/supine.

Not a disease — a manifestation of abnormal BP regulation

Normal aspect of aging Medications (antihypertensives, diuretics, alpha-blockers) Venous pooling (pregnancy, CHF) Neurogenic origins (autonomic dysfunction) Prolonged anesthesia Bedrest — most relevant to PT

Symptoms & Evaluation

Symptoms

Lightheadedness / dizziness Syncope (loss of consciousness) Mental or visual blurring Sense of weakness

Clinical Evaluation

BP: lying → sitting → standing (wait 3 minutes at each) Pulse (may show compensatory tachycardia)

PT Management

Dangle before standing. Monitor BP at each level. Progress slowly. Abdominal binders + compression stockings for recurrent cases. Tilt table for severe deconditioning.

Balance Assessment
Before performing any basic mobility activity — supine → sit or sit → stand — you must assess your patient's static and dynamic balance first. This protects both the patient and you. Always use a gait belt until independence is clearly demonstrated.
Non-Negotiable Rule

Assess balance before mobility. Never attempt supine→sit or sit→stand without first confirming the patient can tolerate that level of postural challenge. A patient who cannot maintain static sitting unsupported should NOT be stood up without maximal support in place.

General Assessment Principles
Progression Framework

(1)Gait belt always — until independence is clearly demonstrated (2)Sitting before standing — confirm sitting balance first (3)Static before dynamic — held position before movement (4)Small challenges before large — start close, work further (5)Within BOS before outside BOS — reach inside base first, then challenge it (6) Progression to higher-level testing is based on performance at lower levels AND other movement precautions

Lateral Transfer — With Sliding Board

▼

Indications

(1) Position W/C at slight angle adjacent to bed; lock; remove armrest and legrest on transfer side (2) Lean trunk away from W/C; lift hip enough to slide board under hip nearest W/C (3) Position opposite end of board in front of W/C drive wheel into seat (4) Open hand on board toward W/C, other hand on bed; lift on extended arms; shift hips toward W/C; repeat (5) Lean trunk away from bed; lift other hip to remove board (6) Replace armrest and legrest; adjust sitting position

Clinician Assistance

Guarding

Stand in front; hands at patient shoulders and knees to block forward movement; verbal and tactile cueing throughout.

Lift at hips using: gait belt · under ischial tuberosities · sides of trousers · draw sheet.

Without Board (sufficient UE strength)

Squat-Pivot Transfer

▼

Patient Instructions — 7 Steps

(1) Shift hips forward; lean trunk forward (2) Place hand on outside arm of starting chair (3) Place other hand on far arm of destination surface (4) Partially straighten legs; lift with arms; clear bottom over obstacles (5) Pivot on balls of feet until hips over destination (6) Lower hips controlled; keep trunk flexed forward (7) Adjust position

Setup — Environment

Lock both surfaces · Remove legrests · Clear obstacles · Non-skid footwear on patient

Patient Position

Hips forward in seat (shifts CoM). Feet beneath CoM, as far back as possible, feet flat, shoulder-width. "Nose over toes" cue for trunk flexion.

Knee Blocking

Gravity creates flexion moments; insufficient strength needs counterforce.
One weak knee: PT flexes hip/knee to place just below patient's tibial tuberosity.
Both weak: PT wedges patient knees between own knees (two-to-two: medial aspect of PT knees against anterolateral patient knees).

Clinician Position

In front of patient · Flexed hips/knees · Wide staggered A-P BOS · Underhand lateral grip on belt · Patient hands on PT forearms or hips — NOT around PT's neck. Watch for fear/"white-knuckle grip."

Stand-Pivot Transfer

▼

Patient Instructions — 6 Steps

(1) Place both hands on W/C armrests (2) Push through legs AND arms — stand fully upright (3) Pivot on balls of feet until directly in front of target surface (4) Reach back with each hand to target armrests/surface (5) Flex trunk slightly; lower yourself into chair with control (6) Adjust position

Counting & Communication

"On the count of 3, you are going to stand, turn, and sit." Stand-pivot can be separate movements with pauses as needed. Give each next direction during transfer. Squat-pivot = all one movement — no separating.

Squat vs. Stand Pivot — Differences

Same: foot position, chair position, controlled descent.
Different: Hand placement (stand: push up from behind; squat: one reaching to surface) · Rise (stand: fully erect, separable; squat: partially erect, all one) · Mode of weight shift.

Abort Criteria

If patient is unstable during rise — leaning backward, creating strain — ABORT immediately. Return to sitting. Regroup. Never power through an unsafe transfer.

Sitting the Patient Down

Patient reaches back to armrests · Assists with lowering · Flexes trunk to get hips back in chair · This keeps CoM over BOS and reduces strain on PT.

Differential Diagnosis & Screening
The patient interview is our most important screening tool — it yields 80% of the information needed to determine the cause of symptoms. The PT's role is not to establish a medical diagnosis, but to determine when symptoms are beyond the scope of PT practice and refer appropriately.
Foundational Case — Why This Matters

A patient with 8-week neck pain was referred to PT with imaging showing cervical degenerative changes. Full cervical and upper quarter examination failed to reproduce symptoms. Brief exercise on gym equipment reproduced the pain; rest resolved it. Referred back to MD → cardiovascular testing revealed multi-vessel coronary artery blockage and evidence of infarction → coronary artery bypass graft 4 days later. His "neck pain" was atypical angina. The PT's clinical reasoning saved his life.

Pain Neuroscience
From Gifford (1998), Louw (2016), Wall & Melzack (2005). Understanding pain as a brain output — not a tissue input — is foundational to patient education, fear reduction, and chronic pain management. ~20% of the population in developed countries experience ongoing pain without apparent biological source persisting beyond normal healing. 116 million Americans with chronic pain ($635 billion/year).
False Assumptions About Pain

"Direct link between tissue damage and pain level" — False. Pain does not reliably correlate with tissue damage. Catastrophic injuries can present with minimal pain; minor injuries can present with severe pain.
"All pain is caused by injury" — False. Pain is a brain output based on perceived threat, not a direct readout of tissue damage.
"Increased pain = increased tissue damage" — False. Central sensitization explains ongoing or worsening pain despite tissue healing.
"Pain is either physical OR psychological" — False. Pain is always both — it is a multisystem brain output integrating sensory, cognitive, emotional, and social inputs.
"With chronic pain, tissues are not healing / damage is ongoing" — False. Tissues heal on predictable timelines. Persistent pain beyond healing = central sensitization driving the experience.
"Nociception = pain" — False. Nociceptors are danger fibers, not pain fibers. Nociceptor activation does not automatically produce pain; the brain decides.
"Pain is an input system" — False. Pain is an output system — a protective response generated by the brain based on perceived threat.

Pain — The Correct Definition
IASP / Gifford Definition

Pain is a multiple system output activated by the brain based on perceived threat. The brain receives sensory signals (nociception), processes them alongside memory, context, emotion, past experience, beliefs, and social cues, then generates pain as a protective output when it concludes the body is under threat.

Mature Organism Model (Gifford 1998)

Three components of the pain experience: Inputs (tissue signals, nociception) → Processing (CNS integration: dorsal horn, ascending pathways, brain) → Outputs (pain, autonomic response, motor changes, immune response). All three components are targets for treatment — not just tissue inputs.

Patient Education Goal

Educate patients that tissue injury can be non-threatening. Goal: reduce the patient's perception of fear or threat. Evidence: pain neuroscience education (PNE/TNE) reduces pain catastrophization, fear-avoidance, and disability — and improves participation in active rehabilitation.

CNS Processing — Central Sensitization
Normal Dorsal Horn Processing

Dorsal horn (spinal cord) receives sensory information from A and C fibers. Interneurons (IN) communicate with peripheral fibers, blocking or inhibiting signals to second-order neurons. Second-order neurons carry signals from spinal cord to brain for interpretation.

Central Sensitization Mechanism

Repeated firing of C fibers → death of inhibitory interneurons. Frequent "danger" signals → brain develops a "memory" for those signals → can respond faster with less stimulus over time. Result: hyperalgesia (exaggerated response to painful stimuli) and allodynia (pain with non-painful stimuli). Sensitization = the CNS problem, not the tissue problem.

Clinical Application

When pain persists without ongoing tissue injury → think central sensitization. These patients need pain neuroscience education, graded exposure, and psychological coping strategies — NOT more structural imaging or passive treatments targeting tissue that has already healed.

CNS Output — The Stress Response
Fight-or-Flight Output

When the brain generates a pain output, it simultaneously activates the stress response: large muscle groups activated · respiration increases · digestion slows · reproductive system inhibited · adrenaline + cortisol released · immune changes + chronic inflammation risk.

Chronic Pain Consequences

Persistent stress response from chronic pain drives: chronic inflammation, autonomic dysregulation, HPA axis disruption, sleep disturbance, mood disorders, and immune suppression. Pain is not just a sensory experience — it is a whole-body threat-response system. This is why biopsychosocial treatment models are essential for chronic pain.

Language in MSK Rehabilitation
Stewart & Loftus, JOSPT 2018 (48:7). "Words are, of course, the most powerful drug used by mankind." — Rudyard Kipling. The words clinicians use can significantly impact clinical outcomes. Pathoanatomical language increases patient fear and catastrophization — often unintentionally. Psychological factors are more effective predictors of pain and disability levels than pathoanatomical factors (Chester et al.).
Why Language Matters
Words Contain Both the Ability to Heal and Harm

Like drugs, words change how a person thinks and feels. A term like "degenerative discs" sounds clinical and mild to a clinician — but can be catastrophic to a patient. Single, offhand statements can heavily influence recovery expectations (Biro 2010). Low recovery expectations are strong predictors of poor outcomes.

Attentional Bias in Chronic Pain

Patients in pain naturally seek information confirming their worst fears (Ben's case: "surgery may be indicated" → "I will need surgery"). Words like "may," "possibly," or "perhaps" are easily dismissed — patients selectively attend to the alarming interpretation. Always consider how the patient will interpret the words you choose.

Therapeutic Emplotment (Mattingly)

Frame therapy around what the patient can do and will be able to do — not what they cannot do. Emphasize purpose and meaning in rehabilitation. Help the patient develop a positive narrative around recovery. This is "therapeutic emplotment" — essential for patient engagement and belief in recovery.

PPE doffing sequence — what comes off first and why in this scenario?
tap
Gloves → Goggles/face shield → Gown → Mask/Respirator → Hand hygiene. Gloves off first because outside is contaminated — you need gloved hands to safely remove other equipment. Mask last because the front is contaminated and you grab by ties/elastics. Hand hygiene immediately after ALL PPE removed.
Why can't you use alcohol hand sanitizer with C. difficile?
tap
C. diff produces spores that are not killed by alcohol. Alcohol-based sanitizer is effective against vegetative bacteria but not spores. C. diff requires soap and water for hands and bleach or EPA-approved spore-killing disinfectant for room surfaces. Standard disinfectant wipes are also ineffective.
Identify the 5 routes of transmission? Which is most frequent for HAIs?
tap
Contact · Droplet · Airborne · Vehicle · Vector-borne. Contact transmission is the most frequent route of HAI. Occurs via direct contact (with patient) or indirect contact (equipment, linen, parallel bars, gait belts). MRSA is the classic PT-relevant contact pathogen.
N-95 vs. In this case, surgical mask — when is each required?
tap
Surgical mask: Droplet precautions (flu, COVID-19, pertussis); filters large-particle droplets; don on room entry, remove on exit. N-95 respirator: Airborne precautions (TB, measles, chickenpox, SARS); must be fit-tested; filters ≥95% particles ≥0.3 microns; don BEFORE entering room, remove AFTER exiting and closing door.
Name the 5 most common pressure injury locations.
tap
95% of pressure injuries occur at these 5 sites: (1) Sacral/coccygeal — most common; (2) Greater trochanter — sidelying; (3) Ischial tuberosity — sitting; (4) Heel — supine; (5) Lateral malleolus — sidelying. All are bony prominences with thin overlying tissue.
Pressure injury staging — what tissue layers are involved in each stage in this scenario?
tap
Stage 1: Epidermis intact (non-blanchable erythema). Stage 2: Epidermis + Dermis (partial thickness — blister/abrasion). Stage 3: + Subcutaneous tissue (full thickness — deep crater, no bone/tendon visible). Stage 4: + Fascia/Muscle/Bone (full thickness — all layers destroyed). Unstageable: Covered by eschar/slough.
Why do you never "reverse-stage" a healing pressure injury in this scenario?
tap
Stages reflect the maximum depth of tissue destruction, not current appearance. Healing fills with scar/granulation tissue — not original tissue. A "healing Stage 4" that looks like Stage 2 still has Stage 4 tissue destruction underneath. Document as "healing Stage 4", never "Stage 2" or "Stage 1."
How often should you reposition a sitting patient in this scenario? A supine patient?
tap
Sitting: every 10 minutes minimum — ischial tuberosity pressure in sitting far exceeds supine. Pressure relief (push-up or weight shift) needed frequently. Supine/prone/sidelying: every 2 hours minimum. Initially check skin after 5–10 minutes in any new position. Increase frequency with any additional risk factors.
What is orthostatic hypotension — definition, cause in PT context, and management in this scenario?
tap
Definition: ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic drop within 3 min of standing. In PT context: most often from bedrest → cardiovascular deconditioning → venous pooling on standing. Management: Dangle before standing; monitor BP at each level; abdominal binder; compression stockings; progress slowly; tilt table for severe deconditioning.
Well's Criteria for DVT — what score means "likely" in this scenario?
tap
Score ≥2 = "likely". Score <2 = "unlikely." Key items: active cancer (+1), paralysis/immobilization (+1), bedridden >3 days or recent surgery (+1), entire leg swelling (+1), calf >3 cm larger (+1), previously documented DVT (+1). Alternative diagnosis as likely = −2. Ultrasound is gold standard for confirmation.
What contracture risk does supine positioning pose and how do you prevent it in this scenario?
tap
Primary risk: ankle plantarflexion contracture (sheets push feet down) and hip/knee flexion tendency. Prevention: footboard to keep sheets off feet; pillow under lower legs to elevate heels; avoid prolonged knee flexion under pillows. Monitor footboard to ensure foot not continuously pressed against it (causing heel pressure injury).
What is "bridging the gap" in positioning in this scenario?
tap
A technique to relieve pressure around a sensitive area (wound, fragile tissue) by supporting the limb proximal and distal to the area — creating a bridge that suspends the problem area off the surface. The sensitive area contacts nothing. Requires more frequent position changes than standard positioning. Can be done with pillows, rolled blankets, or specialized devices.

What PPE does each isolation precaution level require in this scenario?
tap
Contact: Gloves + Gown (always). Droplet: Surgical facemask on room entry; add gloves+gown+goggles if spraying expected. Airborne: Fit-tested N-95 (don before entry, remove after exiting); add gloves+gown+goggles if spraying expected. Neutropenic: Mask + hand hygiene; private room; no flowers/raw food.
Name the 5 components of standard precautions?
tap
(1) Hand hygiene · (2) PPE (gloves, gowns, masks, eye protection) · (3) Respiratory hygiene/cough etiquette · (4) Safe handling of contaminated equipment/surfaces · (5) Safe injection practices. Applied to ALL patients, ALL the time. Based on the principle that all body fluids may be infectious.
Balance assessment progression — what are the 5 framework principles in this scenario?
tap
(1) Gait belt until independence demonstrated · (2) Sitting before standing · (3) Static before dynamic · (4) Small challenges before large · (5) Within BOS before outside BOS. Progression at each level is based on performance at the lower level AND movement precautions.
Transfer algorithm — first question and what happens if the individual is NOT alert?
tap
First question: Is your patient awake and alert? If NO → depends on size/weight: Mechanical Lift · 3-person · 2-person · 1-person Lift. A patient who is not alert cannot actively participate in any pivot or sliding transfer safely.
When is a sliding board transfer indicated? What must your patient have?
tap
When patient cannot bear weight in LEs but has good upper extremity strength. Board bridges surfaces and patient scoots across using UE pushing. No LE WB + poor UE → mechanical or multi-person lift instead.
Standing Assisted Pivot vs. In this case, Standing Dependent Pivot — how do they differ?
tap
Standing Assisted Pivot: Patient WBs but needs significant physical assist to complete the pivot (Mod–Max A). Standing Dependent Pivot: Patient WBs passively through legs; therapist controls the rotation (Mod A). Both require the patient to be alert and able to bear weight in LEs.
List the 3 balance strategies and what level of challenge triggers each?
tap
Ankle strategy: small/slow perturbations; distal→proximal activation; quiet standing. Hip strategy: larger/faster perturbations; proximal→distal activation; narrow BOS. Step/Reach strategy: CoM outside BOS; stepping or reaching to recover; largest challenge. The strategy used reflects the patient's balance capability and the size of the perturbation.
Red flag vs. In this case, yellow flag — distinction and what each requires?

Pain worsens with activity, resolves with rest — why is this a red flag in this scenario?
tap
Cardinal pattern of cardiac or vascular etiology. MSK pain improves with gentle activity; this pattern does not. Pain that appears at a consistent activity level and resolves within minutes of rest → suspect angina, vascular claudication, systemic origin. The opening case (neck pain = atypical angina, multi-vessel CAD) is the quintessential example. Refer cardiac.
What spinal levels innervate the heart sympathetically and what referral pattern does this explain in this scenario?
tap
Cardiac sympathetic innervation: T1–T5 via cervical ganglia and cardiac plexus. Explains referral of cardiac pain to left chest, left arm, jaw, neck, shoulder. Liver/gallbladder (T5–T9 right) → right shoulder (phrenic nerve). Kidney (T10–L2) → flank/low back. Bladder/uterus (S2–S4) → sacrum/perineum. True visceral pain is NOT altered by position change or PT treatment.
What is the PT's role in DDx, and what are we NOT doing in this scenario?
tap
We are NOT establishing a medical diagnosis. We ARE determining whether symptoms are beyond PT scope — and referring appropriately. The interview yields ~80% of information needed to determine cause of symptoms. Constitutional symptoms almost always require physician referral. Document all red flags found, actions taken, and referrals made.
Pitting edema grading — pit depth and rebound time for 1+ through 4+ in this scenario?
tap
1+ = 2mm, immediate rebound · 2+ = 4mm, few seconds rebound (mild CHF, venous insufficiency) · 3+ = 6mm, 10–12 seconds rebound (DVT, significant CHF, lymphedema) · 4+ = 8mm, >20 seconds rebound (severe, systemic — refer to physician). Key: unilateral = DVT red flag; bilateral = systemic cause; non-pitting = lymphedema/myxedema (not DVT/CHF). New 3+/4+ with dyspnea or chest pain = emergency referral.
Wells DVT CPR — corrected surgery timeframe and bilateral leg rule.
tap
Surgery criterion: recently bedridden >3 days OR major surgery within 12 weeks (requiring general or regional anesthesia) = +1. In patients with symptoms in both legs: score using the more symptomatic leg. Probability: >2 = High; 1–2 = Moderate; 0 = Low. Alternative diagnosis as likely as DVT = −2 (prevents false high scores). Ultrasound is gold standard for confirmation.
What is the correct definition of pain and why does it matter clinically in this scenario?
tap
Pain is a multiple system output activated by the brain based on perceived threat (Gifford 1998, IASP). It is NOT a direct readout of tissue damage — it is the brain's protective response. This matters because: (1) Pain can persist after tissue has healed (central sensitization). (2) Treatments targeting only tissue may be insufficient or wrong. (3) Patient education reducing threat perception directly reduces pain. The Mature Organism Model (Gifford): Inputs → Processing → Outputs — all three are treatment targets.
Central sensitization — mechanism and clinical implications.
tap
Repeated C-fiber firing → death of inhibitory interneurons in the dorsal horn → brain develops a "memory" for danger signals → responds faster with less stimulus. Result: hyperalgesia (exaggerated response to painful stimuli) and allodynia (pain with non-painful stimuli). Clinical implication: when pain persists beyond expected tissue healing timeline → central sensitization likely driving the experience. These patients need pain neuroscience education, graded exposure, and psychological support — NOT more structural imaging or passive tissue-based treatments.
Name 4 false assumptions about pain and the correct understanding for each.
tap
(1) "Direct link between tissue damage and pain" — False: pain is a brain output based on perceived threat, not tissue damage severity. (2) "Nociception = pain" — False: nociceptors are danger fibers; pain is a brain decision. (3) "Chronic pain means ongoing tissue damage" — False: tissue heals on predictable timelines; persistent pain = CNS sensitization. (4) "Pain is either physical or psychological" — False: pain is always both — it is a multisystem output integrating sensory, cognitive, emotional, and social inputs. The stress response (cortisol/adrenaline, immune changes) is also a pain output, not just pain sensation.
Stewart & Loftus (JOSPT 2018) — what is "therapeutic emplotment" and name 4 words to replace in this scenario?
tap
Therapeutic emplotment (Mattingly): framing therapy around what the patient CAN do and WILL be able to do — focusing on purpose and meaning, not limitation. Words have drug-like effects; patients in pain selectively attend to alarming interpretations. Four replacements: "bone on bone" → "narrowing/tightness" · "tear" → "pull" · "instability" → "needs more strength and control" · "chronic degenerative changes" → "normal age-related changes." Also: "trapped nerve" → "tight but can be stretched" · "wear and tear" → "normal age changes" · "you'll have to live with this" → "you may need to make some adjustments."
