Ankle & Foot — Anatomy, Pathology & Post-Op

The ankle and foot are the body’s foundation — the interface between you and the ground. With 26 bones, 33 joints, and over 100 ligaments, the foot must accomplish two contradictory tasks: absorb shock as a flexible adapter at heel strike, then become a rigid lever for push-off. The talar dome’s wedge shape, the windlass mechanism, and the arch system make this possible. The lateral ankle sprain is the most common MSK injury in sports, and loss of ankle dorsiflexion cascades dysfunction up the entire kinetic chain.

Osteology & Joints of the Ankle & Foot
The foot contains 26 bones (7 tarsals, 5 metatarsals, 14 phalanges), 33 joints, and over 100 ligaments. The ankle is a modified hinge joint; the subtalar joint provides the triplanar motion that makes walking on uneven surfaces possible.

Windlass Mechanism

The plantar aponeurosis wraps around the MT heads like a cable around a windlass. During toe extension (push-off), the aponeurosis tightens, pulling the calcaneus toward the MT heads → medial longitudinal arch rises → foot becomes a rigid lever for propulsion. This is the mechanical basis of the heel-to-toe gait cycle. Plantar fasciitis disrupts this mechanism.

Ottawa Ankle Rules

X-ray required if: bony tenderness at distal 6 cm of fibula or lateral malleolus, medial malleolus, base of 5th metatarsal, or navicular. Also: inability to bear weight immediately after injury AND for 4 steps at initial evaluation. Sensitivity >98% for fracture detection.

Silverskjöld Test

Differentiates gastrocnemius vs soleus tightness. Test ankle DF with knee extended (both muscles on stretch) then with knee flexed (gastroc relaxed). If DF improves with knee flexion → gastrocnemius tightness (MC). If DF remains limited with knee flexed → soleus tightness.

Acute Compartment Syndrome — Surgical Emergency

5 P’s: Pain (out of proportion, especially with passive stretch), Pressure (tense compartment), Paresthesias, Paralysis (late), Pulselessness (very late — do NOT wait for this).
Most reliable sign: pain with passive stretch of compartment muscles. Anterior compartment = passive PF causes pain. Compartment pressure >30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of diastolic = emergent fasciotomy. Delay >6 hours = irreversible necrosis (Volkmann’s equivalent in leg).

Arterial

Popliteal artery trifurcates below knee: Anterior tibial (crosses interosseous membrane anteriorly → becomes dorsalis pedis on dorsum of foot — palpate between EHL and EDL tendons).

Ankle & Foot Pathologies
The lateral ankle sprain is the most common MSK injury in sports. Know the mechanism, grading, and evidence-based rehab for each condition.

Special Tests

Anterior Drawer: stabilize tibia, translate calcaneus/talus anteriorly with ankle in slight PF (relaxes ATFL). Positive = excessive anterior translation + soft end-feel. Tests ATFL. Talar Tilt (Inversion Stress): stabilize tibia, invert calcaneus. Positive = excessive tilt >10° or >5° compared to other side. Tests CFL.

Chronic Ankle Instability (CAI)

30–40% of lateral ankle sprains develop chronic instability. Combination of: (1) mechanical instability (ligament laxity), (2) functional instability (proprioceptive deficit, peroneal weakness, altered neuromuscular control). Evidence: balance/proprioception training is the most effective intervention for CAI prevention. Single-leg stance on unstable surface, BAPS board, star excursion balance test for monitoring.

Achilles Tendinopathy & Rupture

▼
Tendinopathy

Overuse degeneration (tendinosis, not tendinitis — minimal inflammation). Two types: Insertional (at calcaneal attachment, associated with Haglund’s deformity, calcification) and Mid-substance (2–6 cm proximal to insertion — watershed zone of poor blood supply). Pain with activity, morning stiffness, palpable thickening.
Best evidence: eccentric loading program (Alfredson protocol: 3×15 reps, 2×/day, 12 weeks). Heavy slow resistance training also effective.

Achilles Rupture

Mechanism: sudden forceful PF (pushing off, landing). “Pop” + sudden pain + inability to push off. Palpable gap 2–6 cm proximal to insertion (watershed zone).
Thompson (Simmonds) test: prone, squeeze calf — normal = foot PFs; positive = no PF movement = rupture. Patient can still actively PF (other PFs compensate) — do NOT rely on active PF alone to rule out rupture.
Management: surgical repair vs functional rehab (both have similar outcomes in most studies). Early WB and ROM in both approaches. Avoid excessive stretch for 6–8 weeks.

Plantar Fasciitis

▼
Pathology & Presentation

MC cause of plantar heel pain. Degeneration of plantar aponeurosis at its origin on the medial calcaneal tuberosity. Risk factors: obesity, prolonged standing, tight gastroc-soleus, pes planus, sudden increase in activity.
Classic presentation: sharp heel pain with first steps in the morning (fascia contracts overnight, forced lengthening on first WB). Pain improves with activity but returns after prolonged WB.

Evidence-Based Treatment

Stretching: gastroc-soleus stretching + plantar fascia-specific stretch (cross-leg stretch: seated, cross affected leg over, pull toes into extension — palpate taut fascia band while stretching).
Strengthening: intrinsic foot muscle training (towel curls, short foot exercises).
Taping: low-dye taping for immediate relief.
Orthotics: prefabricated insoles effective for most.
Night splints maintain DF overnight (prevents morning pain). Heel calcaneal spur is NOT the cause — it’s a result of chronic traction. 90% resolve with conservative care in 12 months.

Clinical Examples
Applied clinical scenarios demonstrating examination findings, clinical reasoning, and treatment decision-making.
Clinical Example 1 — Grade II Lateral Ankle Sprain

19-year-old basketball player inverted right ankle. Moderate swelling, ecchymosis over lateral malleolus. Positive anterior drawer, mild talar tilt. Ottawa rules negative. Clinical reasoning: Grade II lateral sprain (ATFL complete + CFL partial). No XR needed (Ottawa SN >98%). Management: PRICE → early ROM → peroneal strengthening → proprioception. 30–40% develop chronic instability.

Clinical Example 2 — Plantar Fasciitis

55-year-old nurse with 6 weeks right heel pain, worst first steps AM. Tender medial calcaneal tuberosity. BMI 32. Tight gastroc. Clinical reasoning: Classic plantar fasciitis. Treatment: plantar fascia-specific stretch, gastroc stretching, intrinsic foot strengthening, low-dye taping, night splint. Spur is consequence, not cause. 90% resolve conservatively.

Why is the ankle most stable in dorsiflexion and most vulnerable in plantarflexion in this scenario?
tap
The talar dome is wider anteriorly than posteriorly (wedge shape). In DF, the wider anterior talus fills the mortise completely = close-packed position (maximum bony congruence). In PF, the narrow posterior talus allows play in the mortise = loose-packed. This is why most sprains occur in PF.
Drop foot: what nerve is injured, where is the vulnerable point, and what muscles are lost in this scenario?
tap
Common peroneal (fibular) nerve at the fibular head (superficial, vulnerable to compression). Loss of deep peroneal → anterior compartment (tibialis anterior, EHL, EDL) = can’t dorsiflex. Loss of superficial peroneal → lateral compartment (peroneus longus/brevis) = can’t evert. Steppage gait + foot slap.
Client with medial heel pain, worst with first steps in the morning. Diagnosis and best evidence treatment?
tap
Plantar fasciitis. Degeneration at medial calcaneal tuberosity. Best evidence: plantar fascia-specific stretch (cross-leg, pull toes into extension) + gastroc-soleus stretching + intrinsic foot strengthening. Night splints maintain DF. Low-dye taping for immediate relief. 90% resolve conservatively.
Silverskjöld test: how does it work and what does it tell you in this scenario?
tap
Tests ankle DF with knee extended (both gastroc + soleus on stretch) then knee flexed (gastroc relaxed, soleus still stretched). If DF improves with knee flexion = gastrocnemius tightness (MC). If DF still limited with knee flexion = soleus tightness. Determines which muscle to target in treatment.
