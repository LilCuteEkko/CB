Ankle & Foot — Anatomy, Pathology & Post-Op

Comprehensive ankle and foot reference covering ligament anatomy, inversion sprain rehab, Achilles tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis, and post-operative protocols.

Ligament Anatomy Achilles & Plantar Fasciitis Inversion Sprain Rehab Post-Op Ankle
Anatomy
Ankle & Foot Anatomy
The foot and ankle complex is the foundation of the kinetic chain. Triplanar motion (supination and pronation from tibial IR/ER) is translated into sagittal plane motion during ambulation. Ankle DF restriction has cascading effects all the way to the lumbar spine. Ligament strength ranking lateral ankle (weakest to strongest): ATFL < PTFL < CFL < Deltoid complex.

Lateral Ligaments — ATFL, CFL, PTFL

▼
ATFL — Anterior Talofibular Ligament

Primary function: resist anterior displacement of talus relative to ankle mortise; talocrural IR; plantarflexion. Most commonly injured in inversion sprain — weakest of the lateral ligaments (lowest maximal load to failure) but highest strain to failure. First ligament torn in an inversion injury in PF position. Accessory functions: resists anterior talar displacement from mortise + IR of talus within mortise.

CFL — Calcaneofibular Ligament

Extra-articular structure covered by peroneal tendons. Spans both talocrural AND subtalar joints (different axes of rotation). Larger and stronger than ATFL. Resists ankle inversion and calcaneal adduction throughout full ROM. Taut in DF; most taut resisting varus when in plantigrade position. If ankle is in neutral, CFL may be injured with ATFL or alone. CFL injury = more profound functional effect than isolated ATFL.

PTFL — Posterior Talofibular Ligament

Intracapsular but extrasynovial. Runs from posterior medial fibula to lateral tubercle of talus. Resists posterior movement of talus. Rarely injured in typical ankle sprain before CFL and ATFL. Movements that injure PTFL: extreme DF + foot ER + limb IR. More commonly involved in syndesmotic (high ankle) sprains. In DF: PTFL maximally stressed, CFL taut, ATFL loose. In PF: ATFL taut, CFL and PTFL become loose.

Medial Ligament — Deltoid Complex

Strongest ligament in foot/ankle. 6 bands total forming a triangular shape (deltoid). Typically injured with eversion ankle sprains. Four main fibers: Tibionavicular (resists lateral translation and ER of talus), Posterior tibiotalar (resists DF, lateral translation, ER), Tibiocalcaneal (resists abduction; tibiocalcaneal ligament is usually first ruptured with eversion injury), Anterior tibiotalar (strong deep fiber; resists abduction in PF and eversion — injury often associated with avulsion fracture).

Syndesmosis, Foot Arches & Postural Assessment

▼
Distal Tib-Fib (Syndesmosis) — High Ankle Sprain

AITFL + PITFL + interosseous membrane. Separation of distal tib-fib joint from rotation of talus in ankle mortise. MOI: excessive DF + tibial ER + planted foot (closed chain). Signs: deep global ankle pain; pain with ER of foot (talus widening the joint); pain exceeds amount of swelling; TTP over anterior distal tib-fib; +ER stress test and squeeze test; typically less swelling than lateral ankle sprain. Always rule out fractures and lateral ankle sprain. ORIF if diastasis present.

Foot Posture — Pes Planus vs. Pes Cavus

Pes Planus (pronated/"flat feet"): talus PF; places stress on medial/plantar structures; can lead to flexible foot during push-off; causes: gastroc-soleus tightness, post. tibialis dysfunction (decreased eccentric foot control). Pes Cavus (supinated/"high arch"): much less common; rigid/stiff foot; prevents normal pronation during stance — decreased shock absorption. Ottawa Ankle Rules: if bony tenderness at distal 6 cm fibula/lat. malleolus, medial malleolus, base of 5th metatarsal, or navicular — X-ray needed. Also: inability to bear weight immediately after injury AND for 4 steps at initial evaluation.

Foot Arches — Three Types

Medial longitudinal arch: highest; calcaneus-talus-navicular-3 cuneiforms-1st 3 metatarsals; creates space for plantar soft tissues with elastic properties. Support: tibialis anterior/posterior, fibularis longus, flexor digitorum longus, FHL, intrinsics, plantar ligaments, plantar aponeurosis. Lateral longitudinal arch: flatter, makes more ground contact; calcaneus-cuboid-4th/5th metatarsals. Transverse arch: coronal plane; metatarsal bases-cuboid-3 cuneiforms; fibularis longus and tibialis posterior maintain it.

Windlass Mechanism

During terminal stance and push-off, toe extension tightens the plantar fascia (like a windlass tightening a rope) — raising the medial arch and supinating the foot for rigid propulsion. The plantar fascia should be rigid during push-off. When the fascia is degenerated or inflamed, this mechanism is impaired — relevant in plantar fasciitis treatment (1st MTP extension stretches plantar fascia). Assess with windlass test at the medial calcaneal tuberosity.

Ankle & Foot Osteology — Complete Bone & Joint Reference

▼
Distal Tibia & Fibula

Tibia: Medial malleolus (palpable medial ankle bump; deltoid lig attaches), plafond (inferior articular surface — ceiling of ankle mortise), tibial articular surface for talus.

Fibula: Lateral malleolus (more distal and posterior than medial — extends ~1cm further; lateral collateral ligs attach). Distal tibiofibular joint (syndesmosis): fibrous joint essential for ankle stability. Stabilized by anterior & posterior inferior tibiofibular ligaments + inferior transverse tibiofibular ligament + interosseous ligament (continuous with interosseous membrane). High ankle sprain = syndesmosis injury.

Tarsal Bones (7)

Talus: body (trochlea/dome = articular surface for ankle mortise — wider anteriorly → more stable in dorsiflexion), neck, head. No muscle attachments. 60% covered by articular cartilage. Blood supply enters distally → AVN risk with talar neck fracture.

Calcaneus: largest tarsal bone. Sustentaculum tali (medial shelf supporting talus — FHL tendon passes under it), calcaneal tuberosity (Achilles inserts posteriorly), anterior process. Calcaneal fracture: axial loading (fall from height); “lover's fracture” — often bilateral, check for associated lumbar burst fracture.

Navicular: medial side, tuberosity palpable (tibialis posterior inserts). Cuboid: lateral side, groove for peroneus longus tendon. 3 Cuneiforms (medial/intermediate/lateral): distal row, articulate with metatarsals 1–3.

Key Joints

Talocrural (ankle) joint: Hinge-type synovial. Talus in mortise formed by tibial plafond + medial malleolus + lateral malleolus. Primary motion: dorsiflexion/plantarflexion. Talus wider anteriorly → more stable in DF (close-packed position), less stable in PF (most sprains occur in PF + inversion).

Subtalar (talocalcaneal): Between talus and calcaneus. Primary motion: inversion/eversion. Critical for adapting foot to uneven terrain.

Transverse tarsal (Chopart's joint): Talonavicular + calcaneocuboid. Midfoot flexibility. Surgeons amputate through this line.

Tarsometatarsal (Lisfranc's joint): Cuneiforms/cuboid with MT bases. Lisfranc ligament connects medial cuneiform to 2nd MT base — injury often missed on initial X-ray. Lisfranc injury: widening between 1st and 2nd MT bases.

Triplanar motion (NWB): Supination = inversion + adduction + PF. Pronation = eversion + abduction + DF. Note: older literature used “inversion” and “eversion” interchangeably with supination/pronation.

Foot Arches, Retinaculae & Vascular Supply

▼
Arches of the Foot

Arches are maintained by 3 mechanisms: (1) bone shape (passive), (2) ligaments (passive — plantar aponeurosis, spring ligament, long/short plantar ligaments), (3) muscles (active — extrinsic: tibialis posterior + peroneus longus; intrinsic: all plantar muscles). The arches function like a spring/shock absorber — store and release energy during gait.

Retinaculae (Tendon Restraints)

Extensor retinaculae (superior + inferior): restrain extensor tendons on dorsum of foot. Contain tendons of tibialis anterior, EHL, EDL, peroneus tertius. Flexor retinaculum (laciniate ligament): medial ankle, connects medial malleolus to calcaneus. Contains (posterior to anterior): tibialis posterior, FDL, posterior tibial artery + tibial nerve, FHL — mnemonic: Tom, Dick, ANd Harry. Tarsal tunnel is deep to this retinaculum (tarsal tunnel syndrome = tibial nerve compression). Peroneal retinaculae (superior + inferior): restrain peroneus longus and brevis posterior to lateral malleolus.

Vascular Supply

Anterior tibial artery crosses interosseous membrane anteriorly → descends on anterior leg between EHL and EDL → becomes dorsalis pedis artery on dorsum of foot (pulse palpable between EHL and EDL tendons). Dorsalis pedis gives off: lateral tarsal artery, arcuate artery (dorsal digital arteries), deep plantar artery (through 1st interosseous space → joins lateral plantar to form deep plantar arch).

Posterior tibial artery descends posterior leg → passes behind medial malleolus deep to flexor retinaculum (pulse palpable here) → divides into medial plantar (smaller, supplies medial 3.5 digits via plantar digital arteries) + lateral plantar (larger, forms deep plantar arch with deep plantar artery from dorsalis pedis). Fibular (peroneal) artery: branch of posterior tibial, supplies lateral leg.

Leg (Crural) Compartments — Framework & Fascia

▼
Crural Fascia & Compartment Boundaries

The leg (between knee and ankle) is divided into 4 compartments by the deep fascia (crural fascia), interosseous membrane, and intermuscular septa. Think of it like a cross-section of an orange — each wedge is a compartment separated by fibrous walls. These walls are non-expandable, which is why compartment syndrome is an emergency (swelling inside a rigid box compresses vessels and nerves).

Superficial fascia: thin, loses fat at knee, contains great/small saphenous veins + cutaneous nerves. Deep (crural) fascia: thick, continuous with periosteum on anterior + medial tibia. Thickens distally to form retinaculae at the ankle.

Dividing structures: (1) Anterior intermuscular septum (crural fascia to fibula — separates anterior from lateral). (2) Posterior intermuscular septum (crural fascia to fibula — separates lateral from posterior). (3) Interosseous membrane (tibia to fibula — separates anterior from deep posterior). (4) Transverse intermuscular septum (divides posterior into superficial and deep sub-compartments).

Acute Compartment Syndrome — Surgical Emergency

Increased pressure within a non-expandable fascial compartment → compromises blood flow → ischemia → necrosis. MC in the anterior compartment of the leg (smallest volume, tightest boundaries). Causes: fractures (esp. tibial shaft), crush injuries, reperfusion after vascular repair, tight casts.

5 P’s (late signs — don’t wait for all of them): Pain (out of proportion, especially with passive stretch of muscles in the compartment), Pressure (compartment feels tense/hard), Paresthesias (nerve compression), Paralysis (late — muscle death), Pulselessness (very late — arterial compromise). Earliest and most reliable sign: pain with passive stretch of the involved compartment (e.g., passive PF stretches anterior compartment muscles → excruciating pain). Treatment: emergency fasciotomy (surgical release of fascia). Compartment pressure >30mmHg or within 30mmHg of diastolic = fasciotomy indicated.

By compartment: Anterior = pain with passive PF + toe flexion. Lateral = pain with passive inversion. Deep posterior = pain with passive DF + toe extension. Superficial posterior = pain with passive DF.

Leg Nerve Pathways — Complete Routes Through the Leg

▼
Tibial Nerve (L4–S3)

Route: Branches from sciatic in popliteal fossa → most superficial structure of the 3 popliteal contents → passes deep to soleus (between gastrocnemius heads) → descends in deep posterior compartment with posterior tibial artery → at ankle: lies between FHL and FDL, posterior-inferior to medial malleolus → divides into medial plantar nerve (like median N of hand — supplies medial 3.5 digits plantar + abductor hallucis, FDB, FHB, 1st lumbrical) + lateral plantar nerve (like ulnar N of hand — supplies lateral 1.5 digits + remaining intrinsics). Also gives off: medial sural cutaneous (joins common peroneal branch to form sural N), medial calcaneal branch (heel sensation).

Common Peroneal (Fibular) Nerve (L4–S2)

Route: Branches from sciatic in popliteal fossa → follows medial border of biceps femoris tendon → wraps around fibular neck (superficial, against bone — MC injured nerve in LE) → immediately divides into:

Deep peroneal N: arises at fibular neck → pierces anterior intermuscular septum into anterior compartment → descends with anterior tibial artery between TA and EHL → supplies all anterior compartment muscles → at ankle continues onto dorsum of foot → sensation: 1st–2nd toe web space only.

Superficial peroneal N: arises at fibular neck → descends within lateral compartment → supplies peroneus longus + brevis → pierces crural fascia at distal 1/3 of leg to become subcutaneous → sensation: distal anterior leg + most of dorsum of foot (except 1st web space and lateral border).

Leg Blood Supply — Complete Arterial & Venous Reference

▼
Arterial Supply

Popliteal artery (deepest structure in popliteal fossa, against posterior femur — vulnerable in posterior knee dislocation) → gives off 5 genicular branches → divides into:

Anterior tibial artery: pierces interosseous membrane proximally → descends on anterior surface of membrane between TA and EDL → at ankle becomes dorsalis pedis artery on dorsum of foot. Pulse: palpable between EHL and EDL tendons on dorsum. Supplies anterior compartment.

Posterior tibial artery: larger terminal branch → descends deep in posterior compartment with tibial nerve → passes posterior-inferior to medial malleolus (deep to flexor retinaculum) → divides into medial + lateral plantar arteries = main blood supply to foot. Pulse: palpable behind medial malleolus. Gives off: fibular (peroneal) artery (supplies deep posterior + lateral compartments via perforating branches), tibial nutrient artery.

Fibular (peroneal) artery: branch of posterior tibial → descends in deep posterior compartment adjacent to lateral intermuscular septum. Supplies posterior + lateral compartments. No artery lives within the lateral compartment — it receives perforating branches from the fibular artery through the septum.

Venous Drainage

Deep veins: accompany arteries (venae comitantes) — anterior tibial, posterior tibial, fibular veins → popliteal vein → femoral vein. Superficial veins: Great saphenous vein (longest vein in body — begins medial foot, ascends anterior to medial malleolus, along medial leg/thigh → femoral vein at saphenous opening). Small (lesser) saphenous vein (begins lateral foot, ascends posterior to lateral malleolus, along posterior calf → popliteal vein). Clinical: varicose veins, DVT (calf tenderness, Homan’s sign — though unreliable, use Wells criteria + D-dimer + ultrasound), vein harvest for CABG from great saphenous.

Nerve Distribution Summary

Deep peroneal N: dorsum between 1st & 2nd toe; motor to anterior compartment + EDB. Superficial peroneal N: dorsum of foot (most); motor to lateral compartment. Tibial N → medial plantar N: medial 3.5 digits (plantar); motor to abductor hallucis, FDB, FHB, 1st lumbrical. Tibial N → lateral plantar N: lateral 1.5 digits (plantar); motor to remaining intrinsics. Sural N: lateral foot. Saphenous N: medial foot. Morton’s Neuroma: sensory branches of MPN + LPN between 3rd & 4th metatarsal heads.

Pathologies
Ankle & Foot Pathologies
Ankle and foot pathologies are organized by anatomical location. Plantar fasciitis is the most common foot condition. 85% of ankle sprains are lateral (ATFL). Achilles tendinopathy: eccentric loading is the cornerstone of treatment. Ottawa Ankle Rules determine X-ray need. Always check if the ankle is the cause of proximal kinetic chain dysfunction.

Plantar Fasciitis / Fasciosis — Plantar Heel Pain

▼
Background & Risk Factors

Fasciosis = degenerated, non-inflamed phase; Fasciitis = inflammatory phase. Degenerative irritation at insertion (medial calcaneal tuberosity). Structures: plantar fascia inflammation + increased thickness + altered heel fat pad + microtears. Risk factors: pes planus/cavus, limited ankle DF, high BMI (non-athletes), tight hamstrings/posterior chain, running, prolonged WB at work, underuse-to-overuse pattern. Demographics: 20–34 y/o and 75+; F > M runners; soccer, running, gymnastics, dance; jobs with prolonged standing.

Presentation (Classic)
  • Extreme pain with first steps in morning (cold, contracted stiff fascia; improves with warming up)
  • Plantar medial heel pain (medial calcaneal tuberosity)
  • More pain while barefoot; pain with prolonged sitting
  • Recent increase in WB activity (new job, training change)
  • TTP at proximal insertion; fascia feels like a hard string
  • Limited AROM/PROM talocrural DF (often bilateral)
  • Posterior chain tightness (Achilles + soleus)
Treatment Protocol

Conservative PT is #1 treatment. Manual therapy: improve DF ROM; STM/MFR to plantar fascia and posterior chain; joint mobs (improve DF and 1st MTP extension); deep friction massage (tool-assisted). Exercise: posterior chain flexibility (gastroc/soleus — downward dogs!); foot intrinsic strengthening (towel curls, marble pickups, toe taps); hip and core NM. Modalities: MHP (moist heat) not ice; KT application. Orthotics + night splints. Other conservative: corticosteroid or PRP injections; immobilization. Surgery (fasciotomy): release of proximal medial insertion — complication: flattening of longitudinal arch (global instability).

Lateral Ankle Sprain — Grades, 4-Phase Rehab & Functional Instability

▼
Grading
  • Grade 1: Minimal to no swelling; tenderness to ATFL; return to sport <2 weeks
  • Grade 2: Local swelling; diffuse lateral ankle tenderness; return to sport 2–6 weeks
  • Grade 3: Diffuse pain, swelling, bruising; return to sport >6 weeks. Grade III with mortise widening = ORIF
4-Phase Rehab
  1. Active inflammatory: Pain + edema control (STM, lymphatic drainage, KT); PROM/AROM; external support for WB; monitor gait — equal stance phase = external support no longer needed
  2. Fibroblastic repair: PWB → FWB; supported balance + proprioceptive training; joint mobs and MWM (improve DF, proprioception, WB tolerance)
  3. Remodeling: Unilateral balance training; UE and LE reaches; dynamic proprioceptive training
  4. Maturation: Jogging → running; sport-specific training; train MOI direction (proprioception in multiple planes)
Functional Ankle Instability (FAI)

Feeling of ankle instability due to proprioceptive and neuromuscular deficits from multiple ankle sprains. Damages afferent structures (capsules, ligaments, mechanoreceptors). Signs: giving way; decreased peroneal strength; difficulty on uneven surfaces; decreased balance (SEBT); inverted heel at heel strike. Conservative treatment: proprioceptive training main focus (4-week balance program reduces self-reported instability and improves joint position sense); NM re-education; stable → unstable surfaces and static → dynamic; wobble board, BAPS, Airex, Dynadisc, BOSU; progress to cognitive tasks with balance.

Peroneal Tendinopathy

Overuse + repeated inversion sprains. Pes cavus posture more common. Young ice skaters, dancers, runners. Signs: pain behind and distal to lateral malleolus; pain with resisted eversion; instability with toe walking. Treatment: identify training errors; proprioceptive training; MFR (tool-assisted); KT; cupping; orthotics for foot posture; joint mobs (address subtalar hypomobility, improve pronation/calcaneal eversion); normalize ankle ROM and strength. Surgery: peroneal tendoscopy; repair if longitudinal tear; retinaculum repair if tendon subluxation.

Achilles Tendinopathy — Noninsertional & Insertional; Achilles Rupture

▼
Noninsertional Achilles Tendinopathy (Most Common)

4–6 cm proximal to Achilles insertion — relatively avascular zone. Tendinosis (intratendinous degeneration without inflammation from repetitive microtrauma or aging) or peritendinitis (inflammation of peritendon from overuse). Demographics: 30–50 years old. Risk factors: decreased DF ROM; decreased PF strength; hyperpronated foot; pes cavus/planus; comorbidities (diabetes, HTN, obesity); long-distance runners; training errors. Presentation: intermittent stiffness + pain with exercise; post ankle stiffness decreasing as exercise continues; TTP of Achilles; thickening; decreased DF ROM (lack of 20° DF in knee ext = shortened gastroc; lack of 30° in knee flex = shortened soleus). Treatment: eccentric PF strengthening (№1 for both types — 3×15, 180 reps/day for 12 weeks per Dutton); MFR anterior chain (not posterior); lower level laser; DF mobility training; foot orthosis; KT/IDN/cupping.

Insertional Achilles Tendinopathy

Pain more distal (bone-tendon junction). Associated with Haglund's deformity and retrocalcaneal bursitis. Older recreational athletes; obese; poorer prognosis than noninsertional. Presentation: morning ankle stiffness; posterior heel pain; swelling that increases with activity (pain continues with activity — unlike noninsertional); TTP at bone-tendon interface; limited DF ROM.

Achilles Tendon Rupture

MOI: pushing off forefoot while extending knee; sudden aggressive DF with full WB; landing on PF foot. Findings: sudden calf pain; audible snap; swelling of calf; palpable defect ("hatchet strike"); positive Thompson squeeze test (squeeze calf in prone — no PF = positive = complete rupture). Males:females = 3:1. Surgery (open repair) = most common.

Haglund's Deformity & Retrocalcaneal Bursitis

Haglund's ("pump bump"): bony prominence of posterior superior calcaneal tuberosity (2–3 cm). Risk: poorly fitted shoes, cavus foot, genetic predisposition, decreased gastroc length. Conservative: soft heel counter, increase shoe size, Achilles stretch and strengthening, joint mobs for DF. Retrocalcaneal bursitis: inflamed bursa anterior to Achilles tendon + superior to insertion. Treat: open-backed shoes, heel lifts, MFR to posterior chain (gastroc/soleus, plantar fascia, hamstrings, ITB), improve DF ROM.

Additional Ankle/Foot Pathologies — Impingement, Tibialis Posterior, Sever's

▼
Anterior Ankle Impingement

Mechanical obstruction from osteophytes — secondary to talocrural hypomobility (decreased posterior talar glide). Gymnasts, soccer players, long-distance runners, ballet dancers; jobs with repeated squatting/lunging. Presentation: pinching in anterior talocrural joint line with squatting, lunging, descending stairs; pain aggravated by forced DF; ankle stiffness/blocking with DF; limited DF; +anterior impingement test (lunge keeping heel on floor = pain). Conservative: posterior talocrural joint mobs; MWM into DF; ankle distractions; MFR posterior compartment; lots of downward dogs!; strengthen dorsiflexors. Surgery: partial synovectomy; excision of osteophytes.

Tibialis Posterior Tendinopathy — 4 Stages

Progressive loss of medial longitudinal arch height (acquired flatfoot). Stage 1: medial ankle/foot ache worse with WB; tibialis posterior weakness; mild swelling; pain with resisted inversion. Stage 2: medial + lateral hindfoot pain; calcaneal valgus and forefoot abduction (toe-out, more in WB); tibialis posterior elongation; reduced inversion. Stage 3: uncorrectable rigidity of rearfoot + forefoot; OA in subtalar joint and transverse tarsal joints. Stage 4: complete rupture; navicular subluxed inferiorly; unable to produce toe-off in terminal stance. Observe WB foot posture — flat foot or pronated is common. Cause: poor eccentric control of posterior tibialis.

Sever's Disease — Calcaneal Apophysitis

Traction apophysitis at Achilles insertion on calcaneus. Self-limiting disease. As bone grows, gastroc has trouble keeping up → increased traction on apophysis. Boys 8–13, often bilateral. Growth spurt athletes. MOI: overuse with repetitive gastroc loading + growth spurt complications. Presentation: intermittent or continuous heel pain; pain with WB; increased pain with first steps in morning that does NOT get better after warming up (distinguishes from plantar fasciitis). Treatment: gastroc-soleus stretching; heel cups/wedges (decrease Achilles tension); avoid barefoot walking short-term; wear cushioned shoes; NSAID.

High Ankle Sprain Rehab Phases
  • Acute: Rigid brace + pain relief; manual interventions for edema
  • Subacute: Light strengthening + balance (without symptoms); 6+ weeks for return to sport; criteria: complete SL hop 15× without pain and good form
  • Advanced: Sport-specific training; SL broad jump, vertical jump, 10 crossover hops at 80% contralateral side
Post-Operative
Ankle Post-Operative Rehabilitation
Post-op ankle rehab depends heavily on what was repaired. Achilles repair: avoid passive DF beyond 0° for first 4 weeks (can overstretch the repair — difficult to regain PF strength). Lateral ligament reconstruction: avoid passive inversion stress to the repair. ORIF: immobilization until bony healing confirmed, then aggressive ROM and strengthening.

Achilles Tendon Repair

▼
Surgical Procedure

Open repair most common. Longitudinal incision proximal to heel. Evacuate wound, re-approximate tendon, suture together. Males:females 3:1 for Achilles rupture.

WB & ROM Progression
  • 0–6 weeks: NWB (splint/cast/CAM boot)
  • Week 4–6: WBAT in CAM boot + heel wedges (remove one wedge per week)
  • Week 8+: WBAT in CAM boot without wedges → WBAT in tennis shoe
  • ROM first 4–8 wks: AVOID passive DF beyond 0°
  • Resisted AROM into PF = allowed early
Key Complication: PF Strength Deficit

Can be hard to regain. Over-stretching the repair → inability to achieve full strength or power. This is why passive DF beyond neutral is avoided in early phases — healing tissue must be protected from excessive tensile loads. Stay on top of wound inspection. RSD/CRPS or chronic pain are possible complications. Return to work/sport: Sedentary work 2–6 weeks; Manual labor/sport 16+ weeks.

Lateral Ligament Reconstruction (Broström / Broström-Gould)

▼
Procedure Types

Broström: direct repair of ATFL and CFL with sutures. Broström-Gould: repair ATFL + CFL + reinforce CFL with extensor retinaculum. Graft: hamstring graft most common for ligamentous replacement when direct repair not possible. Population: younger females; indoor/court sports; Grade III ankle sprain with radiographic instability; failed conservative treatment; symptomatic functional instability.

Rehabilitation

Typically seen 4–6 weeks post-op. WB: Initially NWB within first 6 weeks (surgeon-dependent); splint → cast → CAM boot; progress to WBAT in CAM boot, NWB outside of boot. ROM: AVOID PASSIVE INVERSION STRESS to the repair. Late rehab goals: train proprioception in multiple planes; remember the patient's MOI — need to prepare ankle for the forces that caused injury. Return to sedentary work: 2–6 weeks. Return to manual labor/sport: 3–6 months.

Ankle ORIF — Danis Weber Classification & Rehab

▼
Danis Weber Classification

Type A: fracture below syndesmosis — most stable. Type B: fracture at the level of the syndesmosis — partial or total syndesmotic rupture — most common. Type C: fracture above the syndesmosis — typically with full syndesmotic disruption — least stable. Surgical procedure: fixate bone with hardware (screws, plates, nails); potential ligament reconstruction. Appropriate for anyone — no age restriction.

Rehab Framework

Immobilization via splint, cast, or CAM boot. WB status depends on fracture type, concomitant injuries, and comorbidities. With isolated ORIF, typically no ROM limitations — but ankle is typically stiff and swollen. Progress: PROM → AAROM → AROM → closed chain strengthening. Gait training. MFR to improve gastroc/soleus extensibility (critical following immobilization). Posterior talocrural glides to increase DF. MWM into DF. Restore subtalar joint mobility (calcaneal inversion/eversion; supination/pronation). Complications: DVT, infection, hardware failure, RSD/CRPS, chronic pain.

What differentiates noninsertional from insertional Achilles tendinopathy clinically?
tap
Noninsertional (most common): 4–6 cm proximal to insertion — relatively avascular zone. Demographics: 30–50 y/o; active long-distance runners; better prognosis. Stiffness and pain at start of activity that IMPROVES as exercise continues (tendinosis pattern). TTP at mid-substance Achilles. Pain eases after warm-up. Decreased DF (20° with knee extended = gastroc; 30° with knee flexed = soleus). Insertional: Pain at bone-tendon junction (distal). Associated with Haglund's deformity and retrocalcaneal bursitis. Older, less active recreational athletes; obese; poorer prognosis. Swelling increases with activity AND pain continues with activity (doesn't ease with warm-up). Treatment for both: eccentric PF strengthening = #1 (3×15, bodyweight first, 180 reps/day for 12 weeks per Dutton).
What is the Ottawa Ankle Rule and what are the X-ray indications?
tap
Ottawa Ankle Rules: Clinical decision tool to determine if X-ray is needed following ankle trauma. Only ONE criterion needs to be present. X-ray indicated with bony tenderness at: distal 6 cm of posterior edge of fibula or tip of lateral malleolus; distal 6 cm of posterior edge of tibia or tip of medial malleolus; base of 5th metatarsal; navicular. Most common trigger: inability to bear weight immediately after injury AND for 4 steps at initial evaluation. Sensitivity of Ottawa Ankle Rules: 77%. Compare to Bernese Ankle Rules (sensitivity 55%) — OAR is more sensitive (better rule-out). Use OAR in inversion/ankle trauma; use clinical judgment for fracture suspicion even with negative rules if concerned.
What distinguishes plantar fasciitis morning pain from Sever's disease morning pain?
tap
Plantar fasciitis: Extreme pain with FIRST STEPS in the morning that IMPROVES with warming up (cold, contracted fascia loosens as blood flow increases). Pain returns after long periods of sitting. Age: 20–34 runners AND 75+ sedentary. TTP at medial calcaneal tuberosity (plantar surface). Limited ankle DF. Posterior chain tightness. Sever's Disease (calcaneal apophysitis): Boys 8–13, growth spurts. Heel pain with WB. Increased pain first steps in morning that does NOT improve with warming up — activity makes it worse. TTP at posterior heel (calcaneal tuberosity — Achilles insertion). Often bilateral. Gastroc unable to keep up with bone growth — increased traction on apophysis. Self-limiting. Treatment: gastroc-soleus stretching, heel cups, cushioned shoes, avoid barefoot walking.
